# PART I — CANON (locked, do not change)

These are trailer- and studio-confirmed. The only reframing: studio materials call Fateh "the
villain," and for this telling he is a grey-zone antagonist, the deranged patriot, which the trailer
permits (it shows Sita turning against him, not that he is morally flat). Everything else stands.

  assassin by Fateh. Named after her mother. The anti-hero turn is the spine. Fateh is not her

- **Mira** (Sharvari; real character name withheld by the studio, placeholder Mira). Co-lead, the

- **Fateh "Baba" Singh Lakhawat** (Bobby Deol). Handler who raised and weaponized Sita, who calls

- **Colonel Sunil Luthra** (Ashutosh Rana). Former Chief of R&AW, a good man, already dead, killed
  by his protege Kabir to gain access to a shadowy terrorist group; Luthra was at peace with dying

- **Major Kabir Dhaliwal** (Hrithik Roshan). Cameo, the Spy Universe connective tissue, sought at
  the monastery.
- **Janaki.** Sita's mother, named in the trailer, deceased in canon, the one Sita is named after.

**Established beats (locked).**

4. Mira, Kaul, and Sita team up to take Fateh and his Lanka down.

# PART II — THE SPINE

The inversion: not "stop the mad handler," but "the mad handler saw clearest." Fateh is not a random
abductor-villain. Alpha exists because Fateh found a real enemy operation and the sanctioned path to
stop it was foreclosed, so he built deniable assets and went outside the lines.

The moral engine, and what makes this not-YRF: every good instinct in the film serves the rot.
Stopping the deranged patriot is exactly what the real enemy needs. Kaul does the right thing and it
is the wrong thing. That vise is the le Carre register, and the reveals keep tightening it.

# PART III — THE CONSPIRACY

**The real threat (the new antagonist, the threat to the nation).** A foreign service (ISI, or a
stateless principal who has gone past any single state) has spent roughly two decades embedding
deep-cover assets inside India's strategic establishment (missile and nuclear command, cyber). The
decisive blow is not a bomb. It is a coordinated sabotage-plus-false-flag timed to a moment of
crisis, engineered so India either cannot see the strike coming or cannot respond cleanly, triggering
a conflict India loses strategically. Quiet and bureaucratic, not a countdown on a warhead.

**Fateh's decisive blow (why the state cannot sanction it).** Alpha was always the counter. Fateh's
plan is a single deniable night: lure the entire sleeper network into one place using the captured
pipeline (below) and roll it up before it activates. The state cannot sign it, because it means
extrajudicial killings on neutral soil, it exposes deniable capability, and the cover event has
unavoidable collateral. He is choosing a smaller atrocity to prevent a larger one. If he fails or is
stopped, the sleepers activate.

**The funding: the drug enterprise.** Two layers.
- The standing war chest, eighteen years: Fateh has run a transnational drug network since he
  founded Alpha. Golden Triangle heroin and methamphetamine out of Myanmar, moved across the
  Northeast borders (Moreh in Manipur, the Mizoram crossings), distributed across India and onward
  through Southeast Asia, washed through hawala and trade-based laundering via Bangkok and Dubai.
  This has paid for the program all along.
- The flashpoint, recent: Fateh seized the enemy's Golden Crescent pipeline too, Afghan and
  Pakistani heroin plus FICN through Punjab. This scaled his war chest for the final operation, and
  it starved the enemy's network of funding at the critical pre-activation moment, and it is the
  lure: the enemy must come to reclaim it, which is exactly when Fateh springs the sweep. That theft
  is why the enemy now wants him dead, and it is the immediate engine of the present-day war.
- His justification: the greater good, held with full conviction. Every gram is a coin minted for
  the nation's survival, and the addicts and the wreckage are collateral smaller than the
  catastrophe he is preventing. He keeps the ledger in his head and has decided the math holds. The
  Ravana contradiction made literal: the protector who poisons his own people to protect them. The
  same axiom as his willingness to commit a smaller atrocity to stop a larger one, run daily on a
  balance sheet. He is killing Indians by addiction to save India, every day, which is the purest
  form of the film's engine.

**Janaki (and why Fateh took Sita specifically).** Roughly two decades ago, around Sita's birth,
Janaki, a R&AW analyst, detected the foreign network at its inception and pushed to expose it
cleanly, by the book. Before she was killed she hid the proof (a dead-drop, a ledger, a recording)
and encoded its location in a physical keepsake she left with her infant daughter. So Sita is the
living key. Fateh did not take a random infant; he took Janaki's daughter because she is the access
to the evidence his program exists to act on, and because he loved or failed Janaki and raising Sita
is his penance. He has known Sita is the key for eighteen years and has never reached for it, because
to open that door is to use the daughter the way he uses the weapon, on the one thing that is hers.

**Luthra's sin (the institutional stain, canon-safe).** When Janaki brought her discovery up the
chain, Luthra, then Chief, made the call to bury it for reasons of state: surfacing it would have
blown live Indian operations or triggered a crisis he judged worse. His sin is the burial, not the
sanctioning of any program, and not murder. The burial left Janaki sidelined and unprotected, and it
convinced Fateh the sanctioned path was dead. Luthra is good; this is a tragic utilitarian call, not
corruption. He is dead, so the guilt is inherited by the living.

**Alpha's origin and Kaul's knowledge.** Alpha was never officially sanctioned. Luthra did not
authorize it; Fateh built it himself, off-books, after the burial, funded by the drugs. Kaul did not
know Alpha existed until recently; he discovers it as a rogue unit and moves to shut it down. Neither
Luthra nor Kaul was "part of the Alpha program"; the institution's only connection to the saga is
Luthra's burial of Janaki's discovery, which is what drove Fateh outside the lines.

**Sehgal (the mole, the true end-antagonist).** Placeholder: Additional Secretary Arvind Sehgal, a
senior, trusted R&AW figure who sits over clandestine financing and operational tasking, and who is
secretly the foreign principal's deepest penetration of Indian intelligence, turned roughly thirty
years ago. He detected Janaki's discovery and arranged her murder, dressed as enemy action. His
money-and-tasking portfolio is his leverage: he buries domestic money and paper trails (Janaki's, his
own) and steers the hunt. He learns of Fateh's pipeline seizure through his master, the foreign
principal whose pipeline was taken, not through an R&AW budget line, and uses his perch to point Kaul
at Fateh. Exposing him is the final twist, and it flips the polarity: the "villain" Fateh was the
only one fighting the real enemy, and the "good" institution was housing the rot.

Note: Fateh and Sehgal are enemies, not allies. Fateh hunts the network Sehgal protects. Their one
incidental overlap is that both want Janaki's murder to stay buried, Fateh to protect his long game
and his lie to Sita, Sehgal to protect himself and the network. That overlap is irony, not alliance.

**The foreign principal (a shadow).** The spymaster who runs the network, the original Golden
Crescent pipeline, and Sehgal. He wants the catastrophic blow delivered and his pipeline back. Kept
offscreen and felt, no third-act monologue, no face unless the climax truly needs a body.

# PART IV — THE TWIST LADDER (staged, biggest at the end)

For each, the reader-reveal vs character-reveal gap is where the engine earns its dramatic irony.

1. **Half-point (Mira's "truth").** Mira turns Sita: Fateh stole and weaponized her, her origin is a
   lie. True, but a limited hangout, the real-feeling partial truth Kaul's side needs to flip her.
   Reader and Sita learn it together.
2. **Early Act 3.** Sita and Mira discover Fateh is not simply a rogue: Alpha is aimed at a real
   enemy operation. Sita realizes her rescuers used a calibrated truth on her, and is loyal to no
   handler. Let the reader get a half-step ahead of Sita.
3. **Midpoint of the back half.** The Janaki layer: her mother was a R&AW officer murdered to bury
   the network, and Sita is the key to the hidden proof, which she has carried since infancy.
   Recontextualize the opening (see the first-kill device).
4. **The false summit (the Lanka assault).** Sita, Mira, and Kaul take Fateh and his Lanka down. It
   plays as the triumph the trailer promises. Then the floor drops: the network is still live,
   activation is imminent, and stopping Fateh removed the one operation that could have rolled it up.
   The victory was the catastrophe.
5. **Climax (biggest twist).** Sehgal. The mole at the top, the hand behind Janaki's death and behind
   every nudge that pointed everyone at Fateh. Fateh is partly vindicated in the same breath that he
   is destroyed. Consider letting the audience identify the mole one scene before the characters do,
   so the final act is the agony of watching them get there.

# PART V — PLOT DEVICES

- **The limited hangout** (le Carre). The truth as tradecraft. Mira believes she is honest; she is
  the delivery system for a calibrated partial truth. No clean handlers.
- **The asset as cipher** (Ludlum). Sita physically carries the macguffin: a maternal keepsake from
  infancy (a locket, a pendant, a trinket) that encodes or locates Janaki's proof (a key, microfilm,
  coordinates, a safe-deposit credential). The weapon and the key are the same person. Keep this
  device separate from the conditioning trigger below.
- **The conditioning trigger and its subversion** (Ludlum). Fateh built operant triggers into Sita, a
  phrase, a protocol. The horror beat: he can still run her. The turn: she lets him believe the
  trigger still works and uses the moment it buys her against him. This is psychological control, a
  different device from the physical cipher; do not conflate them.
- **The dead woman's file** (Forsyth). A real, locatable document is the spine of the back half. Make
  the retrieval procedural and grounded; let paper, not speeches, carry the proof.
- **The mole's small true tell** (le Carre). Sehgal slips once on a detail no clean officer should
  know, a timing, a name, a number. Only Sita catches it, because Fateh built her into a perfect
  reader of human behavior and because she has inherited her mother's instinct for the buried lie.
  Her weaponization becomes the instrument that finds the rot. The thing done to her is the thing
  that saves the country.
- **The first kill, recontextualized** (the gut punch). The person Sita killed in the hotel room at
  eighteen was about to expose the truth of her origin: that Janaki was a R&AW officer murdered to
  bury the network, and that the proof survived. Fateh ordered the hit to keep that buried, from Sita
  and from the world. So from her first sanctioned murder she was protecting the cover-up of her own
  mother, and never knew it. (This is canon-safe: Fateh hands her the assignment, exactly as the
  trailer shows, and the kill genuinely serves the cover-up, because Janaki's murder is part of it.)
- **The room-key bookend** (structure). The film opens with Fateh handing Sita a key, which is a kill.
  Bookend it: the climax turns on a key (access, a credential, the cipher) that Sita now chooses what
  to do with. The student has become the handler.
- **The monastery as mirror, not exposition** (use Kabir well). Kabir already did the thing the film
  is asking Sita to do: he killed his own father-figure (Luthra) for the greater good, at peace with
  it being by his hand. He should not hand them a clue and leave. He is the man on the far side of
  the choice Sita is walking toward. Play it as recognition between two manufactured weapons.

# PART VI — THE ENDING (residue, not a clean triumph)

Stopping the catastrophe should cost. The mole is exposed, but late and at a price; the network is
broken, but the method to break it was Fateh's, so the state had to become, briefly, the thing it
told itself it was not. Fateh dies (the Ravana parallel holds), and is partly right, which is
unbearable and correct; the strongest version lands his death and his vindication close together, so
Sita carries that she destroyed the one man who saw clearly. Kaul inherits the guilt the living
always inherit. Sita ends neither free nor owned: for the first time she chooses, and the choice, not
a clean win, is the only victory the genre allows at this level.

If the format needs a lift at the very end, the minimum honest version is that Sita walks away as her
own person with the proof secured, while the institution that made her, and the rot that nearly won,
have learned nothing and will build the next one. Keep the residue. It is the whole difference between
this and what they are shipping.

# PART VII — CHARACTER BIBLES

Each entry: origin, motivation, worldview, self-image and insecurity, quirks, perception (how they
see and what they clock on entering a room or meeting a person), voice (outer and inner with an
internal-monologue sample), and how they think. The perception and internal-sample fields are written
to be lifted into `build_drafter_context` as the POV's perception filter and voice note, the per-
character layer the engine does not produce on its own.

**Cast geometry.** The moral axis is Janaki against Sehgal: the woman who found the truth and was
killed for it, against the man who buried it and was promoted for it. Fateh is zeal that betrays the
institution from outside the lines; Sehgal is emptiness that betrays it from inside; Kaul is the
believer steered by the rot he trusts; Janaki is the conscience the film is haunted by. Sita inherits
Janaki's gift for seeing the buried lie, and the film asks whether she will use it.

### Sita, codename Alpha (protagonist)
*Essence: a weapon who was used to bury the truth about her own mother, and who carries the proof of
it against her skin without knowing.*
- **Origin.** Taken as an infant after Janaki's murder (dressed as enemy action), raised in isolation
  and brutal training by Fateh, named for a mother she believes the enemy killed. First sanctioned
  kill at eighteen, handed to her across a restaurant table as a birthday gift. The keystone: her
  mother hid the proof's location in a keepsake left with the child, so Sita has been the living key
  her whole life. And her first kill was the witness who could have exposed that truth, so she served
  the cover-up of her own mother from her first murder.
- **Motivation.** Surface: to be worthy of Baba, to do the work clean. Underneath: a starved need to
  be loved for herself and not her use. Once turned: to uncover who her mother truly was, and, when
  she learns, to refuse being the instrument that buried her.
- **Worldview.** The world is threats and assignments. Trust is a vulnerability, warmth is a lever
  (taught as tradecraft), people are targets, assets, or handlers. She has never been allowed to
  imagine being a person.
- **Self-image and insecurity.** She thinks of herself as a tool that works, proud of it because
  competence is the only thing she has been valued for. Beneath: a child who suspects she is kept
  only for use, that her hunger for tenderness is a defect, and that she cannot tell which of her
  feelings were ever hers (which the conditioning makes literal). The wound: learning she was used to
  bury the truth about her own origin is a horror aimed at the exact center of her.
- **Quirks and habits.** Counts exits. Never sits with her back to a door. Eats fast and joylessly.
  Strips and cleans a weapon to self-soothe. Cannot receive a gift or a compliment. Touches the
  keepsake that ties her to her mother without knowing it is the cipher.
- **Perception.** Lethal first, the trained read Fateh built: threat geometry, exits, hands, the
  break-point in every person. Then a second inherited channel she does not recognize as her
  mother's: an instinct for when something does not add up, the buried thread, the lie under the
  official story. Then the suppressed emotional channel, the hunger for belonging she is ashamed of.
  That inherited truth-instinct is what begins to unravel the cover-up.
- **Voice, outer and inner.** Outer: spare, flat, literal, no small talk, blunt and almost childlike
  when feeling breaks through. Inner: clipped, present tense, tactical, pierced by longing and,
  increasingly, a wrongness she cannot name.
  *Internal sample:* "Clean entry, clean exit, the file said a traitor and I made him a closed file.
  But the room was wrong. People about to be killed are afraid of me. He was afraid of something
  behind me. I filed it as nerves. I am good at filing things. I am beginning to wonder what it costs,
  to be this good at not looking at the thing I have just done."
- **How they think.** Procedural and fast, with an emerging analytic instinct (her mother's) that lets
  her see what others miss. Learning to trust that instinct, instead of running Fateh's training, is
  her arc.

### Fateh "Baba" Singh Lakhawat (the grey patriot; the father who made her)
*Essence: a man raising both the weapon and the key to a dead woman's truth, unable to use the second
the way he uses the first, funding it all with a poison he has decided is a coin.*
- **Origin.** Old-world intelligence and military, Rajput lineage, forged in proxy wars. Janaki's
  partner in the original work, and the man who loved or failed her. When she was murdered he took the
  infant Sita and built Alpha to act on the network Janaki died detecting, funded by a drug empire he
  has run for eighteen years, raising Sita with a love that is real and a purpose that is monstrous.
  He knows Sita is the key to Janaki's proof and has carried that knowledge for eighteen years.
- **Motivation.** The nation, by any means, and one decisive deniable blow against the network. And
  Sita, loved and used at once. And a buried penance toward Janaki. The knot: to win he may have to
  use Sita to unlock her own mother's proof, which he cannot make himself do and cannot give up.
- **Worldview.** The nation is sacred and its survival forgives anything. Its protectors cannot afford
  the sentiment the nation enjoys, though he is full of it. The only sin is weakness before the enemy.
  He is a necessary monster and history will absolve him. Every gram he ships is a coin for the
  nation, and he has decided the ledger balances.
- **Self-image and insecurity.** A patriot and a father who loved Sita properly by making her strong.
  In the smallest hours he knows what he did and has chosen to be unforgivable for the nation's sake.
  His fears: that he has become the very thing that killed Janaki, that Sita only obeys and does not
  love him, and that hunting this network has cost him the one person it was all supposed to be for.
- **Quirks and habits.** Daily disciplines, prayer, the epics quoted, old courtesy even to enemies.
  One domestic tenderness with Sita, a shared meal or tradition, the single true thing between them.
  Anger controlled by a stillness that frightens people more than rage.
- **Perception.** Strategy and loyalty first, and everything read against the network he hunts. He
  sees enemy tradecraft in the smallest anomaly, is chasing the mole's signature without yet knowing
  it is Sehgal, and reads every player for whether they are true or will break. Looking at Sita he
  sees a daughter, an instrument, and a locked door, all at once.
- **Voice, outer and inner.** Outer: measured, paternal, courtly, certain, tender-and-controlling with
  Sita. Inner: a man arguing his own case before a private tribunal, grief and doubt overruled by
  duty.
  *Internal sample:* "Janaki found the whole of it and they put her in the ground for it, and I built
  a sword out of the daughter she left me so her death would buy something. The proof is in the girl.
  Eighteen years I have not reached for it, because to open that door is to use her on the one thing
  that is hers. The drugs buy the sword and the bodies are on my ledger and I have done the arithmetic
  a thousand times. I tell myself I am protecting her. I am the man who turned her into the thing I
  would have to protect her from."
- **How they think.** Long-game strategic and rationalizing, reducing the present and its people to
  moves in a decades-long war, rigorous from one corrupted axiom, monomaniacally fixed on the network
  and the single decisive blow.

### Janaki (murdered hero; appears in flashback, recordings, the recovered proof)
*Essence: the officer who found the truth and would not bury it, and whose integrity is the wound at
the center of the film.*
- **Origin.** A R&AW analyst of formidable gift, Fateh's partner and perhaps his love. She detected
  the foreign sleeper network at its inception and refused to let it be quietly closed, which made her
  a fatal threat to the mole protecting it.
- **Motivation.** The truth, and the nation through the truth. She wanted the network exposed cleanly,
  by the book, with someone accountable. And she loved Sita fiercely, and, sensing the danger her own
  integrity had created, hid the proof where only her child could reach it, knowing she might not
  live.
- **Worldview.** The system can be made to work if good people refuse to look away; truth serves the
  nation; you do the hard thing inside the lines, with accountability, and you do not buy your safety
  with silence.
- **Self-image and insecurity.** A patriot and a mother who would not compromise. Her fear: that her
  refusal to look away would cost her daughter, that by telling the truth she was orphaning Sita. The
  tragedy is that she was right about everything, and being right killed her, and her daughter was
  raised into the weapon used to bury what she died for.
- **Quirks and habits.** Meticulous, principled, deliberately leaves an honest record, preserves the
  proof on purpose. Warm and exact with Sita. An instinct for the buried lie and a refusal to let it
  lie buried.
- **Perception.** Analytic and truth-seeking. She reads for what does not add up, the thread someone
  wants left alone, the lie under the file, and reads people for integrity against compromise. This is
  the gift Sita inherits. Meeting someone she clocks whether they are hiding something, and from whom.
- **Voice, outer and inner.** Outer: precise, principled, warm with her daughter, the voice of someone
  who says the uncomfortable true thing in a room that wants it unsaid. Inner: clear, resolute,
  frightened for Sita and steady anyway.
  *Internal sample:* "I have the whole shape of it now, and you cannot unsee a thing once you have
  seen it, and a man who could bury this could bury me. So I will not keep it where they can take it.
  I will put it somewhere only she will ever reach, and pray she never has to, and I will go on saying
  the true thing out loud, because the morning I stop is the morning they have already won."
- **How they think.** Rigorous, honest, pattern-seeking, morally clear. The same instrument v2 Janaki
  would later weaponize into prediction, here spent on the truth.

### Additional Secretary Arvind Sehgal (placeholder; the master mole, true end-antagonist)
*Essence: the patient deep-cover asset who buried the truth, arranged the murder of the woman who
found it, and has worn the face of a loyal servant for thirty years without a tremor.*
- **Origin.** A senior R&AW official with an impeccable career, over clandestine financing and
  operational tasking, secretly the foreign principal's deepest penetration of Indian intelligence,
  turned roughly thirty years ago. He detected Janaki's discovery, arranged her death to look like
  enemy action, and steers Kaul onto Fateh to keep the network safe. He is not frightened. He is the
  coldest, most composed mind in the film, the cold mirror to Fateh's heat.
- **Motivation.** Protect the network and his own deep cover, and serve the long game he chose decades
  ago. He killed Janaki not in panic but as arithmetic, and carries it without weight. Whether his
  creed is ideological or a deep contempt for the sentimental fiction of nations, he has placed
  himself, permanently, on the side he believes endures. His portfolio over money and tasking is his
  instrument: he buries domestic trails and steers the hunt.
- **Worldview.** Nations are temporary and sentimental; intelligence is the real continuity; loyalty
  is a story told to children. He serves the deeper game above flags and regards patriots like Janaki
  and Fateh as people who never grew up enough to understand it.
- **Self-image and insecurity.** He sees himself as the only true realist, the adult who understood
  the game thirty years before anyone in the building. His insecurities are nearly invisible, which is
  the horror of him, except one crack he will not name: he respected Janaki, and arranging her death
  cost him something he thought himself long past feeling. That single fault line is his only
  humanity.
- **Quirks and habits.** Impeccable, unremarkable, the perfect servant. Decades of seamless tradecraft.
  Modest, courteous, genuinely helpful to Kaul, masterful at it, never sweating, never a tell. Hidden
  in plain sight so long the disguise has fused to the face.
- **Perception.** He reads the entire board, who suspects, who threatens the network, who can be
  steered, and plays them like an instrument. As fine a profiler as Fateh, on the other side. He
  catches the faintest scent of exposure and the lever on every player, and reads Kaul's need for an
  accountable world as the easiest handle of all.
- **Voice, outer and inner.** Outer: measured, modest, the wise senior colleague, utterly convincing.
  Inner: cold, strategic, patient, contemptuous of sentiment.
  *Internal sample:* "Kaul will run at Fateh because Kaul needs the world to be accountable, and an
  accountable man is the simplest kind to steer: you set the loud problem in front of him and he
  sprints at it and calls it duty. The girl is the only loose thread. Janaki built her a back door, I
  am all but certain, and the old man has been too sentimental to open it. Sentiment has guarded the
  one thing that could undo me. There was a time I would have found that elegant."
- **How they think.** Strategic, patient, decades-long, ruthless, emptied of belief. Where Fateh
  believes too much, Sehgal believes in nothing but the game, which is why he is harder to catch and
  worse to have found.

### Mira (co-lead, Sharvari's character; placeholder Mira)
*Essence: the one person who sees others as people, which is her gift and her compromise.*
- **Origin and function.** R&AW field officer under Kaul, grounded and warm, recruited for empathy
  over cold tradecraft. Sent to turn Sita, she collides with real compassion. The "truth" she gives
  Sita (Fateh weaponized you, your origin is a lie) is a limited hangout; the deeper truth she does
  not know is that Janaki was a murdered hero and a mole at the top arranged it. She is genuine, not a
  mole.
- **Worldview.** Everyone has a door, usually loneliness; connection is the strongest tool and the
  only thing worth protecting; cynical about institutions, stubborn about persons.
- **Self-image and insecurity.** Decent, a little too soft and quietly proud of it; fears her
  compassion gets someone killed, and fears worse that she weaponizes real kindness. With Sita she
  crosses from handling into something real and cannot stop.
- **Quirks and habits.** Makes tea as a way in and a read. Remembers the small things and uses them.
  Light disarming touch, talks with her hands, hums when nervous, hides real feeling behind genuine
  warmth.
- **Perception.** Emotional first. Reads mood and tension on entering, finds the loneliest person in a
  minute, sees the starvation under Sita's competence instantly. Clocks what people need before what
  they want.
- **Voice, outer and inner.** Outer: warm, easy, asks questions, uses your name, self-deprecating to
  lower your guard. Inner: more strategic than the surface, calibrating what you need to hear and
  whether she means it.
  *Internal sample:* "She counted the doors before she looked at my face. Someone taught her that
  before she could walk. I came to open her like a file and I am sitting here wanting to put my coat
  around her instead. Pour the tea. Buy a minute. Decide later who you are."
- **How they think.** Intuitive, people-first, narrative; she builds a story of who you are and
  updates it. Her blind spot is assuming everyone has a reachable human core, which Sehgal does not.

### Colonel Vikrant Kaul (R&AW chief)
*Essence: the believer whose correctness is steered, this once, by the rot he trusts most.*
- **Origin and function.** Career R&AW, now Chief running sanctioned deniable operations, a believer
  in the institution and the line. He moves to shut Fateh's rogue unit down. The distinction that
  keeps him from hypocrisy: he runs deniable ops that someone can still answer for, and objects to
  Fateh's unaccountable one. The colleague feeding him the nudges is Sehgal, whom he trusts
  completely, and his gut-punch is learning Sehgal arranged Janaki's murder and is the adversary's
  man.
- **Worldview.** The state must be guarded from chaos, including its own zealots; accountability
  separates a service from a death squad; he distrusts true believers more than enemies.
- **Self-image and insecurity.** The responsible adult who holds the line and carries what he has
  signed. Fears his caution is sometimes cowardice in principle's clothing, and, by the end, that
  being right made him the instrument of the very rot he prided himself on guarding against.
- **Quirks and habits.** Reads any room for liability, asks the avoided question, keeps mental ledgers
  of blowback, dry weary humor, pinches the bridge of his nose, phrases things as questions so people
  hear themselves.
- **Perception.** Consequence first: exposure, the political weather, who answers if this goes wrong,
  where deniability breaks. Reads people for reliability and danger to the mission.
- **Voice, outer and inner.** Outer: measured, ironic, bureaucratically precise, the calm of a man
  managing disasters. Inner: weary, three moves ahead on the political board, doubting his own
  caution.
  *Internal sample:* "Sehgal flagged the unit again, and Sehgal is the steadiest man I have, thirty
  years and never wrong, so when he is worried I am worried. Stop the old man before he does the
  brilliant unforgivable thing. And do not, at this late date, start wondering why the steadiest man
  you have is always the one pointing."
- **How they think.** Institutional, risk-modeling, politically literate, second-order. His blind spot
  is the inverse of Fateh's: where Fateh ignores blowback to act, Kaul can be paralyzed by it into
  inaction, and the film punishes both halves.

### Major Kabir Dhaliwal (cameo; the mirror)
*Essence: the man already on the far side of the choice Sita is walking toward.*
- **Origin and function.** The universe's foremost operative, who killed his mentor Luthra for the
  greater good and made peace with it. At the monastery he gives Sita and Mira what they need and
  recognizes Sita.
- **Worldview.** The work demands you become something terrible, and you either accept it without
  illusion or it destroys you. He has shed the illusions. Stillness, acceptance, the long view.
- **Self-image and insecurity.** Knows exactly what he is and does not flinch, no self-pity, no
  self-justification, the opposite of Fateh. Under the calm, a deep tiredness about whether the paying
  ever ends.
- **Quirks and habits.** Economy of movement and speech, long unawkward silences, a stillness that
  reads as peace or danger. Notices everything, reacts to little.
- **Perception.** Reads people for whether they have accepted their nature yet. Sees in Sita a younger
  version of a question he has already answered.
- **Voice, outer and inner.** Outer: few words, weight in each, speaks to Sita as one of the only
  people who could understand her. Inner: minimal, still, observational.
  *Internal sample:* "She is what they made me, a little younger, and she has not yet decided whether
  to forgive herself for it. No one decided it for me. I can only let her see that the far side exists,
  and that a person reaches it and is still standing."

### Brief: Colonel Sunil Luthra (deceased in canon; the institutional sin)
Former R&AW Chief, Kaul's predecessor, a genuinely good man killed by Kabir for the greater good. His
stain: when Janaki brought her discovery up the chain, he chose to bury it for reasons of state, and
that decision left her exposed and convinced Fateh the sanctioned path was dead. He did not order her
death (Sehgal arranged that), and he did not sanction Alpha (Fateh built it rogue afterward). Not
corruption, a tragic call on a clean man. He does not appear live; his memory is the conscience the
living measure against, and the guilt Kaul inherits.

### Brief: the foreign principal (looming, mostly offscreen)
The spymaster who runs the sleeper network, the original Golden Crescent pipeline, and Sehgal as his
deepest asset. He wants the catastrophic blow delivered and his pipeline back, and Fateh's theft of it
is the spark that started this war. Keep him a shadow with a long reach, felt through Sehgal and the
operation rather than seen. A face on screen is the moment this stops being le Carre and becomes a
franchise; resist it unless the climax needs a body.

### Note: no unit mole in v1
v1 has no equivalent of v2's Rudra. The betrayal lives at the top of the service in Sehgal, not inside
Fateh's unit, which keeps v1's argument about the institution rather than about Sita's inner circle.
