At ten past seven the man came out of the sweet shop on Ajmal Khan Road with a box tied in pink string, and Sita, watching from the chai stall across the lane, marked the time the way she had been taught to mark everything, without writing it down.

His name was Harish Bedi and he was a careful man in the small things. He had parked his Innova in the one slot the shopkeepers kept clear by long arrangement, and he tipped the boy who guarded it before he tipped anyone else. He wore a half-sleeved shirt the colour of weak tea and carried a little weight at the belt that his tailor had learned to forgive. The dossier said hypertension, two stents in 2021, a fondness for Black Label that his cardiologist had pleaded with him to give up. Standing in the warm exhaust of Karol Bagh at the end of a working day, he looked like none of those things. He looked like a man who had bought sweets for someone he loved.

He made a call before he started the car. Sita could not hear it across the lane and the traffic, but she could read the softening of him, the way his shoulders came down, the small private smile a man keeps for his own household. A daughter, the file said. Studying for the chartered accountancy exams in Pune. He would be telling her he had sent the money. He was a man who sent money; it was the whole of what he was, in the end, though he himself would have said he was a man who exported home furnishings to the Gulf, and his books, beautiful and false, would have agreed with him.

The Innova pulled out. Sita left a hundred-rupee note under her glass, weighted with the saucer so the wind off the passing buses would not take it, and walked the other way.

She did not follow him. There was no need. She knew where he was going because Baba had told her where he was going, and Baba's tasking had never once in eleven years sent her to the wrong door.

The hotel was an unremarkable four-storey business place off the main road in Paharganj, the kind that kept a marble lobby the size of a drawing room and let the rest go to laminate and patched carpet above. It traded on discretion and air-conditioning. Men like Bedi kept rooms in places like this for the meetings that did not happen in offices, the ones where a foreign visitor could come and go without leaving a name on a building's register. Bedi held 304 on a standing monthly arrangement under a company that owned three other companies, none of which made anything.

Sita had arrived that afternoon as Mrs Anita Verma, a textiles buyer from Surat, and the identity had survived the front desk because it was built to survive better scrutiny than a front desk could bring. The Aadhaar she had offered was real in the only sense that mattered, registered, biometrically clean, attached to a face that the database would confirm was hers. There was no fiction in the document for a clerk to catch. The fiction was higher up, in places no clerk would ever look. That was how Baba built things. He did not give you a lie to defend. He gave you a truth that pointed the wrong way.

She had spent the afternoon learning the building the way she learned all buildings, with her feet. The lobby camera covered the main desk and the lift doors in a single fixed cone; she had walked it twice and found the dead arc behind the pillar where the housekeeping trolley stood. The stairwell had a camera on the ground floor only, an economy the management had made and forgotten. The corridors had none, which surprised no one who had ever stayed in such a hotel and was glad of it. Facial recognition was a thing of the great public arteries now, the metro and the airports and the markets where the police ran their vans, and it had made the country a hard place to move through with a face you wanted no one to keep. But this hotel was not the country. This hotel was a man's blind spot, and Bedi had chosen it for the same reason she could use it.

She had the room next to his, 305, and a connecting habit of mind that did not need a connecting door.

At a quarter to eight she heard him on the stairs. She knew his tread by then, the slight drag of the right foot that the file had not mentioned and that no file ever caught, the things a body told you only if you waited for them. She heard him let himself into 304 and she heard the television come on, a news channel, the volume of a man who wanted noise rather than information. She gave him eleven minutes. He would pour a drink. He would loosen his belt and put the sweet box in the small fridge for the morning, for the colleague he would not have. He would wait for the visitor he was expecting from the Dubai side, the one who was coming to brief him on a consignment that had gone wrong at Jebel Ali, because that visitor had called him two days ago and that visitor's number had been Sita's to arrange.

He was expecting a man. He would not be alarmed by a woman, which was the older and simpler advantage, the one that no biometric age had taken away.

She checked herself once in the mirror, not for vanity but for traces, the way you check a weapon. Gloves the colour of skin, thin enough to feel a pulse through. A cotton dupatta she could draw across the lower face on the stairs. In the cloth bag, a half-bottle of the Black Label he favoured, bought for cash in a different part of the city under a different face a week before, and two glasses from her own room, and the thing itself, in its small insulated sleeve.

The thing itself was not dramatic. People imagined the work was dramatic and it embarrassed her, faintly, how little of it was. A syringe, a fine one, twenty-nine gauge, the needle short. Potassium chloride in a strength that did one thing and did it without argument. In a man of Bedi's history it would read, to the tired police surgeon who would not even be summoned, as the heart simply stopping, which was the truth of it. The potassium would climb in his blood after death the way it climbed in every dead man's blood, so that even if some unlikely diligence opened him up, the number would say only that he was a corpse, which everyone already knew. The art was not in the chemical. The art was in the choosing of the man, and the man had been chosen long before he reached her. Baba's people did that part. She was the last six inches of a long arm.

She crossed to his door and knocked the way the Dubai man would have knocked, twice, then once.

"Haan?" Through the wood, wary and not wary, the voice of a man whose whole life was meetings he did not want a record of.

"Mr Bedi. I'm from Mr Farooqi's office." She kept her Hindi educated and her manner brisk, a little tired from travel. "He couldn't come himself. He sent me with the file and his apologies."

A pause. The television muted. She could feel him weighing it, the small machine of his caution turning over, and finding nothing to catch on, because there was nothing. Farooqi was real. Farooqi's office was real. Farooqi simply did not know, this evening, that he had sent anyone.

The chain came off. The door opened on a man in his undershirt with his belt loosened, exactly as she had pictured him, the smell of whisky and air freshener behind him and the news anchor frozen open-mouthed on the screen. He looked at her and recalibrated, the way men did, downward and then upward, deciding she was harmless and then deciding she was someone's clever idea of how to put him at ease, a woman to soften a hard conversation about a lost consignment. Both of those decisions were the ones she wanted him to make.

"Farooqi sent a lady," he said, half a complaint and half a compliment, and stood back to let her in.

She had been eighteen the first time, in a better hotel than this one. Fateh, whom she had called Baba since before she could remember choosing to, had taken her to dinner for her birthday at a place with white cloths and a man who played the piano badly, and at the end of it he had laid a room key on the table beside her plate, the small flat weight of it, and had not said anything at all. She had understood. She had gone up alone and done what was needed and come down and finished her dessert, and he had watched her eat it with an expression she had spent the years since trying to name and had settled, finally, on pride. There had been no test in it and no ceremony, only a handing over, the way a father gives a child a thing he has been keeping for her until she was old enough to hold it. Walking into Harish Bedi's room she felt the old steadiness come down over her like a hand on the back of the neck, and she was grateful for it, the way you are grateful for a thing that has never failed you.

"Mr Farooqi is very concerned about the Jebel Ali matter," she said, and set the cloth bag on the low table, and let him see the half-bottle as she lifted it out, casual, a courtesy from one careful man to another by way of his messenger. "He said, take this, sit, hear her out before you say anything to anyone."

Bedi laughed, pleased and a little rueful, a man being managed and enjoying it. "He knows me, that bastard." He found the glasses she had set out and did not question that she had brought two; a host does not question hospitality. He poured for them both, a heavy hand for himself and a polite splash for her, and she let him. When he turned to the window to look down at the lane, the way men do when they are about to talk about money, she tipped from the small vial into his glass the few drops that would make him slow and warm and untroubled in a few minutes, and not before, and she did it in the time it took him to say that the consignment had been clean, that he had done nothing wrong, that Farooqi could check the manifests himself.

"He has checked them," she said gently. "That's why I'm here."

He drank. She touched her own glass to her lip and did not. They talked for six or seven minutes about the lost container and the Dubai end and the price of everything, and she listened with real attention, because the listening was part of the dignity she could still give him, and because a man who feels heard stays in his chair. She watched the drug arrive in him the way she had watched the night arrive over a hundred surveillance positions, gradually and then all at once. His sentences lengthened. He rubbed his face. He said the room was warm and she agreed that it was.

"You should sit back," she said. "You've had a long day."

He sat back. His glass tilted in his hand and she took it from him before it spilled, set it down, and that small service was the last thing Harish Bedi understood was being done for him. His eyes were going. He looked at her with a dawning that was not yet fear, more a child's confusion, the question of a man who has trusted the arrangement his whole life and feels the arrangement move under him.

She came around the chair. She found the fold at the back of his knee where the skin was soft and the mark would sit among the small blemishes of a heavy man's body and never be sought, and she put the needle in and pushed the plunger steadily, and she held his shoulder with her other hand, firm, the way you hold someone who is being sick, the way you hold someone so that they are not alone.

He arched. His heel drummed once on the carpet. His mouth worked and air came out of it in pieces, and among the pieces were words, or the wreckage of words, the way the dying spend their last breath on the thing that has been sitting closest to the front of them. She did not lean down for them. She had heard the dying talk before and she had learned that it was almost always a plea or a name, and that the name was almost always a god's or a daughter's, and that none of it was hers to keep. But the room was small and quiet with the television muted and the words came to her whether she wanted them or not.

"Not me," he said, or something that wore those bones. And then, with the last of it, wet and certain and aimed at nothing: "Wrong man. Tell him. Wrong."

Then he was heavy under her hand in the particular way that only the dead are heavy, and the drumming stopped, and the room held nothing but the hum of the air-conditioner and the silent news.

She did not move for a moment. They all said it. The guilty said it most of all, because the guilty had spent the longest rehearsing their innocence, building it room by room until they half believed it themselves, until at the door of death they reached for it out of habit and found it threadbare in their hands. Faraway in another year a man in white linen had told her that the ones who deserve it least are the ones who beg, and the ones who beg least are the ones who deserve it most, and she had filed it where she filed all of Baba's truths, which was somewhere below thought, where it did its work without being looked at. Bedi had washed the proceeds of more dead men than she would ever kill. His clean false books had moved the price of a hundred kilos of Afghan heroin through Dubai and back as bath linen and brass lamps, and the bath linen had been real, and the lamps had been real, and that was the genius of it and the obscenity of it both. He was not the wrong man. There was no wrong man. There was only the man Baba's hand had reached.

She let the thought close over the words and seal them under.

The arranging took four minutes and was the easiest part, because the truth needed almost no help. She put his own glass back in his slack hand and pressed his fingers to it and let it fall as it would have fallen, so the print and the spill told the only story the room would ever be asked to tell: a heavy man, a hard day, a heavy drink, a heart that had been warned and warned. Her own glass and the half-bottle she had brought went back into the cloth bag, every surface she had touched wiped with the patience of someone who has all the time in the world and knows that haste is the only thing that leaves a trace. The syringe she capped and sleeved. She turned the television's sound back up to the level he had set it, because a neighbour who had heard noise should go on hearing it, and she let the news anchor finish his sentence about the rupee.

At the door she listened, drew the dupatta across the lower half of her face, and stepped into the corridor that had no eyes. The stairwell took her down past the camera she had measured, and she kept to its blind side with her head turned and her gait changed, shorter in the stride, a different woman's walk, the small grammar of disappearing. She left through the service passage by the kitchens where the staff were loud over a cricket score and no one looked up at a woman who walked as though she belonged, because belonging was nine-tenths of invisibility and she had always belonged everywhere and nowhere, which was the same thing.

Mrs Anita Verma would check out in the morning, unhurried, and pay her bill in cash, and be a face the system held and could do nothing with, because the face had done nothing the system could see. The syringe and the gloves and the half-bottle would come apart over the next two hours into four separate small deaths in four separate parts of the city, a clinic's sharps bin here, a hotel skip there, the river last of all, so that no one place held the whole of the evening. By the time anyone thought to wonder how Harish Bedi had died, there would be nothing to wonder with.

She walked until the lane gave onto the main road and the city took her in, the horns and the wedding lights strung over a shopfront and the smell of frying, the whole enormous indifference of it. Only then, in the back of an auto with the meter running and her face turned to the dark, did she take out the clean phone, the one that had no apps and no name and one purpose. There was a number in it that belonged to a vegetable wholesaler in Azadpur who did not exist, and the wholesaler used Signal like everyone used Signal, and to the wholesaler she sent the line she had been given, which meant nothing and meant everything.

Aunty reached home safely. Thank you for arranging.

The reply came before the auto had reached the next light, and it was Baba, she knew it was Baba though the words were a stranger's, because she had spent her whole life learning to hear him through other people's mouths.

Good. She was tired. Let her rest now.

Sita put the phone away and sat back against the cracked vinyl and let the steadiness loosen at last, by degrees, the way it always did when the work was behind her, a tide going out. She thought, with the small private warmth she allowed herself once a job was clean, of him reading those three lines somewhere in a building she was not cleared to enter, and setting the phone face down, and knowing without doubt that his hand had closed exactly where he had aimed it. That was the whole of what passed between them now, a homecoming and a thank-you, two strangers' sentences carrying everything a daughter could not say to a father and a father would not say back. It was enough. It had always been enough. She trusted him past the reach of any argument, which was not a thing she examined, because the examining of it had never once been asked of her.

Behind her, dwindling, a man lay in a chair in front of the news with a sweet box cooling in the little fridge for a colleague who was never coming, and three words drying on his lips that she had already put away where they could not reach her.
