The flat was on the third floor of a builder's block in Mayur Vihar, the kind that went up in the eighties and had been quietly subsiding ever since. Sita had let herself in with the key Baba left under the meter box, climbed the stairs because the lift had a smell she did not trust, and found the place furnished the way all the safe flats were furnished: out of inventory. A teak sofa with a customs seizure tag still wired to one leg. Two dining chairs that did not match the table. A steel almirah, a ceiling fan that ticked on the downstroke, a framed print of the Golden Temple that someone in the building's procurement had bought by the dozen. Nothing here belonged to anyone. Nothing here could be made to speak.

She had come two hours early, which was her habit. She walked the rooms first, then the landing, then the stairwell down to the parking, reading the building the way she read everything, for the line she would take out of it if the night went wrong. There was a back way through the kitchen balcony to the neighbour's, and a drainpipe that would hold a person of her weight for the first ten feet and probably not the rest. She noted it and forgot it and put the kettle on.

Baba came at nine with the launderer.

She heard them on the stairs before the knock, Baba's slow tread and a second man breathing through his mouth, and then Baba's particular double tap on the door, knuckle and then the flat of the hand, the way he had taught her to knock before she was tall enough to reach the latch. She opened it and stood back and they came in out of the corridor light, and she saw the launderer properly for the first time.

His name, she had been told, was Menon. A soft man going to fat, mid-fifties, in a safari suit the colour of weak tea, and he carried a leather folder against his chest the way a child carries a schoolbook into an exam. His eyes went to her and slid off. People's eyes did that. She had spent eleven years learning to be the thing in a room that eyes declined to settle on, and the work had taken.

"My daughter," Baba said, which was the word he used in front of others, and it was both true and a lie and she had stopped being able to tell where the seam ran. He did not introduce her by any name. He never did.

Baba was sixty-one and looked it only in the hands. The face was still the face from her childhood, broad and unhurried, the eyes that seemed always to be forgiving you something. He wore a grey bush shirt and chappals and he had the gift, which she had watched all her life and never learned, of making a borrowed room feel like his study. He set a cloth bag on the table, took out a thermos and a tin of the salted biscuits he liked, and gestured Menon to the chair that faced the window.

That was the chair you put a man in when you wanted the light on him and the dark behind you. Sita took the sofa, off to the side, where she could see them both and the door. She did not speak. Her job tonight, as far as she had been told, was to be present, and to be a particular kind of present, the silence in the corner that a frightened man cannot stop measuring himself against.

"Mr Menon has had a difficult week," Baba said, pouring tea into three steel glasses without asking. "Mr Menon has decided to be useful instead. Isn't that right."

"I have always been useful." Menon's English was the English of a man who had done his business in it for thirty years, the consonants over-articulated, the confidence a thin skin over something that had begun, this week, to come apart. "I want only to be clear that I came to you. Nobody brought me."

"Nobody brought you," Baba agreed, in the voice he used for soothing horses and informants and, once, a long time ago, a girl who would not sleep. "You came. Drink your tea."

Sita watched Menon's hand on the glass. The tremor was real. You could simulate a great deal, the bowed shoulders, the wet eyes, the willingness, but the small involuntary things were honest, and the glass shook against his lower lip and a bead of tea ran down the steel and he did not notice. So. He was not playing. Or he was playing and frightened both, which was the usual condition of men who survived in his line.

"Tell her what you told me," Baba said. "In the car. About Bangkok."

It was the first time he had pointed Menon at her, and the man turned in the chair as though a lamp had been swung his way. He looked at Sita and tried to read her and could not, and that failure did to him what it did to all of them, made him talk faster.

"There is a — a method," he said. "For the Triangle money. The product comes down through Moreh, you understand, from the Myanmar side, the yaba and the heroin both, since the fighting started it is a flood, an absolute flood, the prices have collapsed at source. So there is more cash than the old channels can — can digest. You cannot put it in a bank. Aadhaar, KYC, the UPI leaves a footprint on a chana-seller now. So it goes the old way. Cash to a man in Imphal, a chit, a number on a phone call, and the same value comes up clean in Bangkok as an invoice. Rubber gloves. Frozen prawn. Granite slabs. You over-bill the prawn by forty per cent and the difference is the wash." He wet his lips. "I write the invoices. I have written them for nine years."

"And the Dubai leg," Baba said.

"Gold." Menon said it almost fondly. "Always in the end it is gold. The Bangkok money buys gold, the gold flies to Dubai as scrap, the scrap becomes a jewellery receivable, the receivable becomes a wire that any bank in the world will shake hands with. By then it has been three commodities and four countries and it is as clean as your morning bath." He spread his soft hands. "I am telling you the architecture. The architecture is not a secret. What is a secret is the names. The names are what I have."

Sita said nothing. She had learned the architecture, in pieces, over years, the way you learn a city you have only ever entered after dark, and none of it was new to her. The flood out of the Triangle was real. She had stood at the Moreh gate eighteen months ago in another life and watched the trucks and understood that the border there was a suggestion. What interested her was not the lecture. It was Baba, the angle of him, the patience. He already knew all of this. He had not brought Menon here to be educated. He had brought Menon here so that she would see the shape of the thing, and that was a different lesson, and it was for her.

"Tell her the other thing," Baba said softly. "The thing about who it all answers to."

Menon's face did something complicated. The fear in it changed register, became the older, deeper fear of a man who has understood that the room he is in is more dangerous than the men hunting him outside it.

"I don't — I gave you that. I gave you that in the car, I don't see why the —"

"Because she is the one who will use it," Baba said. "And she likes to hear a thing from the mouth, not the file. Indulge me."

The launderer looked at Sita again, and this time he held it longer, and she let him, kept her face the still water it had been since he walked in. Somewhere below in the colony a man was trying to start a scooter and failing, the engine catching for half a second and falling silent, then catching again, a small dogged sound that went nowhere. The fan turned its slow circle, the blade-shadow crossing the print of the Golden Temple and crossing back.

"There is one organisation," Menon said, very quietly now, as if the walls had relatives. "Not one man. One organisation, that sits across both. The Triangle and the Crescent both. You understand what I am saying. The eastern product, the Myanmar product, and the western, the Afghan heroin that comes down through Punjab with the fake notes folded into it — for forty years these have been two different worlds, two different mafias, they do not even speak the same language of crime. And someone has been quietly putting a roof over both. The same wash. The same gold in Dubai. The same three or four invoices in my own handwriting, you understand, I have seen the western money and the eastern money cleaned through the same prawn." He shivered, the whole soft bulk of him. "Whoever has done that is not a smuggler. A smuggler thinks in trucks. This thinks in maps."

The room was quiet for a while.

Sita understood, then, why she had been called early to walk an empty flat. Baba had wanted her to arrive at this the way you arrive at a summit, after the long approach, so that the size of it would land. For eleven years she had been given men one at a time. A name, a habit, a window. She had taken Bedi three days ago in a hotel bathroom with a syringe and a salt the body makes for itself, and she had thought of Bedi as a man, a single man with a wife somewhere and a weak alibi of innocence on his lips, and she had been right to think so, because that was her altitude. Baba's altitude was this. Baba had not been hunting men. Baba had been reading a map that no one else in the building had been willing to believe existed, drawing a line through forty years and two mountain ranges and a dozen agencies, and the men she removed one at a time were only the places where his pencil had pressed hard enough to leave a mark.

She felt something she did not often allow herself, which was awe, and it wore her father's face.

"You see why he is valuable," Baba said to her, not to Menon. "Not the architecture. Every desk in Lodhi Road knows the architecture. Him." A small nod at the sweating man. "He is the only person living who has touched both sets of paper with the same pen. He is the join."

"And what happens to the join," Sita said. They were the first words she had spoken since hello, and Menon flinched at the sound of her voice as though it had a temperature.

"That depends on the join," Baba said pleasantly. "On whether the join goes on being useful, or becomes a loose thread that someone less patient than me decides to pull." He turned the steel glass a quarter circle on the table, a man with all the time in the world. "Mr Menon understands the difference. He is an intelligent man. He survived nine years writing prawn invoices for people who shoot accountants for sport. That is not luck, that is intelligence, and intelligence is something I can work with."

It was beautifully done. She had watched him do it her whole life and it still moved her, the way he gave a cornered man back a sliver of his dignity and made him grateful for it, made the leash feel like a hand on the shoulder. Menon's breathing eased a fraction. He had been told he might live. He had not been told the terms, but he had been told he might live, and a man will sign almost anything for that, and Baba knew it, and the cruelty of it was so gentle that it took Sita a moment, as it always did, to find the cruelty at all.

"We have an owner," Baba said then, and the word had a particular weight in their world, and he said it for her benefit. "Everything we do has an owner. A man whose career can answer for it if the day ever comes. That is the whole difference, you see, between what we are and the people we hunt. They are deniable to no one. We are deniable to everyone except the one man who signs." He did not say the name, and he did not need to, because she carried it: Kaul, the Colonel, whose signature sat under eleven years of her life like a foundation course you never saw and never doubted. "And above the signature there is the financing, and the financing has a steward who has carried it cleanly for thirty years and never lost a rupee, or a life he did not mean to lose. So you are not in the hands of cowboys, Mr Menon. You are in the hands of an institution. That should comfort you. Institutions are patient. Only individuals are in a hurry, and only men in a hurry make the mistakes that get you killed."

Menon nodded as if he had been blessed. Sita gave the steward his name in the privacy of her own head, the way you touch a thing to be sure it is where you left it. Sehgal. Additional Secretary. Thirty years and not a smudge on him. She had never met the man and did not expect to; he was a level of the architecture that did not descend to where she worked. But she knew the weight of him the way you know the keystone of a bridge you cross every day without looking up, and the knowing was its own kind of floor under her feet.

She let her eyes drift, while Baba moved into the slow careful part of it, the dates and the names and the leather folder coming open at last, and the held edge of her attention slackened the way it was allowed to slacken only when Baba had the room, and a memory came up through the floor of her the way they sometimes did, uninvited.

She was eighteen. There was a restaurant with white cloth, a waiter who pulled her chair out, a card on the table boasting that the place had fed Delhi since 1947, as if that were an achievement and not merely an endurance. She had never been anywhere like it. Fateh had ordered for both of them because she did not know how, and he had watched her eat as though it were a thing worth watching, and she had been so happy that her face ached with it, because he had remembered the day, because he had brought her somewhere that the men cleaned their nails for. Then, when the plates were cleared, he had set a hotel key on the cloth between them and said nothing, and she had understood, and wiped her mouth and folded her napkin and gone up. The room was two streets away. It did not take long. She left the key on the dresser where it was supposed to be left and came down by the service stair, and her father was waiting with the bill already paid, and he had walked her out with his hand light between her shoulder blades. That was all. She had been a girl with a father in a fine place, and then she had been the other thing, and the two had happened across one evening without any seam she could afterward find.

She did not flinch from it. She had long ago stopped expecting it to wound her and was, each time, vaguely surprised that it did not. He had made her. Before him she had belonged to no one and nothing, a name on no register that mattered, and he had looked at her once and seen what she could be, and given her the use of herself. If the use was this, then this was what she had instead of a self, and she had decided a long time ago that it was enough. More than enough. It was the only home anyone had ever let her keep.

"— and she will be your contact," Baba was saying, and Sita came back into the room without any visible seam, the way she had been taught. "Not me. You will not see me again, Mr Menon, which you should regard as the best news you have had all year. You will see her, or you will see no one, and if you see no one, you should already be running, though it will not help."

Menon looked at her one last time, and now there was no failure to read her, because she had let him read exactly one thing, and he had read it, and his soft face went grey under the window light.

Baba capped the thermos. He gathered the tin of biscuits, untouched, back into the cloth bag. He did everything slowly and nothing was wasted, and Sita watched his hands, the only old part of him, and felt the thing she felt for him that had no clean name in any language she knew, that was love and was ownership and was the gratitude of the made thing for the maker, all of it fused at a heat that had long ago stopped letting the elements separate.

At the door he paused and looked back at the launderer, alone now in the seized chair with his folder of names, in the borrowed flat that belonged to no one.

"You said something just now," Baba said, mild, almost curious. "You said whoever built this thinks in maps. Not in trucks." He let it sit. "You should hope, Mr Menon, for your own sake, that you never find out how right you are."

Then he went out onto the dark landing, and Sita followed him, because she always followed him, and the door clicked shut on the man and the fan ticking down its slow last revolutions behind them.
