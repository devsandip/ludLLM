The fax came in at 11:40, and Janaki heard it before she saw it, the encrypted line in the corner of the financial cell clearing its throat and beginning to print. She did not get up at once. She had learned, in eleven years of reading other people's money, that the documents which mattered were rarely the ones that announced themselves. They arrived quietly, in the third paragraph of a routine summary, in a figure that was correct to the rupee when it had no business being so precise.

She finished the cable she was on. A Punjab field report, third copy, the kind that crossed her desk four times a week now: a seizure on the Amritsar side, fake currency notes in the new design, four lakh face value, moving with a consignment of heroin that the local police had logged as forty kilos and the customs men had logged as thirty-one. The discrepancy did not interest her. The discrepancies always existed; men skimmed, men lied, men wrote down the number that kept them alive. What interested her was the courier's onward instruction, recovered from a chit in his shoe, naming a hawala office in Karol Bagh she had seen twice before this month.

The office did not launder the way the others laundered. That was the thing she had been circling for six weeks without letting herself say it.

She rose and went to the fax and tore the sheet free and read it standing under the tube light, which buzzed and flickered the way it had since winter, the way the maintenance requisition would not fix until the new financial year. The cell was empty. Brar had gone home at nine. The night man, Dutta, was two rooms away with his tea and his transistor, the cricket from somewhere, Australia, the volume kept low out of a respect for the building rather than for her.

The fax was from the liaison desk in Dubai, relayed and re-encrypted through the station, and it concerned a trade-finance instrument: a letter of credit drawn on a Sharjah trading company against a shipment of dates and dried fruit to a buyer in Ludhiana. Eighty-one lakh. The goods were real. The dates would arrive, would be eaten, would leave no trace. What did not fit was the buyer. A firm registered eleven months ago, no prior import history, and a paid-up capital that had been topped up twice from an account in Bangkok that Janaki had seen, two weeks earlier, receiving a transfer she had flagged and then released because she could not say what was wrong with it.

She carried the sheet back to her desk and sat down and opened the buff folder she had been building and not naming.

Inside it, in her own hand, was a list. Not of transactions. Anyone could list transactions; the building drowned in lists of transactions. Hers was a list of the wrong direction.

Money laundering, when you stripped the textbooks away, was a problem of escape. Dirty money wanted to leave. It wanted to get out of the heroin, out of the currency racket, out of the smuggled gold, and into something that looked like a hotel or a film or a builder's third tower, somewhere it could rest and be respectable. The whole apparatus she had spent her career mapping, the hawala chits and the over-invoiced cashew exports and the Dubai property and the Bangkok shells, all of it pointed one way. Out. Up. Away from the crime and toward the daylight.

What she had been seeing for six weeks pointed the other way.

Money was coming in. Clean money, or money washed so thoroughly upstream that it arrived as clean as a christening, and it was not resting. It was being placed. Small amounts, by the standards of the flows she watched. A pension trust here that had quietly taken a new corporate subscriber. A vendor there, an electronics firm in Hyderabad that did sub-assembly work, real work, on contracts she was not cleared to see the far end of but could feel the shape of, the way you feel a wall in the dark. A telephone company's regional accounts. A man, twice, just a man, a salary appearing in an account for a person who already had a salary, the second one routed so that the first one would never know to ask.

It was not theft. Nothing was being taken. That was what had kept the cell from seeing it, what had kept her from saying it for six weeks. The arithmetic balanced. The money went in and stayed in and did nothing visible at all, and a system built to catch escape was blind to arrival, because arrival looked like investment, looked like prosperity, looked like the country getting richer, which in 2008 was the only story anyone in Delhi wanted to be told.

She had seen it because she read the chits. Not the summaries. The chits, the actual recovered scraps, the courier's shoe-leather, and she had noticed that the same three hawala offices that handled the Crescent flow, the Afghan heroin and the fake notes coming down through Punjab, were also handling these placements. The same plumbing. The drug money paid for the pipe, and through the pipe, in the other direction, against the current, something else was being fed in.

Whoever was doing this owned both ends. The dirty river and the clean one. They had built a single machine that took narcotics out of India and put something quieter back.

Janaki sat very still and let herself, for the first time, finish the sentence.

They were not washing money. They were buying positions. They were seeding people and accounts and small grey firms that touched, at one remove or two, the things a country guards: a sub-assembly here, a network switch there, a man with a clearance and a second salary. Not many. Not yet. The volumes were small because the thing was new. She had not caught a network. She had caught a network being born, the first capital being laid down the way a man lays a foundation in the dry season for a house he means to raise over years.

The tube light buzzed. Somewhere Dutta's transistor produced a small flat roar; a wicket, or a boundary. India playing far away in the dark.

She thought, with the part of her mind that never stopped being a mother even when she most needed it to: I should be home.

Tara would be asleep. She was always asleep by now, fist against her cheek, the ayah dozing in the chair with the radio off because Janaki had asked. Eight months old and already she slept like someone keeping a secret, that complete unbothered surrender that Janaki could stand over for ten minutes at a stretch and not understand how she had made it, how a thing so finished had come out of her own unfinished life. The locket lay against the child's chest at night because Janaki could not bear to take it off her even to sleep, her own mother's locket, worn thin at the edges, the clasp she had had repaired in Karol Bagh by a man two streets from the very hawala office she now watched, which was a coincidence and which she had nonetheless not been able to stop thinking about all week, as if the city were too small to keep its halves apart.

She put the thought down. She had a discipline for it. She picked it up and put it down the way you put down a hot dish, quickly, before it marked you.

The work first. The work was the only protection any of them had, and it was no protection at all.

She drew a fresh sheet and began, in pencil, not pen, to draw the thing as a single diagram. The three hawala offices at the center. The Crescent flow coming down, heroin and notes, paying its way. The trade instruments through Dubai and Bangkok feeding clean capital back up the same channels. And then the placements, the little arrowheads going out into the body of the country, into the funds and the firms and the men, each one labelled with the reference she could cite and dated to the cable that proved it. Twelve arrowheads. She had twelve. Six weeks ago she had had three, and had told herself it was nothing, the way you tell yourself a lump is nothing.

When it was drawn she looked at it for a long time, and what she felt was not triumph. Analysts who felt triumph made mistakes; she had watched it happen to better men than herself, the pleasure of being right corrupting the rightness. What she felt was a kind of vertigo, the particular sickness of seeing a thing whole that you would now have to make other people see in parts.

Because the diagram raised a question she could not answer from her chair, and the question was simple and it was the only one that mattered.

How long.

Twelve placements in six weeks of her attention. But her attention was six weeks old. The machine was not. The plumbing was old; the Crescent pipe had been running since before she joined; the hawala offices had grandfathers. If someone had been feeding capital the wrong way up an existing pipe, they could have started a year ago, two, and she would not have seen it, because nobody had been looking in this direction, because the country had decided to be rich and a rich country does not audit its good fortune.

She would need the back files. Five years of them. She would need to pull the chits, the actual recovered chits, not the digests, and re-read them for direction, and that was hundreds of folders in three buildings and a justification she would have to write and sign and send up.

Up to whom.

She knew the answer, and the answer was the second sickness, smaller and colder than the first.

It went up to clandestine financing. It went up, eventually, to the Additional Secretary's desk, to Sehgal, because everything that touched off-books money met Sehgal somewhere on its way to a decision. Thirty years of him. The man the building set its watch by. She had briefed him twice, both times badly, both times leaving with the sense of having been weighed by someone who had already read the file faster than she had written it and was being kind about the parts she had missed. There was no one she would rather take this to. There was no one whose judgment she trusted more to tell her whether she had found a war or a clerical error.

She would take it to him. Not tonight. She would build it first, make it unarguable, twelve arrowheads becoming forty, the dates incontestable, so that it could not be filed as the enthusiasm of a tired woman on a night shift. She would make it a thing that could not be put down.

She did not know, could not know, sitting under the buzzing light with her daughter's grandmother's locket eight kilometres away rising and falling on a sleeping chest, that she had just decided the manner of her own death. The reader of her file, when it came, would not be a stranger. But Janaki had no file yet. She had a pencil diagram and a buff folder and the clean conviction, the most dangerous thing an honest officer can own, that the truth was on her side and that the men above her served the same country she did.

She reached for the BlackBerry to check the time and saw she had three messages, all from the ayah, all the same: baby fine, sleeping, come when you can. The woman sent them every two hours without fail, as instructed, because Janaki could not work without them and could not say so. She typed back: leaving by one. She did not believe it as she sent it.

Then she did the thing she had not planned to do, the thing that came from no policy and no training and that she would not, afterward, in the few weeks left to her, be able to fully explain even to herself.

She did not photocopy the folder. Copies lived in registries; registries had keepers; a keeper was a question someone could ask. Instead she took out a fresh sheet and copied the diagram a second time, in her own hand, smaller, the references abbreviated to a private shorthand she had used since college, the dates encoded by a rule only she knew, the three hawala offices reduced to three initials that meant nothing to anyone who had not sat where she sat. A version of the truth that was useless to a thief and complete to herself. She folded it twice and did not put it in the folder.

She told herself it was caution. The cell had been quiet too long; the maintenance man had not fixed the light in three months but someone had moved her chair last week, a centimetre, she was almost sure, and she had laughed at herself for noticing and noticed anyway. She told herself an analyst kept a private index of her own work and that this was no different.

It was different. Some animal part of her, older than tradecraft, had understood the diagram before her mind would say it aloud: that a thing built to own both ends of a river would have, somewhere along its length, men who could not afford for her to draw it twice. And the same part had already begun, without permission, to think about where a folded sheet could live that was not a registry and not a safe and not anywhere a building would think to look, because the building was exactly the body the disease had entered.

She thought of Tara's locket. The worn edges. The clasp mended in Karol Bagh. A keepsake passed mother to daughter, a thing no man searched, a thing that travelled by being loved.

She put the thought down, fast, before it marked her. She was tired. It was past midnight. She had found something tonight that would take months to prove and she was already, absurdly, thinking like a hunted woman when she had told no one and no one knew.

But she did not unfold the sheet and put it back in the folder. She slid it into the inner pocket of her bag, against the lining, behind the photograph of the child she kept there, and she felt the small ridiculous comfort of it lying close to the picture, the two of them together, the secret and the reason for the secret, and she did not let herself ask why a copy in her bag was safer than a copy in a registry of the Research and Analysis Wing of the Government of India.

The fax line cleared its throat again. Another sheet beginning. Routine, almost certainly. The documents that mattered did not announce themselves.

Janaki did not get up to read it. She sat with her hand flat on the closed folder, and across the city her daughter slept with a dead woman's locket on her chest, and the night ran on toward an hour that, looking back from very far away, someone would call the beginning.
