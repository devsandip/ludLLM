The grain market at Khemkaran slept the way border towns sleep, which is to say with one eye open. Sita watched it from the second floor of a commission agent's office, the kind of place where a man in a white kurta weighed other men's harvests and skimmed his half-percent off the top of the nation's bread. The arhtiya whose name was on the lease had gone to Ludhiana for a cousin's wedding and would stay there until Tuesday, paid not to wonder why. Below her the sacks of basmati stood in their grey ranks under a single bulb, and beyond the shutter the fields ran flat and dark toward a fence she could not see but could feel, the way you feel the edge of a roof in the night.

She had three screens and a notebook. The notebook was the only one she trusted.

On the left screen a thermal feed pulsed from a small quadcopter Alpha had walked out into the cane two hours earlier and set to hover at the edge of its battery's patience. White shapes moved against the cool ground: two men, a motorcycle laid on its side, the long low smear of the irrigation channel they were using for cover. On the right screen a map with no labels, only the grid references she had written herself. The middle screen was dark. She kept it dark on purpose. A lit screen in a window was a beacon, and she had learned a long time ago that the difference between a professional and a corpse was usually the number of lights you left burning.

Her phone sat in the steel biscuit tin on the far table with Menon's, both of them wrapped, both of them off. They had crossed into the district that afternoon on SIMs registered to a dead pensioner in Bathinda, and she had powered them down the moment the work began. Everything left a wake now. Every tower handshake, every face that walked under a camera, every rupee that moved on the rails the banks could read. So you went the other way. You went back to the old country, to cash and paper and a man's word, and you moved like your grandfather had moved, and the satellites looked down at a grain market and saw grain.

"They're late," Menon said.

He was sitting on the floor against the wall with his knees up, a fifty-eight-year-old man folded like a clerk waiting for a train. In the bulb's wash his face had the soft greyness of someone who had spent forty years indoors counting what other men carried. He was the most valuable human being in the operation and he looked like the manager of a failing cooperative bank, which he had also, at one point, genuinely been.

"They're not late," Sita said. "The drone's early. The drop is keyed to the moon."

"You sound like him."

She did not answer that. She watched the thermal feed.

The pipeline they were taking tonight was not theirs and never had been. For three years the consignments had come the same way: heroin pressed into the soft bricks they called sabz, and with it the notes, the beautiful counterfeit five-hundreds that the printers across the fence had perfected to the point where a teller would pass them without a second look. The two cargoes traveled together because they served the same paymaster, and that paymaster was not a smuggler. Smugglers wanted money. This one wanted India, slowly, the way damp wants a wall.

Baba had explained it to her in the safehouse with the patience of a man explaining the tide. There was a network being grown inside the country, he had said, planted over twenty years into the places that mattered, the command rooms and the cipher rooms, the desks where the missiles and the reactors were spoken of in acronyms. And the network drank money, and the money came up this pipe, through Punjab, in heroin and forged paper, and out into the hawala channels and back as something clean. Cut the pipe, he had said, and you do three things with one cut. You take the war chest for yourself. You starve them at the very moment they are reaching to switch their people on. And you make them come looking for whoever did it.

She had understood the first two. The third she had filed away as his business, the way she filed away most of his reasons. She was very good at the cut. The reasons were above her pay, and she had made her peace with that at eighteen in a hotel corridor with a key warm in her palm.

On the screen one of the white shapes lifted its head. A new heat had come into the frame from the north, low and fast, skimming the cane: not a man, not an animal. The drone from the other side.

"Now they're on time," she said.

The Pakistani machine came in heavy, a hexacopter laboring under a payload it had not been built for, and it dropped its load where the field GPS told it to and turned for home before the package had stopped rolling. The two receivers were on it at once. Sita watched them quarter the ground, find the bundle, sling it across the motorcycle's tank. Routine. They had done this two dozen times for a master who paid in advance and asked no questions, and they had no reason to think tonight was any different from the others, which was precisely the condition she had spent two weeks engineering.

"Alpha," she said into the throat mic. It was a closed handset, line-of-sight, no network, the kind of thing a forester used. "They have it. Let them clear the channel and start the run. Take them at the culvert, not before."

"Copy." Alpha's voice was a flat thing with the wind under it. He was somewhere in the cane with three men and a Bolero with its plates swapped, and he would not move until the motorcycle was committed to the lane, boxed by the irrigation ditch on one side and the field on the other, with nowhere to do anything clever.

"No noise."

"There won't be."

She believed him. Alpha did not waste rounds and did not waste words and had once, in a courtyard in Moreh, persuaded four men to lie face down in the dust simply by the manner in which he closed a car door. She had handled men like Alpha for years. They were easy. It was the Menons who were hard, the ones who carried the whole map in their heads and knew exactly what they were worth and watched you, always, to see whether you had worked it out too.

The motorcycle's heat-shape pulled out of the channel and onto the field lane and began to run.

"You'll kill them," Menon said. Not a protest. An audit.

"No," she said. "We need them. Frightened men who already know the route are cheaper than new men who don't. Alpha takes the cargo and the bikes and lets them walk home with a number to call."

"My number."

"His." She nodded at the dark middle screen as though Baba sat behind it. "Then, in a month, when they trust the new arrangement, yours."

Menon's mouth moved into something that was not a smile. "You've thought about it."

"I think about everything. That's the job he gave me."

She had been Menon's handler for nine days. Before that he had answered to Baba alone, the single thread running between the eastern networks and the western ones, the one man who could sit in a teahouse in Mandalay and a majlis in Dubai and be welcomed at both, because over decades he had made himself indispensable to people who would happily have killed each other in any other circumstance. Baba had handed him to her in the safehouse the way a father hands over the keys to a house he built, and she had felt the weight of it then and felt it now. You did not give a man like Menon to someone you intended to keep at a distance. You gave him to family.

On the screen the white shapes converged.

It was, as Alpha had promised, without noise. The Bolero came across the lane and stopped, and three heats stepped down from it, and the motorcycle's heat slewed and fell. For a few seconds there was a knot of bodies, very close, very still, the choreography of men who have understood in one breath that the situation is total and resistance is suicide. Then two shapes were kneeling in the dust with their hands laced behind their heads, and a third shape, Alpha, lifted the bundle off the tank and held it the way you hold something you have come a long way for.

"Clean," Alpha said. "Two packers. Heroin and paper, both. Confirming."

"Confirm and split," she said. "Paper goes the way we said. Brown goes nowhere until I clear it. And give them the number."

"Giving it now."

She let out a breath she had not known she was holding. Not relief. The work was not done; the work was barely begun. What she had felt tighten in her chest for the last two hours and now felt loosen was simply the part of the operation that lived in the dark with rifles and motorcycles, the part that could go loud and bloody and end up on a phone somewhere and from there in front of a magistrate. That part was over. What came next was paper, and paper was where she was strongest, and paper was where the operation actually happened.

She turned to Menon.

"Now," she said. "Walk me through the settlement."

He unfolded himself off the floor with the small grunts of a man whose knees had filed for retirement. He came to the dark table and took out a stub of pencil and a single sheet, and he drew nothing, because Menon never drew anything; he simply pointed at the empty paper as though the structure were already printed on it and he were merely reading.

"The cargo we took tonight," he said, "was already paid for. That is the beauty of it and you must understand it or you will frighten yourself. The men across the fence have their money. They were paid weeks ago through a settlement in Sharjah, against an invoice for marble, twelve containers of Pakistani marble that does not exist and will never sail. So tonight we have not stolen heroin from anyone who is owed. We have stolen heroin from the buyer. From the network. The supply chain does not know its customer has changed."

"And the next consignment."

"The next consignment we order ourselves. We become the buyer. We pay the same printers, the same chemists, the same patient men, through the same marble, and they are delighted, because to them nothing has changed except that the new buyer pays a day faster." He set the pencil down. "This is why he wanted it whole. Not raided. Inherited. You do not blow up a pipe. You change the name on the meter."

She had heard versions of this from Baba, but Baba spoke in scripture and Menon spoke in invoices, and it was Menon's version that she would have to be able to run when Baba was not in the room.

"And our side," she said. "Tonight's brown, when it sells. Where does the money land?"

He turned the empty sheet a quarter turn, as if to face the question.

"Cash first, always. The street is cash, it has to be, because every other rupee in this country now has a name and an Aadhaar number and a banker who must ask where it came from. So the brown sells for cash across four states, and the cash piles up, and a pile of cash is a problem, not a prize. A pile of cash cannot buy a building or a man's son a seat in a college or a colonel a holiday. It must be made to forget where it was born." He touched the paper. "We hand it to the hawala. A hundi here, against a hundi there. The cash goes to a man in Karol Bagh, and the same value comes up, the same night, in dirhams, in Deira, with no wire, no swift, no border crossed by anything but a man's word and a torn note that matches its other half. From Dubai it is clean enough to begin to work. We put it into gold, into a jeweller who over-bills. We put it into rice. We over-invoice the rice, the good basmati, sailing legally out of Kandla to a buyer in Bangkok who is ours, and the buyer pays too much, and the difference is the dirty money walking through the front door of a bank in a clean shirt." He almost smiled again. "By the time it comes home to do its work it has been a marble that did not exist, a dirham, a gram of gold, and a sack of rice. No man alive could follow it. Several have tried."

"Bedi tried," she said, before she could stop herself.

Menon looked at her. He knew the name. Of course he knew the name; he had probably built half the structures Harish Bedi had spent his last years trying to map.

"Bedi," he said carefully, "looked at the rails. He did not look at who owned the rails." He let that sit. "It is not the same thing, beta."

She did not tell him that she had stood close enough to Bedi to smell the bergamot in his cologne and the fear under it, that she had watched the man's heart stop on a schedule she had set, that he had said, with the last clear breath available to him, that he had done nothing. She did not tell Menon any of it, because Menon was hers to run and not the other way round, and because the only person to whom she had ever told such things was Baba, and Baba had taught her long ago that the work weighed less when you handed the weight to him.

The middle screen was still dark, but her watch buzzed once against her wrist, the soft double pulse of the closed channel asking to open. She crossed to the biscuit tin, took out a third handset, the one that never touched a network, and put it to her ear.

"It's clean," she said. "We have the pipe."

"How much," Baba said. No greeting. He never wasted a greeting on her; greetings were for people you were managing, and she was not managed, she was made.

"Tonight, one consignment. But Menon says we don't own a consignment, we own the route. We're the buyer now. They don't know they've been sold."

There was a silence on the line that she had learned to read as pleasure. Baba was not a man who said well done. He paid in silences, and she had spent twenty years learning their denominations.

"Do you understand what you took tonight," he said at last.

"A pipe. Heroin and paper, out of the Crescent, through Punjab."

"You took their lungs." His voice was very quiet and very even, the voice he used for the things he believed all the way to the floor of himself. "For twenty years they have been planting men in our walls. Patient men. Men who will wake one morning when they are told and turn a key the wrong way in a room we are not allowed to name. And the thing that feeds those men, the thing that keeps them warm and patient and asleep, came up this pipe. Tonight you put your hand over its mouth."

She watched the white shapes on the thermal feed begin to disperse, Alpha's men melting back into the cane with the cargo, the two frightened packers already small and trudging on the road toward whatever home they would not sleep in.

"There will be a cost," Baba went on, and now he was not talking to her so much as letting her overhear him talk to God. "The brown we move will find arms to ruin. Boys in the colonies, in Maqboolpura, in the villages where they bury more sons than they marry. I know the streets we will poison. I have counted them." A breath. "And I have weighed them against the other thing, the morning with the key turned the wrong way, and they do not balance, beta. They are not even close. A protector who keeps his hands clean is not protecting anything but his own sleep. Every gram is a coin minted for the country's survival. The wreckage is real and it is mine to carry and it is smaller, it is so much smaller, than what waits on the other side of doing nothing."

She had heard it before, in pieces, but never assembled like this, never laid end to end with the dead boys named. It did not horrify her. That was the thing she would not have been able to explain to anyone who had not grown up the way she had grown up, with him. It steadied her. There was a man in the world who had looked at the worst arithmetic there was and refused to flinch from either column, who poisoned with one hand precisely so that he could shield with the other, and who carried both and did not pretend the poison was medicine. Ravana with all ten heads turned the same way, toward the thing coming for his island. She had given her life to him a long time ago. Nights like this she remembered why.

"Sita," he said.

"Baba."

"The pipe is the bait as much as the bread. When they find their lungs are gone, they will not lie still. Someone will be sent to learn who did this." A pause she did not yet know how to value. "I want them to find a thread. I want them to pull it. And I want you to be standing at the other end when they do."

The line went dead before she could ask which thread, or whose, and she stood for a moment in the dark with the silent handset against her cheek, listening to the grain settle in its sacks, aware that somewhere out beyond the fence a machine was already turning for home with nothing to carry, and that she had just been told, in the gentle voice he kept for scripture, that she was no longer only the cut. She was the lure on the hook as well.

Behind her Menon folded his empty sheet of paper into quarters and put it in his pocket, as careful with the nothing on it as he would have been with a confession.

"He's pleased," Menon said. It was not a question.

"He's setting something," she said.

The old man nodded slowly, the way men nod at a weather they have seen before and survived only by luck.

"Then we should both be very careful," he said, "to be standing where he wants us, and not an inch to the side of it."

She put the handset back in the tin and closed the lid, and reached up, and turned off the single bulb.
