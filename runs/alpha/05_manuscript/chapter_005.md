The Karol Bagh address was a jeweller's, which was correct. Cash and gold sat behind the same counters here as they had for sixty years, and a man could carry a great deal of either out the door without a single rupee touching a bank, a UPI handle, or the long memory of the state. Sita had chosen it for that. She had also chosen it because the lane in front of it bent twice in ninety metres, which meant that anyone standing still long enough to watch the shop had to choose where to stand, and a man who has chosen where to stand has told you something about himself.

She came in from the metro side on foot, in a chikan kurta the colour of weak tea, a cloth bag of vegetables on her shoulder because a woman with onions in her bag is a woman going home. She had spent forty minutes reaching a lane she could have reached in twelve. That was the discipline and she did not resent the time. The afternoon was the colour of brick dust. On the municipal poles the facial-recognition cameras kept their slow blind vigil, and she walked beneath them with her chin down and her steps uneven, because the algorithms read a gait as surely as a face, and she had no wish to be a clean frame in anyone's archive.

The meet was for four o'clock. The principal was a Marwari named Goenka who moved money the old way, ledger against ledger, a number spoken on the telephone in Dubai becoming a tile of gold in a vault in Chandni Chowk, no wire, no trace, nothing for the blockchain people to chew. He was folding the Crescent money now, the river she had taken in Punjab ten days ago, into rails that had carried Triangle proceeds for years. It was delicate work, marrying two flows without either curdling, and Goenka did not like to be rushed, and so the meet was a courtesy as much as a transaction. She would drink his tea. She would let him complain about the rate. She would leave with nothing on her person but a date and a figure held in her head.

She did not go to the shop. She went past it.

This was the part that could not be taught, only survived into. She walked the bent lane at the pace of a woman with somewhere dull to be, and she let her eyes do the work without her head moving, and she read the static. A scooter mechanic, sleeves black to the elbow, a real one, his hands never still. A vegetable cart, the seller calling his prices, the brinjals already going soft, a real one. A boy on a phone in a doorway, thumbs moving, a hundred thousand of him in this city. And at the mouth of the lane, where it gave onto the wider road, a man in a grey bush shirt looking at his own phone outside a shuttered shop.

She clocked him and let him go. One sighting is nothing. One sighting is a man waiting for his wife, a man who has had a fight at home, a man killing twenty minutes before a film. The trade did not begin with one sighting. It began with two.

She walked to the end, bought a packet of biscuits she did not want from a kiosk, because buying something is a reason to have come, and she turned and went back on the opposite footpath. She gave it the full sweep this time, slow, the shop windows doing the work of mirrors. The mechanic. The cart. The boy gone from the doorway, which was correct, boys did not last. And the grey bush shirt, no longer at the shuttered shop, now thirty metres deeper into the lane, on her side, his phone away, his hands empty.

Two.

Something in her chest went very quiet and very wide, the old clearing-out, the place where fear should have been and instead there was only attention. She did not change her pace. That was the cardinal thing, the thing the amateurs got wrong and died of: the body wants to answer a sighting, the shoulders want to square, the step wants to lengthen, and all of it tells the watcher he has been seen, and a watcher who knows he has been seen does one of two things, he breaks off or he closes, and you do not want to be the one who decided which. So she kept the onions on her shoulder and the boredom on her face and she read him in the glass of a sari shop while pretending to price a bolt of green silk.

He was good. That was the first true thing she understood about him and she filed it without weighing it. He did not look at her. He had found a reason to be where he was, a paan stand, and he was buying, and his eyes were on the paan-wallah's hands, and only a person who had stood where he stood would know that the angle he had chosen gave him the door of Goenka's shop in the corner of his vision the entire time. He had not chosen badly. He had chosen the one spot in the bent lane from which the door could be held without facing it. She had thought, an hour ago, walking it, that there was only one such spot. She had been right. He was standing in it.

A narcotics man would not know to stand there. A narcotics constable looked at the thing he wanted, the way a dog looks at meat. This one looked at everything except.

She walked on past Goenka's door without slowing, without a glance, and turned right at the bend, out of the bush shirt's eyeline, and in the four seconds the bend bought her she catalogued what she would need. Not the shop, abandoned now, Goenka could wait, Goenka would understand a silence the way a fish understands deep water. She needed the shape of it. How many. Where the second one was, because there was always a second one, a man alone is a man, a man with a partner is a service, and the difference was the difference between a leak and a hunt.

She found the second one at the chai stall by the temple, and her certainty cooled by a degree, because he was wrong. He was too still. The bush shirt had been right and this one was too still, a heavyset man in a checked shirt with a glass of tea going cold in front of him, and his stillness was the stillness of a man told to hold a position and frightened of doing it badly. An anchor. A body to fix the box while the good ones floated. She watched him not drink his tea and she revised, downward, with a small private relief she would later remember and not forgive.

Because that was what she decided, in the bent lane, with the brick-dust light going amber: that the bush shirt was the talent and the checked shirt was the ballast, and that one talented man with one nervous helper was not a service. A service did not put its weakness where a target could read it in a glass of tea. A service floated six and you never caught the edge of the box, you only felt it, a pressure, a wrongness, and went home and lived. She had felt that pressure twice in eleven years and both times she had simply gone home and Baba had cleaned it later, quietly, and she had never learned the faces. This was not that. This was a face she had caught in two sightings inside ten minutes, and the trade said a watcher you can catch that fast is a watcher who has not done it long, or who has been given more ground than men to cover it, or who has been sent in a hurry.

A hurry. She held the word and turned it over and it fit the relief she wanted. A hurry meant local. A hurry meant somebody downstream had snagged a thread of the Punjab river, a customs man who had been paid and grown curious, a state-police inspector who had noticed the Crescent flow thicken and put a tail on the Karol Bagh money because the money was the only thing he could see. Low. A penetration at the level of the rupee, not the level of the work. The kind of thing that happened when you took a pipeline whole and the old owners' debts came knocking in the form of frightened men with badges.

She believed it because it was survivable. She knew, even then, in the cold clear part of her, that she believed it partly because it was survivable, and she let the knowing pass like a stone going under water, because a person could not work and also stand outside herself watching the working, not in a live box, not with the light going.

She gave them nothing more to read. She walked herself out of the lane the way she had come, neither fast nor slow, and she did the thing that would tell her whether she was right, the thing that cost nothing and proved everything. She crossed against the traffic at the main road, threading the scooters, the way a local woman does without thinking, and on the far side she stopped at a fruit seller and bought four mosambis and stood weighing them in her hand, facing back the way she had come, an ordinary woman with all the time in the world.

If it was a leak, the bush shirt would follow her across and lose her in the crossing, or hold the shop and let her go, because a leak follows the money and the money was Goenka and Goenka was behind her now. If it was a hunt, if it was the thing she would not name, the box would already have a man on this side of the road, ahead of her, because a box does not chase, a box receives, it puts a body where you are going to be and lets you walk into it.

She watched the crossing for ninety seconds, weighing the mosambis, and the bush shirt did not come across, and the checked shirt did not come across, and no new face arranged itself in the amber light ahead of her with the studied carelessness of a man who has been told where she will be. The footpath ahead was only people. A schoolgirl. Two boys with a cricket bat. A woman exactly like herself with a bag of exactly the same vegetables.

There. She let the breath out slow through her nose. A leak. A frightened constable's tail, run on the cheap, one good man borrowed from somewhere and one bad one to mind him, and they had held the shop because the shop was the money and she was only a woman who had walked past it. They had not followed her because they did not know she was anything. She was clean. The work was clean. Goenka could be reached tonight by a different route, through the boy who ran his errands, a number passed in a way no camera would ever resolve into a meeting.

She paid for the mosambis with cash, because a UPI handle was a name and a name was a thread and she did not give threads to fruit sellers, and she walked to the metro with the small held attention of a professional who has had a near thing and closed it out. At the station mouth the lenses on their black domes swivelled across the crowd, indifferent, and she gave them the bored profile of the kurta and the bag and the woman going home, and she descended into the cool tiled light and let the train take her three stops the wrong way before she changed and came back, and by the time she surfaced again the city had swallowed her whole and there was no one in the world who could say where Sita had been at four o'clock.

She did not call Baba until she was sure, and being sure took another two hours and a change of clothes and a phone she had never used and would never use again, bought for cash from a man in Lajpat Nagar who took a photocopy of someone else's Aadhaar for the KYC and asked no questions, the small daily corruption that kept the trade alive in a country that had tried to make every citizen a number. The clean phone she treated as dirty, which was the only way to treat a phone. She walked while she used it. She walked a long anonymous stretch of road where the poles were old and bare and the faces uncounted, and she called the number that changed every Tuesday, and when the line opened she heard him breathe before he spoke, and the breathing was home, the way it had been home in a restaurant when she was eighteen and a hotel key had been warm in her hand.

"I went to see the jeweller," she said. There was no jeweller and no shop and no Karol Bagh in the words she used; there was a grammar they had built over eleven years, in which a thing was named by what it was not. "The lane was crowded."

A pause, the length of a man putting down a cup. "How crowded."

"Two." She walked. A truck went by trailing diesel and noise and she waited it out. "One who knew where to stand. One who didn't. The one who didn't was holding the door."

She felt him weigh it down the line, the silence not empty but working, and she gave him the rest before he asked, because he would want it shaped and she had spent two hours shaping it.

"It's small, Baba." She heard her own certainty and was steadied by it. "It came from underneath. The river's been running thick for ten days and somebody down at the level of the rupee felt the current change and put out a hand. A customs man, a state inspector, somebody who can see the money and nothing above it. They held the money, not me. They didn't follow me across the road. If it were yours, or the kind of thing that pretends to be nobody's, I'd never have caught the edge of it. I caught the edge of it inside ten minutes. That's not patience. That's a borrowed man run in a hurry. It's a leak, low down, and I think it's the cost of taking the pipe whole. The old debts are walking around with badges."

She stopped at a hoarding for a cement company and stood in its shadow and listened to him think. When he spoke he did not tell her she was right and he did not tell her she was wrong, which was his way, and which she read as agreement because she wanted to.

"You closed it out clean?"

"Clean. The jeweller never saw me. I'll reach him tonight through the boy."

"Then reach him through the boy." A breath. "And the borrowed man. The one who knew where to stand." Something moved under his voice, slow, the way a large fish moves under still water, there and gone before the surface broke. "Give me what you have. The build of him. The shirt. The side of the face."

"Grey bush shirt. Forty, give or take. Right-handed, he reached with his right for the paan and kept his weight on his left, an old man's habit in a young man, maybe a knee, maybe service. He held the angle without facing it. He's been taught." She heard herself and added, because honesty was a tool and she used it on herself the way she used it on a lock, "Better than the work should need, for a leak. But the other one was ballast, Baba. A service doesn't hang its weak man in a window. They sent who they had."

"They sent who they had," he repeated, and she could not tell, down the changing line, whether he was agreeing with her or reading the sentence back to himself to hear how it sat, and she did not ask, because asking was for people who did not trust, and she trusted him the way the eye trusts the dark when there is nothing else to do but walk through it.

"I'll watch the rails for a week," she said. "If it's a leak it'll show again, lower, grabbing at the money. If it shows higher I'll know I was wrong."

"You won't be wrong," he said, and it was the only thing he said that she would turn over later, in the months when later came, because she could not decide even then whether he had meant it as comfort or as a thing he already knew, and the not deciding was the closest she would come, that evening, to the truth standing in the bent lane behind her in a grey bush shirt, holding a door it had been sent to hold, waiting with a patience she had mistaken for haste.

She ended the call. She broke the phone open with her thumbs against the kerb, the way she had broken a hundred of them, and she dropped the two halves into two different drains a kilometre apart, and she walked home with the mosambis in her bag, an ordinary woman in the amber dusk, going where being seen was nothing at all.
