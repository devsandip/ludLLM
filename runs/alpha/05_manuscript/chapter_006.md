The ceiling fan in the cybercafe at Nehru Place turned too slowly to move the heat. It only stirred it, carrying the smell of warm plastic and other people's cigarettes from one end of the long room to the other. Twelve terminals, partitioned by sheets of laminated chipboard, most of them taken by boys checking cricket scores and a man in a courier's uniform typing a letter with two fingers. She had taken the corner machine, the one whose monitor faced the wall, and she had paid for two hours in cash. At the desk by the door a register lay open beside a saucer of fennel seeds. She had written Lalitha Rao, and a telephone number that belonged to no one, and the boy had not looked up.

It was the kind of place that left no mark on her. That was the whole of its value. The terminals at Lodhi Road logged every query she put to the registry, every file she touched, the date and the time and the workstation, and somewhere a man whose job it was to watch the watchers reconciled those logs against the tasking she had been given. To pull what she needed from her own desk would be to stand in the doorway of the very room she was trying to see into and announce her interest. So she had come here instead, to a grey-market arcade of computer shops and pirated software and STD booths, where for twenty rupees an hour she could be no one at all.

She was looking at the Ministry of Corporate Affairs filing portal. It had come online two years before, and most of the country had not yet noticed; you could now sit in a cybercafe and read the directors and the registered addresses of a company that, a decade earlier, would have cost you a week and a friendly clerk and a chai-money bribe. She typed in the corporate identity number she had copied onto the inside cover of a matchbook, and the page resolved, slow as everything here was slow, and gave her three names and an address in Karol Bagh that was a first-floor accountant's office above a sari shop.

She did not write any of it on the machine. She wrote it on paper.

The paper was the thing. She had learned that in her first year, from a deputy director who was dead now, a careful Malayali who never trusted a typewriter ribbon because a ribbon kept a confession of everything it had struck. Paper you could carry. Paper you could burn in a kitchen sink, or fold into a child's picture book, or leave in a place no one would think to look because it was so ordinary. She kept hers in a manila folder inside a cloth jhola bag, and on top of the folder, so that anyone who opened the bag would find them first, were a packet of cotton wool, a tin of Cerelac, and a yellow rattle. The bag of a tired woman going home to a baby. Which, after all, she was.

The pattern had taken her eleven weeks to see, and once she had seen it she could not stop seeing it, the way you cannot unhear a clock once you have noticed it ticking.

It had begun as money. It nearly always began as money. Small placements, none of them large enough on its own to trip a wire, moving through the hawala settlement that ran between Dubai and the city, dressed as trade: a consignment of dates over-invoiced here, a parcel of textiles under-invoiced there, the difference settling quietly into accounts in Bombay and Chandigarh and here in the capital. Trade-based laundering was the dullest thing in the world to read. That was its protection. No one promoted his career by spending three years on the freight tonnage of cardamom. She had spent three years on it, because somewhere in the third year she had understood that the accounts the money settled into were not the accounts of greedy men. They belonged, when you traced them past two or three patient removes, to people: a procurement officer in a defence laboratory, the brother-in-law of a man who handled cipher traffic, a contractor with a pass to a place he had no honest reason to enter. The money was not being stolen. It was being planted. Someone was buying loyalty in advance, the way a careful gardener puts in bulbs in autumn for a spring he intends to be alive to see.

She had taken that much to the registry, in March, in the proper way, in a single-copy memorandum to the proper desk. And the memorandum had gone in, and nothing had come out. No tasking. No request to widen. No summons. It had simply ceased, the way a stone ceases when it goes into a well, and after a fortnight she had understood that she had told someone, and that the someone had decided the well was the right place for it. That was the night she had begun keeping two of everything. One for the registry. One for the jhola bag.

Tonight, in the corner of the cybercafe with the fan turning over her head, she had the second copy spread in her mind, because she did not even trust the paper out of the folder in a public room. And she had found the thing she had been afraid she would find.

It was the watch on the laboratory officer. She had requested it herself, in January, a low-grade physical surveillance, two bodies, a fortnight, to see who the man met and whether the deposits in his brother's account answered to anything in his diary. It was the most ordinary request in the service. And in the second week the watch had been stood down. Resources, the chit had said. Reallocation of resources, signed off through the tasking desk, and she had grumbled and let it go, because every officer in the building lost a watch to reallocation sooner or later and you did not make an enemy of the tasking desk over two bodies for a fortnight.

She had let it go in January. She did not let it go now. Because she sat in the cybercafe and she put the two halves of the year together, the money on one side and the tasking on the other, and she saw that the watch had been stood down in the same week that one of the planted accounts, the laboratory officer's, had taken its largest single placement. Someone had pulled the eyes off the man at the precise moment it most mattered that no eyes were on him.

And that, she understood, with a cold that started somewhere below the breastbone and spread, was not a thing a foreign service could do.

She made herself think it through slowly, the way she would make a junior think it through, because the cold was the enemy of clear thought and she did not have the luxury of being wrong. A foreign principal could plant money. Of course he could; money was water, it found any crack, it needed no passport. A foreign principal could turn a man, buy a man, blackmail a man, run a man for twenty years. All of that lived outside the building and reached in. But a foreign principal could not stand down an Indian surveillance order. He could not sign a reallocation chit through the tasking desk at Lodhi Road. He could not reach into the body of the service and lift its hand away from a man's throat at the moment of squeezing. Only the service could do that to itself. Only someone inside, with the authority to move bodies and the authority to move money, both at once, in the same week, to the same end.

She sat very still. On the screen in front of her the company filing waited, ignored, its three director names patient. Behind her one of the boys laughed at something and another said arre yaar in mock despair, and the courier with two fingers hunted for a key. The world went on being the world. She turned the proposition over and looked for the place where it broke, because she wanted it to break.

Two authorities. Money and tasking. In the architecture of the building those two lived in different rooms, under different deputies, behind different doors, by design, because no sane service let one man hold both. Financing was clandestine and walled off; operational tasking was its own kingdom with its own jealous king. To move money and to move bodies you would need to sit above both, or astride both, at the one desk in the whole edifice where the two streams ran together before they branched.

There was one such desk. She knew whose it was. She had known whose it was before she let herself say it, and she had spent twenty minutes not saying it, checking the filing, reading the date stamps, doing anything rather than write the name on the paper, because the name was Arvind Sehgal and the name could not be true.

Additional Secretary. Thirty years a servant of the state, near enough. A man whose career was held up in the induction lectures as a thing to imitate, who had been steady in '71 and steady through the bad years after, who had buried colleagues and outlived directors and never once been touched by the rumours that touched everyone. The building did not merely trust Sehgal. The building did not know how to imagine itself without him. If you had stood in the corridor and said his name and the word traitor in the same breath, the men who heard you would not have been angry; they would have been embarrassed for you, the way one is embarrassed for a man who has begun to talk to himself. It was not that the proposition was forbidden. It was that it was absurd, and the absurdity was the strongest wall around him, stronger than any clearance, because it lived in the head of every officer who might ever have looked.

She tested it for the thing she most feared, which was herself. An analyst who has hidden a folder in a baby's bag wants, more than she will admit, to be right; wants the pattern to be sinister because a sinister pattern justifies the folder. She knew that disease. She had seen it eat careful men. So she argued his innocence for him, harder than he would have argued it himself. A clerk's error, two unrelated decisions that fell in the same week by the ordinary cruelty of coincidence. A genuine reallocation; the placements traced to him only because everything traced to him, because everything in clandestine financing went past his desk and so his fingerprints were on the whole library, guilty and innocent volumes alike. That last was the strong objection. She sat with it for a long time. And then she set against it the one fact the coincidence could not eat: that her own memorandum, the proper one, the single copy to the proper desk, had gone into the well and made no sound. Coincidence could stand down a watch. Coincidence could not also reach across the building and smother the very report that would have noticed the watch being stood down. Two coincidences pointing the same way and serving the same man stopped being coincidence and became a hand.

The fan turned. She breathed out, and found that she had been holding it.

So. The rot was not coming over the border. That was the comfort she had let herself keep all spring, the analyst's comfort, that the enemy was outside and the building was the building, sound, hers, the place she went home from to a child. The enemy was not outside. The enemy had a parking space. The enemy signed chits in a hand the registry recognised, and drank the same bad tea, and would nod to her in the corridor tomorrow with that dry, kind, unhurried courtesy that everyone took for the proof of his decency. The money she had been chasing for three years and the protection that money bought were not two enemies. They were one man's two hands, and the man sat near the top, and the top was where everything she might do to him would have to pass on its way to anyone who could act.

That was the part that closed around her now like the heat. There was no one to tell. She had already learned in March what the registry did with the truth: it kept it for Sehgal. To take it higher was to take it through Sehgal, or past him, and there was no past him that did not run through doors he had spent thirty years making sure he could see. Kaul himself, the Chief, would receive any such accusation the way the building received it, as the unravelling of a man she should pity, and he would be gentle and he would be wrong and within the week Sehgal would know precisely who had said it and from how much paper. She could trust no desk, no deputy, no friend, because she could not know which of them the planting had already bought, and the only safe assumption was the cruel one: that the network she had spent three years reading was wide enough that anyone might be in it, and that the one certainty in the whole building was the man she could least afford to be certain of.

So she would tell no one. She would build it alone, on paper, in a folder under the Cerelac, and she would build it the way you build a case you may one day have to hand to a stranger, because the only person she could imagine ever trusting with it was someone outside the building entirely, someone who did not yet exist for her, someone she would have to find or make. Dates. Account chains. The reallocation chit, if she could get a copy of it that did not announce her getting it. The settlement records out of Dubai. Each fact ugly and dull and verifiable, each one able to stand on its own in front of a person who had every reason to disbelieve it, because by the time it was read she might not be there to vouch for any of it.

She caught the shape of that thought before she finished it, and set it down, and did not pick it up again. There was a child at home.

She logged off the terminal. She did not clear the history, because clearing the history was itself a thing a watcher noticed; she left the company filing open and a half-typed search for a cousin's wedding hall above it, the residue of an ordinary tired woman, and she gathered the jhola bag with the folder safe at the bottom under the rattle and the tin. At the desk the boy was eating his fennel seeds and watching a small television. He took her chit and her coins and did not raise his eyes from the screen, where a newsreader was saying something about the markets, and the date along the bottom of the picture read the ninth of May, and she went out into the dark of the arcade and the diesel and the long stairs down.

It was on the third step, with the bag against her hip and the heat of the day still rising off the concrete, that the last piece arrived, and it was not a comfort. She had spent eleven weeks learning to see the fingerprint: a watch stood down, a report smothered, money moved and covered in one week by one hand. She had been so pleased, in the cold analytic way, to have seen it at last. And only now, on the stairs, did she follow the thought to its end and understand that a fingerprint is a thing left by a hand, and that the hand was attached to a man, and that the man had spent thirty years learning to notice exactly the kind of attention she had been paying all spring. If she could read him from the traffic, he could read her from the queries. Not these, not the cybercafe, not Lalitha Rao who paid in coins. But the others. The watch she had asked for in January. The memorandum in March. The interest, patient and specific and now eleven weeks old, of one analyst in one slow river of money.

She went down the rest of the stairs at the same unhurried pace as everyone else, because pace was a thing that could be watched too, and she thought, with no drama at all, the way she would have noted a freight tonnage: he has had eleven weeks longer to look at me than I have had to look at him.
