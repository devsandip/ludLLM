The collection point that week was a wholesale cloth merchant's office above a shuttered showroom in Katra Neel, in the lanes behind Chandni Chowk where the buildings lean into each other and the sky is a grey seam between them. Sita had chosen it herself. Three ways out, two of them through the warren of stairs that connected the upper floors of the whole block, and a third along a parapet to a chawl that emptied onto Naya Bazar. A place where a phone lost its grip on the towers and a camera lost its angle. The merchant did not know her name. He knew only that on a Thursday a woman would come for an envelope of paper that balanced his books in a currency no bank would recognise, and that he was not to look at her face longer than courtesy required.

She came at half past two, when the lane was thickest, handcarts and porters and the lunch crowd from the food stalls, because a watcher hates a crowd as much as a thief loves one. She wore a salwar the colour of weak tea, a cloth bag on her shoulder, a phone she did not use. She had walked the approach twice over forty minutes, once from the Fatehpuri end and once doubling back from the spice market, reading the lane for the things that did not belong to it. A parked two-wheeler with a man who did not dismount. A shop boy who swept the same metre of step. The small, wrong stillness in a moving world. There had been nothing.

The envelope was thinner than she expected, which meant the merchant had shaved his float again, which meant a conversation Menon would have to have with him, not her. She did not count it in the room. She put it flat against her ribs under the kameez where the cloth was loose, said the two sentences that closed the meeting, and went down the back stairs into the smell of dye and damp stone.

It was on the parapet that the day turned.

She had a sightline there, three storeys up, across the corrugated roofs to the mouth of the lane she would not now use. And in that mouth, in the shade of an awning where a man sold lemons from a pyramid, a woman was standing. Not moving. Not buying. A woman of perhaps forty in a grey kurta, ordinary as a brick, with a cloth bag of her own and a stillness that Sita felt before she understood it, the way you feel a held breath in a dark room.

Sita did not stop walking. To stop is to confess. She kept her pace along the parapet, let her eyes slide off the woman and onto the pigeons and the water tanks, and counted. One. Two. At four she looked again, casually, a woman judging the weather, and the lemon-seller was alone. The grey kurta was gone.

It proved nothing. A woman could buy a lemon and leave. A woman could be waiting for her husband, her sister, a porter with a load. Delhi was full of women standing still in the middle of the day because they had nowhere they were required to be. Sita told herself all of it, fluently, the way she told herself things, and did not believe a word of the comfort because her hands had already gone cold and certain and her body had decided the matter without consulting her.

Because she had seen the grey kurta before.

Not the kurta. The stillness. In Karol Bagh, three weeks gone, at the money meet she had walked away from clean, there had been a watcher with exactly this quality, this economy, this gift for being scenery. She had filed it then as police. Customs, perhaps, a slow departmental thing nosing at a hawaladar's traffic, the kind of attention that washed over you and moved on if you gave it nothing to hold. She had told Baba so. He had not argued.

She came off the parapet into the chawl and became, in the space of a stairwell, a different woman. The cloth bag went into a doorway and a jhola of vegetables came out of it, onions and a bunch of coriander showing at the top, because the eye that is hunting a woman with a shoulder bag will pass over a woman with vegetables. She took the dupatta off her head and wore it across her shoulders instead. She changed her walk, the small things, the length of the stride, the angle of the chin. Eleven years of this lived in her hands and her feet and asked nothing of her mind, which was free to be afraid.

The cleaning run took her ninety minutes and she did it as if her life were the stake, because she had been taught that the day you assumed it was not was the day it was. Out at Naya Bazar into the press of porters. A cycle-rickshaw four hundred metres and off it early. Through the cloth market against the grain of the foot traffic, watching the reflections in the glass of a sari shop for the face that turned when she turned. The Metro at Chandni Chowk, in through one gate and along the platform and out through another without taking a train, the cameras eating her from every corner of the ceiling, and that was the part she hated now in a way she had not hated it five years ago, the cameras, because a camera did not get bored and did not go home and did not forget your face, and somewhere a machine was learning the geometry of cheekbones it had no business knowing. She kept her head at the angle that gave them least. She had practised the angle.

By the time she crossed the river she was as clean as method could make her, and she had not seen the grey kurta again, and she trusted that absence not at all.

She did not call. A call was a thread, and a thread could be pulled. She went to the dead letter arrangement, a particular chalk mark on a particular pillar of a particular flyover, and made the sign that meant she needed to see him, today, not urgent enough to break cover but soon. Then she went home to the rented room in Lakshmi Nagar that was not her home, that was a set with a woman living in it, and she sat on the floor with her back to the wall where she could see the door and she waited for her hands to be warm again.

He sent for her that night.

The house was in one of the colonies off the Ring Road, a doctor's bungalow with a brass plate and a watchman who was not a watchman, set back behind a hedge of bottle-brush that killed the streetlight. She came in through the kitchen as she always did. He was in the back room with the single lamp and the two chairs and the smell of the cardamom tea he drank when his stomach troubled him, and when she saw him the cold thing in her chest loosened by a degree, because that was what the sight of him did, had always done, since she was a girl with a hotel key in her fist and a dead man behind a door she had just closed.

Baba.

He did not look up at once. He was reading something, two pages of typescript, and he let her stand a moment in the way that was not rudeness but ritual, the small silence that said the world could wait while he finished a sentence. Then he set the pages face down, which she noticed, and looked at her over the top of his spectacles, and read her the way he read everything.

"You ran a route," he said. Not a question. He could see it on her, the residue of ninety minutes of fear worn off but not gone.

"Twice now," she said. She sat. She kept her voice flat because he disliked drama and because flatness was its own kind of report. "Karol Bagh. And today, Katra Neel, on the way out. A woman. Forty, give or take. Grey kurta today, but it isn't the clothes. It's the way she stands. The same way both times. Still, like furniture. Patient." The word came out heavier than she meant. "Patient" was the word that had been sitting in her since the parapet, and saying it aloud made it true in a way she had not wanted it true.

He listened without moving. That was a thing about him. Lesser men nodded, made the small noises that said I am listening, because they needed you to know they were generous enough to attend. Baba simply attended, completely, and the attention was the most frightening and the most consoling thing she knew.

"Tell me what she did," he said.

"Nothing. That's what frightens me. She did nothing both times. In Karol Bagh she was at the chai stall across from the meet, and she never looked at it once, which is exactly what a person looking at it would do. Today she was at the lemon-seller's, in the mouth of the lane I'd have used if I hadn't gone over the roof. Four seconds and she was gone." Sita pressed her thumb into the heel of her other hand, a thing she did not know she did until she caught herself doing it. "If she's surveillance, she's good. She's better than police. Police get bored and lean on something. They check their phone. She didn't check her phone."

"And you cleaned."

"Ninety minutes. The market, the Metro, the river. I was clean when I crossed. I'd put money on it." A beat. "I'd have put money on Karol Bagh too."

He took off his spectacles then and folded them and set them on the typescript, and he looked at her with the bare eyes, and she made herself hold them. The lamp put half his face in shadow. He was old now in a way that had crept up on her over the years, the loose skin at the throat, the spots on the backs of the hands that had once been so sure on her shoulder. It did not make him less. It made him more, the way a blade is more frightening when you can see it has been used.

"You did everything correctly," he said at last. "You read the parapet. You took the high route. You changed your shape, you cleaned for ninety minutes, you did not call, you used the chalk. Eleven years and you are still the most disciplined instrument I have ever held." He let that sit. He knew the weight of his own praise; he spent it like a man who knew the value of money. "Now listen to me, because I am going to tell you what you already know but cannot feel tonight, and I need you to feel it."

She waited.

"There is a hunt," he said. "I have told you there would be. I have told you that you are the bait and that bait is, by its nature, watched. Did you imagine the watching would feel like nothing? It feels exactly like this. Like a woman in a grey kurta who is too still. That is what success feels like from the inside. We have pulled the pipeline out from under them." His voice did not rise. It never rose. "We have taken their war chest at the moment they needed it most, the moment before they meant to wake their sleepers, and they are frightened, and a frightened animal stops hiding and starts hunting. So they hunt. They put eyes on the traffic. They send patient women to stand at lemon-sellers' stalls. And every patient woman they spend on you is a patient woman they are not spending on the thing they are truly trying to protect." He leaned forward an inch. "You are not the prey, Sita. You are the lamp. The moths come to you so that I can count them. The day you stop seeing the watchers is the day to be afraid, because it will mean they have found a way to watch you that you cannot feel. This"—he opened one hand toward her, toward the residue of her fear—"this is the system working."

It was the thing she had come to hear. She knew that even as it worked on her, the way a child knows the hand on its forehead is checking for fever and still leans into it. The fear did not vanish. It changed state. It went from a thing that was happening to her into a thing that had a place in a design, and a thing with a place in a design could be carried, because the design was his and the design had never once been wrong in eleven years, not about Bedi, not about the restaurant, not about the man behind the hotel door whose face she could still see if she let herself, which she did not.

"If she's that good," Sita said, and stopped, because the next part was hard to say to him and she made herself say it anyway, "if she's a service watcher and not police, then she isn't alone. You don't run a watcher like that single. There's a desk behind her. There's a file with my movements in it, growing."

"Yes," he said. Simply. He did not flinch from it and so she did not have to. "There is a desk. There is a file. Let it grow. A file is a snake that eats itself, because the more it knows the more it has to explain to the man it reports to, and the man it reports to"—and here something moved at the back of his eyes that she did not have the standing to ask about—"the man it reports to may not want the questions that file is asking. Trust me that I am closer to that desk than that desk is to you. Trust me that I have an answer for the man who owns it." He sat back. "Have I ever sent you somewhere I could not reach you?"

"No."

"Have I ever been wrong about the ground?"

"No."

"Then be wrong with me, if it comes to that, and not right alone. That was the bargain when you were eighteen and it is the bargain now." He picked up his spectacles again, which meant the matter was closing, which meant comfort, the ritual of it, the world resuming its proper shape. "You will not change the routes. If you change the routes she learns that you have seen her, and a watcher who knows she is seen is replaced by one you have not learned yet. Let her watch. Be ordinary. Be the woman with the vegetables." A flicker that on another man would have been a smile. "You went to the vegetables today. I could see it on you when you walked in. The onions. Good. You think with your hands even when your head is afraid. That is why I chose you."

She felt the praise land where it always landed, in the hollow place that had been waiting for it since before she had a word for what it was. She was aware, distantly, the way you are aware of weather in another city, that there had been a moment on the parapet when the cold in her hands had been trying to tell her something her training would not let her hear. The moment was gone now. He had folded it up and put it away with his spectacles and the face-down pages, and she let him, because the alternative to letting him was a thing she had no architecture to hold.

"Drink some tea before you go," he said. "You look like a woman who has not eaten."

She drank the tea. It was too sweet, the way he made it, the way he had made it for her at eighteen in a different room when her hands had shaken so badly the cup had chattered against her teeth and he had wrapped both his hands around hers until they stilled. They did not shake now. She had not let them shake in front of him in a decade. But he watched her drink as though they might, as though some part of him preferred her the girl who needed both his hands, and she drank slowly to give him that.

When she left it was past midnight and the colony was asleep and the bottle-brush hedge killed the streetlight as it was meant to. She went out the kitchen way and down the service lane and she ran a short instinctive check at the corner, three seconds, the reflection in the dark window of a parked Maruti, because the body does what the body has learned whether the mind has been soothed or not.

In the window she saw the empty lane, and the streetlight two hundred metres off, and under the streetlight, at the edge of the cone of sodium yellow where the light gave up, the shape of someone standing.

Not moving. A woman's outline. A cloth bag.

Sita's heart did a single hard thing in her chest and then was quiet, because Baba's voice was already in her, doing its work, telling her that this was the lamp and the moth, that this was the system working, that the day to be afraid was the day she stopped seeing them. She held the reflection one more second to fix the outline, the way you fix a face you mean to know again.

The streetlight buzzed and dimmed, the way the old sodium lamps did, and when it steadied the cone of light was empty, and the lane was only a lane, and there was no one under the light at all.

She walked home the long way and did not look back, because he had told her to be ordinary, and she had never in eleven years failed to do the thing he told her.
