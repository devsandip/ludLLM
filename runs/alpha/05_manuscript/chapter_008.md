The bus was the wrong bus and she had taken it on purpose.

It went south down Aurobindo Marg when she needed to go north, and she rode it three stops past any reason a person might have, standing in the press of bodies with her hand on the chrome rail, watching the doors. Not the people. The doors. People could be changed out, a fresh face at every junction, and a service that knew its work would change them. Doors could not lie. A man who boarded behind you at one stop and stepped down at the next, and was replaced at his exact post near the rear exit by another man of the same idle patience, told you something the faces never would.

She had set herself rules before she left the room that morning, the way Baba had taught her to set them, aloud, so that the discipline lived outside her and could not be argued with later when the heat and the fear started to bargain. No phone. The phone stayed switched off in a tin in the room, because a phone was a confession that walked beside you, and even dark it could be made to whisper. No card, no UPI, no scan. Cash only, the soft folded notes she kept for exactly this, so that she left no transaction behind her like a dropped glove. No autorickshaw hailed from an app, because the app remembered. Her own scooter stayed under its cover three lanes from the room, because the plate would be read forty times between here and Old Delhi and the reading would be kept.

And no looking. That was the hardest rule and the only one that mattered. You did not look for the watcher. The amateur looked, and the looking was the tell that lost you nothing and gained the other side everything, because now they knew you knew, and a hunt that knew you knew became a different and faster animal. You did not look. You built a shape in the city that a follower had to pass through, and then you read the water for the stone.

She got down at the fourth stop, into the white noon, into the diesel and the heat that stood up off the road like something with weight. She did not break stride. A woman in her late thirties in a grey kurta with a cloth bag on her shoulder, hair pulled back, a face the cameras would file under nothing. She had dressed to be forgettable and that, too, was a kind of armour and a kind of trap, because the people sent after her would have dressed the same way, and in this city of a hundred million careful nobodies the trick was never to find the careful nobody. The trick was geometry.

She walked into the market because markets were old and the cameras there were old, hung crooked over jewellers' doors by men who cared only that their own thresholds were seen, blind to the lanes between. She knew the dead ground the way a sailor knows reefs. Two years ago she had walked these same lanes on a different afternoon and mapped where the lenses pointed, and the map did not change because nobody in a lane like this had money to move a camera. She passed under the jeweller's eye and then she was in the grey, in the canyon of cloth shops and the stink of frying and the racks of synthetic saris breathing out their chemical sweetness, and here, for ninety metres, she could not be watched by a machine. Only by a person. And a person, unlike a machine, had to be somewhere.

She slowed at a bangle stall and let her hand move over the glass without seeing it.

The leg behind her, the long open stretch of Aurobindo Marg and the bus, had been the question. This narrow place was where the answer had to show itself or stay hidden, and a hidden answer was its own answer. If she were truly travelling, truly a woman going from a southern errand to a northern one, no one on God's earth would have followed her into this lane, because the lane went nowhere a stranger would want. It fed back on itself. It was a pocket. To be in it you had to mean to be in it.

A boy pushed a handcart past her. A pair of women argued over a price in Punjabi. An old man slept upright on a stool with his chin on his chest. She turned the bangles in the light and in the periphery, in the soft unfocused edge of her vision that Baba had drilled into her until it was sharper than the centre, she let the lane behind her exist without examining it.

A man at the mouth of the lane had stopped to take a call.

That was all. A man, thirty-five, untucked checked shirt, ordinary, a phone to his ear at the place where the lane opened onto the wider road. People stopped to take calls. It was the most natural thing in the world and that was exactly why it was the oldest move in the book, because a phone to the ear bought a man the right to stand still and face any direction and watch, and excused his eyes, and gave his mouth something to do. She did not look at him. She bought the bangles. She paid in coins, and the small ordinary transaction gave her the four seconds she needed to know that he had not moved, and that he was not speaking, because a man who is truly on a call shifts his weight and laughs and looks at his shoes, and this man stood like a post and his lips did not go.

One man. One man was nothing. She held to that the way she held to the rail on the bus. One man was a coincidence, and a coincidence you photographed in your mind and walked away from and never trusted and never feared, because to fear one man was to start looking, and looking was death.

She walked deeper into the pocket, to its blind end, where a chai stall sat under a tarpaulin and the lane simply stopped against the back wall of a godown. There was no reason to come here. There was no through road, no second mouth, nothing. She came here because there was no reason. She bought tea she would not drink and stood with the glass burning her fingers and she gave the lane four full minutes by the slow count in her chest, and she watched its single entrance in the warped chrome of the tea urn, a long smeared world bent around the metal.

The man with the phone did not come in. Of course he did not. A trained man would not follow into a cul-de-sac; he would hold the mouth and let her come back out to him, and pass her off. So the question was no longer the man. The question was the mouth. She would walk back out the only way there was, and she would read what the mouth had become while she stood with her tea, because a static box re-forms itself when its quarry goes to ground, and the re-forming is a thing you can see if you know the before.

When she came out of the lane the man with the phone was gone and a woman had taken the bench across the road.

Sita did not stop. She turned north, unhurried, a woman with errands, and she let the picture sit in the soft edge of her eye and develop like film in a tray. The woman on the bench was perhaps forty, a green dupatta over her hair against the sun, a steel tiffin bag beside her, the most domestic object in the world. She had been there, Sita was certain, before the tea. She had a stillness that the lane did not give to its own. The people of the lane were always going somewhere, even the sleeping man dreamed of going somewhere; this woman had arrived at her destination and her destination was a bench with a view of a mouth.

She had seen her before.

The knowledge came not as a face but as a posture, the particular economy of a body that has learned to wait without seeming to wait, and Sita's stomach did a thing it had not done in years, a small cold drop, because she had seen this woman at the edge of two other afternoons and had filed her each time under nothing, under the city, under the ten thousand patient women of Delhi who sit and wait for buses and husbands and the heat to break. She had let her dissolve. Twice.

She kept her pace. North, into the cloth bazaar's wider artery, where the cameras came back and she had to assume the machines had her again, her grey kurta, her cloth bag, her ordinary forgettable shape now a thread some screen somewhere could follow junction to junction if a screen wanted to. She had planned for that too. Ahead, four hundred metres on, was the footbridge.

The footbridge was the whole purpose of the day and everything before it had been preparation. It crossed the road in a single covered span, narrow, one body wide where the beggars sat, a choke through which all foot traffic had to thread one at a time, and it had only the two ends. A person on the bridge had committed. There was no melting away on a bridge, no shop to step into, no call to take, no cross street. You went up one stair and along and down the other, and anyone behind you was trapped in the same tube of air with the same two choices, follow or fall away, and either choice spoke.

She climbed. She did not look back climbing, because the climbing itself, the turn of the body on the stair, gave her the road below in one honest sweep, and she took it the way you take a photograph, without lingering on any frame of it. Green dupatta, half a road back, climbing too. The checked-shirt man nowhere, which meant he had been passed off, which meant there were at least the two of them and the woman was now eyes and someone else was now legs, and a watch that hands you off like that, smoothly, without a stumble, between a man and a woman who do not know each other's faces, is not two people. It is a team that practises. It is resourced.

At the crest of the bridge she did the thing that had no innocent explanation.

She stopped, half-turned, opened her cloth bag, and made a show of having forgotten something, a small theatre of a woman who has left her purse or her list at the last stall, and then she turned fully and went back down the way she had come, against the thin current of bodies, back over the crest, back toward the stair she had just climbed. There was no reading of this that was honest. No one crosses half a bridge to go back. A traveller does not do it. Only a woman testing the water does it, and she had decided in the room that morning that she would spend this one move, this single unambiguous tell, because by now she needed the answer more than she needed her cover.

She watched what the move did to the bridge.

The green dupatta did not falter, and that was the proof, because an innocent does falter; an innocent meeting a woman coming the wrong way on a one-body bridge makes the small dance of who-goes-left, the apology, the eye contact. The woman in green did none of it. She turned at the rail as though the view of the traffic had taken her, smooth as oil, her back coming round so that her face never met Sita's, her whole body re-tasking itself in the half-second the move had bought, and below, on the road she had cleared with her own eyes ninety seconds ago, a small white car that had not been parked there was now parked there, hazards going, a man at the wheel not getting out.

That was the box adjusting. That was the stone in the water.

She came down the stair she had climbed and walked back south, the wrong way, into her own backtrail, and she felt the whole apparatus behind her flex and re-pour itself around the reversal, felt it the way you feel weather change, not seeing any single thing but knowing the pressure had dropped. A man she had never seen lit a cigarette at a paan stall who had not been smoking when she passed the first time. The white car did not follow, because a car that followed a U-turn was a confession; it would leapfrog, it would be ahead of her now at the next junction, prepositioned, and she would find it there waiting and that finding would be the final word.

She made herself walk. She made herself unhurried. And under the discipline her mind did the arithmetic, the cold ledger Baba had taught her to keep, and the arithmetic was the thing that turned the afternoon over and showed her its underside.

Count the bodies. The checked shirt at the lane. The woman in green who had been three afternoons and was now this one. The white car and its driver. The new man with his cigarette. Four she had drawn out in two hours with one pocket lane and one bridge, and the rule of the trade was that what you saw was the smallest fraction of what was deployed, that for every body that showed itself a hunt of this grade kept three in reserve you would never see, the legs and the eyes and the car you had not made. Twelve. Fifteen. A floating box that could hand a woman off between a man and a woman without a seam, that could conjure a parked car onto a cleared road in the time it took to turn at a rail. That did not come cheap. That was not a customs cell with a budget for two scooters and a long lens. That was not the Enforcement Directorate sweating a hawala node, waiting for the money to move so they could photograph the bag. She had told Baba it was that. Days ago, in the cool of the safehouse, she had told him it was small, a local thing, a watch on the rails, and he had soothed her and she had let herself be soothed.

She had been wrong, and the wrongness arranged itself now into a single clean shape, the way the bangles had lain in their tray.

A watch on the rails sits on the rails. It holds the meet, the transfer, the doorway where the money breathes; it is patient and it is geographic and it does not care where the courier sleeps, only where the courier hands over. A watch on the rails would have let her ride the wrong bus south and dropped her cold, because south of Aurobindo Marg there was no money, no node, no work, nothing the network touched. They had not dropped her. They had ridden the wrong bus with her. They had come into a pocket lane that fed nothing and held its mouth, and they had taken a footbridge that crossed nothing of theirs, and they had re-poured themselves around a reversal that had no operational meaning at all, because the thing they were covering was not a place and not a transaction and not the money.

It was her. Her body. The grey kurta and the forgettable face. They had started on her at a point that had nothing to do with the work, and they had stayed on her through legs that had nothing to do with the work, and the scale of them, the practised seamless expensive weight of them, was the kind of weight a service spent only on a principal. On someone it had decided was worth fifteen bodies and a floating box and three patient afternoons.

She was the principal. She was the quarry.

The word arrived without heat, the way the worst things always arrived, as fact and not as feeling, and her feet kept their unhurried pace down the artery toward the junction where she knew the white car would be waiting somewhere ahead of her, prepositioned, patient as the woman in green. She did not look for it. She had stopped needing to look. She knew the shape of the day now and the day's shape was a hand closing, and she had walked into its palm believing herself the one who closed hands.

At the junction she did not turn for home.

Home was three changes and forty minutes north, the room, the tin, the dark phone, and home was the one direction she could not now take, because to go home was to give them the room, and to give them the room was to give them the thread back to Baba, and the thought of leading this weight to his door went through her colder than the dropping in her stomach had. She stood at the junction in the white noon with the traffic shoving past and she understood, in the same flat ledger-voice, that the next thing she did had to be the opposite of every instinct that wanted to run to the one safe place, because there was no safe place that stayed safe once she walked toward it.

She bought a bus ticket from a man at a window, cash, to a destination she had no intention of reaching, and she let the bus that came be the right bus, going east, away from the room, away from Baba, into the part of the city she had no reason to enter.

Behind her, at the edge of the frame, a green dupatta moved to the same stop.
