The power failed at a quarter past eleven and the ceiling fan died with it, the blades freewheeling in the dark until she could count them. Janaki did not move. She sat with the candle unlit and listened the way she had taught herself to listen, sorting the sounds of the colony into the ones that belonged and the ones that did not. The inverter in the Khuranas' flat coughed and picked up the stairwell light, a thin amber line under her door. The night watchman dragged his lathi along the railings on the floor below, the same lazy scrape he made every night between rounds. A scooter changed gear out on the ring road. Rain ticked in the drainpipe. None of it was wrong.

She let her breath out and struck the match.

By the candle she found the child first, because she always found the child first now, before the work, before her own hands. The baby slept on her back in the cot with both arms flung up beside her head in the attitude of someone surrendering, the way the very young and the very tired sleep. Three weeks old today. The damp heat had stuck a curl of black hair to her forehead. Janaki watched the small chest lift and settle, lift and settle, and felt the old animal arithmetic move under her ribs, the sum that had nothing to do with files. Then she carried the candle to the dining table and sat down to the other arithmetic, the one that did.

The table was laid out the way she had laid it out every night for a fortnight, with the discipline of a woman setting a place for a guest who would not come. A school exercise book, ruled, its cover printed with a map of India and the price in rupees. A brown medicine bottle with the label soaked off. A microcassette in its hinged plastic case. A strip of 16-millimetre film, cut to length, curled inside a tin that had once held throat lozenges. And the pendant, on its square of cotton wool, catching the flame.

She had built the case the only way a person could build it when the building itself was against her. Nothing left the office on paper, because paper left a hole where it had been and the hole could be counted. So she had photographed instead. Three weeks of nights in the records annexe with a copy stand and the half-frame camera, turning pages under a desk lamp angled at the ceiling so the light through the frosted door looked like a clerk working late, which was all she was, a clerk working late. She had shot the financing dockets, the tasking memos, the account numbers that bled from a money-changer's back room in old Delhi into a defence pension trust, into a procurement cell, into the dull grey arteries of the things that were never supposed to be reachable from a back room in old Delhi. She had taken the spools to a man in the film lane behind Jama Masjid who reduced wedding footage to keepsake strips and asked no questions of a woman in a plain sari who paid in cash and gave a name that was not hers. He had handed her back the strip in the lozenge tin and told her it would outlast her. He had meant it kindly.

The exercise book was her own hand. She had copied the ledger out in the private shorthand she had used since training, the placements in their sequence, the dates, the way the money walked from the hawala into the institutions one careful step at a time over two years, never enough at once to trip a threshold, always patient. Patience was the signature. Whoever was doing this was not in a hurry, and people who were not in a hurry were the ones who frightened her, because they expected to be alive a long time and to win.

The cassette was the part she did not like to touch. On it was a conversation she had recorded across a tea-stall table from a frightened sub-broker who had thought he was buying her silence and had instead sold her thirty minutes of detail, his voice low and quick under the hiss of the kerosene stove. And after his voice, recorded alone in this flat at two in the morning with the child asleep, her own. A flat account of who she was, what her finding was, where the corroboration sat and how to read it, spoken once, plainly, the way you would brief a stranger you would never meet. A dying declaration, if you wanted to call it that. She had not called it that. She had called it insurance, and refilled the kettle, and gone on.

The proof was not on the table. The proof was already gone, set that afternoon into the place she had chosen for it a month before, while the light was kind and a woman with an infant in a cloth sling against her chest was the most ordinary and most invisible thing in the city. She had taken a bus and then walked, the baby warm and milky against her sternum, into the broken ground behind the old tombs where the archaeology people put up their signboards and never came, where the goats grazed and the lovers hid and nobody looked twice. She had found the loose course of masonry she had loosened on an earlier walk, lifted out the steel cash-box she had cemented behind it, fitted the exercise book and the tin and the cassette into the oilcloth, sealed the seam with candle wax, and closed the lid on its small brass lock. Then she had pressed the stone back and smoothed the dust with the edge of her foot and stood a while as if resting, jogging the child who was not crying, and walked out the way she had come. The box would outlast her too. Everything outlasted her now. That was the whole design.

What was on the table tonight was the way back to it.

She picked up the pendant. It was silver, a teardrop the size of a thumbnail, with a small red stone set in a raised bezel, the kind of thing an aunt gives a girl child for luck, the kind of thing a hundred thousand girl children wore against their skin in this city and were never parted from. She had taken it to an old setter in the lane of the jewellers, an arthritic man who worked by a bench lamp and a loupe and had set stones for two generations of brides. She had asked him to fit the stone, and to do one thing more that she had explained as sentiment, a mother's foolishness, a wish to seal a memento of the child's grandmother inside where it could not be lost. He had grumbled and obliged and set into the hollow of the bezel, under the stone, soldered flat and hidden, a flake of brass cut and filed to a shape she had drawn for him. He thought it a charm. It was the bit of the key, the bit that mattered, the wards filed to the lock of the box behind the stone in the broken ground. A key worn against a heart for a lifetime, that no search would ever find because no search looks for a lock inside a locket.

On the back of the teardrop she had engraved the rest herself, that afternoon, with a steel scribe and a magnifying glass at this same table, working until her eyes ached. To a stranger it read like a birth record, the sort a devout family has cut into a child's first silver: a date, a saint's day, a verse abbreviated to its first letters. The date was not the child's. The verse was not scripture. Read in the way she had recorded on the cassette, the way she had spoken aloud to no one at two in the morning, the date gave the bearing and the pace, and the saint named the ground, and the letters of the verse, taken as numbers in the old reckoning, gave the course of stone to lift. It was crude. It was also, she had decided, the only kind of message that could survive in a country where everything else could be subpoenaed, intercepted, burned, or bought. A landline could be tapped. A file could be pulled. A man could be turned. A child grows, and walks, and carries her grandmother's luck against her skin, and tells no one, because she does not know there is anything to tell.

That was the part Janaki had not let herself look at directly until now, with the candle low and the rain steady and the small chest lifting and settling in the cot.

She had told herself the cipher was for her own retrieval, a memory aid disguised, a thing she would read back herself when the moment came to surface it through a man she could trust. But there was no longer a man she could trust, and she had stopped pretending there was on the morning her finding came back to her down the chain with a single line in another hand across the top of it. Not refuted. Not even argued. Buried. Filed to a registry that did not produce what it swallowed. She had stood in the corridor with the docket in her hand and understood, in the cold clean way you understand a thing your body has known for weeks before your mind consents, that the burial had not come from below. It had come from above her, from a desk she could not see, signed by an authority she was not permitted to question, and that the reasons given, operational sensitivity, the risk of blowing live work, the danger of a worse crisis if it surfaced, were the precise reasons a guilty man and an innocent one would both put on the same page. She could not tell them apart from where she stood. That was the genius of it. The network did not need to corrupt the whole house. It needed one room near the top, and the instinct of every honest man in the building to defer.

So the cipher was not for her. She had known it for a fortnight and gone on writing it anyway, the way you go on doing the next correct thing because to stop is to admit the size of the wave. The cipher was for whoever came after, in a year or in twenty, and the only courier she had who could not be tapped or turned or pulled was asleep in the cot with her arms flung up in surrender.

She set the pendant down and pressed her palms flat against the table until the wood stopped swimming.

She had not been followed today. She was almost sure of that, and the almost was the truest part. There had been a grey Maruti outside the colony gate on Tuesday and again on Thursday that she had not been able to account for, and a man at the STD booth on the corner who had let three callers go ahead of him while he read a newspaper he never turned a page of, and a wrong number twice in one night that rang four times and stopped before she could lift the receiver, which was not a wrong number, which was someone confirming a body in a flat. She had logged each of these to herself the way she logged everything, without drama, in the private register that did not lie to her even when she wished it would. Singly they were the city. Together they were a pattern, and she read patterns for a living, and the pattern said that the desk she could not see had decided she was a problem that solved itself if left alone long enough in a city where analysts had accidents and women who asked the wrong questions were robbed on the wrong street.

She had thought, when she began, that proof was a shield. That if you assembled the truth carefully enough, in enough forms, sworn and sealed and corroborated, it became a thing that protected you, because no one would dare move against the keeper of so dangerous a thing. She saw now how childish that had been. The proof was not a shield. The proof was the reason. As long as the finding lived only in her head it could die quietly with her and be called an accident. But she had not let it live only in her head, she had cut it into silver and sealed it in oilcloth behind a stone, and the moment they understood that, the calculation changed, and the accident would not wait, and it would not be only hers. A keeper of so dangerous a thing does not protect her household. She becomes the most dangerous thing in it.

The candle guttered. She trimmed it with her fingers and did not feel the burn until a moment after.

She went to the cot. The child had turned in her sleep and lay now on her side with one fist against her mouth, and Janaki stood over her and made herself look, properly, the way she made herself look at everything, without flinching from the size of it. She had carried this one through a year she could not tell anyone about, had felt her quicken on the night the docket came back buried, had given birth to her in a nursing home under her own name because to do otherwise would have been the one anomaly that confirmed the watchers' suspicions. An ordinary mother. An ordinary child. The most ordinary thing in the city, and the most invisible, and that was exactly why she would do.

She had told herself she would not do this. She had told herself there had to be another courier, a lawyer abroad, a journalist, a foreign service she could walk into and lay it all on the table and let strangers carry the weight. She had run each of those down in her mind through the long nights and found the flaw in each, the point at which the chain passed through a hand that could be reached, and the network's hand was longer than any chain she could build of grown people who could be frightened or paid. There was only one courier whose loyalty could not be bought because she did not yet have a loyalty, who could not be made to talk because she could not yet talk, who would carry the key for a lifetime without once being tempted to spend it. The cruelty of it was complete and she had examined every face of the cruelty and found no way around it, only through.

She lifted the chain. It was short, a child's length, soft links she had checked herself for any rough place that might mark the skin. She slid her hand under the warm weight of the small head and the child stirred and made a sound, not quite a cry, and Janaki held still until the sound went back down into sleep. Then she brought the two ends of the chain together at the nape and worked the clasp shut with the tips of two fingers, slowly, the way you defuse a thing, and let the teardrop settle into the hollow of the child's throat where the pulse beat, the brass against the small fast heart.

There. It was done. The only copy that could not be searched, the living index to a box behind a stone, asleep with both fists now and the silver rising and falling on her breath.

Janaki sat back on her heels on the floor by the cot and let herself, for the length of one held breath, feel the whole of it without the discipline. She had hung a death warrant around her daughter's neck, and called it luck, and the worst of it, the thing she would carry into whatever the grey Maruti was waiting to deliver, was that she would do it again. There was no version of the next twenty years she could see in which the child was safe and the truth survived, and she had chosen the truth, and dressed it as a grandmother's blessing, and put it on her baby in the dark.

She would write nothing down about tonight. She would tell no one. In the morning she would take the child to the milk depot at seven the way she did every morning, and nod to the watchman, and be ordinary, and watch the grey car and the man with his unturned newspaper, and go on being a clerk who had asked one question too many and then, the building would be relieved to see, appeared to have thought better of it. She would buy herself a little time with her own obedience. Perhaps a month. Perhaps less.

She blew out the candle and the dark came down complete, and in the dark the colony went on with its small permitted sounds, the inverter, the watchman, the rain in the pipe. Then, under all of it, low on the wet road, an engine slowed where no engine should slow, and idled, and did not move on.

Janaki did not get up. She stayed on the floor by the cot with her hand resting against the warm small back, counting the breaths, and listened to the engine the way she had taught herself to listen, sorting it, knowing already which kind of sound it was.
