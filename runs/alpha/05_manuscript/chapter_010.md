The fan turned over the room four times a minute and moved nothing. Sita lay on the cot with her shoes on, counting the revolutions out of habit, the way other people prayed, and listened to the bazaar below come awake under her window. Saharanpur did not sleep so much as lower its voice. Carts. A shutter going up on its runners. The first bus to Deoband working through its gears at the chowk. Twenty-two days now, and she had learned the town's sounds the way she had once learned a target's evening walk, until the absence of a sound would wake her before the presence of one.

She had done everything correctly. That was the part she returned to, lying there, the way the tongue returns to a broken tooth. No phone that could be switched on. A handset bought for two hundred rupees off a boy in Ambala who sold them without a SIM, and no SIM in it, so it was a clock and nothing more. Cash, always cash, broken into worn notes so that no fresh sequence could be walked back to a single withdrawal. She had not touched a UPI code in three weeks; she ate where the menu was chalked on a board and the man took coins. She had crossed the cantonment areas only at night and only on foot, head down, in the dead lanes the cameras did not cover because there was nothing there worth watching. She had changed her hair from the woman in Karol Bagh, dulled it, cut it herself with nail scissors over a newspaper she had then burned in the lodge's back yard. The face was the problem that could not be solved, so she gave it as little as possible to read: a cheap cotton suit, plastic chappals, a married woman's mangalsutra she did not own the right to wear. A schoolteacher's sister, visiting, of no interest to anyone.

The lodge took cash and a photocopy of an Aadhaar card that did not survive a second look, because the boy at the desk did not give it one. Forty rupees extra and he wrote down whatever name you gave him. She had given Sunita Rana. She answered to it now without the half-beat of delay that gives a false name away, and that small mastery had pleased her, the way the work always pleased her, until she remembered there was no one to report it to.

So she had done everything correctly, and at half past nine in the morning, when she came back up the stairs with a packet of glucose biscuits and a half-litre of water, the woman was sitting in the room's single chair with her hands folded in her lap like a relative who had come a long way.

Sita's body knew her before her mind allowed it. The watcher. Karol Bagh in the spring, across the road from the money meet, reading nothing in a parked Wagon R. The edge of the frame at the Inderlok footbridge. A face she had filed under threat and downgraded twice, and each time the downgrade had been a mercy she paid herself because the truth was too large to carry.

The knife was in her right hand before she had decided on it, low against her thigh, the point turned in. Two steps to the chair. The window behind it was barred. The door was at her back and she had not heard it relock, which meant the woman had a way through doors. There was a second exit and it was three floors down through a kitchen that smelled of last night's dal.

"I came alone," the woman said. "There's no one on the stairs and no one in the lane that's mine. You can check the lane from here if you stand to the side of the window. I'd rather you did, actually. You'll settle faster."

She had a low voice, unhurried, the Hindi of someone educated in three languages who had decided to be plain. She was perhaps fifty. A grey cotton kurta, no jewellery, sensible sandals, a cloth bag at her feet with the corner of a steel tiffin showing. She looked like a woman who took the slow train and carried her own lunch. Her hands stayed folded. She had made of her own stillness a kind of offering, and Sita understood the offering and was not moved by it, and was moved by it, and stood very still herself with the knife turned in.

"My name is Mira," the woman said. "It will do. You won't believe whatever name I give you, so I'll give you one that's easy to say." A small pause, the length of a courtesy. "Sit, if you like. Or stand. I'm going to talk for a while, and none of it requires anything from you. That's the only thing I'd ask you to notice today. I'm not asking you for anything."

Sita did not sit. She moved to the side of the window the woman had named and looked down into the lane, keeping the chair in the bottom of her vision, and the lane was empty in the way lanes are when someone competent has cleared them, which told her nothing she could use and everything she did not want to know. She let the silence run. Silence was hers, usually. She had broken men with it across tables in three countries. She waited for the woman to fill it because that was what they always did.

The woman did not fill it. She let it sit between them as if it were a third person they both knew, and she watched Sita with an attention that had no appetite in it, and after a while it was Sita who could not bear the shape of the quiet and turned from the window.

"Say it," Sita said. "Whatever it is."

"Your mother used to keep a brass tin of fennel by the stove," Mira said. "You don't remember it. You were too small. But you put fennel on the windowsill of every place you've stayed since you were old enough to keep house, and you don't know why you do it. There was a tin in the kitchen of the flat in Lajpat Nagar. There's a twist of it in your bag now, in the inside pocket, gone soft. I'm not guessing. I'm telling you what's in a file."

Sita said nothing. Her hand did not move. There was indeed fennel in the inside pocket, bought from the same man who sold the biscuits, and she could not have told anyone why she had bought it, and she did not let her face do anything at all.

"That's the kind of thing I have," Mira said. "Small things. Useless things. The size of shoe you wear when no one is making you wear heels. That you read the last page of a book first, always, since you were a girl, because you cannot bear not knowing who is left alive at the end." She turned one hand over, palm up, an old habit of showing it was empty. "Big things too. But I find people don't believe the big things. They believe the fennel."

"You have a file," Sita said. "Files are written by people who want something. What do you want."

"Right now? Nothing. I told you. Later, perhaps, I'll want you to do something difficult and I'll have no right to ask it, and you'll do it or you won't. But not today." Mira reached down, slowly, narrating the reach with her eyes so that the knife would understand it, and took the tiffin from the cloth bag and set it on the floor between them. "I brought food. You've lost weight you couldn't spare. The boy downstairs has been watering your tea, by the way. You should make him do it in front of you."

Sita did not touch the tiffin. But she catalogued it, the way she catalogued everything, and she understood that the catalogue itself was the woman's work, that every small true and kindly thing was a stone laid in a road that led somewhere, and she walked the road in her mind looking for the place it turned.

"You ran the right way," Mira went on, conversational, as if they had known each other a long time and were only catching up. "Away from the man, not towards him. You bought yourself nothing but you didn't spend it on yourself, you spent it on him, on the distance between you and him, so that whatever found you would not find him. That was well done. It's the most loyal thing I've ever seen one person do for another, and he will never know you did it, because the kind of man who teaches a person to do that is the kind of man who arranges never to be in the room when the bill comes."

"You don't know him," Sita said, and heard the heat in it, and despised the heat, and could not take it back.

"No," Mira agreed, gently, as if to a child who had said something true by accident. "I know what he's built, which is a different thing. I know there's a unit with no name that doesn't appear on any establishment list, that draws no pay anyone can trace, that the state will look you in the eye and swear does not exist, and will go on swearing it while they bury you under a different name. I know it's funded out of a pipeline he took off other men this spring, Afghan brown and printed notes through Punjab, and that the money is real and the deniability is real and the two of them are the same fact. I know that a thing with no owner is the cheapest thing in the world to spend. You've spent things like that yourself. You know exactly what they cost, which is nothing, which is why they're useful."

Each sentence was a true one. Sita weighed them as they came and found no false note in any, and that was itself the danger, because a thing all true is a thing being built towards a single brick that is not.

"And I know," Mira said, and here her voice did not change at all, which was how Sita knew the brick was coming, "that on your eighteenth birthday he took you to a restaurant with white cloths and a man at the door in a coat, and he ordered for you, and partway through the meal he put a hotel key on the table by your hand. And you understood it. You'd been waiting years to understand it. You went up in the lift and you let yourself into the room and there was a man asleep on the bed, and you did the thing you'd been made for, and you came back down and finished your food because he was still sitting there and you didn't want him to see your hands shake."

She had it almost whole. Sita stood with the knife turned in and felt the restaurant rise around her, the cold of the air conditioning, the weight of the key, Fateh's face across the table neither proud nor unproud, only watchful, the way he was watchful now in her memory of him as he had been watchful in life. Almost whole. And then the brick.

A man asleep on the bed, Mira had said.

It had been a woman. A woman of perhaps forty in a green sari, awake, sitting up against the headboard reading a magazine, who had looked at Sita over the top of it with an expression Sita had never afterwards been able to name, and who had not screamed.

The relief came up through her like cold water and she let none of it show. There. There it was. The seam. The whole beautiful true garment had a thread in it that was not hers, sewn by someone who had read a file and filled a gap, and the gap was the one place no file could reach because no one living had been in that room but Sita and the woman in green. The file was poisoned at the source. She had it now. She held it and it steadied her more than the knife.

She almost said it. The discipline that had kept her alive eleven years closed her mouth a half-second before her contempt opened it, and she let the woman go on believing the lie had landed, and she stood inside the small clean room of her own certainty and breathed.

"You're thinking I've got something wrong," Mira said.

Sita said nothing.

"Good. Hold on to it. Truly. Find the thing I've got wrong and hold it, because that's how you'll know not to take everything I say like medicine." Mira smiled for the first time, and it was a tired smile, without triumph in it. "I'd rather you trusted one true thing you found yourself than ten I handed you. People who swallow everything from me are no use to anyone. They believe the next person too."

Something in that she did not like, the kindness of being told to keep her own counsel, and she could not find the edge of it to cut, and so she kept the one true thing she had and said nothing and watched the woman's hands.

"Ask me how I found you," Mira said.

"No."

"Sensible. Because either I tell you something that frightens you, or I tell you a lie, and you can't tell which, so the question only costs you and never pays. You learned that from him." She gathered the cloth bag without standing, an old woman's economy of movement. "I'll leave you the food. The tiffin's mine, I'll want it back, which is one reason I'll come again, so you can stop reading the chair for whether I mean to." She did rise then, slowly, both hands visible. "There's a flour mill on the Court Road with a green door, runs all day, loud as the grave. The woman at the till is mine. Tuesdays and Fridays, between four and six, you can leave a word with her or take one, and no one watching this town will ever wonder why a housewife buys atta. That's all. No number. You won't carry a phone and you're right not to."

She moved to the door, and at the door she stopped, and Sita's hand tightened, because this was where they always put the knife in, at the threshold, with the back half-turned.

"One more small thing, then I'll leave you alone," Mira said, not turning. "The story you were told, about your mother and the men who came, and the one good man who carried you out of it. I won't argue it with you. I only want to say that the people who carry children out of fires are sometimes the people who set them, and that the difference matters less than you'd think to the child, who is grateful either way, and grows up to be exactly what she's needed to be. I'm not asking you to believe me. I'm asking you to notice, one day, when you're safe, that you've never once checked. A woman who reads the last page first. Who has never turned to the first."

Then she was gone, and the door did not catch as a locked door catches, and her sandals made almost no sound on the stairs.

Sita stood for a long time in the middle of the room with the fan turning over nothing above her. The mother and the fire. That was the lie, the second seam, clumsier than the first because larger, and she gathered it up beside the man-on-the-bed and held them together as evidence of the woman's whole rotten craft, and the holding did not steady her this time the way it had before. It would not sit flat in her hand. She turned it and turned it and it kept a small weight that the first lie had not had, and she could not find why, and she stopped trying to find why because the trying went nowhere good and the not-trying was the only ground she had left to stand on.

She did not eat the food. She did not throw it away either, which she noticed, and filed, and could not account for.

Towards evening she did what she had done every evening of her life when the shape of the world came loose at the edges. She sat on the floor with her back against the cot, and she reached for him. Not the burner clock in her bag, not any line, those were closed to her by her own hand for his sake. She reached for him inwardly, the way a hand finds the banister in a dark stairwell and knows the house by it, for the low certain voice that had always been waiting at the other end of every fear she had ever brought it, that had told her once that the dying lie to live and she had believed it and gone to sleep. She reached for it and the voice was there, it was always there, it said what it always said.

And she waited, the way she had waited a thousand evenings, for the certainty to close over the crack the way water closes over a dropped stone. It did not close. The voice was there and it said its true and steadying thing in its true and steadying tone, and tonight, for no reason she could put a name to, it lay on the surface of her like oil on water and would not go in.

She took her hand from the place where the fennel was in her bag and laid it flat on the cool of the floor, and sat there in the dark a long while after it had become clear that, tonight, the voice would not be enough to put her to sleep.
