The kettle had boiled twice and she had not made the tea. That was the first thing Sita noticed about herself, the way you notice a limp in someone else: the small competence gone slack. She stood at the counter of the Lajpat Nagar flat with the gas off and her hand flat on the cold steel, and behind her Mira sat where she had sat for the better part of the night, in the one chair that did not face a window, with a cloth shopping bag at her feet as though she had come from the vegetable market and not from wherever she truly came from.

"You should sit," Mira said. "You've been on your feet since two."

"I'm fine standing."

"You're not. But suit yourself."

Sita made the tea after all, because making it gave her hands a task and her eyes somewhere honest to rest. She measured the leaves into the pan, the milk, the sugar Mira had not asked for and would not refuse, and she watched the surface lift and thought about the door, the window with the painted-shut latch, the fire escape that was not an escape because it ended at a grille someone had welded over in 2019. She had cleared this flat in eleven minutes the day she took it. She knew its exits the way she knew the bones of her own hand. None of that was any use to her now and she understood, pouring, that this was the point. The threat in the room did not come through a door.

She set the glass down in front of Mira and kept her own.

"You said you had something to show me." Her voice came out level. She was grateful for it, the way a swimmer is grateful for a plank. "You've been saying it for three hours. You talk around it like it's a wound you don't want to touch. So touch it."

Mira looked at her for a long moment. She was an unremarkable woman, fifty perhaps, with reading glasses pushed up into grey-threaded hair and the patient stillness of someone who had spent a career in rooms where the loud ones lost. Sita had seen her before. The edge of a frame in Karol Bagh. A bench near the transfer in Connaught Place, weeks ago, before any of this had a shape. She had filed her then as background and been wrong, and the being-wrong sat in her now like a stone she had swallowed.

"It isn't a wound I'm protecting," Mira said. "It's yours. I've been slow because once I show you, I can't unshow you, and you'll spend the rest of your life knowing it. I wanted to be sure you were ready to carry that. I've decided you're never going to be ready, and that's not a reason to wait."

She bent, unhurried, and lifted the cloth bag onto her lap. From it she took a manila folder, soft at the corners with handling, the kind that lived in steel almirahs in government offices and came out smelling of damp and old gum. She did not open it. She set it on the low table between them, square to the edge, and put two fingers on it.

"Before this," she said, "I want you to do something for me. Tell me the story. The one he told you. About the fire."

"Why."

"Because you've never said it aloud to anyone but yourself. And the things we only ever say to ourselves go soft. They stop being checked." Mira folded her hands. "Tell me, and we'll look at it together, the way you'd look at a cover legend before you trusted your life to it. That's a thing you know how to do. I'm not asking you to feel. I'm asking you to assess."

It was a clever framing and Sita knew it was clever and it worked anyway, because it gave her the only posture in which she could bear to approach this at all. Assess. She could assess. She had assessed a man's pupils as he died and told the truth about what she saw.

"I was a few weeks old," she said. The words came in his cadence, which was the only cadence she had for them. "There was a fire. In the colony where my parents lived. It wasn't an accident. The people my father had crossed set it, to kill the family, because of what he knew and would not give them. My mother died getting me to the window. Baba was there. He'd been watching the house. He came up through the smoke and took me out and he raised me because there was no one left. He raised me himself." She stopped. "He didn't have to. He told me that, once. He said he had no obligation and he took one anyway."

"That's the whole of it?"

"That's the whole of it."

"It's a good legend," Mira said. It was not unkind, the way she said it. It was professional, and the professionalism of it was the cruelest thing in the room. "It's economical. It explains your gratitude, it explains why there are no relatives, it explains the silence around it, and it makes a man who took a child into a man who saved one. Every load-bearing question, answered, and answered in his favour. You've built a life on it. I'd have built one too." She drew a breath. "Now assess it as evidence. What's in it that you've seen with your own eyes?"

Sita opened her mouth. Closed it.

"Take your time."

"The smoke," she said at last, and heard how thin it was. "I have the smell of smoke. Sometimes. I've always had it."

"You were three weeks old. You have nothing of three weeks old. You have a smell that he gave you a story for, and the story is so good that the smell now arrives already explained. That's not memory. That's a caption." Mira's two fingers had not left the folder. "Everything you know about the night that made you, you know because he said it. There is no document in your hand. There is no second voice. There is one source, and the one source is the man the story makes a hero of. If an agent brought you a legend like that, sourced like that, single-stranded, the source its own beneficiary, what would you do with it?"

Sita said nothing. She had trained operatives. She knew exactly what she would do with it. She would not run it. She would not let it near anything that mattered. She would send it back and tell them to find corroboration or burn it.

"You'd reject it," Mira said, finishing the thought she had refused to finish aloud. "You'd reject it on the tradecraft alone, before you ever asked whether it was true."

"He has no reason to lie to me." It came out of her too fast, the reflex of eleven years, and she heard it land in the room like a dropped glass. He has no reason. She had said it to herself ten thousand times and it had always closed the matter. Tonight it hung there, and for the first time some cold and careful part of her, the part that watched pupils, asked the next question. What if he had every reason. What if the reason was the size of a life.

Mira heard the crack in it. She did not press into it. That, Sita would think much later, was how she knew Mira was good: she let the silence do the work she would have spoiled by speaking.

Then she opened the folder.

She did it the way a surgeon lays out instruments, each thing set down where it could be seen and reached, nothing hurried, nothing hidden behind anything else. Photocopies, most of them, grey and slightly skewed, the way a page goes when the original is brittle and the glass is old. A few originals, brown with age, the staple-rust bleeding a small constellation into the paper.

"I'm not going to tell you what these are," Mira said. "I'm going to let you read them. You'll trust your own eyes before you trust my mouth, and you should. Take whatever you need to find the forgeries. Look for them. I'd be insulting you if I asked you not to."

Sita sat down. She had not meant to. The chair simply took her.

The top sheet was a First Information Report, the printed proforma of a Maharashtra police station, the kind of document she had handled in a dozen operational contexts and could read for tampering the way a jeweller reads a stone. A date in the autumn of the year she would later be told was the year of the fire. A registered complaint. A woman's name she did not know, and then, lower, in the box for particulars, a phrase that her eye reached and her mind refused, so that she had to go back and read it as a sequence of separate words before she would let them be a sentence. Death by strangulation. Female. The body of an infant not recovered from the premises. Suspected removed.

She read it three times. The diacritical marks of the Marathi were right. The station stamp had the correct registration sequence for its district and year; she knew the format, she had forged adjacent to it. The case number ran continuous with the entries either side, which a fabricator working from a single inserted page almost never got right, because they never had the register. Whoever had pulled this had pulled the whole register and photographed the run, so that the page sat inside its own true neighbourhood of unrelated crimes, a stolen scooter above, a land dispute below. You could fake a page. You could not fake the indifferent traffic of a real police station around it.

"There was no fire," she said. The words were not a question and they were not quite hers. They came out of her in a voice she did not recognise, flat, examining.

"There was no fire." Mira touched the next sheet forward. "Your mother was killed in her home. Strangled. It wasn't a colony of people the enemy wanted dead. It was one woman, and a thing she'd seen, or had, or refused to hand over. I don't have all of that. I have this." Her finger moved. "And I have you not being in the house when they came back."

The next sheet was a death certificate, the woman's name again, the cause as the FIR had it, and Sita made herself read the name properly this time, syllable by syllable, and felt nothing she could have predicted. Not grief. There was no woman attached to the name for her to grieve. What came instead was something stranger and worse: the sudden, vertiginous understanding that there had been a woman, an actual woman with this actual name who had been alive and then strangled in a room, and that this woman was the hinge on which her whole self turned, and that until ninety seconds ago Sita had not known her cause of death because the man who knew it had given her a fire instead.

He had given her a fire instead.

She set the certificate down very carefully, squaring it to the table edge the way Mira had squared the folder, because her hands wanted a small correct task and this was the only one available.

"You're looking for the seam," Mira said, watching her. "You've looked at three documents and you haven't found it, and that's frightening you more than if you had. So look at this one. This is the one I'd attack, in your place."

It was a photograph. A photocopy of a photograph, the tones crushed, but legible. A municipal record of some kind, a ledger of admissions to a children's home run by a trust, the columns ruled by hand. A date some eleven days after the death certificate. An entry: female infant, approximate age four weeks, no name, brought by. And in the box for brought by, not a name. An initial, and a designation, in the abbreviated officialese of the time. The designation was one she knew. It was the cover grade Fateh had used in that decade. She had seen it on two other documents in her life, in contexts she had been told to forget, and her memory, which did not fail, did not fail now.

"That could be anyone," she said, and heard the plea under it, and despised the plea.

"It could. The initial could be anyone's. The grade could be a coincidence. One document, you throw out. That's correct procedure and you should." Mira's voice stayed level, almost gentle, the voice of someone laying a heavy thing down slowly so it would not crash. "So don't take it alone. The admission is eleven days after the death. The infant not recovered from the premises, per the FIR. An unnamed female, four weeks, delivered by a man holding that grade. And then." She moved the last sheet forward, and this one was not grey. This one was a colour photocopy of a page from a trust's annual report, glossy stock, a charitable list of patrons and the years of their support, and one name among the donors carried a small printed footnote of thanks for a child placed in a good home, and the name was the legend Fateh had worn longest, the one Sita had first known him by, before he became Baba, before he became everything.

"Discharged," Mira said, "to the same man, under a different name, three weeks after he brought her in. He put her in, and he took her out. That's not one source telling you a story. That's a trail of clerks who never met each other, in offices that never spoke, each writing down a true and boring thing on a true and boring day, and the true and boring things only line up one way. They line up to a man who took a living child out of a murdered woman's house and then, after a decent interval, made himself her saviour on paper." She sat back. "Find me the forgery, Sita. I'll wait. You're better at it than anyone I could hire. Find it and I'll get up and leave and you'll never see me again."

Sita did not look for the forgery.

She did not look because she already knew she would not find it, and she knew that the way she knew a poison's onset, in the body before the mind, a coldness that started under the breastbone and went out along the arms. Eleven years of certainty did not break the way a board breaks, all at once. It broke the way ice breaks under a foot that already feels the give, a white line racing out from the heel faster than the fear of it. She sat with the documents fanned in front of her in their patient, indifferent order and she did the only thing she knew how to do, which was assess, and the assessment came back the way it had come back in a thousand operational rooms, clean and pitiless and not caring at all what she wanted it to say.

The trail was real. The documents were real. The man who took her was the man who told her he had saved her.

Both of those things could be true. She made herself hold the door open for the version that let her live: that he had taken her from the house to keep her safe, that the fire was only a kindness, a softer shape pressed over a thing too sharp for a child, that a man might lie about the manner of a rescue and still have performed one. That was possible. She turned it over and looked at it from underneath, the way she would a target's alibi, and found it did not fully close, because a man who had only softened a horror for a child would have given her the woman's name. He would have given her a grave. He had given her neither. He had given her a fire that never burned, with the family dead so there would be no one to ask, and he had let her carry the smell of it for thirty years as proof.

You did not erase a mother to spare a child. You erased a mother to spare yourself the questions a name would raise.

"I want to call him," she said. The sentence surprised her. It was the oldest reflex she had, older than the work, older than the kills: when the ground moved, she went to Baba, and he set his hand on the back of her neck the way he had since she was small, and the ground came back. She had done it after Karol Bagh. She had done it after the man with the watcher's stillness too close on the platform. Every time the world had tilted, his voice had righted it, and she reached for the phone in her jacket now out of pure animal habit, the way a hand reaches for a rail on a lurching train.

Her fingers found the burner. They did not pull it out.

She sat with her hand inside her jacket, against the cheap warm plastic of the phone, and for the first time in her life the thought of his voice did not steady her. It did the opposite. She imagined the call, the two rings, the way he would say her name, soft, unsurprised, already soothing, and she understood with a clarity that was its own kind of grief that she could no longer hear that voice as comfort. She would hear it as a hand laid over her eyes. She would sit there holding the phone and assessing the voice, listening for the seam she had not found in the paper, and whatever he said, true or false, she would not be able to stop herself measuring it. The measuring had started. It would not stop now. He had built a daughter who could read a dying man's pupils, and tonight she had turned that instrument, for the first time, toward him, and it would never again point fully away.

She took her hand out of her jacket, empty.

Across the low table Mira watched her do it, watched the hand come out without the phone, and something in the older woman's face eased, not triumph, nothing so cheap, but the spent quiet of someone who has carried a weight a long way and set it down exactly where it had to go. She did not say anything. She let Sita sit in the wreck of it. Outside, far off, an autorickshaw worked its way up the lane in second gear, and a dog answered it, and the ordinary night went on being the night for everyone who was not in this room.

"He took me," Sita said, to the documents, to no one. Testing the shape of it in her mouth. It did not crack apart on her tongue the way she had half hoped. It held. That was the worst of it. It held.

"He took you," Mira said.

Sita looked up. Her face was dry; she would think about that later too, that nothing in her wept, that the thing was too large and had come in too low to reach the part of her that cried. What had it reached, then. She did not know yet. She knew only that the floor she had stood on her whole life had been a floor laid over water, and that she could feel the water now, cold and patient, through every place she touched.

"What else," she said. Her voice had gone very quiet and very steady, the voice she used in the last minute before work, and she did not choose it; it simply arrived, because some part of her had already understood that the question had only one possible next word and that she was going to have to follow it the whole way down. "There's more. You've shown me the first room. You don't carry a folder like this for one room." She met the older woman's eyes. "What else did he build out of me."

Mira reached into the cloth bag again, and did not bring anything out, and left her hand there, resting on whatever it held, the way a person rests a hand on a door they have not yet decided to open.

"Tomorrow," she said. "Tonight you've already stopped believing him. That's the whole of what tonight was for. The rest will still be true in the morning, and you'll be better able to carry it then." She stood, slow, a hand to the small of her back, an old woman again, the menace folded away into the shopping bag and the reading glasses. "Don't call him. Whatever else you do. Once you call him, he'll know you've started counting, and you've only just learned how."

She let herself out. Sita heard the latch, the second latch, her own careful tradecraft executed by someone else's hand, and then the flat was hers again and the documents were still on the table, fanned, patient, real, telling their small true boring facts to the empty room.

She did not move for a long time. When she finally reached out, it was not for the phone. It was for the death certificate, and she drew it to her, and she read the name of her mother for the fourth time, slowly, learning it, the way you learn the name of a person you are only ever going to meet like this, on paper, eleven days too late and eighteen years and then eleven more, in a borrowed flat with the tea gone cold, with the man who knew it all already, somewhere across the dark city, waiting for a call that for the first time in her life she was not going to make.
