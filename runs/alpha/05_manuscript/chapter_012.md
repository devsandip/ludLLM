The room was on the third floor of a lodge in Paharganj that took cash and asked for a photocopy of an Aadhaar card she did not give them. She gave them instead a printout from a different name, a Maharashtra licence with a face close enough to her own under the counter's bad light, and forty rupees folded into the register so the boy would not photograph it on his phone. He photographed it anyway, from habit, and she watched him do it and decided it did not matter. By the time anyone pulled that frame she would be two cities gone.

She had killed her own phone on the second day. Not switched off. Killed: the SIM snapped, the handset left in the cistern of a station toilet still ringing, so that whoever followed the signal would arrive at a porcelain tank in New Delhi railway station and learn nothing except that she knew how to make them waste a morning. After that she moved on cash and on foot and on the buses that had no working cameras, and she ate where the menus were handwritten, and she did not look up at the poles where the white domes turned, because in this city the domes had faces inside them now and a face that lingered was a face that got matched.

For eleven years she had read other people's streets like an X-ray, looking past the crowd for the one shadow that stood wrong. Now she read her own. It was the same instrument turned around, and it sat badly in the hand, the way a familiar word looks wrong when you stare at it too long.

She had time. That was the new thing, the unbearable thing. The work had never given her time. The work gave her a target and a window and a clean way out, and inside that geometry there was no room for the question of what any of it meant. Now there was only the question, and a single bulb, and a ceiling fan that ticked on its downstroke, and twenty-five days.

She made herself do it as a case.

That was the only way she knew to hold a thing without flinching from it. She would build the file the way she had built files on men she was sent to end: from the outside, from the verifiable, refusing the story they told about themselves. She had a subject. The subject was Baba. The subject was every word he had ever given her, and she would treat each word now as a claim and not as ground.

She started where Mira had made her start, because she could not help it, because it was the wound. The fire. She had carried the fire for as long as she had carried anything. The heat of it, the way Fateh's coat had smelled of smoke when he carried her out, the certainty she had built her whole life on: that the enemy had come in the night and burned her mother and that one man had reached into the burning and taken her and kept her. She had loved that story. She understood now that she had loved it the way you love a room you have never been allowed to leave.

Mira's paper said her mother had been strangled. Said it in the dry vocabulary of a post-mortem, ligature, hyoid, petechiae, words that did not burn. Said there had been no fire in the building that night, that the fire was in the next year's record and the next street's address, that someone had taken a true death and a false fire and married them so that a child would grow up pointing her grief in the wrong direction.

She had checked, in the way she could check. She had not been able to reach the body or the building or the year. Those were analog things, paper things, sunk in some district registry that no phone could open and no account could hold, and she had no clean way to walk into a records room in her own face. But she had been able to do the smaller test, the test of the seams, and the seams were what undid her.

Because she remembered the fire, and a person does not remember being three weeks old.

She had always known that and never once turned to look at it. The memory had a smell and a temperature and the texture of a man's coat, and an infant has none of those, an infant has nothing it can keep. So the memory was not a memory. It was a thing she had been given. Someone had described the fire to her, patiently, across years, in the voice she trusted most in the world, until the description grew a smell and a heat and became indistinguishable from a thing she had lived. She had been, she saw now, very well made.

That was the word that frightened her. Made.

She got up and stood at the window with the bulb off so her silhouette would not print on the curtain. The lane below ran with the late traffic, a man pushing a handcart of cylinders, two boys on a scooter without lights, the steam off a chai stall fogging the one camera she could see. She counted the exits the way she always counted them and the count steadied her, and she let it, and then she made herself stop, because the steadiness was also a thing he had built. He had built her calm. He had built the place inside her that went quiet over a target's last breath. She did not know anymore which of her own reflexes were hers and which were his hand still moving inside her after the fact.

She returned to the file.

The restaurant. Her eighteenth birthday. He had taken her somewhere with cloth napkins and a sommelier, the first time she had seen a man whose only job was wine, and he had ordered for her and watched her eat and then he had laid a hotel key on the cloth between the bread and her hand. Not said a word about it. Let her understand. She had gone up in the lift with the key warm in her fist and she had let herself into a room where a man sat watching cricket with his shoes off, and she had done the thing she had been raised every day of her life to do, and she had come down and finished her dessert, and Fateh had looked at her with such love that she had felt, for the only time she could name, completely safe.

She had told that story to herself a hundred times as the story of her belonging. The night he made her family. She turned it over now and read it cold, as evidence, and what she saw was a man auditioning a weapon and finding it sound. The napkins were not for her. The wine was not for her. He had needed to know the thing would fire when he pressed it, and he had taken it somewhere beautiful to press it, so that the first kill and the deepest love would set in the same hour and never come apart again. That was not a father. That was an armourer. She had been, on her eighteenth birthday, proof-tested over dessert.

She found she was sitting very still, the way she sat before the moment, and she made her hands open on her knees.

She would not weep. She had not decided not to. The machine in her simply did not turn that way, and she could no longer tell whether that was strength or another thing he had soldered shut, and the not-being-able-to-tell was the whole of her grief, if grief was even the word.

Now read the rest, she told herself. Read all of it. Build the file to the end.

Because there was more than the fire and the napkins. There was the war.

He had told her about the war in the safehouse with the maps, the long architecture of it, forty years of his life laid out like rail lines on a board. The network seeded into the country across two decades, men sleeping inside the missile rooms and the cyber cells and the command, waiting for a signal from a principal who had spent twenty years and a great deal of money planting them. He had told her the drugs were a war chest and a poison both, that every gram he moved was a coin minted to starve that network at the hour before it woke, that the wreckage of it, the boys in Punjab with their arms full of holes, was a smaller catastrophe than the one he was buying off. He had said it without flinching. The protector who poisons his own to keep them. She had believed it the way you believe the ground.

And the foundational thing had been a lie. So now she had to ask the question she had never been permitted: was the war a lie too?

This was where the file turned dangerous, because she could feel the pull of the cleaner shape. Mira had given her the cleaner shape without ever quite saying it. If the fire was scaffolding and the birthday was a proof-test, then perhaps the war was scaffolding on a larger frame, the grandest story of all, the one that turned an empire of heroin and hawala and a stable of made killers into a holy thing. A man does not like to think himself a king of poison. He likes to think himself a shield. Give him a secret war that no one else can see and he can be a shield forever, and never have to count the arms full of holes, and keep his weapons loving him while he does it. The war explained everything and could be checked by no one. She knew what a story like that was worth. She had built smaller versions of it herself, on operations, to make an asset do a terrible thing and sleep afterward.

She thought of Bedi.

She had not thought of him on purpose in twenty-five days and now he came up whole, the launderer in his flat with the fish tank, the way the potassium had taken him, and the thing he had said with the last working part of his mouth. That he was innocent. She had filed it under the noise that dying men make. They all said something. Few of them said it was deserved. She had washed her hands of his face the way she washed her hands of all of them, by trusting that the man who pointed her had pointed true.

And now she did not know that the man had pointed true. Now she had to hold open the possibility that Bedi had been exactly what he said, a money man who moved money, no more inside any sleeper network than the boy downstairs steaming the camera with his chai, and that she had stopped his heart in his own front room because one story required his death to keep being a war and not a business. The thought did not arrive as grief either. It arrived as a kind of vertigo, the floor of eleven years going translucent, every face under it suddenly possibly only a face.

Stop, she told herself. You are doing his work for him in reverse. You are letting one proven lie convict every true thing in the building. That is not tradecraft. That is a frightened person.

But she could not get back to the ground. That was the thing about the ground. Once you knew it was painted on, you could not unknow it, even where it might be real underneath.

She decided to run the probe.

She had a protocol with him, an old one, from the years before she could imagine needing it, the emergency string that meant come, I am blown, find me. A mark in a particular place, a stub of blue chalk on the third pillar of a particular flyover in the chaos of Sarai Kale Khan, where the buses idled and no single camera owned the whole arc and a hundred thousand people a day made the ground meaningless to any face-match. She had never used it. She used it now, in the grey before dawn, walking past the pillar with her hood up and her chin down and her thumb dragging the chalk in the agreed short stroke without breaking stride, and then she walked on and bought a ticket she did not use and folded herself into the place she had chosen the day before, a tea shop with a long sightline and two ways out, and she watched the pillar and she waited to read him.

This was the test that mattered. Not who came. What came. If the string was clean and only a courier came, soft, alone, empty-handed, then he was protecting her and the network around her still believed she was theirs to save. If a team came, if she read corners being held and a vehicle sitting wrong and the unhurried geometry of men who had been sent to retrieve and not to rescue, then she had her answer about what she now was to him. An asset that knew too much. A weapon that had begun to read its own armourer.

She watched for four hours, and a man came to the pillar, and she could not tell.

That was the thing she had not been ready for. He came alone, and he was good, and he looked once at the mark and did not touch it and bought a samosa he did not eat and stood in the lee of the pillar reading nothing on his dead phone, and he was either a courier sent in love or the lightest possible edge of a net laid in patience, and she sat with all her craft and her four hours and she could not read which. Because there was no second man to find, or because the second man was as good as the first. Because the vehicle was clean, or because they had walked. She had every instrument she owned trained on the question and the question gave her nothing back.

And then she understood, sitting over a glass of tea gone cold, that she had never had an instrument for him. Eleven years and she could read a stranger's street in a glance, and she had no needle that swung toward Fateh, because she had never built one, because the place where the needle should sit had been filled from the start with trust, poured in early and set hard, and you cannot read a man you were assembled to believe. The trust had been the blindness. He had not hidden from her. He had simply made sure she would never think to look.

That was reading her handler. Not seeing through him. Seeing, for the first time, the shape of the thing that had always kept her from seeing through him, and seeing that it was him.

She left the tea on the table and the man at the pillar to his cooling samosa and she did not go back to the lodge. She walked an hour and changed her line three times and washed up in the late afternoon at the place Mira had told her to go if the ground ever broke, a flat above a tailor's in a colony where the lanes were too tight for a car to wait without being seen.

Mira was there. Of course she was there. She let Sita in without a word and put the bolt across and looked at her with a steadiness that Sita read now the way she read everyone, helplessly, professionally, and what she read was this: clean hands, a watcher's economy, the calm of someone who has told you the truth and is waiting to see if you can carry it. Mira had no tell. Sita looked for the seam in her the way she had looked for it in the fire, and there was none. The woman believed what she had given. That was the verdict her whole trade returned: Mira was not running a story. Mira had simply told her what was so.

"You used the string," Mira said. It was not a question.

"I needed to read him."

"And?"

Sita sat down because her legs had decided it for her. "There's nothing to read. There never was. He built me without a door." She heard how flat her own voice was. "I keep asking why. Why the fire, why the war, why any of it, the network, the boys in Punjab, all of it dressed as a country being saved. I keep waiting for the answer to be enormous."

Mira sat across from her, close, and her face did not change, and she said the small thing, the clean thing, the thing that fit through the door Sita's whole exhausted craft had just left open. "It isn't enormous. That's the part they can't let you see. He needed a weapon, and you were there, and a man like that will build a cathedral over an appetite so he never has to call it one. There's no war, Sita. There's a man, and what he wanted, and the children he was willing to make to get it."

And Sita, who had stood over Bedi's last breath, who had inherited a pipeline and washed its money and counted it a coin minted for the nation, felt the cleaner shape come down over the wreckage of the larger one and fit, and hold, and bear weight. It was simpler. It cost her less to believe than the war did. It asked her only to grieve and not to wonder.

She did not see Mira's certainty for what it was, that it was a door too, set early and set hard, in a woman who had also been given a story and had no needle that swung toward the rest of it. She took the sincerity for the whole, because sincerity was all her trade knew how to test, and the woman was telling the truth.

"All right," Sita said. She found she had decided something, somewhere on the walk, without consulting herself. "Then I want what he was protecting with all of it. Whatever's at the bottom. I want to be the one who reaches it before he does."

Mira watched her for a moment longer, and then she nodded, and something in her settled, the look of a handler whose asset has finally taken the wire, and Sita read that too, and called it relief, and was wrong about which of them it belonged to.
