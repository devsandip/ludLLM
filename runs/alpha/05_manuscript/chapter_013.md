The needle file made a sound like a small animal worrying at a bone, and each time it grew louder than the refrigerator's hum she lifted it from the silver and waited, counting, until the street outside settled back into its own noises.

The white Ambassador had been at the corner since nine. It was parked with two wheels on the kerb under the dead sodium lamp, the way a car parks when no one inside intends to get out, and a film of dust lay on its rear screen that never thinned, because the car never went anywhere to shed it. A family car clears its own glass; it is driven to the market and the in-laws and the dust comes off on the road. This one only sat. She had watched it from behind the curtain without moving the curtain. The engine ran for warmth or for the radio, she could not tell which, and twice in the night a man had got down to relieve himself against the wall of the DDA flats opposite and got back in without looking up at her windows, which was itself a kind of looking. They were not in a hurry. People who were in a hurry did not bring a flask.

She had perhaps a month. She had worked that out the way she worked out everything, from the grain of it: from the fact that the report she had walked up to the floor above three weeks ago had not come back down, that the man who took it from her had told her in the corridor, kindly, the way you speak to someone whose grief has made them tiresome, that she should take her maternity leave now and not think about Sehgal's accounts, that it was being looked at by people whose business it was. From the fact that the looking, when it came, had come to her own street and not to his. They would wait until the leave was logged and the building had forgotten her face. Then they would come in the small hours with a story already written, a gas ring left on, a fall in the bathroom, a young mother undone by her nerves. She knew the grammar of it because she had read the files. She had spent eleven years reading the files.

So she had a month, and tonight she had the hours until the four o'clock feed, and she was spending them filing a hole in a piece of silver no wider than a thumbnail.

The pendant had cost her ninety rupees in Dariba Kalan, cash, from a tray of fifty that were all the same, a flat openwork disc of the kind a grandmother buys to hang on a black thread against the evil eye. The man had wrapped it in newspaper without looking at her. There was no slip, no name, no telephone number written on the back of a card. That was the whole of its virtue. A document could be subpoenaed, photographed, lost in a registry, burned. A document had to be carried by an adult who could be followed home. But no one on the earth stripped a charm off the neck of an orphaned baby. It would go where the baby went, through whatever hands took her, across whatever border, into whatever house, and it would still be there, warm from her skin, when the child was grown and the men who were frightened of it were dead.

That was the reasoning. She had checked it the way she checked an assessment, looking for the place where she was telling herself what she wished were true. She had not found it. The amulet was the only courier the network could not reach, because the network did not believe in babies. Men who thought in terms of command structures and pre-positioned assets and the long patience of a thirty-year placement did not look at a black thread and see a route. They saw superstition. She was betting the country on their contempt.

The disc, as it had come to her, showed a vine with a flower at four points, the usual bazaar pattern, cut clean through so the light came round it. She was not changing the vine. She was changing the gaps. With the smallest of the needle files, the one meant for the slots in spectacle hinges, she was lengthening one negative space here, opening a bridge there, closing another with a bead of solder she had melted off a broken earring against the candle. The eye that read the metal would read the flowers. The eye that read the dark between them, that had always read the dark between them, would read a number.

She had been such an eye since she was six.

Her mother used to hide the New Year coins in the house, a game, and the others would lift the cushions and shake the books and find nothing, and she would stand in the doorway and look at the room and know. Not the coin. The wrongness around the coin, the cushion sitting a half inch proud, the shadow under the almirah that was the wrong shape for an empty floor. She read the gap, the reverse, the seam. It was why she had been good at the work and why the work would kill her, because she had read the seam in Sehgal's accounts where everyone above her read only the smooth and trusted surface of a man with thirty unblemished years, and she had been unable, being made the way she was made, to look away from it.

She set the file down and turned the disc over in the lamplight and could not be sure. That was the trouble with making a thing for an eye you had never met. She held it at arm's length, the way age would one day make a reader hold it, and let her focus drift, and for a moment the flowers dissolved and the dark between them stood up off the metal and there it was, the figure she had cut, plain as a milestone. Then it was flowers again. She breathed out.

The child made the small grinding sound she made before waking, and Janaki was across the room and bent over the cot before she had decided to move, one finger laid against the swaddle, and the sound subsided into the deep working of an infant's sleep. Three weeks old. No name yet on any register. She had told the hospital she would come back to file it and she had not gone back, and the absence of the name had begun to seem to her like the one clean thing she had done, the one fact about her daughter that was nowhere on paper for anyone to pull. The men in the Ambassador did not know what the child was called. Neither, properly, did she. She called her Munni, which was no name, which was every small girl in the country, which could be wiped off a tongue and leave nothing.

She went back to the table. She still had the second layer to do and it was the part she trusted least, because it depended not on the eye but on the turn of mind behind the eye, and a turn of mind was a thing she could only hope she had passed on the way she had passed on the shape of her hands.

On the back of the disc, in the flat field the openwork left, she was scratching a couplet. Four lines of a cradle song, the kind any mother might have engraved, the kind a jeweller would carve without reading. She had spent two evenings on the lines and they were, on their face, only love: sleep, my small one, the boat is on the river, the lamp is in the window, the road will keep till morning. A child who grew up and read them would feel her mother in them and weep and understand nothing, and that would be a mercy; Janaki had built it so that nothing in the surface ever forced the child to dig. The song asked to be taken as a song.

But a child made the way Janaki was made would not be able to leave it as a song. That child, somewhere past her twentieth year, idle and grieving and turning the only thing she owned of her mother over and over in her fingers, would catch on the word that did not sit right. River, where the song wanted sea. Lamp, named twice, the second time wrongly. The mind behind the eye would worry at the wrong words the way the file worried at the silver, would feel the cushion sitting proud under the smooth verse, and would not rest until it had pulled the lines apart and found, under the boat and the lamp, the marker on the old road, the count of furlongs north of the river, the side of the tower where the mortar was new.

She had to believe the child would be that child. She had no other religion left.

The proof itself was already gone out of her hands and into the ground, and the memory of putting it there came to her now with the file in her fingers, unbidden, the way the body offers up the thing it is most afraid of. Four days ago, before the Ambassador, before they had closed the distance, she had taken the morning bus to Sonipat and walked back along the verge of the old Grand Trunk Road in the white heat with a cloth bag, an ordinary woman with an ordinary bag, until she came to the Mughal milestone tower that stood in a field of bricks where no one stopped any more. There was a niche in its northern face at the height of her shoulder, behind a stone that came away when she worked it. She had put in the tin, an old Dalda tin with the lid pressed down on a lining of candle wax: inside it the exercise book in which she had copied, in her own hand, every transfer, every shell company, every line that ran from the hawala desks through Sehgal's signature into the bank accounts of men who sat at the heart of the strategic establishment and waited for a word from a master none of them had ever seen. And with the book the small cassette from her dictaphone, on which she had said all of it again aloud, in case her hand was doubted, dated, named, her own voice steady, which had surprised her. Then she had pressed the stone back and smeared road dust into the seam and walked on, and a truck had blared at her, and that was all. No phone had touched the place. No wire ran to it. It would sit there in the dark inside the brick while governments changed and the men who feared it grew old and reassured, and it would answer to one thing only, which was a girl with the right eye reading the right song.

She had given her daughter the keyhole and buried the door three hundred years deep in the road, and she could not decide, scratching the silver, whether she had done a brilliant thing or a monstrous one.

The Ambassador's engine cut.

She went still. In the new silence she could hear the refrigerator and the small wet machine of the child breathing and, far off, a pi-dog and then a second answering it. A door of the car opened and did not slam, was pressed to. She did not go to the window. She knew what going to the window did, the pale oval of a face behind a curtain at half past three telling them everything they needed to know about whether she had understood yet. She made herself pick up the file. She made herself not stop the small sound of it, because a sound that stopped was louder than a sound that continued, and she filed two more strokes into the dark between the flowers while a man stood somewhere below her window and decided something, and her hands did not shake until after she heard the door press shut again and the engine turn over and the radio come back, and then they shook so that she had to lay the silver flat on the teak and hold her own wrist.

Not tonight, then. They were only reminding her that the distance was theirs to close.

She put the room back the way a room should look at half past three. The thing she could not leave was the paper, the two evenings of working drafts, the sheets where she had set the cradle song down in column after column and tested each word against the number it had to carry, crossing out, fitting, the whole architecture of the cipher laid bare in her own hand. A clever man would need an hour with those sheets and the pendant would be nothing but proof against her child. She took them to the sink one at a time and burned them over the drain, holding each by a corner until the flame reached her fingers, and crushed the black flakes to powder under the tap and washed them down, and rinsed the spoon she had used for the solder, and put the needle files back in the roll of cloth they had come in and the roll behind the rice tin in the kitchen, where it was a sewing kit, where it meant nothing. The candle she pinched and stood in the drawer. The pendant she did not put down. She carried it to the cot.

The thread was the cheap black cotton they sold by the reel, doubled and waxed so it would not rot against skin for years. She had tied a hundred practice knots in the afternoon until she could do it without looking, a flat reef that would hold and not slip and not strangle, sized for a throat that would thicken over twenty years and never quite outgrow it. She lifted the child's head in her palm, the whole skull no bigger than an orange, and passed the thread under the warm nape, and brought the disc to rest in the hollow at the base of the throat where it would lie hidden under every collar the girl ever wore, and tied the knot in the dark by feel, and her fingers did not shake now because there are things the body will do steadily when it will do nothing else.

"There," she said, which was the only word she said aloud the whole night.

The cold of the metal, or the weight of it, such as it was, woke the child. Not to crying. The eyes came open, the dark unsteady eyes of a thing three weeks in the world, and swam, and found nothing, the way they always found nothing, and then snagged on the one strong thing in the room, which was the lamp. They held to the light because a newborn holds to light, because the bright edge against the dark wall is the only border its eyes can yet draw, and Janaki knew that, knew it precisely, had read it in the same clinical paragraphs that told her how a skull knits and a cord dries. The child was not reading the gap beside the lamp. The child could not read anything. The child was a few weeks of appetite and reflex with her whole self still ahead of her in the dark, unwritten, unowned, not yet anyone's.

And Janaki, kneeling, watched the unfocused eyes rest on the bright margin of the lamp and felt herself reach to make it mean what she needed it to mean, felt the want rise up under her like water under a boat, the want to see in this blind newborn stare the eye she was staking everything on, the proof that the bet was already won. She caught herself in the act. She had spent the night hunting for the place where she told herself what she wished were true, and here it was at last, the false note in her own account, the one wrong word in her own cradle song, and she had nearly let it pass for the plain reason that it was the one she most wished not to find.

So she did the hardest thing she did all that long night, which was to refuse herself the comfort. She would not know. She would never know. She would be dead long before the girl was old enough to fail or to pass, and the file would sit in the brick keeping faith with no one, and the song would be only a song unless a mind she would never meet bent over it and could not let it lie. She had built a door for a key that did not yet exist and might never exist, and she had no way on earth to test it, and that, not the men in the car, was the thing she could not bear.

She let it be unbearable for a moment. Then she put it down, the way she put everything down, and laid her finger in the child's palm, and the small hand closed on it by the only law it knew, gripping, holding, wanting nothing but to hold, and that at least was true, that needed no eye to read it.

"I'm sorry," she told her daughter, in the ordinary voice in which she might have apologised for a cold room. It was not the men she was sorry for, nor the dying she could already feel arranging itself around her. It was the weight at the base of the small warm throat, the road she had laid into a child who had asked for nothing but milk, the long task she had no right to set and had set anyway because there had been no one else, no adult the network could not reach, no clean hand in the whole building. She had made her daughter the courier of her own murder. Whether the girl ever read it or not, she would carry it. That much Janaki had decided for her, kneeling on the floor at half past three, and could not now undecide.

Outside, the engine of the white Ambassador idled on, patient as a clock. The child held her mother's finger and slept again, the cheap silver warming against her skin, and somewhere south of them the city began, very faintly, to grey.
