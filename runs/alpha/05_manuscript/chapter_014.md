The building had a dentist on the ground floor and a name on the second that meant nothing: Saraswati Commercial Services Pvt. Ltd., painted on frosted glass in a font chosen to be forgotten. Mira took the stairs, not the lift, and Sita understood why without being told. A lift was a box with one exit and a camera in the corner; stairs gave you a landing, a turn, a half-second of warning. They climbed in silence past a man mopping the half-landing who did not look up and was, Sita saw by the way he had set his bucket against the wall rather than the centre, not a man who mopped floors.

She had been brought in the back of a Maruti with the rear windows tinted past the legal line, her phone left behind in the safe flat because a phone in Delhi now was a confession that wrote itself: cell handovers, the silent ping to a tower, the face the street cameras lifted and matched against a database before you had finished crossing the road. Mira had taken it from her at the door with a flat palm, the way you take a weapon, and Sita had given it up the way she gave up weapons, easily, because keeping it would have meant she did not trust the people she had decided to trust.

That was the new arrangement of herself. She had decided. She turned the decision over again on the stairs, testing it the way she tested a knot, and it held.

The second floor was one long room behind a steel door that a young man opened from inside after Mira knocked in a pattern. Cool air, the particular dryness of a place run on a generator and a split unit because the grid could not be relied on and a power cut at the wrong moment cost more than diesel. No windows that mattered; the two that existed had been papered over with brown packing tape in a cross-hatch, against blast and against the long lens of a building across the road. The light was all from above, white and even.

She read the room the way she read a target's flat. Six people, four of them at trestle tables with laptops whose camera lenses were blinded with squares of electrical tape. A printer. A kettle. Along the far wall, a board.

The board was the thing. In an age when everything lived in a server somebody could subpoena, steal, or read over your shoulder from a chair you would never find, someone here had decided that the spine of the operation would live on cork and string and paper, in one room, behind one steel door, where it could be carried out in a single fire-safe box or burned in a sink. It told her more about the people running this than any briefing would. They knew that somewhere inside their own service there was a leak that had never been located, a man or a line of men who could read whatever touched the registry, and so they had built a clean room inside a house they suspected was dirty and put nothing in it that a wire could carry out.

Not orphans, then. Not a unit hiding from the people who owned it. The opposite. Somebody upstairs owned this, signed for it, would answer for it; and these people in this room were the firewall that kept the owner's order out of the hands of the thief they had not yet caught. She felt an old professional respect, cold and clean. She had spent her life on the other arrangement, the one with no room and no owner at all.

"Tea?" Mira said.

"No."

"Have it anyway. It buys you a minute to look without looking like you're looking."

So Mira knew her, a little. Sita took the steel cup of over-boiled chai and held it and let her eyes move along the board over the rim of it.

Photographs. Hawala nodes mapped as a constellation, red string running from a sari shop in Karol Bagh she recognised, from a gold dealer in Zaveri Bazaar, out across a gap of bare cork that stood for an ocean, to Bangkok, to Dubai. Trade invoices pinned in clusters: cashew consignments that had never been weighed, polished granite that did not exist, the over- and under-invoicing that turned heroin money into the clean receipts of a trading company exactly like the one whose name was on the frosted glass downstairs. She knew this map. She had built half of it from the other side. Forty days ago these had been her rails. She had inherited them the way a son inherits a house, walking through rooms already furnished.

And in the centre of the board, where the string converged, a face.

It was an old photograph and a bad one, taken long-lens through the windscreen of a car, the glass throwing a band of sky across the upper third of the frame. But it was him. The set of the shoulders. The way the head was carried slightly forward, attentive, a man always listening for the thing under the thing. Somebody had written beneath it in marker, in a careful clerk's hand, a single word that was not his name because they did not have his name. They had written ALPHA.

She had called him Baba.

Sita drank the tea. It was too sweet and she was grateful for the heat of the cup against her palms because her palms had gone cold, and a cold palm was a tell, and she did not want Mira to read her the way she had just read the room.

"You see how thin it is," Mira said quietly, coming to stand beside her, both of them facing the board like two people in a gallery. "Twenty years he's been a rumour. A unit with no letterhead. Funded out of the very thing it claims to be fighting." She tilted her head at the constellation of red string. "There's no file to pull. No officer who signed for him. If we put him before a magistrate tomorrow the service would stand up in court and say, with clean hands, that no such man exists and no such unit was ever raised. Which is true. That's the genius of it. He built a thing nobody owns, so there is no one to break, and nothing to seize, and no order anyone can produce."

"Then how do you hold it." Sita kept her voice level, an operator asking an operator. "You can't follow paper that isn't there. You can't follow him on a phone he doesn't carry."

"You follow the only thing he can't do without." Mira nodded at the strings. "Money has to move. Even his. Especially his, now that he's swallowed the Crescent pipeline whole. He's sitting on more cash than he's ever held, and cash is heavy, Sita, cash is physical, cash has to be washed and the washing leaves a tide line." She said it without heat, a teacher pleased with a clean proof. "He spent twenty years invisible. Then four weeks ago he took the whole pipeline at once, and the surge of it lit up every rail we'd ever flagged. He thinks he's choking somebody. He doesn't see that the choke is a flare."

*He thinks he's choking somebody.* Sita held still around the sentence. There was a somebody. Mira said it lightly, as though it were a thing Baba had invented, a justification he told himself in the dark. But Sita had stood in a Punjab warehouse and listened to him explain, with the patience of a man teaching arithmetic to a child, that every gram was a coin minted for the survival of the country, that the addicts were collateral smaller than the catastrophe, that there was a network buried twenty years deep inside the defence apparatus and the only way to starve it was to take its purse. He had said it the way other men recited scripture. She had believed it because she believed him, and now she did not believe him, and so the network had gone the way of everything else he had told her: into the bin marked his lies.

But she did not say that to Mira. She had learned, in eleven years, that you do not hand your handler the thing she has not asked for. Mira believed the money was only money. Let her.

The steel door opened.

She felt the room change before she turned. It was nothing showy: the young man at the nearest laptop sat up a centimetre straighter, an analyst killed a side conversation mid-word, the woman tending the kettle set down her cup. The kind of attention that is not commanded and therefore means the most. Sita had spent a life learning to read who held a room, and the room told her, in the second before she looked, that the man who had entered held it completely and had never once asked it to.

He was past sixty, dressed the way senior men in that service dressed when they wanted to be no one: a grey bush shirt, dark trousers, sandals, a cloth bag over one shoulder of the sort a professor might carry to a lecture. Reading glasses pushed up into thin grey hair. A face that had been handsome and was now simply trustworthy, the lines all in the right places, the lines of a man who had listened to a great many people across a great many tables and had, on the whole, helped them. He moved without hurry. He let the door close behind him before he spoke, and when he spoke it was to the youngest person in the room.

"The figures from Dubai," he said. "Did the second tranche reconcile?"

"Off by four lakh, sir. We think it's a courier's cut, not a discrepancy."

"Find the courier's name before you decide what it is." Gently. "Four lakh is a man's loyalty. I'd like to know who we're paying for, and whether he knows he's been bought." A small smile. "Get it to me tonight. I'll be up."

Mira had straightened beside her. Not to attention; Sita watched the older woman's face and saw something rarer there than deference, a kind of rest, the way a person's shoulders drop when they come into a house where they have always been safe. "Sir," Mira said. "This is the source we discussed."

The man turned. He looked at Sita the way a good doctor looks at a patient, taking in the whole before the part, and the look had no edge in it that she could find, and she was very good at finding edges.

"Arvind Sehgal," he said, and did not offer the rest, the rank, the Additional Secretary, the thirty years; he offered only the name, the way a man secure to his roots offers only the name. "You've come a long way to be here. I won't pretend I know what it cost you."

"You're the one who finds the money," Sita said. It was not a question. She had read it off the board: every tasking sheet pinned beside the constellation carried the same two initials in the corner, a countersign, A.S., and the financing of a hunt that officially had no budget had to come from somewhere, conjured out of contingency lines and reallocated heads and the quiet favours of a man trusted enough that no one audited him. He owned the purse. He owned the tasking. In a deniable operation those were the only two real levers, and he held both, and the room loved him for it.

Something moved in Sehgal's face. The recognition of a professional being read correctly by another professional, and not minding.

"Someone has to," he said. "It's unglamorous work. The young people here" — a fond tip of the head at the laptops — "want to chase the man. I spend my days making sure there is diesel in the generator and a clean account to pay the courier in Bangkok, and a name on every rupee, so that when this is finished no one, ever, can stand up and say we did it in the dark." He set his cloth bag on the nearest table. "You are wondering why we work like this. Cork and string. No machine in the room you would trust with your own life." He had read her glance at the board exactly. "It is not because we have no master. We have one. Colonel Kaul carries this, and above him men who can be named, and every order in this room is signed before it leaves it. We work like this because somewhere in our own house there is a man we have not yet found who answers to the other side, and until we find him we keep our master's orders where his hands cannot reach them. Secrecy is not the same as orphanhood. A thing can be hidden and still belong to someone."

"He is hidden and belongs to no one," she said, and tilted her chin at the photograph.

"That is the whole difference between us." Sehgal pulled the reading glasses down, looked at her over them, pushed them up again, a small fidget of a man thinking. "We are both secret. Both deniable. If it goes wrong, the state will say neither of us existed. But when it goes wrong for me there is still a man whose name is on the order, who stands up and answers and goes to prison if prison is owed. When it goes wrong for him there is no one. The order was never written. The owner was never there. That" — he nodded once at ALPHA — "is not a secret service. It is a man who has made himself into weather. You cannot try the rain."

It went into her like water into dry ground. For eleven years she had been a weapon with no name on the order, an instrument Baba could disown with one sentence, and she had called the rootlessness of it love. And here was a tired man in sandals telling her, without contempt, that there was another kind, a kind that hid the same way and yet still had a man at the top who would answer for the dead. She had not known how badly she wanted to belong to something that answered until he named it.

"Why tell me," she said. "You don't owe a source a philosophy."

"Because you are not a source." He said it without cruelty, which was worse than cruelty; it was simply true and he had decided she could bear it. "A source gives you what he has and goes home. You have no home. Mira has made that plain to me. I don't propose to use you and discard you the way he used you. I propose, if you'll have it, to put your name on an order with mine beside it. To task you against the thing that made you, with paper behind it and a man who answers above you, so that whatever you do from here is a thing the state did, and owns, and not a thing you did alone in the dark for a man who lied to you about your mother."

The room had gone quiet around the two of them. Mira was very still.

He knew about her mother. Of course he knew; Mira would have reported it up, the documents, the strangulation, the fabricated fire. Sita had thought of it as the most private wound she carried, and here it was already in the file, already weighed and entered, and instead of feeling exposed she felt the strange relief of a thing being held by more hands than her own. They knew. They had taken the weight of it into their accounting. She did not have to carry it as a secret any more.

She looked again at the photograph at the centre of the board. The shoulders. The forward-tilted, listening head. ALPHA in a clerk's careful hand.

"What's the tasking," she said.

Sehgal did not smile, which she respected. A lesser man would have smiled, would have shown her he had won. He reached into the cloth bag and took out a single sheet, unmarked at the top, a courier-sheet of the kind that carried nothing a server could lose, and laid it on the table face down, and rested two fingers on it.

"Find his money before you find him," he said. "The man you cannot reach. The money you can. When we have the money we have the leash, and when we have the leash" — the two fingers pressed, once, on the blank sheet — "I will put it in your hand, with my name on the order and the Colonel's above it, and you will do what you were made to do, but this time someone answers for it. This time it was decided in a room, by men who can be named, not by weather." He lifted his fingers. "That someone will be me. I want you to know it going in. Whatever happens, it was my order. Not yours."

It was the most generous thing anyone had ever said to her, and it was said by a man who held the purse of the hunt and the strings of the laundering both, a man trusted utterly by a clean and frightened room, a man whose initials were on every order pinned to the wall.

She put down the steel cup. Her palms were warm now.

"All right," she said.

Sehgal nodded, once, the nod of a man closing a ledger that balanced, and picked up his cloth bag, and on his way to the door he stopped beside the analyst who was four lakh short. "The courier's name," he said softly. "Tonight. A man's loyalty is the one figure we can never afford to round off." And then he was gone, the door sighing shut on its steel, and the room let out the breath it had been holding, and Mira was looking at Sita with something close to gladness, and the photograph of the man Sita had called Baba watched them both from the centre of the board, his head tilted forward, as though even pinned to the cork he had heard the thing under the thing, and was waiting, patiently, for the rest of them to catch up.
