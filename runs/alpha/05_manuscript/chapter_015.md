The handoff was supposed to take ninety seconds.

Sita had given it three nights of watching before she let herself believe it. The front was a hosiery exporter on the lip of the Sadar Bazar wholesale lanes, a firm called Anand Knitwear whose declared turnover would not have kept its own shutters painted, and whose accountant drove a car the firm could not have bought him. That was the seam, and she had found it herself, this past month, teaching her own eye to read the language of paper rather than blood: not the money that moved, but the money that arrived without a reason to. She had pulled the firm's import-export code, matched it against a Bangkok consignment agent that surfaced twice in the ledgers she had lifted, and found where the two did not agree. Trade-based laundering left a residue the way a tide left a line on a wall. You learned to read the wall.

She had chosen her position with the old care. A first-floor stockroom across the lane, vacant, its lock a five-lever job she had picked in under a minute and re-set behind her so it would read shut. From the gap in the shutter she could see the exporter's side door, the loading mouth, and the two ends of the lane where it fed the bazar. She had walked the area in daylight and counted the cameras: four municipal, two private, one of them angled to take faces at the corner where the autos turned. She did not go near that corner. The face was the thing now. A face was a key that opened every door at once, and she had spent her life being a woman without one.

The phone in her pocket was a clean handset on a SIM bought through a vendor who sold KYC the way other men sold tea, a stranger's thumbprint on a stranger's papers, three layers of other people between her and the number. Even so she kept it dark. A dark phone in a known cell was a held breath; a lit phone was a shout.

At eleven forty the courier came.

He came on foot from the bazar end, a thin man with a jute sack carried the way you carry weight you are pretending is light. Sita marked the slope of his shoulder and the short step. Cash, then, not notes for show but bricks of it, and she thought of the fake currency that travelled the Crescent rails alongside the heroin and wondered which this was, real money washing or false money seeding, and decided it did not matter tonight. Tonight she was a camera. Sehgal wanted the rail, not the man. You followed the money because the money could not change its face.

The side door opened before the courier reached it. That was the first wrong thing. A door that opens before the knock is a door that was watching the lane.

She did not move. She had learned long ago that the body wants to react to a wrong thing and that the reaction is what gets seen. She let her breathing stay long. The courier went in. The door stayed open a beat too long behind him, a hand on its edge, and in the spill of the stockroom light she saw the hand was wrong too: not a clerk's hand, not soft, the wristwatch worn face-in the way a man wears it who does not want it catching light when he raises a weapon.

Two wrong things. Two was a pattern. Eleven years had taught her that you do not wait for the third.

She was already turning from the shutter when the floor behind her took a man's weight.

There is a sound a building makes when someone who knows buildings moves through it, which is almost no sound, a pressure more than a noise, the air in a stairwell changing shape. She felt it on the back of her neck before she heard the board. She had re-set the lock behind her. He had come through it anyway, or come another way she had not found, and that meant he had been in the building before her, or had watched her enter and waited, and either way it meant the stockroom had not been her hide. It had been the box they let her climb into.

She moved on the thought, not after it.

The first man was close, closer than the board had promised, and his hand found her shoulder while her own hand was still rising. He was good. He took her shoulder to turn her into the wall and put her face where he could see it, and she understood with a cold falling clarity that the face was what he had come for, that somewhere a screen was waiting to be fed, and she gave him an elbow instead of a face, short, into the soft place under the ribs, and felt his breath go out against her ear in a grunt that was almost intimate.

It did not finish him. Men like this you did not finish with one. He kept his grip and drove her into the shelving, and the steel uprights rang and a stack of cloth bolts came down soft as bodies, and his forearm came across her throat from behind, the proper way, the blood choke, the one that does not bruise and does not mark and takes you down in seven seconds into a dark you do not feel arriving.

She had four seconds of arithmetic left. She spent two of them well: chin tucked into the crook of his elbow to buy artery, heel raked down his shin to the instep, the small ugly economical things. He did not let go. The dark began at the edges the way it does, a narrowing, and through the narrowing she reached for the next move and found instead, rising up out of some floor below thought, a voice.

Baba's voice. Low, unhurried, the voice that had taught her to breathe in the back of a car when she was nine and shaking; the voice she had spent the last month trying to drown in Mira's documents and the photographs of a woman strangled in a house that did not burn. The voice she had decided was a lie. It said the old words, the words from before words had been a thing she chose, and they came in Punjabi, soft as a hand on the head: *Saans le, dhiye. Ghar paas hai.* Breathe, daughter. Home is near.

And her body answered it.

She felt it happen and could not stop it happening. It was not a thought and so there was nothing to argue with. Her spine went long. The fight drained out of her arms, not as surrender but as economy, every wasted gram of panic poured off, and in its place came a calm she had not summoned and did not own, a calm with edges, a calm that knew exactly what to do. Her hands, which had been clawing, stopped clawing. They went, both of them, to the precise points the calm directed: one over his choking forearm to take its slack, the other low, to the small fixed-blade she carried against the spine, and she drew it and turned it and put it into the meat of his thigh, high, inside, where the big vessel runs, the placement she had never been taught for this man because she had not chosen it, the calm had chosen it, and his arm came off her throat with a sound that was not a word.

She did not pause to watch him fall. The calm did not permit pausing. It moved her: out of the shelving, low, three steps to the stairwell, down the stairs on the outside edges where they did not sing, into the lane, and it turned her left, away from the camera corner, the right way, the way she would have chosen, except that she had not chosen it. She was a passenger in her own competence. The lane gave way to a covered arcade and the arcade to a service gully behind the cloth wholesalers, and the calm walked her through it at the pace of a tired woman going home, not fast, never fast, fast is a shout, and her breathing was the long count, and somewhere underneath the calm a small cold part of her stood apart and watched all of this and was afraid.

Because she knew this route.

She had never walked it. That was the thing the cold part of her kept returning to, turning it over, refusing it. She had never walked this gully in her life and yet her feet knew the next turn before she came to it, knew which shutter had a gap to slip behind, knew that the gully fed a vegetable lane and the vegetable lane fed a road and that across the road, two hundred metres down, behind a tyre repair shop that would be shut at this hour, there was a rally point. A cache. A door with a particular key. A place that was safe.

A place that was his.

She stopped.

She stopped in the mouth of the vegetable lane with the rotting-green smell of it in her throat and the calm still flowing through her like a current she was wading against, and she stood absolutely still and made herself ask the question the calm did not want asked, which was: who put the rally point there. She had not chosen it. She had not, three nights ago, walking the area, marked a tyre shop and named it a fallback. She would have remembered doing that. It was the kind of thing she always did and she had not done it here, and yet her body had been moving toward it with total confidence, the way you move toward a thing you set yourself, except she had not set it, which meant it had been set for her, long ago, by someone who had known she would one night be running through exactly these lanes and would need somewhere to run to.

No one knew the future that well. No one could have salted this particular gully years in advance against this particular night.

Unless the gully did not matter. Unless what had been set in her was not a map but a behaviour: that when the words came and the calm rose, she would seek the nearest fallback of a certain shape, a shut shop, a low door, a cache, and go to ground there and wait, and that he, knowing the shape, would know where to look. He did not need to know the lane. He only needed to know that she would always, always, run to a place like this, because he had built the running into her the way you build a homing line into a bird. The route was hers. The destination was his.

The knife was still in her hand. She looked at it and did not remember the decision to keep it out, and that was the same thing again, the small things done without her, and a nausea went through her that had nothing to do with the man bleeding in the stockroom.

She made herself put the knife away. It was harder than it should have been. Her hand wanted the next thing, the cache, the door, the waiting, and she had to spend will on it the way you spend money you cannot afford, peeling her own body off the program one decision at a time. *Not that way.* She turned right instead of crossing the road. Right was wrong, right was toward the bazar lights and the cameras she had spent three days avoiding, right was the stupid amateur direction, and she took it precisely because it was not the direction the calm wanted, because she needed, just then, to do one thing she had unmistakably chosen, even a stupid thing, to prove to herself that there was still a self in here doing choosing.

Her hand, somewhere in the turning, had come up to her chest. She found it there without having sent it: pressed flat over the little silver thing she had worn against her skin since before she had words, the pendant, the one constant of a life with no others, warm now with the heat of her own body. She had reached for it the way she always reached for it when the ground moved, a child's gesture she had never grown out of. Tonight the reaching frightened her too, because everything that came from before she could remember was suspect now, every reflex a possible string, and she did not even know who had hung this around her neck or why, only that her hand went to it like a prayer to a god she had stopped believing in. She made herself take the hand away. She did not, could not, suspect what the metal held; she only knew she had stopped trusting the parts of herself that arrived without asking.

She walked into the noise of the bazar and let it close over her. A chai stall, still open, a knot of porters around it. She bought a glass she did not want and stood with it and let her hands be a woman's hands holding tea while her mind worked the thing the way she would work a lock, by feel, in the dark.

It was very simple, once she stopped flinching from it. Mira had given her a frame and the frame held. Fateh had not raised a daughter. He had built an instrument, and an instrument has controls, and tonight one of the controls had been thrown. Not by him. He was not here; she had not heard from him in thirty-one days; he did not know where she was, or so she had let herself believe. It had been thrown by circumstance, by a forearm across her throat and seven seconds of dark, the conditions he had wired it to, and it had done exactly what he had built it to do. It had saved her life. That was the obscene part, the part Mira's documents had not prepared her for. The leash was kind. It had reached down into the place where she was dying and it had lifted her out, competent and cool and lethal, and it had asked nothing of her except that she go where it led.

He had said the words a thousand times when she was small and she had thought they were love. *Saans le, dhiye.* Breathe, daughter. He had said them to calm her and he had been training her, every time, pairing the calm to the phrase the way you pair a bell to a dog's hunger, so that one day the phrase alone would make the calm, and through the calm he could steer her, and she would feel steered as comfort. She had carried the trigger inside her for thirty years thinking it was the one warm thing in her, and it was the lock on the cage.

And the part that put the cold all the way down to the soles of her feet was this: tonight the voice had come up out of her own memory, unbidden, no one speaking it. If a memory of the phrase could throw the control, then the phrase itself, spoken aloud, in his voice, to her face, would not be a thing she could resist with will and a wrong turning down a vegetable lane. Will had been enough tonight because tonight there had been no one on the other end of the leash. If he ever stood in front of her and said the words, her body would go where he sent it before the small cold watching part of her had finished forming the word *no.*

She set the tea glass down on the stall's plank, exactly squared, and noticed she had squared it, and unsquared it, a small deliberate sloppiness, a flag planted in herself.

She would not tell Mira. The thought arrived clean and certain and she examined it with some surprise. Not yet. Mira would want to know the mechanism, would want to test it, would want, perhaps, to use it; Mira's people were people too, and a control is a control whoever holds the other end. And she would not, would never, go to the tyre shop, or any fallback shaped like it, and she made herself memorise that prohibition the way she memorised exfil routes, *the kindness is the trap, the calm is the leash, when it comes for you walk into the cameras,* because the only defence she could see against a door someone else could open in her was to know exactly where the door was and to refuse, every time, by an act of will, the warm thing on the other side of it.

She filed it away. That was the word she used to herself, the operational word, *filed,* because it let her set the fear down somewhere with edges instead of carrying it loose. A buried trigger, a phrase, a protocol; owner not present tonight but capable of presence; counter-measure: refusal, route-breaking, and silence. She would write none of it down. Some things you kept only in the one place no one had yet learned to read.

The man in the stockroom was either dead or finding a phone. The face he had wanted was walking, now, into the thickest light of the bazar, where a thousand other faces would dilute it on every screen between here and morning, and Sita let herself be one of them, ordinary, tired, going home.

Except that she had no home, and tonight she had learned that the nearest thing she had ever been given to one was a word in her father's voice that could fold her up and carry her wherever he chose. She walked away from the only direction her body wanted to go, and her heart kept time too fast all the way to the road, and she understood that she was, for the first time in her life, hiding from herself.
