The lift to the seventh floor was out again, so she took the stairs, the brown folder held flat against her chest the way you would hold a child, and by the third landing she was breathing harder than the climb warranted and knew it was not the climb. Twenty-three days since the delivery. Her body had not yet decided whose it was. Under the cardigan the cotton was damp and she had pinned a folded handkerchief into the cup of her bra and it had not been enough, and she could smell, faintly, beneath the institutional smell of floor polish and old paper, the particular sour-sweet of the infant she had left sleeping in Pushpa's arms in Vasant Kunj at seven that morning. Her arms, climbing, felt wrong. Empty in a way that had a shape.

The folder was not heavy. That was the thing she kept returning to. Four years of a man's careful arithmetic and it weighed less than the baby.

The Chief's outer office had the hush of a place where the air-conditioning was set for men in jackets. The PA, Narang, a thin person with a Parker pen clipped where another man would wear a flower, looked up and then did not quite look at her. There was a cup of tea going cold at his elbow and a green telephone and a grey telephone and, on the credenza behind him, the fax machine with the encryption sleeve over its handset, blinking its single amber eye. He told her the Chief would not be long. He had told her that at four o'clock. It was now twenty to five.

She sat. She put the folder on her knees and her hands on the folder and waited, and watched the amber eye blink, and thought about who in the building had a key to that machine, and which of the keys she could no longer assume was clean.

That was the change in her, these last weeks. Not fear exactly. A recalibration. She had spent eleven years inside this service believing that the building was a building, that a corridor was a corridor and a colleague a colleague, and the discovery she carried in the folder had quietly dismantled all of it. A corridor was now a sightline. A colleague was now a question. Somewhere in this organisation, perhaps on this floor, a man had been bought a long time ago and had stayed bought, and had spent twenty years threading other men into the places where the country kept its missiles and its codes, and she had found the thread, and pulling it had taught her that the floor she walked on was not floor.

She had copied everything. Twice. One set was in the folder on her knees. The other was where the building could not reach.

"Mrs Iyer." Narang was holding the inner door. He had used the married name, which she had stopped using at work two years ago, and she let it pass. "He'll see you now."

Luthra's office smelled of cigarettes he was no longer supposed to smoke and of the sandalwood the cleaners rubbed into the desk. He was at the window with his back to her when she came in, a square, settled man in shirtsleeves, the braces of an older generation crossing his back, looking down at the car park and the gulmohar trees gone leafless for the season and, beyond the wall, the slow red bleed of the Ring Road in the failing light. He did not turn at once. He let her take the chair. It was a way of arranging a conversation, making the other person sit while you stood, and she had seen him do it to station heads and she understood that he was doing it to her now and that he had decided the shape of this before she walked in.

"You should be at home," he said to the glass. "Three weeks. My wife wouldn't let me in the same room as her for forty days, either child. Quite right too." He turned then and came and sat, heavily, behind the desk, and folded his hands, and looked at her with eyes that were kind and tired and gave away absolutely nothing. "Janaki. You sent me a note that frightened my PA. Tell me what's so urgent it couldn't wait for your leave to finish."

She did not reach for the folder yet. She had rehearsed this in the car, in the queue at the petrol pump, at two in the morning with the baby at the breast and the curtain not quite meeting the frame. Lead with the structure, not the name. Let the architecture do the persuading before he can decide to disbelieve a man.

"Four years ago," she said, "I was asked to look at anomalies in some trade settlements out of Dubai. Over-invoiced consignments, Bangkok leg, the usual hawala washing. I expected narcotics money. Crescent product through Punjab, the fake currency that rides with it. That's what it was, at the bottom. But the money didn't behave like drug money."

"Money behaves how it's told."

"This was being told something unusual." She kept her voice level, the analyst's register, flat and unhurried. "Drug proceeds want to disappear. This wanted to arrive. Specific amounts, into specific hands, over specific years. Not bribes. Bribes are lumpy and they stop. This was a salary. Somebody has been paying salaries for the better part of two decades to a set of people, and when I mapped where the people sit, sir, the map is the thing I came to show you." Now she opened the folder. She did it slowly. She laid out the first sheet, then the second, squaring them on the desk with her fingertips, turning them to face him. "Strategic Forces. The missile programme. Two in the cyber cell at the new directorate. One who vets the people who vet the people. They were placed. Patiently. Over twenty years. Somebody has been growing a second service inside ours and watering it from the drug rails."

He looked at the sheets. He did not pick them up. A man who picks up a paper has begun to own it; he kept his hands folded and only looked, the way you look at a snake on a path to see whether it has noticed you.

"You've shown this to whom."

"No one." It came out smoothly because she had practised it, and because it was a lie, and the smoothness of her own lying to the Chief of her service was a sensation she filed away to be sick about later. The other set, the second set, she would not give him. Some animal part of her had already decided that, before her reasoning had caught up, and her reasoning, catching up, agreed. "I logged nothing in the registry. I came to you."

Something moved behind his eyes at that, and she could not read whether it was relief or sorrow, and the not-reading frightened her more than either would have.

"Good," he said. "That was correct."

He let a silence open. Outside, far down, a horn, then the long sigh of brakes. He took a cigarette from the drawer, did not light it, turned it end over end on the blotter.

"You have a name," he said. "You've buried it three layers down in your little presentation because you're afraid of it. Give it to me, Janaki. We're neither of us children."

She gave him the third sheet. On it, the convergence: the accounts, the timing of the placements against the postings, the one office through which every wire had at some moment passed for clearance, the signature initials in the corner of a sanction that should never have carried this man's reach. She did not say the name. She let the paper say it.

He read it. For the first time he picked up a sheet, and held it at the distance his eyes now needed, and read it, and put it down with the unhurried care of a man returning a thing to exactly where it had lain. And he did not say, that's impossible. He did not say, you've made a mistake, Arvind has thirty years, you've confused a clerk's error for treason. He did not defend the man at all, and it was the absence of the defence that told her the worst of it, because she had come prepared to fight disbelief and there was no disbelief in the room. There was only a tiredness that had been waiting for her.

"Sit back," he said quietly. "You're leaning over the desk like you mean to climb it."

She sat back. Her heart was going.

"Let me tell you what you've done," Luthra said. "You've done it very well. That's not flattery, it's the problem. A lesser officer would have brought me a suspicion and I could have sent her away to find proof and the years would have done the rest. You've brought me proof. You've left me nowhere to stand." He turned the unlit cigarette over again. "Now let me tell you what I know that you don't, and you'll forgive an old man for not showing you my third sheet."

"Sir."

"There are operations running off the back of those rails that are holding things in place you cannot see from where you sit. Live things. People whose breathing depends on certain accounts staying open and certain men believing they are unobserved. If I move on what you've put in front of me, if I so much as ask the wrong man a careful question, those accounts close in a night and what they were holding up comes down. Not metaphorically. Comes down. I would be trading a poison I can watch for a fire I cannot." He said it without heat, a man reciting a sum he had done many times and disliked every time. "And there is the other matter. The larger one. Suppose I take your sheets to the Cabinet Secretary tomorrow. Suppose I am believed, which I would not be, not without a year of corroboration I cannot run without alerting the very people it indicts. What have I told the government of India? That for twenty years its missiles and its codes have been read by a foreign hand through its own intelligence service. That nothing it knows can be trusted. You think a state survives knowing that? You think this house survives it? We would not be reformed, Janaki. We would be dissolved, and something cleaner and stupider and blinder put in our place, and the men in your map would simply walk across to it, because the one thing your map proves is that they are patient and we are not."

"So they win," she said. "Because winning is the thing we can't afford to admit they've done."

"They don't win." He leaned forward now, and for a moment the kindness in his face was real and that was the cruellest part of it. "They are contained. Quietly. Slowly. By men who know what you know and have known it longer and have learned to carry it without setting fire to everything around them. There are ways to seal a man off, to feed him chaff, to let a network go grey from the inside without ever telling it it's been found. That work is being done. You have stumbled into the middle of a room in the dark and you are about to switch on the light, and you imagine the light is a kindness. It is the most dangerous thing in the building."

She understood then that he was not going to act. That the meeting she had carried up seven flights of stairs against the wrong instinct of her own body had a single purpose for him, which was to find out what she had, and to whom, and then to put it in a drawer. The proof was not too thin for him. It was too good. She had brought him the one thing he could not be allowed to have surface, and he had received it the way you receive a live grenade, with both hands and great care, in order to set it down somewhere it would not go off.

"Give me the folder," he said gently.

She looked at it. Her copy. The first set. She thought about Pushpa's arms and the curtain that did not meet the frame and the second set in the dark where the building could not reach, and she slid the sheets back square and closed the cover and put her hand flat on it for one moment, and then she pushed it across the desk.

"There's a recording," he said. He had read her too well, the way he read everyone, it was why he sat in this chair. "Your note mentioned a recording. And a ledger. Originals."

"In a bank box." Which was true. The box existed; it was empty now, and what had been in it was somewhere else, and she let him believe the box was the thing because letting him believe was the only protection she had left and she was learning, very fast, in this chilled and sandalwood-smelling room, to hoard her protections. "I'll bring you the key."

"Bring it Monday." He did not write it down. "And Janaki. You'll close this. In your head as well as your registry. You'll finish your leave, you'll go home to that baby, and when you come back I'll have something undemanding for you, Bangkok desk, regional flows, a posting a young mother can keep regular hours on. You've done the service a greater turn than it will ever be able to thank you for. The thanks for this kind of work is that nothing happens. You'll have to learn to find the silence satisfying." He stood, which meant it was over. "Most of us do, in the end."

She stood too. Her legs took the order a second late.

At the door he said her name once more and she turned, and he was standing by the window again with the dead cigarette in his fingers and the red of the Ring Road behind him, and he looked, for just that moment, not like a man who had buried something but like a man who was being buried under it, slowly, and had been for years, and had stopped expecting to be dug out.

"You came to the right person," he said. "Remember that, whatever you decide to feel about it tonight. You came to the only man who could carry it. Imagine if you'd gone to the wrong one."

She said, "Yes, sir," and went out past Narang and his two telephones and the fax with its blinking amber eye, and down the seven flights because the lift was still out, and her footsteps came back at her off the stairwell walls, and she counted them, the way she counted to settle the baby, and they did not settle her.

In the car park the gulmohars stood black against a sky going the colour of a bruise. She sat in the Maruti with the door open and one foot on the gravel and did not start the engine. She was thinking with the clean, cold precision that came to her only when there was no longer anything to hope for, which she would later understand was the most useful frame of mind she possessed and the one that cost the most to enter.

He had not denied it. That was the whole of it. A guilty man denies. A frightened man bargains. Luthra had done neither, because he was neither; he was a steward, and a steward's sin is the gentlest in the catalogue and the most fatal, the sin of deciding that a truth is too expensive and that the people who cannot afford it must therefore not be told. He would carry her folder to a drawer and the drawer would become a grave, and the man whose initials sat in the corner of that sanction would go on walking the corridors with thirty years of trust behind him, watered and patient, and there was now precisely one person in the service who could prove it and her name was on a note that had frightened a PA.

She had come up those stairs believing there was a clean channel. That if you found the rot you took it to the top and the top, by definition, was where the cleaning happened. She had believed it the way you believe the floor is floor. Now she sat in the dark of the car park and felt the last of that belief go out of her, not with a crash but the way the warmth goes out of a body, evenly, from the edges in.

The second set existed. The recording, the ledger, the real ones, were where the building could not reach. That was no longer enough. A hidden thing was only as safe as the hider, and the hider was a woman with a face the wrong people had now been given a reason to remember, and a registry note, and a Monday appointment to hand over a key to a box that would tell them nothing and would tell them, by its emptiness, everything.

She would need a courier the network could not reach. A channel that ran outside every channel they owned, because they owned them all; she saw that now, the whole grid of trusted hands, and there was not one of them she could put this into.

She closed the car door. In the silence she could hear, very small, the tick of the cooling engine, and under it nothing, and into the nothing she let herself think the thought all the way through for the first time, calmly, as a problem with a solution rather than a horror, because she had no one left to bring the horror to.

There was one person in the world the network had no reason to watch, and no way to reach, and every reason to ignore. She was twenty-three days old and she was asleep in Vasant Kunj in Pushpa's arms, and she had her mother's eyes.

Janaki started the car.
