The road gave out forty kilometres short of the place, the way every map had promised and Sita had not quite believed. After Joshimath the metalled surface frayed to gravel, then to a track of frost-heaved stone that the hired Bolero took at walking pace, the driver crossing himself at each blind switchback though there was no church in three hundred kilometres. Then the track gave out too. They paid the man in cash, in the soft worn notes of a country that still trusted paper above all the bright instruments it had built to replace it, and he turned the vehicle in nine careful movements and was gone before the dust had settled, glad to be quit of them.

Mira shouldered the smaller pack. Sita took the other. They had left their phones in a steel box in a guesthouse in Rishikesh, batteries out, SIMs snapped, because a phone was a confession that travelled at the speed of light, and the men who might be looking for them read confessions for a living. Up here it hardly mattered. There had been no bar of signal since the second pass. The last tower Sita had clocked was a leaning lattice above a tea stall, its red light blinking at a valley that no longer answered.

She found she did not miss it. That was the thing she turned over as they climbed: how little she missed the weight of the thing in her pocket, the small animal need of it. Eleven years she had lived inside its glow, taking her instructions, reporting her successes in the flat grammar of a courier. Without it she felt amputated and clean at once, like a woman who has set down a bag she carried so long she stopped feeling the strap.

The path went up through deodar and then through nothing, the trees surrendering to a slope of grey scree and the wind that came off the snowfields with the patience of something that had never been hurried in its life. By noon the air had thinned to a substance she had to work for. She was fit, fitter than most soldiers, and still her lungs hunted at the top of each breath and did not quite close on what they wanted. Mira walked behind her, slower, her breath a wet rasp. Neither spoke. There was nothing the altitude allowed you to say that was worth the oxygen.

She thought about how you went to ground.

She knew the doctrine. You broke your pattern, you broke your wallet, you broke the chain of people who could be leaned on to give you up. You went where the cameras were not, where facial recognition had nothing to compare you against because no lens had ever logged your face against your name. A city was a net of glass eyes now; she had spent her working life threading through it, learning where the seams were. But a city was also where you could disappear into a crowd of a million strangers, and the men who hunted her knew that, and so the cleverest thing was not to hide in the crowd at all. It was to go somewhere there was no crowd. Somewhere the satellites passed over twice a day and saw rock and saw rock and saw rock, and a man who looked like rock did not trouble the analysts in Bangkok or wherever they sat now, drinking their cold coffee and waiting for an anomaly.

Kabir Dhaliwal had not troubled an analyst in six years.

That was the legend, anyway, and Sita had learned to distrust legends from the inside, having been one. They said in the trade, in the careful half-sentences men used when they did not want to be quoted, that Dhaliwal had been the best of them. Not the most decorated; the decorations went to men who could be photographed. The best. The one they sent when the thing could not be done and had to be. And then one day he had simply stopped, walked off the board mid-game, and the men who owned him had spent good money confirming he was not dead and then, finding he had nothing left to sell or tell, had let him go the way you let go of a debt you cannot collect.

He had walked this road. That was the whole of it. That was why she had come.

---

The monastery announced itself first as a smell, woodsmoke and butter and something mineral under it, and then as a line of prayer flags so bleached by sun and wind that their colours had gone to the grey of old bruises. The buildings sat in a fold of the mountain where a stream came down, low whitewashed walls and a single hall with a roof of beaten metal that had long ago given up its shine. There were perhaps a dozen monks. None of them looked up as the two women came in through the gap in the wall. Sita understood that this was not indifference. It was discipline of a kind she recognised. They had been told, or they had decided, that the fewer faces they fixed on a stranger, the fewer they would have to account for later, and the mountain taught everyone that lesson in the end, monk or murderer.

A boy of nine or ten took them to him without being asked, which told her they had been expected, or that strangers came so seldom there was only ever one person they could mean.

He was in the kitchen.

She had built a picture on the climb and she let it fall away, because it was wrong in every particular and the wrongness was instructive. She had expected the ruin of a hard man, the gauntness, the eyes that had seen too much and now saw nothing. What she found was a man in his middle fifties, broad across the shoulders, sleeves pushed up forearms scarred white in two places she catalogued without meaning to, kneading dough on a board worn concave by years of the same motion. He had a beard going grey and the unhurried economy of movement she associated with only two kinds of people: the very old, and the very dangerous. He did not stop kneading when they came in.

"There is tea," he said. "You will want it. The first night is the worst for the head."

His Hindi had the soft Punjabi underside she had heard all her life, the cadence of the men who had raised her, and something in her chest turned over and she put it down hard.

Mira said his name, the whole of it, Major Kabir Dhaliwal, the way you say a name to a man to tell him you know more of it than he has offered. He received it without expression. He turned the dough, folded it, leaned his weight into it.

"No one is a Major up here," he said. "Sit."

They sat on a bench by the wall. The kitchen was warm, the only warm room she would find in the place, she suspected, and the warmth made her aware of how the cold had got into her on the climb, into the joints of her hands. A monk poured tea from a battered kettle, salt tea, thick with rancid butter, and she drank it because he had said to and because her body needed the salt and the fat more than her tongue objected to it.

She let Mira talk. That was the arrangement, unspoken: Mira was the one who explained things, who built the case, who laid the documents on the table. It was how she had come to Sita in the first place, with paper and patience, and Sita had learned since then that paper was a kind of weapon too, that a true thing shown in a partial light did exactly the work a lie would do and left cleaner prints. She did not hold it against Mira. Mira believed what she said. That was the difference between Mira and the man who had made Sita, and Sita was still learning to measure it.

Mira said they had come for counsel. She did not say from what. Dhaliwal let her get most of the way through it and then he stopped kneading, finally, and wiped his hands on a cloth and looked at them both for the first time, and Sita felt the look the way you feel a hand pass over you in the dark, knowing exactly where it has been.

"You did not climb eleven hours," he said, "for counsel. Counsel you can buy from a lawyer in Delhi. You climbed because you wanted to look at someone it had already happened to." He looked at Sita when he said it, not at Mira. "Which one of you is the one it is happening to?"

Sita said nothing. The silence did her answering.

---

He gave them a cell each, off the second courtyard, with a wooden cot and a single high window and walls that breathed cold all night. Before he left her at the door he stood a moment in the low frame, and she saw past him into his own cell across the way, the door standing open, and on the sill of his window two objects sat in the last of the light.

One was a watch. A diver's watch, steel, the bezel scratched to dullness, the kind a certain sort of man had bought himself in a certain sort of decade because it would survive what he intended to survive, water and pressure and the small violences of the work. It was not on his wrist. He had taken it off and set it on the sill where the light could reach it, and she understood that he no longer needed to know the time to the second and could not quite bring himself to throw the thing away, and that the two facts lived together in him without resolving, the way such facts did.

The other was a photograph. A woman, young, laughing at something out of frame, the print faded to the colours of the prayer flags, a corner soft with handling. It was propped against the wall behind the watch where the wind from the window could not take it. Sita looked at it for as long as she dared and then looked away, because there are things you are not meant to see in another person's room and this was one, the whole of a vanished life set out on a windowsill in two objects, the instrument of who he had been and the reason he had stopped being it, and she had walked past a hundred safe houses in her life and never once seen a man's soul laid out so plainly and she did not think he had meant for her to. Or perhaps he had. With men like this you never knew what was carelessness and what was a message left where you would be sure to find it.

She lay on the cot in the cold and did not sleep.

She let herself think the thing she had carried up the mountain and not yet looked at directly.

They said Dhaliwal had killed Luthra.

Colonel Sunil Luthra, who had been Chief once, in the years before the building learned to call its Chiefs by softer titles. Luthra who had made Dhaliwal, taken him in young and shaped him and pointed him at the dark and pulled the trigger that sent him into it, every year for years, until the boy was the weapon and the man was the hand. And one day Dhaliwal had needed to get inside a group that no clean man could get inside, a group that demanded a price at the door, and the price was a corpse with a name on it the group could verify, and the name Dhaliwal had brought to the door was Luthra's.

They said Luthra had known. That was the part of the legend that had brought her up the mountain, the part she could not make sit still. They said the old man had understood what his protege needed and had let him have it, had sat down across from the boy he had made and arranged his own face into peace and let the boy do the thing he had trained him to do. That a man could love the instrument he had built enough to be killed by it, and that the instrument could love the man enough to take the gift and use it and walk away and live.

She did not believe it. Or she did not want to believe it, which she had learned were different sizes of the same thing.

Because if it was true, then there was a road through this. Then a made thing could unmake its maker and not be destroyed in the act. Then the leash did not have to be cut, which she had begun to suspect could not be cut, only turned, used, the slack in it become for one moment a weapon in the hand of the dog. She thought of a voice she carried that was not in any phone, a voice that could still reach into her and arrange her body the way Luthra had arranged his face, calm and obedient and ready. She thought: he believes he still owns me. And she thought: a man who believes he owns you has handed you the one thing he does not know he has given.

But she did not know if the price of using it was the price Luthra had paid. She did not know if you walked away from killing your maker or only seemed to, the way Dhaliwal seemed to, kneading bread at the top of the world with a dead woman's photograph on his sill and a watch he could not wear and could not throw away.

That was what she had come to find out. Not whether it could be done. Whether you survived the doing.

---

She found him before dawn in the courtyard, sitting on the cold stone with his back against the wall, watching the line of light come down the snowfield opposite. The cold was savage and absolute. He did not seem to register it. She lowered herself beside him, leaving a body's width between them, and said nothing for a long time, and he let her, which she took as the first real courtesy he had shown her.

"They tell it wrong," he said at last, as if they were already mid-conversation. "Whatever they told you. They always tell it wrong, because they were not in the room and a thing has to be small enough to carry before they can pass it along." He turned his head and looked at her, and there was nothing cruel in the look and nothing kind, only attention, which she had come to understand was the rarest thing one of them could offer another. "You want to know if it leaves you whole."

She did not ask how he knew. It was the only question worth eleven hours of climbing and he was a man who had spent his life learning what brought people up mountains.

"Yes," she said.

He looked back at the snow. The light was on the high field now, a hard rose colour with no warmth in it.

"Ask me an easier one first," he said. "The hard one will still be here. The hard ones are very patient. That is how you know them." He drew a slow breath, the way you learned to up here, taking what the air would give and no more. "Who made you?"

The wind came down off the field and moved the prayer flags on the wall above them, and Sita found that her hand had gone, without instruction, to the small worn shape that hung against her skin where it had hung since before she had memory, the one object of her own vanished life that she had never once taken off, and that she did not yet know how to read.
