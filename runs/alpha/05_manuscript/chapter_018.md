The cold at that altitude had a grain to it, like something milled. Sita had slept four hours on a pallet of yak-felt in a cell that held nothing but the pallet and a clay jar of water gone skinned with ice, and she woke before light with the old reflex intact: locate the exits, locate the breathing of others, locate the weight at her own throat. The pendant was there, where it had always been, cool against the hollow of her collarbone. She did not remember a morning without it. Her fingers found it the way other people found a scar.

Mira was already up. Sita heard her through the plank wall, the particular quiet of a woman who slept in her boots, and she let her be. Whatever Mira was, she had brought them here, across two borders and a forged transit permit and a twelve-hour climb on a road the maps still called seasonal, to a man the service spoke of the way villagers spoke of a tiger that had stopped killing. The foremost of them. The one who had walked out.

He was in the kitchen court when she came down, and he did not look up.

There was a hearth of blackened stone and a kettle of beaten copper on a trivet, and Kabir Dhaliwal crouched beside it feeding the fire with split rhododendron, one stick at a time, with the economy of a man who had counted the woodpile against the winter and found it short. He wore a quilted robe the colour of wet ash and a watch that had stopped. She had marked the watch the night before. It sat on his wrist with the hands at some hour that no longer meant anything, and he had not taken it off, which told her more than the photograph on the sill had told her, the faded one, a young officer and an older man squinting into a sun forty years dead.

"Sit," he said. "The tea wants ten minutes. You can earn it by being honest for ten minutes, which is longer than most people manage."

She sat on the worn plank across the fire from him. Up close he was older than the legend and younger than the silence. Sixty, perhaps. The face had been broken at the cheek once and reset by someone competent. His hands as they laid the wood were a craftsman's hands, unhurried, and she watched them because hands did not lie the way faces had been trained to.

"You climbed in the dark for the last hour," he said. "You didn't use the torch."

"The moon was enough."

"It wasn't. You came up that switchback by feel because a light makes you a target on a ridgeline and your body decided that before your mind voted on it." He set the last stick and sat back on his heels. "How long have you been the kind of person whose body decides?"

"As long as I can remember."

"That is the correct answer and also the saddest one." He lifted the kettle's lid, looked in, set it down. "Tell me what you came to ask. Not the long version. Mira will have a long version, your friend, she has the look of a person carrying a brief. I'm asking you. One sentence."

Sita had not slept enough to have her defences arrayed, and perhaps he had counted on that too, the hour and the cold doing his work for him. The sentence came out before she could dress it.

"I want to know if you can break from the man who made you and still be a person afterward."

Kabir looked at her for the first time then, full on, and his eyes were the disappointing part. She had wanted them to hold something. Wisdom, or ruin, some legible toll. They held only attention, the flat even attention of a man reading a sky for weather.

"No," he said.

The fire ticked. Behind her, in the doorway, she felt Mira arrive and stop, and Kabir's gaze flicked over her shoulder and came back, having priced the new arrival and shelved her.

"No," he said again, gentler, which was worse. "You don't get to be a person afterward. That door is the one you walked out of when you let him make you in the first place, and it doesn't open the other way. What you get to be is something else. Whether the something else is bearable is a separate question, and it is the only question, and I am not going to lie to you about it the way the people who love you will."

"Nobody loves me," Sita said. It was not self-pity. She offered it the way she would have offered a coordinate.

"Then you are ahead of where I was." He reached for two bowls from a shelf and a third, set them on the stone. "Sit down, Mira, the brief can wait, the tea cannot. You've both come a long way to be told things you knew before you started walking."

Mira came and sat. She had the documents with her, Sita knew, the photographs and the certificate of a death by strangulation that had rearranged the architecture of Sita's life in a single afternoon, and Sita watched her decide not to produce them yet. Mira was good. Sita had stopped trusting her and not stopped admiring her, the way you could admire a lock that had held.

"You were R&AW," Mira said.

"I was a number in a building. So were you, I'd guess. So was she, though she'd have been told it was something prouder, with a better story attached." He poured. The tea was the salted, churned kind, butter beading on the surface, and the steam off it was the only warm thing in the court. "We don't say the name up here. The mountain doesn't care and the monks pretend not to, and after six years you stop saying it even in your head, which is the part they don't warn you about. You lose the vocabulary for the life. Then you can't explain yourself even to yourself. It is a mercy disguised as a loss."

"Six years," Sita said.

"Six years in February." He drank. "You want me to tell you it gets quieter. It does. You want me to tell you the quiet is peace. It isn't. It's just quiet. People who haven't done the work mistake the two. Monks especially." A flat thing that was not a smile moved across his mouth and was gone. "Drink. You insult the host and the cow."

She drank. It was vile and it was exactly what her body wanted, salt and fat and heat, and she felt it reach her hands.

"You killed the man who trained you," Mira said.

Sita went still. It was a clumsy stroke, naming it like that, the kind of move you made to see what jumped, and she expected the old man to close like a fist. He did not. He set his bowl on the stone with great care, as though it were the only thing in the world that might break, and he was quiet long enough that the fire became loud.

"There was a teacher I had," he said at last, and he did not say killed and did not give the man a name, and Sita understood that the obliqueness was not evasion. It was the only register in which the thing could be carried. "He found me when I was nineteen. I was good at the entrance tests in the way that a wire is good at conducting current, which is to say I had no resistance and called it talent. He gave the talent a direction. He gave it a country to point at. For a long time I thought he had given me myself, and you will recognise that feeling, both of you, because it is the feeling they engineer, it is the product they are actually selling. Not the skills. The sense that you were nothing and they made you something and therefore you belong to them, the way the work of a hand belongs to the hand."

"Yes," Sita said. She had not meant to.

"I know." He looked at her, and there was nothing kind in it and nothing cruel. "I knew before you came up the hill. There's a way you carry the throat. You've spent a life protecting a soft thing at the centre and you don't know what it is anymore, you only know to cover it." His gaze did not drop to the pendant and she was absurdly grateful, and a half-second later furious at the gratitude, because it was the gratitude of an animal that had been taught to be glad of small mercies. "He was not a cruel man, my teacher. That is the thing nobody believes. They want the men who make us to be monsters because a monster is easy to leave. He was kind, and disciplined, and he carried the weight of decisions that would have broken louder men, and he was wrong, and his wrongness was going to cost more than any one life, and the only door to the place I needed to reach ran through him." He turned the stopped watch on his wrist a quarter turn, an idle motion, his thumb finding the cold rim of the case without his looking down. "So I went through him. And he let me. That is the part I have not been able to set down. He looked at me at the end and he understood what I was doing and why, and he forgave me before I'd finished, and a man who forgives you takes away the only thing that would have made it survivable, which is the right to hate him."

Nobody spoke. A bell sounded somewhere above them, two notes, and feet shuffled across a far courtyard, the unhurried traffic of men who had renounced hurry.

"That's the comfort I don't give," Kabir said. "People climb up here wanting absolution, or wanting permission, which is the same drug in a cleaner bottle. They want me to say: I did the unforgivable thing and look, I have a fire and a bowl of tea and a roof, so the unforgivable thing can be lived past. And I do have them, all three. But I tell them the truth instead, which is that I keep these things the way a building keeps a tenant after the fire. The structure stands. Nobody lives there."

Sita found her own voice rough when it came. "Then why are you alive."

"Now that," he said, "is the first real question either of you has asked."

He rose, the joints of him complaining in the cold, and crossed to the sill where the photograph stood, and he did not pick it up. He stood over it with his back to them and the grey light came in past his shoulder.

"I'm alive," he said, "because the thing he was wrong about was true. The country he pointed me at was real. The harm I stopped by going through him was real harm, and it did not happen, and a number of people who do not know my name woke up the next morning and the morning after that, and will keep waking, and none of them will ever thank me or should. That's the only ledger that balances. Not my peace. Not my sleep. The mornings of strangers." He turned. "If you are here because the man who made you broke your heart, go home, there's nothing for you up here, heartbreak is for people who get to keep their hearts. But if you are here because somewhere underneath the lie he told you there is a true thing, an actual harm, something that is going to happen unless someone who can do the unbearable does it, then we can talk. And you should understand before we open that conversation that talking to me will cost you the version of this where you come out clean. There isn't one. I'm the proof there isn't one. That's what I'm for now. I'm the warning the service leaves at the edge of the map."

Mira shifted. "We didn't come for absolution."

"You didn't. You came for a method." He came back to the fire and crouched and fed it another stick, and the practical motion seemed to settle him, to drain whatever the photograph had raised. "You have a target you can't reach, or can't reach and survive, and you've heard I once reached one like it, and you want the technique. I can hear it in how you sit. You've got the brief in a bag and you're waiting for me to ask." He glanced up at Mira. "I'm not going to ask yet. First I want to know what she wants." He tipped his chin at Sita. "Because she's the instrument in this, isn't she. You're the one with the brief. She's the one with the edge. And nobody has asked the edge what it wants, because nobody ever does. The edge isn't supposed to want."

The accuracy of it went into Sita like cold water into a wound she had not known was open. She thought of Baba's voice, which had lived in her so long she could not always tell it from her own thought. She thought of the way her body had killed three men in a ditch while her mind stood somewhere behind her own eyes, watching, because his voice out of memory had told it to, and how she had understood in that ditch that the leash was not a metaphor. That if he ever spoke the words to her aloud, near enough to be heard, the will she was so proud of would be a paper wall.

She did not say any of that. She had decided, in the ditch, that she would tell no one about the leash, and she held to the decision now even as the old man's eyes invited the opposite, because the decision was one of the few things that was still hers.

"I want," she said slowly, "to know if there's a way to do it where the thing he built into me doesn't win."

Kabir was very still. Something moved behind the flat attention, fast, and was put away.

"He built things into you," he said. Not a question.

"Everyone's maker does. You said so yourself." She kept her voice level. "Direction. The country to point at. Belonging."

"That's what I said." He held her eyes a moment longer than was comfortable, and she had the sudden cold certainty that he had heard the other thing under the words, the specific thing, the protocol, the leash, and that he was choosing, the way she had chosen, to leave it unopened on the stone between them. Professional courtesy, perhaps. Or a test of whether she would reach for it herself. "There's always a thing they build in that they don't tell you about. The belonging is the part you can see. There's a second part, lower down, that they install precisely so you can't get at it. That's the one that decides whether you walk out of this or it walks out wearing your face." He poured the last of the kettle into her bowl though she had not finished. "I won't tell you you can beat it. I'll tell you the only people I've known who got near were the ones who stopped trying to beat it and started using it. You don't break the leash. You let him believe he's holding it, right up until the moment the slack is worth more than the freedom, and then you spend the moment. Once. You get one."

"And after," Sita said.

"After, you have what I have." He gestured at the court, the kettle, the cold, the dead instrument on his wrist. "You have the mornings of strangers and a structure with nobody in it. That's the price and there is no discount and the men who tell you otherwise are selling you to someone." He stood again, finished with them for now, she could feel the audience closing like a door easing shut. "Eat. Sleep again if you can. Tonight you can show me your brief, both of you, and I'll tell you whether the harm is real or whether you've been pointed at it by someone with a use for you, because that, in the end, is the only question that decides anything. Whether the harm is real." He paused at the edge of the court, the grey light taking him. "Mine was real. I check it every morning. It is the closest thing to prayer I have left."

He went out toward the upper courtyard and the two notes of the bell, and the cold came in to fill the space he had occupied at the fire, and Sita sat with the bowl warming her hands and the pendant cold against her throat, and found that her fingers had gone to it again without instruction, turning it, working the small weight against its chain. The same motion he made on the watch, she thought, two people worrying at the one object each of them had never managed to take off.

Mira was watching her do it. Sita stopped, and let her hand fall, and did not explain.
