The relief came at four in the morning, twenty minutes before its hour, and that was how she knew.

For six weeks the watch outside her flat had kept to the half-hour, men who valued their sleep and changed over with a stretch and the flare of a match, careless because carelessness was permitted them. Tonight the new crew slid into the parked Esteem without a word and the old one walked away without lighting up, and the car under the neem did not so much as rock on its springs. A disciplined handover. Somebody had spoken to them. Somebody had told them the job had changed from watching to waiting.

Janaki stood a half step back from the window where the curtain did not quite meet the frame, and she counted, because counting was the only prayer she had left. Two in the car. One she could not see who would be at the mouth of the lane by the transformer, because that was where she would have put him. The municipal van that had broken down opposite on Tuesday and not been towed. The new gardener in the colony who pruned hedges that did not need pruning and never sweated. She had been adding these to a column in her head for nineteen days, the way she had once added remittances and shell accounts into the column that had brought her here, and the column was full now. It balanced. When a column balanced you stopped adding and you acted on the total.

The total was that they had decided. The month she had bought herself by being obedient, by going to the office and filing her nothings and signing the register and letting them see her drained and beaten, that month was spent. She had felt it spend itself in small subtractions. The fax line to the Cabinet Secretariat that returned only the carrier tone. Luthra's PS, who for thirty years had put her calls through, now telling her the Chief was in meetings, would be in meetings, was so very sorry. The colleague in Accounts who had drunk her tea for a decade and last week looked through her in the corridor as though she were already a notice on the board. People do not cut you for cruelty. They cut you because they have been shown the file, and the file says you are weather, and one does not befriend weather.

She let the curtain settle and went and stood over the cot.

The child slept on her back with her arms thrown up beside her head, surrendered the way only the very new can surrender, certain of nothing and afraid of nothing. Three weeks and four days. Janaki had stopped being able to look at her for long. It was not grief. Grief was a thing for afterward, for people who had afterward. It was that the sight of her undid the arithmetic, and she could not afford to be undone, not for the hours she had left, perhaps a day, perhaps less.

She reached in and with two fingers checked the cord at the small throat, the way she had checked it forty times a day since she had tied it. The pendant lay where it should, warm from the child's skin, a plain disc of dull silver no pawnbroker would look at twice, no customs man itemise, no killer trouble to remove from a body. On it the marks that were not decoration. In her the lullaby that the marks indexed, and in the lullaby, folded three ways and salted with the lies that only a reader with her own eye would strip out, the place. The river that was not a river. The clock that did not tell time. The tree you counted from. She had built it so that she could no longer think the location plainly even to herself; when she tried, her mind ran it as the song, and that was correct, that was the design. A thing you cannot say cannot be taken from you in a room with a table and a wet towel. The ledger and the tape were where the song said, and the song was on the child, and the child was the only courier left in the world that the men in the Esteem could not reach, buy, frighten, or already own.

Except that the child was here. In the box with her. And a box with two in it kills both.

That was the problem she had carried for nineteen days, turning it like a stone in the mouth, and tonight, with the early relief, it had become the only problem. The proof was placed. The key was placed. What remained was to get the key out of the kill zone before they closed it, and to do it in such a way that they did not understand a key had moved at all.

She made tea, because it was four in the morning and her hands wanted occupation and because a lit kitchen at this hour was the sort of small human untidiness watchers expected of a woman coming apart. She drank it standing, and she did the last of the planning the way she had been trained, backward from the moment of greatest danger to the present, so that every step earned the step after it.

The moment of greatest danger was the gate. Whoever carried the child past the watch and out of the colony had to be a person the watch had already filed as furniture, a person whose coming and going they had stopped seeing.

There was exactly one.

Kamla came twice a day, at seven and at six, a thin woman from the resettlement blocks beyond the ring road who had buried two of her own and had milk and a heavy quiet and no documents that touched Janaki anywhere. No ration card in the same ward. No name on any list that ran through the building, because Janaki had paid her in cash from the start and told the colony, when the colony asked, that she managed alone. The watch had logged Kamla in the first week and dismissed her in the second. A servant. They counted servants the way one counts lampposts, once, and then never again. Janaki had watched them stop watching her, and had understood even then, dimly, without letting the thought finish, that she was keeping a door.

The rest assembled itself. Kamla came at seven with her own jute bag, the one she carried her slippers and her tiffin in, the one she carried out again at the bottom of the morning. The watch had seen that bag enter and leave a hundred times. It would see it leave once more. What it would not see was that the child had left, because the child would still, to every eye that mattered, be in the flat: the bolster rolled in the cot under the blanket with the small machine-knitted cap at the head of it, the radio low on All India Radio the way she kept it for the feeds, the bathroom light, the kitchen light, the shape of a sleepless mother crossing and recrossing behind a curtain that did not quite meet. She could hold that performance for a day. She would only need to hold it until a train had cleared the Delhi yards.

She wrote nothing down. She had learned that from the work and now the work was paying her back, a debt she had not known it owed. She put together what Kamla would carry that the song did not. Cash, more than the woman had ever held, divided into three places on her person so that a single search would not take all of it. A second-class sleeper ticket she had bought herself a week ago at the City station counter with a face nobody connected to her, Old Delhi to a junction far enough south that the accent changed and the watch-lists thinned, paid in notes, no name that was hers. A folded paper with a different name for the child and a story to go with it, dead husband, dead mother, an aunt, the dull unanswerable grief that makes officials wave you through a barrier rather than meet your eyes. And the one instruction that mattered, which she would give by mouth and make the woman say back to her twice: the chain does not come off. Not to wash her, not to dress her, not for any reason, not ever, do you hear me, not if a sahib offers you the price of a house for it. The chain stays on the child until the child is a woman, and then it is hers to do with as she likes.

That was the bet. She let herself look at it squarely once, because an analyst who would not look at the weakness in her own assessment was no analyst. The bet was that this woman, who could not read, would keep faith with a piece of metal she did not understand for twenty years out of nothing but the memory of a frightened mother in a kitchen at four in the morning. And the bet beneath that bet, the one she could do nothing at all to hedge, was that somewhere down the years the child would grow up with her mother's eye, the eye that strips a lie out of a true-seeming line, and would one day turn the warm disc over in her fingers and see that the song was not a song. Two bets, both unwitnessable. She would be dead long before either resolved. She had spent her whole career refusing to act on intelligence she could not verify, and here at the end she was staking everything she had ever been on a chain of inference she would never see close. There was a kind of cleanness in it. She found she did not mind.

At seven Kamla came.

Janaki had the kitchen warm and she sat the woman down and held both her hands across the table, which she had never done, and she watched the woman understand from the holding that this was the day the cash had always been buying. She told it plainly, because frightened people remember plain things. The child's mother had bad people who wished the child harm. The child must go away today, this morning, in Kamla's bag, asleep, and Kamla must get on a train and go to the junction on the ticket and from there go wherever a careful woman with money could vanish, and never come back to this city, and never tell the child where she was born or to whom. And keep the chain on.

Kamla did not weep and did not argue, and Janaki loved her for it in the spare way she had left to love anyone. The poor do not need the danger of power explained to them. The woman only asked, once, whether the mother would come later. Janaki said yes, because the lie cost her nothing and bought the woman steadiness for the gate, and steadiness at the gate was the whole of it. She made Kamla say the ticket and the name and the chain back to her twice. The second time the woman got it word for word, flat and exact, and Janaki nodded and let go of her hands.

She fed the child herself for the last time, from the bottle she had made up, holding her in the crook of her arm at the window with her back to the lane so that any long lens saw a mother's shoulder and a mother's bent head and nothing of the small face. The child drank with her whole serious attention, fists working, eyes open and fixed on her mother's eyes in the unhurried way of the very young who do not yet know that anything ends. Janaki looked back at her and did not say any of the things. There would be no version of this the child could carry, so she carried none of it to her. She only fixed the face. The particular face. So that whatever came next in the small lit room, this would be the thing behind her eyes.

Then she gave a little of the syrup the dispensary sold for colic, enough to make the heavy morning sleep heavier, and she settled the child into the jute bag on the folded shawl with the slippers and the tiffin arranged over and around so the shape was a shape and not a child, and she rolled the bolster into the cot and set the small cap at the head of it and turned the radio to the seven-thirty bulletin and left the bathroom light burning.

At the door Kamla shouldered the bag the way she shouldered it every morning of her life, off the right shoulder, swung once to settle it, an animal habit no actor could have given her. That was the thing Janaki had been counting on and could not have bought. She did not embrace the woman. An embrace at the door was a thing the watch had never recorded and would therefore record now. She put her hand flat on the bag, once, over where the warmth was, and took it away, and opened the door, and Kamla went out into the colony morning grumbling something ordinary about the price of onions, loud enough to be nothing.

Janaki went back to the window that did not quite close and stood where she always stood and made herself into the performance. She moved a shape across the gap. She let the radio carry. And she watched the gate.

Kamla came down the path with the bag off her shoulder and her chappals slapping and she passed the gulmohar and the transformer and the broken municipal van, and she did not hurry, and the man Janaki could not see by the transformer did not step out, and the two in the Esteem did not turn their heads, because a servant leaving with her bag at the bottom of the morning is weather, is a lamppost, is the thing the eye has been trained off. The woman reached the colony gate. She spoke to the chowkidar, the same nothing she spoke every day. She went through. She turned left toward the bus that would take her to the City station, and the morning closed over her, and the car under the neem did not move, and no one in all that quiet street understood that the only thing in it that mattered had just walked out of their hands carrying it on her shoulder like a bag of slippers.

It had worked. Janaki stood very still and felt the working of it go through her, not as relief, which was another thing for people with afterward, but as the particular emptying an analyst feels when the assessment proves out, when the world does for once what the column said it would. The key was clear of the box. The box now held only her, and a bolster, and a light left burning, and a performance she would keep up as long as her body would keep it, because every hour the watch believed two of them were in here was an hour Kamla put rails between the child and these men.

She understood, fully and without drama, what she had just bought and what the price was. She was the decoy now. She had to stay, and stay convincing, and that meant she had to be here when they came. She had made herself the bait that holds the trap shut on nothing, so that the thing the trap was built for could go free down a line they did not know existed. It was the cleanest piece of work she had ever done and there would be no debrief, no file, no name on it, and the men who had ordered her death would close it out as a heart that gave way under strain, a sad clever woman who had not been able to bear her disgrace, and they would never know they had been beaten, which she found, standing there in the cooling kitchen, was the part she could live with least and would have to.

She did not know their faces. She had never known them. The reach that had returned her fax tone, emptied Luthra's diary, turned the eyes in the corridor, that reach went up past the last name she could put to it and into a dark she had not been given clearance to see, and she would die without seeing it, which was, she supposed, exactly the condition they required of her. She let herself hate it once, the facelessness, the way it had used the whole grey weight of the state against one woman and one ledger and would call that reasons of state. Then she put the hate away too, because it was no use to her and the performance did not need it.

She made fresh tea. She moved the shape across the gap in the curtain. The bulletin gave way to film songs and she left them on, low, the sound of a flat where a baby still slept.

At a little after nine the telephone rang.

She let it ring three times, the way a tired woman would, and lifted it, and a voice she knew slightly, pleasant, departmental, a voice from a floor above her own, said how good it was to catch her at home, and that the Chief had freed an hour tomorrow after all, eleven, the small conference room, just the two of them, to talk things through properly, no need to bring anything.

No need to bring anything. She looked across the room at the cot with the bolster in it and the small cap at the head, and she said that eleven would be fine, and that she was grateful, and that she would be there.
