The frost had got into the brass jug overnight, a skin of ice across the mouth of it, and Kabir broke it with his thumb the way another man might break the seal on a letter. He did not hurry. He poured the water into a blackened pan and set it on the small gas ring and waited, and Sita understood that he had done this ten thousand mornings and would do it ten thousand more, and that the waiting was not patience. Patience implied somewhere you would rather be.

She had not slept. The cell they had given her was a stone box with a window the size of a hand, and through it she had watched the valley fill with cloud and then empty again, and she had turned his words from the night before until the edges came off them. He had spoken in riddles then. About leashes you do not break but spend. About the one moment of slack a built animal is permitted, and the price of it. She had come up the path this morning, across the courtyard where the flags hung stiff with cold, to ask him to say it without the riddle.

He felt her there. He always knew where the room's other body was; she had clocked that in him within the first hour, the way she clocked it in herself, the involuntary census of exits and angles that no monastery would ever drain out of a person who had it built in.

"You want the plain version," he said. Not a question.

"I want to know if you can say it plainly," she said. "Or whether you only have it in the other shape now."

He took the pan off the ring before it boiled. He poured the water over leaves in two tin cups and handed her one, and she held it for the heat and did not drink.

"I killed Luthra," he said. "I put my hands on him and I killed him, and he let me do it. That is the plain version."

The flags moved once, outside, and went still.

"He was the nearest thing I had to a father. There is no version where that is softened, so I have stopped looking for one. He made me. Everything I am that is worth anything, and everything I am that should be drowned at birth, he put there with his own hands, the way you bait a hook." He drank. "And there came a day when the only door into a thing that had to be stopped was a door he was standing in. They would not open it to a man who had not paid. The currency was him."

"And he knew."

"He worked it out before I did." Something moved at the corner of his mouth that was not a smile, that was older than a smile. "That was the worst of it. I came to him with it half-formed, thinking I was bringing him a problem, and he had already costed it. He sat me down. He told me where to stand so the light would be behind me. He told me which of the two ways I was considering would leave less for the people who would have to clean it up. He was teaching, to the end. He was teaching me how to kill him competently because an incompetent job would have wasted the death."

Sita said nothing. The tea had cooled enough to drink and she did not drink it.

"You want to know whether I made my peace," he said.

"Yes."

"I did. That is the part nobody believes, so I will say it slowly. I made my peace. Not because it stopped hurting. It has not stopped. I made my peace because the thing on the other side of the door was real, and it would have eaten a great many people who never knew his name or mine, and it did not eat them. That is the whole of my peace. It is a smaller object than people imagine. They think peace is a warm room. It is not. It is one fact you can stand on. Real harm was coming and I stopped it, and the cost was him, and he agreed the cost. I stand on that. There is nothing under it and nothing beside it, but it holds my weight."

She had come for exactly this and now that she had it she found she could not look at him. She looked instead at the things he had kept. The watch on the sill with its hands stopped. The photograph going the colour of weak tea behind its glass. She had taken them, the first night, for grief left out where it could be tended. She saw now they were not for tending. They were ballast. A man who had hollowed himself out on purpose needed something heavy in the hold or the next wind would have him.

"You are not telling me to do it," she said.

"No."

"You said last night you would not give me absolution."

"I have none to give. I barely have my own." He set the cup down. "I am not your confessor and I am not your instructor in murder. You came up a mountain to ask a man who killed his maker whether the man is still standing. Look at me. I am still standing. That is all the answer there is to that question, and it is worth less than you think, because you are not me and your maker is not Luthra."

Outside, across the courtyard, Mira had come out into the cold and was standing under the eave with her hands pushed into her sleeves, not looking at them, looking at the valley, the way a person stands who is letting two other people finish. Sita registered her and filed her and came back.

"Then what is worth something," she said.

He was quiet for a while. Down the slope a bell sounded, once, and a boy's voice, and then nothing.

"Sit down," he said. "I will give you the one thing I have that is worth the climb. It is not about your maker. It is about your hunt."

She sat on the low bench against the cold wall. He did not sit. He moved while he talked, slowly, picking up a length of split pine and turning it and setting it down, and she realised he could not say a true thing standing still, that the body had to be doing something honest with its hands or the mouth did not trust itself.

"A hunt has a natural shape," he said. "You have run them. You have been one. When the quarry is real and the hunt is honest, it behaves like water. It finds the low ground. The resources flow toward where the quarry actually is. The pressure rises evenly. The net closes by its own logic, and nobody has to push, because greed and fear and the chain of command all pull in the same direction without being told. An honest hunt, you can predict it the way you predict weather. It does the obvious thing because the obvious thing is also the thing every man in it gets promoted for doing."

"I know what an honest hunt looks like."

"Then you will know a steered one, because it lies against that grain." He weighed the pine in his hand. "A steered hunt moves wrong. It is too fast in one quarter and asleep in another, and the speed does not match where the quarry has gone. It spends money on ground the quarry never touched and starves the ground where the quarry sleeps. It is given exactly enough to stay convinced and never quite enough to see the whole board. The men in it are not lied to. That is the craft of it. They are told the truth in pieces, and the pieces are chosen, and each man's piece flatters his own appetite so that he carries it gladly and never asks for the next piece along."

Something turned over in her, low and cold, and she kept her face still over it.

"You read it," Kabir said, "by not watching where the hunt looks. Any fool watches where the hunt looks. You watch where it refuses to look. An honest hunt has no blind spots it loves. It will turn over its own mother. A steered hunt has one patch of ground it always has a reason not to enter, and the reasons are always good, the reasons are always operational, the reasons will always survive a meeting. That patch is the hand. Whatever the hunt is being kept away from, that is what the hand is protecting. Find the thing the hunt is too busy or too principled or too short of men to look at, and you have found the man with his palm on its back."

"And if you are inside the hunt," she said. "If you are a piece of it."

"Then it is harder, because you have your own piece and your piece flatters you too." He set the pine down for the last time. "You ask yourself one question and you ask it cold. What would I be doing this morning if I had been given the whole of it instead of my slice. If the answer is the same, your slice is honest. If the answer is that you would be standing somewhere quite different, then you are not a hunter. You are an instrument, and the shape of the hunt is not yours, and you should go very quietly and find out whose it is before you take one more step where they have pointed you."

The cell had gone very quiet. She was aware of the gas ring ticking as it cooled, of the cold coming up through the stone into her boots, of Mira still under the eave with her face to the valley.

"You are not telling me about Luthra anymore," she said.

"I never was. I told you about Luthra in two sentences. The rest of the night I was watching you not ask the question you came to ask." He looked at her then, full on, and there was nothing kind in it and nothing cruel, only the flat attention of a man checking a weld. "You did not climb a mountain to find out if you could survive killing the man who made you. You already know you could. You have killed easier men for worse reasons and slept. You climbed it because somewhere on the way up you stopped being sure the hunt you are inside is honest, and you did not have the words to say so, and you needed to hear another man say there is such a thing as a hand on your back that you cannot feel."

She did not answer. She found she was turning the cup in her hands, slow, the way he turned the pine, and she made herself stop.

He let it sit. He was good at letting things sit; it was the most dangerous thing about him, more dangerous than the hands. Then he gathered the cups, hers still full, and tipped them out into the cold, and the steam came off the stone and was gone.

"I have given you what I have," he said. "It is a method and it is a mirror, and a man can hand you both and still have handed you nothing, because the looking is yours to do and I cannot do it for you. Go down the mountain. Do not decide anything up here. Up here the air is thin and a person mistakes the lack of oxygen for clarity. Decide it where you have to live with it."

She stood. At the door she stopped, because the discipline he had given her had already turned over in her hands and found its first edge, and she did not want to carry it out into the courtyard without saying it, in case saying it made it less true.

"My hunt," she said. "The one for me. It is owned. It has a name on it at the top, a real one, sanctioned, an owner who can answer for it. I have a man inside it who put his own name and his orders behind my work. He offered me the one thing nobody had ever offered me, which is that somebody would have to answer for what I did. I took it like water."

"And?"

"And by your method that is the most expensive thing a steered hunt can buy. It is the slice that flatters. I have spent eleven years being a thing nobody owned and nobody would answer for, and a man walked up and offered me an owner, and I was so thirsty for it I never once asked what the offer was steering me away from."

Kabir said nothing, which she understood now was the only answer he ever gave to a true thing, the silence that did not correct you.

"I do not know whose hand it is," she said. "You did not tell me that."

"I cannot tell you that. Nobody can tell you that from up here. That is the part you carry down."

She went out into the white light of the courtyard. The cold took her breath for a second and gave it back. Mira pushed off the wall and came toward her across the flags, hands still in her sleeves, her face open with the particular warmth she wore when she had something to give, and Sita watched her come and felt the method turn one more time in her chest and seat itself, terrible and quiet.

Because Mira too had given her a truth. A hard one, a real one, proof of her mother and the strangling and the made-up fire, enough truth to lift her clean out of one life and set her down in another. And Sita had taken that like water as well. She had not asked, not once, in all the days since, what the truth had been chosen out of. What had been left in the dark on either side of the slice she had been handed. She had assumed, because it hurt to receive, that it must be the whole. As if pain were a proof of completeness. As if no one would ever steer a person by giving her exactly the wound that turned her in the direction they needed her to face.

"You look like you got what you came for," Mira said, smiling, close now, kind.

"I think I did," Sita said, and smiled back, and let nothing else move in her face, and for the first time since the woman had walked into her safe house she looked at Mira and could not tell, by looking, whose hand was on her own back.
