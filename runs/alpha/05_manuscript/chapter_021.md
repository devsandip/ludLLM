The milk had to be tested against the inside of the wrist, and Janaki had learned to do it in the dark, by feel, the way her own mother must once have done for her in a house she could no longer picture. Too hot and the child refused. Too cold and she fussed, and a fussing child carried down a concrete stairwell at five in the morning in a sleeping government colony, which was the loudest sound in the world.

She held the bottle against her wrist and counted. The geyser had not heated; there had been no power since two. She had warmed the milk on the gas with an old steel pan, watching the blue ring of flame, and the small domestic competence of it had steadied her more than any of her training ever had. The child took the teat. Her eyes were open, dark and unhurried, the only unhurried thing left in the flat.

Outside, the lane was wrong.

For eleven nights the car had idled at the mouth of the lane, beyond the neem tree, where the streetlight had failed and no one had come to mend it. An Ambassador, white once, grey under the sodium of the next lamp along. She had not needed to see the men in it. The engine had been enough; they ran it for the heater and for the radio, and at intervals they let it die and then woke it again, and she had timed the intervals the way she timed everything, because timing was the only weapon an analyst was issued. She knew their shift change at one. She knew the cigarette one of them smoked at the open window, the small orange coal of it, the only honest light on the street.

Tonight there was no car. No coal. No engine waking and dying.

She stood at the edge of the window with the child against her shoulder and read the absence the way she would have read a redacted line in a file. A watch was withdrawn for one of two reasons. Because the subject had ceased to matter. Or because the watching was over and what came next did not require men sitting in a cold car pretending to be no one. She had not ceased to matter. She had spent three weeks making certain of that, leaving the lights on at the hours they expected, buying vegetables from the same cart, carrying the empty file to the office and back so that the men in the Ambassador would report a frightened woman going through the motions, a woman who had been warned and had understood the warning, a woman worth a month of patience.

She had bought the month with obedience. The bill had come due early.

It was twenty past five. Kamla came at half past, every third day, with the milk and the morning's coriander, a widow from the servants' quarters behind the colony who had buried two husbands and asked no questions of anyone, least of all of a junior officer's wife with no husband and a sudden infant and too much fear in her eyes. Three days ago Janaki had given Kamla a name and a bus, and four hundred rupees folded small, and a story: a daughter in trouble in Bareilly, a grandchild who must travel quietly, the bus from Anand Vihar that left at six. Kamla had taken the money and folded it into her blouse and looked at Janaki for a long moment with the flat, unsurprised eyes of a woman to whom the world had explained itself thoroughly, and had said only, the child will need the cloth caps, it is cold on the GT Road. That had been the whole of their conspiracy. No paper. No telephone. A widow and a bus.

At half past five Kamla did not come.

Janaki waited until six and made herself drink tea, because the body needed it and because there was a discipline in ordinary acts that the fear could not reach. The child slept now in the cot, on her back, fists beside her head in the attitude of complete surrender that only the very young could afford. Janaki sat on the floor beside the cot with her back against the wall and the cold of the concrete coming through her shawl, and she understood, with the clean and almost restful certainty of a hypothesis confirmed, that the route was burned. That Kamla, who asked no questions, had been asked one. That somewhere a man had shown the widow a photograph or a fold of notes thicker than four hundred rupees, and the bus to Bareilly was a road they had already walked ahead of her.

She had built three ways out. Two had failed in the building, swallowed by the registry, by Luthra, by the courtesy of men who agreed with every word she said and would not move a paper to save her. The third had been the widow and the bus, the smallest and the last, the one without paper, and they had found even that. They had found it because they could afford to find everything. That was the shape of the thing she had uncovered: not a man, not even Sehgal with his thirty years and his impeccable corridors, but a reach, a patience that had been working twenty years before she put a pencil to it, that owned the registry and the chief and the widow and the bus and would, by eleven o'clock this morning, own the manner of her death and the words written underneath it.

She had an appointment at eleven with the Chief. She had understood it, when it was made, for what it was. You did not summon a frightened woman to the eighth floor to comfort her. You summoned her to be certain of where she was at a given hour, so that the hour could be arranged.

They would not wait for eleven. The withdrawn car told her that. They would come in the dead grey before the colony woke, while the watchman dozed at the gate and the milkmen had not yet started their rounds, and they would make it read as something the newspapers already had a vocabulary for. There was a vocabulary ready. The city was full of it that month, the word everyone used now, the foreign hand, the men who came across with intent. A woman alone, an officer, a flat broken open, a fire perhaps. It would not even need to be elegant. Grief and a general fear would do the editing for them.

She had perhaps minutes. The arithmetic of it was simple and she did not flinch from it. She had spent the fear already, three weeks of it, used it up the way you used up a currency, and what was left now was only the work.

She lifted the child from the cot.

The pendant lay against the small breastbone on its thread, a flat disc of dull silver no larger than a thumbnail, the kind of thing a grandmother gave a granddaughter, a thing too ordinary for any man to take twice. She had made it herself, in a way, though the silversmith in Daryaganj had cut and closed it and never known what he was closing. The location was in it. Not engraved, nothing so crude. The clasp, the weight, the worn place on the rim where a thumb would rub, these were the index, and the key to the index was the song. She had folded the key into a lullaby across nine nights, line by line, each line a bearing and a count and a name that only sounded like nonsense, the way every lullaby was nonsense, a string of soft words a mother sang while her hands did other things. She had tested it against herself in the dark until it sat in the memory the way a hymn sits, below thought, retrievable for a lifetime by a body that did not know it was carrying anything at all.

It was a bet, and she would never see it paid. That was the part she had made her peace with last, and least. She was placing the whole of it, the network and the names and the dead-drop ledger in its tin and the tape coiled beside it, the work of a life and the lives the work might yet save, on a three-week-old child with her father's straight brows and her mother's grave, unhurried eyes, on the chance that the child would grow, and grow into the kind of woman who looked at a thing twice, who could not let a wrong line in a file alone, who would one day turn the worn place on a silver disc beneath her thumb and hear, under the nonsense, her mother's hand reaching forward through twenty years to put a sword in hers.

She would not know if she had judged the child right. No mother did. She only had to judge her this once, and have no way ever to learn the answer, and do it anyway. That was motherhood, she thought, stripped of everything but its bones. You loaded the future onto something that could not yet hold its own head up, and you let it go.

She sang to her. Very low, her lips at the small warm ear, she sang the whole of it through, all nine lines, in the half-light, while downstairs at the gate a bolt slid and the watchman did not call out, which meant the watchman had been spoken to as well.

She heard them on the stairs. Not the noise of men breaking in; the worse, quieter noise of men who did not need to hurry. A scuff, a pause, the particular soft tread of people who had done this before and would do it again on some other staircase next week. Two, she thought. Perhaps three. The bolt of her own door was nothing to them; she had known that always, had kept it more for the ritual than the resistance.

She did not scream. A scream was a thing you spent when it could buy you something, and it could buy her nothing here but a neighbour at a window, a witness who would be edited out by morning, one more grief in a colony of them. She crossed instead to the inner room, the child against her, and laid her in the deep drawer of the steel almirah, on the folded saris, the way you laid a child where a thing falling would not fall on her. She did it without haste. The child watched her, fists beside her head, and did not cry. Good girl, she thought, in the language she had never been able to teach her and the child had somehow already learned. Quiet girl. Watch everything.

The lock gave. The door came open against the chain, and the chain against the screws, and the screws against the soft old plaster, all of it in one short sound that was almost gentle.

There were two in the front room and the third came past them and she saw at once that the third was the one who mattered, because the other two looked at her and he did not. He looked past her, at the inner door, at the room beyond it, with the mild, sorting attention of a man taking an inventory. He was not young. He had a stillness she recognised because she had spent her career describing it in other men, in cables, in the careful nouns of assessment. The other two were instruments. He was the hand that held them, and he had not come for her at all.

He had come for what was in the drawer.

Something in her that had been an analyst for fifteen years insisted, even now, on understanding. She had thought of them as one thing, the reach and its servants, all of a piece. They were not. The two by the door were Sehgal's, or Sehgal's master's, sent to close a file written in flesh. This one was something else, drawn to the same room by a different appetite, and the knowledge arrived too late to be of any use except the small terrible use of telling her that her death and her daughter's taking were not even the same crime, only two crimes that had agreed to share a morning.

"Where is the file," one of the instruments said. Not the child. They did not know about the child, or did not care; the child was not their tasking. He said it without heat, the way you asked the time.

"Burned," she said. It was true and it would buy her daughter a few seconds and it was the last useful sentence of her life, so she made it good. "Three weeks ago. There is nothing in this house but me."

He believed her or did not; it made no difference to his hands. He came around the cot and she felt the forearm before she understood it, across the throat, the weight of a man who did this with the economy of a trade, and she did not fight it the way the body wanted to, with the nails and the heels, because that bought nothing and cost the seconds. She let her hands stay open. She had read, somewhere, in some grey report on some grey afternoon, that it was quick, that the dark came at the edges first and closed like a shutter, and the report had been honest. The edges went. The room narrowed to the inner door and the drawer beyond it she could not see and did not need to.

She did not think of the man's arm or the ceiling or the smell that had begun, sharp and chemical, kerosene or spirit, one of the instruments wetting the curtains for the fire that would write the official sentence under all of this. She thought of the song. She held it in the front of her mind the way you held a hand, all nine lines at once, and she pushed it, with the last of whatever she was, across the room and into the drawer and into the child, an act with no mechanism, a prayer in the body of a woman who had never believed in prayer, carry it, carry it, you are the only paper they cannot reach.

The shutter closed.

The still man waited until the arm came away and the woman on the floor was a thing and not a person, waited the extra moment that the other two never thought to wait, the moment that was the whole difference between his trade and theirs. Then he went into the inner room, past the almirah, and stopped, and after a breath he opened the deep drawer.

The child looked up at him out of her mother's eyes. She did not cry. There was a disc of silver on a thread against her breastbone, dull, worn at one edge as though a thumb had already loved it for years, and the man's gaze passed over it and registered it as a grandmother's trinket, beneath notice, a thing too ordinary to take from a child. He lifted her out, settling her against his shoulder with a competence that should not have been in those hands, and behind him a curtain caught and went up with a sound like a sigh, and the grey window filled with light that was not the dawn.

He carried her down the stairs as the smoke began, out past the watchman who saw nothing, into the cold of the lane and the long road that began there, and the silver disc rode against the child's skin, warm from her mother, keeping its single sentence, going where it was carried.
