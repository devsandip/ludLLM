The road gave her up grudgingly. The shared Sumo took the switchbacks below the monastery in second gear, brakes smelling, the driver crossing himself at each shrine cut into the rock, and Sita sat against the window with her bag between her boots and watched the deodars thin into terraced barley and the barley into the first tin roofs of the valley. She had spent four days in a place with no signal and one cold tap, and her body had adjusted to the silence the way it adjusted to everything, by treating it as a condition to be survived. Now the world came back in increments. A cell mast on a ridge. A hoarding for a telecom plan. The driver's phone in its cradle, lit, talking to towers she could name.

She turned her own phone over in her hand without switching it on. It was the clean one, bought for cash in a lane off Paharganj from a boy who had photographed a stranger's Aadhaar to satisfy the KYC and pocketed the difference. Clean was a word that meant less every year. The moment she powered it on it would shake hands with the nearest tower and lay down a point on a map, and the map was not hers. She put it back in the inner pocket and left it dark.

Kabir had given her nothing she could carry out in writing. He had given her a method, and a method weighed nothing and could not be seized at a checkpoint, which she supposed was the point of him. *Watch where the hunt refuses to look.* He had said it the way he said most things, as if reporting the weather to a man who would shortly be standing in it. A real hunt is hungry. It goes everywhere, turns over everything, because it does not yet know where the answer is. A steered hunt has manners. It has a list of places it will not go, and the list is the confession.

She had laughed, in the way you laugh at a thing that has already begun to frighten you, and asked him how you read a list of absences. You cannot photograph a hole. He had looked at her for a while and said that you could not, but you could walk the edge of it until your foot found the place where the ground stopped. And then, because he never gave a thing without its price, he had added the warning she kept hearing now in his flat dry voice: when you find the edge, do not fill the hole with the first thing that fits. The first thing that fits is the thing someone wanted you to find.

So she would walk the edge. And she would be slow about what she let herself believe she had found there.

---

The town at the bottom of the valley had a lodge above a tyre shop, forty rupees more for hot water that did not come. She took the room at the back, paid cash, gave the register a name that was not hers and a number that rang a dead SIM, and noted without comment the CCTV dome over the entrance, its cable looped and zip-tied to the drainpipe, the lens filmed with two seasons of dust. It would record her as a shape. Facial recognition wanted a frontal capture in good light; she had given the dome the top of a cap and the line of a jaw, and that was all the gift it would get.

She bolted the door, set her bag against it, and sat on the floor with her back to the bed and a sheet of lined paper torn from a child's exercise book she had bought downstairs for this purpose. Pen, not pencil. She wanted to feel the commitment of ink. Everything she was about to do, she would burn in the squat toilet down the corridor before she slept, and flush the ash, because paper was the one medium the men hunting her could not reach into from a distance. They could own her phone. They could own the towers and the cameras and the face she wore. They could not own a thing she had not let touch the network.

She wrote the case down the way Baba had taught her to write anything, which was the first irony and she let it sit there unexamined for the moment. A column of events, each with a date, each stripped of feeling. She had eleven years of training that made the hand steady while it set down the facts that were unmaking her.

Karol Bagh, she wrote. The transfer meet. The watcher she had clocked at the cycle stand and filed, in her report to Baba, as customs or low police. She had been wrong. She had been wrong with great confidence, and the confidence had been a thing put into her, she understood now, the way a watchmaker puts a spring into a case so the hands will move and the owner of the watch will never once think about the mechanism turning under the glass.

The second too-close encounter. The third. The counter-surveillance run on which she had finally understood, with the floor going out from under her, that she was not protecting a location or a transaction. She was the target. Not Alpha as an idea. Sita as a body, with a face and a gait and a set of habits, hunted by something with the budget and the patience of an institution.

Then Sehgal. The clean office, the thirty years of impeccable service worn like a good coat. *The hunt for Alpha is owned. It is Kaul's. It is sanctioned.* The offer that had moved her, she could admit, more than she had let him see: a name behind her work, an order she could point to, accountability, the thing she had never had in eleven years of being pointed at men in hotel rooms. He had given her a task. Trace Alpha's money. The one thing, he said, that Baba could not hide.

She underlined the task once and then sat looking at it.

This was the edge of the hole. She could feel her foot near it.

Trace Alpha's money. A reasonable instruction. A trained instinct rose to obey it even now; she let the instinct stand and walked around it. What had she been told to look at? The money flowing out of Baba's hand. The hawala rails, the trade-based washing through Bangkok and Dubai, the fake notes coming down the Crescent line into Punjab. All of it pointed outward, downstream, into the wreckage Baba made. She had been given a long, bright corridor to run down and the corridor went away from the building she stood in.

And what had no one asked her to look at?

She wrote it small, because it frightened her to give it size.

*How they found me.*

The hunt had reached her with a precision that customs did not have and low police did not have and that no foreign service operating blind on Indian soil could have had. To hunt Alpha that closely you had to know Alpha. You had to know the tradecraft, the fallbacks, the cadence of the meets, the way Baba spaced his contacts and salted his transfers. You had to know it from the inside, the way only the man who built it knew it, or the men he had once shown it to. The hunt had Baba's grammar. It read his sentences before he finished them. Sita had spent four years thinking of herself as the most dangerous thing in any room she entered, and the thing hunting her moved through the world the way she did, and that was not a coincidence she could file under customs.

So the hunt knew Alpha intimately. And the only place that knowledge lived was the home service. Which the state knew, of course. The state had built the hunt. Kaul owned it. That was not the hole.

She made herself slow down. Kabir's voice, dry as the high air: *don't fill the hole with the first thing that fits. Stand at the edge. Tell me what the ground is on the other side.*

The other side of the hole was a thing she had carried out of Baba's own mouth, years ago, in a safehouse with the kettle ticking. He had given her his justification the way other men gave gifts, as if he were handing her something precious and a little heavy. Every gram of poison, he had said, was a coin minted for the survival of the country. He was bleeding the drug trade not to enrich himself but to starve something. A network. Two decades of patient burial, men placed inside the strategic establishment, the missile commands, the cyber rooms, the places where a country keeps the things that let it refuse to die. Sleepers, financed through exactly the rails he had spent his life inside, which was why he could touch them, why he could squeeze the artery and feel the thing at the far end gasp. He had recently taken the Crescent pipeline whole. He had told her it scaled his war chest and starved the enemy at the same hour, the pre-activation hour, the worst possible hour for them. He had said it as a thing accomplished, his voice flat with the particular calm she now knew to mistrust, because the same flatness had been set into her own chest somewhere she could not reach to pry it out.

She had believed that story when it was a story about a great patriotic war and her place in it as the blade. She set it down now beside the case on the floor and looked at the two things together, the network Baba was trying to kill and the hunt that was trying to kill Baba, and she felt the floor begin, very slowly, to tilt.

If the network was real. If men had spent twenty years embedding themselves in the body of the service. Then those men, at the moment Baba's hand closed on the pipeline, at the moment the money stopped reaching them, would do the one thing any organism does when a hand closes on its throat. They would reach for the hand. And they would reach for it with the tools available to them, which were not foreign tools, not for men who had spent two decades becoming the building. Their tools would be the building's tools. A sanctioned hunt. An owner who could answer for it. A task force given a true and terrible target, Alpha, the rogue assassin unit funded by narcotics, the thing the state would disown the instant it was named.

The hunt was not lying about Alpha. That was the cleverness of it, and she sat very still as the cleverness opened in front of her like a drop. Everything the state believed about Alpha was true. It was off the books. It was funded by the poison. It had no owner who would stand up for it. It was exactly the monster they said it was, and so the hunt to destroy it needed no fabrication, no forged warrant, no whisper that did not survive daylight. You did not have to invent a reason to kill Alpha. You only had to make sure the hunt for the monster never turned its head and asked the one question that would have unmade everything.

Who benefits when Alpha dies.

Not the country. The country lost its only blade against the sleepers the day Alpha fell. The men who benefited when Alpha died were the men Alpha was bleeding. And those men were inside.

She put the pen down because her hand had stopped being reliable.

This was the hole, or near enough that she could feel the ground give at the lip of it. The hunt did not turn toward the home service's own money, the clean and trusted men who sat over the rails through which two decades of sleeper financing would have had to pass. It ran the other way, outward, at Baba's wreckage. The rot the state feared was a rogue handler beyond its walls. The rot she was beginning to read was a hand inside the walls, steering the hunt away from itself with a target so true that no one would ever think to check whether the hunter wanted the prey caught for the reasons he gave.

And then, almost before she had finished the thought, her mind handed her a man.

The man who had given her the corridor. The man with thirty unimpeachable years and the portfolio for clandestine financing, who had set her running outward and made it feel like accountability, who sat precisely where the money would have moved. The fit was so clean that for a moment she let herself rest in it, the way an exhausted swimmer lets a current carry her, and then she heard Kabir again and her whole body went cold.

*The first thing that fits is the thing someone wanted you to find.*

She made herself take her foot back from the edge. She did not know that it was him. She knew that he fit, and the fit itself was the thing to distrust, because a man who had built a hunt to hide inside would have built it knowing that a clever asset, run far enough, might one day turn and look back up the corridor. If you were going to be looked at, you arranged to be the obvious answer at exactly the moment the asset became too dangerous to keep, so that when she came back up the line she would find a door already ajar and walk through it certain she had broken it open herself. The neatness was not proof. It might be the next room of the trap.

Or he was clean, an honest servant of thirty years being moved like a chess piece by someone he trusted, his name and his portfolio borrowed to give the lie its weight. Or the hand was somewhere else along the rails entirely, some quieter man she had never been allowed to meet, and Sehgal was only the face the building showed her. She did not have the ground to choose between these. She had a contour. A hand inside, somewhere over the money, steering a true hunt at a true monster so that no one would count who was left standing when the monster fell.

She did not write his name. She found she could not, as if writing it would let it out of the room and let it close around her in turn. She wrote, instead, three words and underlined them once: *a hand inside.* And she left the rest as dark, and was careful, this once, not to read shapes into the dark.

---

She got up after a while because the cold of the concrete had reached her hips and stood at the window with the curtain held an inch off the frame, watching the lane. A dog crossed it. A man pushed a handcart of cylinders. The tower on the ridge blinked. Nothing watched the lodge that she could see, which meant nothing.

She had come up the mountain carrying two stories about Baba and she understood now that she would have to carry them both back down, because neither would close. Mira's story was clean and it had moved her because it answered the oldest ache she had. Fateh stole you. Fateh murdered the mother and built the rescue and shaped the orphan into the knife. Your origin is a lie. Sita had checked that story against the documents and against her own bones and found it true, or true enough that she had let it carry her this far, into Mira's chain, away from the only home she had ever owned.

But Mira's story had a shape that troubled her now, and Kabir had given her the eye to see the shape. It was too finished. A man who stole a child and made her a weapon for his own enrichment did not also spend forty years bleeding himself white to starve a network that paid him nothing. A flat villain did not choose the smaller atrocity. A flat villain did not lie awake, as she had seen Baba lie awake, with the arithmetic of the dead behind his eyes. Mira believed her own story; Sita did not doubt that, had never doubted it, the woman was not a liar. But a person could hold a true thing and mistake it for the whole thing, could pick up the corner of the cloth and call it the cloth. Baba had stolen her. That was true. And Baba had also been, for forty years, the only man with his hand on the throat of the thing now reaching back to kill them both. The state called him a rogue. Mira called him a thief. And the thing she could not get either of them to account for was that the men who most wanted him dead were the men he was trying to destroy, which meant the two stories agreed on a verdict while pointing at the wrong defendant.

He was not what either of them claimed. She did not yet know what he was. She suspected, standing at the window with the curtain biting into her fingers, that she had never known, that the man who had made her had kept the truest part of himself out of her reach the way he kept everything important out of reach, in places the network could not touch.

Her hand had gone, without her sending it, to the small weight that lay against her breastbone, the thing she had worn so long she only noticed it when she stopped to notice anything at all. She had had it before she had words. It had survived the fire that had not been a fire, survived eleven years of work and four years of war, survived the cheap clasps she had replaced it on twice. She turned it once between two fingers, an old motion, the motion of a woman steadying herself on a familiar stone, and let it fall back against her skin without looking at it. It told her nothing. It never had. It was only the one thing in the world that had always been hers, and she held it the way the orphaned hold anything that was theirs before they could be made to doubt it.

She let the curtain fall.

The discipline came back to her then, cool and welcome, the part of her that did not feel because feeling was a luxury of people with owners who answered for them. She had a shape now. Not proof. Kabir had warned her there would be no proof, that the hole did not photograph, that she would have to act on the contour of an absence and live with the chance that she had read the dark wrong. She had a hand inside the state, somewhere over the money, a name she had refused to certify and refused to clear. She had a man who had given her a true target to keep her eyes off something. And she had Baba, somewhere below the snow line, believing he ran her still, his trigger seated in her like a round in a chamber, eight syllables and the dark and her own hands going where his voice sent them.

She gathered the sheet of lined paper, the column of dates, the small frightened word *how they found me*, and the three words she had let herself write and the one name she had not, and she took it all down the corridor to the squat toilet with the broken cistern. She tore it into pieces no bigger than a thumbnail, the training making her hands thorough even now, and she burned them over the pan with the cheap lighter, one fistful at a time, and watched the ink go brown and then black and then nothing. She flushed the ash with a bucket. She washed her hands in the cold tap that the forty extra rupees had not warmed, and she looked at the woman in the spotted mirror without warmth and without quite enough recognition, and she thought: I will go to Mira, and I will tell her about the hunt, and I will not tell her the half of it I have just buried in a drain in the hills, because Mira is steering too, in a direction I have not yet found the edge of.

She turned off the light. In the dark she could hear the tower on the ridge doing nothing, and her own breath, and the small habitual nothing of the weight against her chest, keeping a secret it had kept her whole life, waiting, as it had always been waiting, for her to learn how to read it.
