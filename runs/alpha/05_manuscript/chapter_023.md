The room smelled of raw cotton and cold tea, and the munshi had been laying out the parchis for two hours by the time Sita understood that she had been reading them wrong her whole life.

He worked the way old men work, without hurry, licking a thumb to part the flimsy slips, building them into towers by code-word and not by date, so that the table between them looked less like an account and more like a game whose rules only he remembered. He was sixty, perhaps more, a Marwari who had washed money for three principals before her and would wash it for three after, and he did not know her name. He knew only that she now stood where Bedi had stood, that the Crescent flow answered to her, and that a woman who could end a man's heart without a mark could certainly end his. So he showed her everything, which was its own kind of lie, because a man who shows you everything is counting on your not knowing what to look at.

She had come down out of the mountains with one instruction from Kabir and no comfort. Watch where the hunt refuses to look. She had watched, and she had seen the thing she reported to no one: the service chasing Alpha outward, across the Northeast, into Moreh and the Mizoram crossings and onward to Bangkok, while it kept its eyes carefully shut to the rails inside the country. A hunt that ran from the trouble. She had thought, when she first saw it, that the blind spot was the answer. She had been wrong about that too. The blind spot was not the answer. It was a curtain. The answer was behind it, and to reach the answer you had to do the harder thing, which was to stop looking where you were steered and map, slip by slip, what the steering was built to hide.

So she stopped asking the munshi where the money went. She began asking him where it stopped.

"This one," she said, and touched a slip near the base of one of his towers. A code-word she did not recognize, a figure that was almost insultingly small. "And this." Another, the same word, three months older by the colour of the ink. "These two settle against nothing."

The munshi's thumb hovered. For the first time in two hours he was not entirely relaxed. "They settle," he said. "Over the year they settle."

"Show me against what."

He could not, and they both knew it within a minute, because there was no counter-entry, no trade invoice over-billed or under-billed, no gold, no answering balance in Dubai or in the cloth markets of Bangkok that the rest of the book leaned on like a man leaning on a wall. The drug money behaved as drug money was supposed to behave. It came in dirty and lumpy and frightened, and it pushed and shoved to become clean, to become a flat in Gurgaon or a consignment of Surat polyester or a line in a jeweller's stock, anything that would let it disappear into the daylight economy and stop being itself. That money wanted out. She had moved oceans of it; she knew its panic the way she knew her own pulse.

This other money did not want out. It came in small and regular and unafraid, the same modest figure to the same dead code-words, quarter after quarter, year upon year, and it did not run for the daylight. It landed somewhere and stayed. It behaved, she thought, the way a salary behaves. The way a retainer behaves. It was not laundering at all. It was payroll.

She sat back. Outside, below the shuttered window, a generator coughed and caught, and the single bulb over the table steadied. She let the munshi pour her more of the cold tea so that her hands would have something to do that was not picking up the slips again.

Payroll wants people. You did not pay a flat or a consignment quarter on quarter for years; you paid a man. You paid a man to be where you needed him, to keep his clearance current, to make the call you would one day ask him to make, and you paid him small precisely so that he never felt bought, only kept. And you paid him through a hawala channel that you had stapled to a drug pipeline, because a drug pipeline is loud, and the loudness covers you, and any investigator who picks up the thread will be so dazzled by the heroin and the fake five-hundreds coming down through Punjab that he will never stop to count the quiet little slips that settle against nothing.

She thought: that is what the curtain hides. Not theft. People. And not three or four people. She made the munshi walk her back through the dead code-words, every one he could find, and there were more than a man could carry in his head, spread thin across years, some of them so old the ink had gone the brown of dried blood. Two decades of them, near enough. Twenty years of small unafraid money turning up quarter after quarter and staying, finding the same kinds of soil again and again, the kind of soil where a clearance mattered, where a posting mattered, where a single man on a single night could open a door that should never open. A signals desk. A command linked to the things that flew and the things that waited in silos. The rooms where the country kept the keys to its own throat.

She had been told, once, by Baba, in the flat smooth voice he used for the things he most needed her to believe, that India had a wound in it that had been kept open for twenty years by a patient enemy, and that the heroin was the price of cauterizing it. She had taken it then as the kind of grand justification men reach for when they want their cruelty to wear a flag. Ravana, he had said once, almost fondly, the protector who poisons his own to save them, and she had thought it self-flattery. She did not think it self-flattery now. She was looking at the wound. It was in the munshi's towers, slip by patient slip, and it was nearly grown.

An enemy, then, grown inside the house the way you plant a hedge against a wall so that one season it owns the wall. She did not need the name of whoever paid to know the thing was real. She did not even need to know where the paymaster sat, in what city, behind what flag; the books did not lie about the shape of a thing, only about its purpose, and the shape was unmistakable. Somebody had built a second service inside the first, paid for out of the same purse that paid for the poison, and the official hunt had been pointed away from it with great care. An army quartered in the house, two decades in the building, and the men who lived there in uniform did not know they were sharing their rooms.

That was the second thing, and it arrived quietly, without drama, the way the worst things do. The hunt was not blind. A blind hunt would have stumbled into one of these channels by accident in twenty years; chance alone would have tripped it. This hunt had never tripped. Someone was keeping it on its leash and walking it past the open door every single day, smiling, and the only way to keep a hunt that disciplined for that long was to own it from inside. The service was being driven. Not penetrated at the edges, the way every service always was. Driven, from a chair near the top, by a hand that decided each morning where the dogs would not be allowed to sniff.

She had circled this and flinched from it before. In the mountains she had let herself name a man and then unname him, because the fit was too clean: Sehgal, who had given her a desk and an owner and a name to hide behind, the Additional Secretary over clandestine financing and operational tasking, thirty unblemished years, the building's idea of a safe pair of hands. She had decided he was too obviously the answer to be the answer, and congratulated herself on the discipline, and she saw now that the congratulation had been the trap and not the suspicion. Because she had not, then, had the money. She had only had the man, handed to her, easy to mistrust precisely because he was handed to her. Now she had the money, which she had dug out herself, slip by slip, from a book he did not control and a munshi who had never heard his name.

She made herself do it properly, the way Kabir would have made her. She asked: where would a man have to sit to keep this hunt walking past this door for twenty years. Not to commit the crime. To keep it invisible. He would have to sit over the financing, so that the channels never raised a flag where flags were raised. He would have to sit over the tasking, so that the operatives who might have wandered into the quiet money were always sent somewhere loud instead. He would have to be the man who decided what the hunt was for, and the man who decided what it would never be allowed to find, and there was, in the whole architecture she had been given, exactly one chair that was both. It was the chair of the man who had pulled hers out for her and asked her, gently, with his good and tired and thirty-year-honest face, to trace Alpha's money, knowing she would trace it outward, into the Triangle, away from the door.

The name came up out of her like a stone out of cold water, and it did not feel like discovery. It felt like recognition. Sehgal. And in the same breath, before she could even decide whether to believe her own arithmetic, the deadline came up beside it, because she had been staring at the forward column of the munshi's book without seeing it, and now she saw it.

The quiet money had stopped.

Not all of it. The old retainers ran on. But the channel that fed them, the dirty loud purse the whole thing hung from, the Crescent flow that came down through Punjab in heroin and counterfeit notes, that purse had a hole in it now, and she knew exactly where the hole was, because she had cut it. She had taken that pipeline. She had stood over the seizure herself, Baba's instrument, and inherited its washing, and felt clever about the war chest she was building, and never once asked what she was building it out of. She had not enlarged Baba's treasury. She had drained the network's. The same act. He had let her think it was greed because greed was simpler to run than grief.

She found the date the way you find the floor of a well, by dropping the figures and listening for the splash. The retainers were committed forward, quarter on quarter, obligations a man cannot quietly suspend without his hedge of men understanding they have been abandoned and beginning, each alone, to wonder whether to run or to talk. The dirty purse that met those obligations was dry as of the last reconciliation. She walked the balance forward against the commitments and watched the book go under, and it went under, by her arithmetic, in something under three weeks. The next settlement after the dark of the month. Call it the fourteenth. On the fourteenth the network would open its book and find it could no longer pay the men it had spent twenty years keeping, and a thing that can no longer be kept must either be reclaimed or used.

That was the lure. She understood the whole shape of it now, and the understanding was so total that she had to set the tea down because her hand was not steady. Baba had not seized the pipeline for money. He had seized it to starve them at the one moment starvation could not be survived, the moment just before they were ready, so that they would have to come for the pipeline to keep their treasury, or activate their people before the treasury died, and either way they would have to move, and move all at once, and stop being invisible for one night. He had spent the poison of a generation to buy a single night when the enemy would be standing in the open. The drug trade, every gram of it, had been a coin minted for this. He had told her so to her face and she had heard a man excusing himself, and it had been, all along, an order of battle.

And the hand inside the service knew it too. That was why the hunt was pointed at Alpha and at her, away from the door, with such patience. The hand was not only hiding the network. It was racing Baba. Whoever sat in that chair had three weeks, the same three weeks, to roll up Alpha, to take Baba's operation off the board and reclaim or refloat the pipeline, before Baba's trap closed on the night the network had to move. Two clocks, wound to the same hour, and she was the figure they were both reaching for, because she now held the pipeline. She was not the bait any longer in the way Baba had meant it. She was the prize. Whoever reached her first won the war.

She thought of Mira, who would be back in the safe flat by now, who had given her a true thing and believed it was the whole thing: that Baba had stolen her and built her and lied to her about a fire and a dead mother. All of it true. None of it the war. Mira's truth had a man for a villain and stopped there, the way a torch lights one face in a crowd and leaves you certain the rest of the room is empty. If Sita carried the name and the date back into that flat and laid them on the table, Mira would do with them the honest, ruinous thing: take them up the chain, to people Sita could no longer place on the board, into a service that was being driven from inside by the very man the name belonged to. The name would walk straight back to Sehgal's desk. She had not seen what happened to the last analyst, whoever they had been, who carried a true thing into that building. She did not need to have seen it. The building was the kind of building where you did not need to see it to know.

So she would carry nothing to Mira. She had decided already, in the part of her that decided faster than she could watch, the same part that answered Baba's voice with murder. She would tell Mira she had traced the money to the Triangle and lost it in Bangkok, which was where the hunt wanted everyone to lose it, and she would let her own handler walk her past the door like all the others, and she would hold the name and the date in her two hands like a live round she had no clean barrel to fire from.

Because there was no clean barrel. That was the last thing, and it was the worst. You could not arrest an Additional Secretary on a munshi's parchis and a woman's arithmetic. You could not put Sehgal in a room and ask him questions, because the room belonged to him; he owned the men who would hold the door. And the thing that had to be done by the fourteenth, the rolling up of an army hidden inside your own house, on a night that would leave bodies on neutral soil and deniable capability bare to the world, was a thing the state could not own. No minute, no file, no Chief's signature, not even Kaul's, would ever stand over it. If it was done, it would be done by men the state would disown before the bodies were cold, out of a purse made of heroin, on the order of a man with no paper. It could only be done by Alpha. It could only be done by Baba.

She sat in the cotton smell with the bulb steadying and unsteadying over the dead men's slips, and her hand went up, without her sending it, to the small worn shape that had hung against her breastbone since before she had memory, the one thing of her own she had never been given by him, and she held it the way a child holds a thing in the dark. The only person who could stop the war was the one who had made her out of a lie and would speak four words and watch her body obey. To save the country he had bled himself white to save, she would have to walk back into the reach of the only hand in the world that could still close around her throat without touching her.

She did not move for a long time. Then she gathered the forward column of the munshi's book in her eye, the date where it all went under, and she fixed it the way she fixed a face she meant to find again, and she stood.

"Burn these two words out of the book," she told the old man, naming the dead code-words, "and forget you ever settled them against nothing."

He looked up at her then, slow and level, the way a man looks at a sky that has changed while he was working. "And if someone comes asking why the book is short?"

"Tell them," she said, already at the door, "that the woman who owns the pipeline reads slowly, and finished late."

She went down the dark stair into the cotton lanes, counting, against her will, the days to the fourteenth.
