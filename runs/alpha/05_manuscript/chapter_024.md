The flat sat above a shuttered tyre showroom in Karol Bagh, a third-floor address that paid its rent in cash and asked nothing of anyone. Sita let herself in with the key she had carried eleven years and stood a moment in the dark, reading the room the way she had been taught to read every room before she trusted her own weight to its floor. The chair turned a few degrees from the window. One glass upturned on the draining board. And under the closed smell of an unaired flat, the smell of the cigarettes he was not supposed to smoke any more.

He was home. He had been waiting.

"You took the long way." Baba spoke from the chair by the window without turning on the light. Below them a generator coughed and caught, and the showroom's dead neon stuttered once and gave up. "Seven days. I allowed you four."

"I had reading to do."

"The munshi's books."

She did not answer. She crossed to the second chair and sat, keeping the width of the table between them, keeping her own hands inside her own sight. On the train down she had rehearsed the order of this, what she would say and in what sequence, and now that she was in the chair the order was worth nothing, the way a plan is worth nothing the instant the door comes off its hinges. So she watched his face instead. Forty years of a face she had read more fluently than her own, and she found, sitting across from it, that she no longer knew the grammar of it.

"You knew I'd go to the munshi," she said. "You left the thread where I'd put my hand on it."

"I left a great many threads, beta. You always pulled the one that carried the load." He leaned forward and the streetlight took him: an old man's jaw gone soft, the eyes behind it not soft at all. "You came the whole way down to ask me something. Don't sit there marking my paper. Ask."

So she asked the smallest question, because the small ones cannot be turned aside.

"Who is Alpha for?"

He let the silence run. A scooter went by below, and a dog took it as a personal insult, and the noise folded back into the larger noise of a city that did not sleep so much as lower its voice.

"You think I raised a unit to kill launderers," he said. "Bedi. The men in Dubai. The hawala brokers sweating in their Bangkok offices. You think eleven years of your life went to tidying the account books of the drug trade." He shook his head, slow. "They were never the target. They were the price of the chair. You do not get to sit at that table unless you have money on it, and I needed to sit at that table for one reason. To see who else was sitting there."

"And who was sitting there."

"Patience." The word came gentle, almost fond, and she felt the old reflex stir in her, the want to be still and to be told, and she despised it, and let none of it reach her face. "In 2006 a man I trusted brought me a junior analyst's work. Suppressed. The analyst dead inside the month, ruled a foreign hand. The paper said a principal across the border had begun something patient. Not couriers, not bombers. Those we know how to bleed. This was a different kind of animal. They were buying people. Our people. Clean ones, gifted ones, recruited young and watered for twenty years, the way you plant a mango orchard you don't expect to eat from in your own lifetime."

She held still. "Planted where."

"Where it decides wars. Strategic Forces. The missile programme. The cyber directorate. Command and control." He said it the way a man reads a storm warning for a coast he has already cleared. "Not assets who photograph documents in a stairwell. Assets who rise to become the men who sign the documents. By the time they wake, they are not spies inside the system. They are the system. You cannot purge what you cannot find, and you cannot find it, because the men who would run the search are the men who were bought."

The room had gone cold to her, or she had. She thought of the dead code-words in the munshi's ledger, the twenty years of disbursements dressed as freight and commission and pilgrimage allowance, the sheer patience of the thing, and understood for the first time that she had spent a week reading the household accounts of a standing army.

"You took it to Luthra," she said. "The Chief."

"I took it to the one man above me I would have died for." A muscle shifted at the corner of his mouth and stilled. "He confirmed every line. And then he buried it. With his own hands. Not because he was bought, beta, understand that. He was the cleanest man I ever served under. He buried it because to surface it was to blow three running operations that brushed against it, and to tell the nation its missile crews might answer to Rawalpindi, and he judged that the panic would do more harm than the disease. Reasons of state. He sat me down in his garden and told me the country could outlive a hidden cancer longer than it could survive the surgery, and he made me believe it, because he believed it himself." Baba opened his hands on the table, empty. "That was the day the sanctioned road ended. Not the day they refused me a unit. The day the best man in the service looked at the proof and chose the silence. After that there was no door left to knock on that didn't open onto the same shut room."

She made herself say it flat. "So you went outside the lines."

"I went where the lines don't reach." He took up the cigarette packet, turned it over twice, did not open it. "If the state cannot act, and the men inside the state are the disease, then what is left is one old man with no paper, no badge, no owner who can ever be made to answer. Alpha has never existed. There is no file. If they take you tomorrow, the Republic will look you in the eye and swear it has never heard your name, and it will be telling the truth, because I built the thing so that the truth would protect them." He looked at her. "And so it would protect you."

She did not let that one land. She had come prepared for the man Mira had sketched her: the thief who stole infants and dressed the theft up as a war. She had come to use him and have done with him. The man across the table was not arguing with the sketch. He was not even disputing it. He was simply standing next to it, vastly larger than it, so that the smallness of the drawing was the thing that unsettled her now.

"The drugs," she said. "Don't dress them either. I've stood in the wards in Punjab. I've seen what your coins buy."

"I have seen them too." Something crossed his face that she had no name for, and was gone. "I have counted them. Every gram that moves through a pipeline I hold is a gram that poisons a boy in Tarn Taran so that I can buy the rope to hang the men who would sell the whole country wholesale. I do not pretend it is clean. I do not tell myself the boy does not matter. I tell myself he matters less than the catastrophe, and then I carry him. I carry all of them." He held her eyes. "You want me to be a monster, because a monster is a thing you can walk away from with a clear back. I will not make it that easy. The trade is an atrocity. It is the smaller one. I chose it with my eyes open, every morning, for forty years, and I will answer for it to whatever is waiting for men like me. But I will not let you file it under greed. Greed would have been gentler to that boy. Greed would have left the orchard standing and taken a cut."

Her hand had risen to her throat, to the thin chain she had worn since before she had language for the world, the worn pendant warm under her collar. She did not know why. She made the hand come back to the table.

"The pipeline," she said. "The Crescent line you took. The fake notes, the heroin, the whole rail up through Punjab. Why now. Forty years of not being greedy and then you reach out and take the biggest prize on the board. That isn't patience. That's a man grabbing."

And for the first time Baba smiled, and it was terrible, because it was the smile of a man who had waited a long while for exactly this question out of exactly this mouth.

"Because it is theirs," he said. "The Crescent rail is not a drug line that happens to fund the network. It is the network's treasury. Every disbursement in the munshi's books, every payment to every sleeper for twenty years, every retainer that kept a colonel's loyalty pointed at Rawalpindi instead of Delhi, all of it ran on that rail. I did not steal a fortune. I cut an artery." He leaned in, and the streetlight dug trenches into his face. "They have an orchard twenty years grown and no water left to give it. Sleepers are expensive, beta. They have mortgages and mistresses and habits and sons at foreign universities, and they have every one of those things because the rail paid for it, and three weeks ago the rail stopped paying. So the principal has a choice now. He can let the orchard die on the branch, twenty years and a king's ransom wasted. Or he can move before he loses it: switch on what he has while he still has it, or come to reclaim the rail and turn the water back on."

She saw it then, the way you suddenly see which wall a whole house is standing on after years of living inside it blind.

"You're not starving them," she said slowly. "You're flushing them."

"I am making them move." His voice had dropped almost to nothing. "A sleeper is invisible because he sleeps. The whole genius of the design is that it costs nothing to wait and everything to act, and so they wait. I have made the waiting cost everything. They have to come to the rail. The man who comes to reclaim a treasury is no longer a line in a ledger. He is a man, in a room, on a night that I have chosen. They will converge on the one thing they cannot survive without, and when they converge they will be, for one hour, in one place, findable." He sat back. "Twenty years they were a list no one could read. For a single night they will be a room I can walk into."

"And then?"

"And then it ends. One night. One sweep. Off every book that has ever been kept. The state cannot put its hand to it, because it means killing on soil that is not ours, with a capability that does not exist, against men who in law have not yet done a thing, and there will be people in that room who should not be there, there always are, and no government on earth could put its name to that." He spread his hands. "Which is why it has to be a unit the country has never heard of. Which is why it has to be me. There is no one left above the disease to give the order, so the order has to give itself."

She thought, unwillingly, of a cool office and a man with thirty clean years behind him offering her accountability with his own name on the order, and she felt the deduction rise to meet the memory the way a fish rises to a fly, and she made herself not strike. The fit was too good. A man that placed, that plausible, surfacing at exactly the hour she needed a face to hang the steered hunt on, that was either the truth or the most elegant lure on the board, and Kabir had spent a week teaching her never to take the shape that was left lying out in the open for her hand. So she put the face down, unnamed, and kept only the structure, which was the one thing she could trust: the hunt that would not look at its own money was not blind. It was a dog turned outward, guarding the thing behind it.

"There's a hand inside," she said. "Whoever he is. He knows what your sweep is for, and that's why the hunt is steered onto you and away from the rail. He isn't shielding the state from Alpha. He's shielding them from Alpha, and spending the state to do it."

Something moved in Baba's eyes, and she could not tell whether it was pride or grief. "Now you are reading the right book," he said softly. "Yes. There is a hand inside that turns the dogs away from its own treasury and points them at me. I have known there was such a hand for two years. I still cannot put a face to it I would trust, and you are right past my teaching not to reach for the one they have set out for you to reach for. What you carried me out of the munshi's freight columns was not a name. It was the orchard. The whole size of it, twenty years and every branch counted. I had the shape of the thing. You brought me its scale." His voice thickened by a degree and steadied. "That was yours. Not the building's. Yours."

She let the praise run through her without closing a hand on it, the way Kabir had shown her, the way water runs through a fist. It cost her all the same. It always cost her, with him.

"How long," she said. "Until they converge."

"Days." He said it without weight, which was how she knew its weight. "The rail has been dry three weeks. They are at the lip of it now. A man with a twenty-year orchard does not let it die on a calculation. He comes for the water. When he comes, I take the night I have built. There's a name for it, the place, an old joke of mine. Lanka. The fortress you burn from the inside, with the demon's own people lighting the fires." The smile again, shorter. "I have been building toward that one night since before you could stand on your own legs."

And there it was, the turn she had refused to let herself see coming, though she had carried it in her body the whole way south. She had come to learn his shape so she could break him clean, the way Kabir taught: weaponise the leash, spend the one moment of slack, do real harm only to stop greater harm. She had come believing she would find a thief to be cut loose from. Instead she had found a man who had spent his entire life as the only blade the country owned against a thing the country could not even admit to having, and who was three days from the single stroke that justified the rest, and who needed, for that stroke, the one instrument he had ground to an edge with his own hands.

Her.

He watched her see it. Of course he watched her see it. He had built the eye she was seeing with.

"I cannot do Lanka without you," he said simply, and the gentleness came back, the gentleness that had been the architecture of her whole life. "I have men. I have money now, more than I have ever had. But I have one operative who can walk into a room full of the most careful people on earth and leave it a morgue without lifting a pulse anywhere in the building. I made her for this. I did not know, when I was making her, that this was the night I was making her for. But it was always this night, beta. Everything was always this night."

She held his gaze and felt the conditioning move in her, vast and patient and tender, the whole machinery he had laid down before she had words to refuse it. She thought of the phrase she had learned was buried somewhere inside her, the words that would take her hands out of her keeping and put them in his, and she watched his mouth, and she waited to see whether, cornered, he would reach for it.

He did not. He only looked at her, an old man asking and not commanding, and that was the cruellest thing he had done all night, because a command she could have hated cleanly.

"You stole me," she said. Her voice did not shake, which surprised her. "Whatever else is true under it. You took me from somewhere and you built me into this and you let me believe a fire."

"Yes." He did not look away. "Every word of that is true. I will not pretend otherwise, and I will not ask you to forgive it tonight." He rose, slow, the spare economy of an old man's joints, and went to the window, and stood with his back to her, looking down at the dead neon and the city beyond it that did not know what it was about to be spared, or sold by. "I ask only that you decide what to do about me after Lanka. Not before." A pause. "There is no after for me in any case. So it is not a large thing I'm asking."

Below them the generator died, and the dark came up out of the street into the room and took the last of the light off his shoulders. She sat in it with her hands open on the table and her pulse moving against the worn metal beneath her collar, and for the first time in eleven years she could not have said, if a stranger had stopped her in the street and asked, whose she was.
