The hawaladar had been dead eleven days and his flat above the cloth market still smelled of him: attar, cold cardamom, and the sourness of a man who had slept among his books because he trusted no safe but the one behind his own ribs. Brij Mohan Sehrawat. Forty years a reconciler of other men's money, and in the end his heart had done the work for everyone, which was the only mercy in the trade. Sita had not touched him. She had no reason to think it was her doing, and that was its own kind of relief, a thing she did not examine.

They had the flat for the night. Menon had bought it from the dead man's nephew with a story about unpaid commissions and a roll of notes thick enough to make the story unnecessary, and the nephew had handed over the keys and a candle, because the power in this lane came and went like a tide, and gone now since ten.

So they worked by the candle and by Menon's pocket torch, in a room with one steel almirah, a charpai, and ledgers stacked to the height of a standing man.

"He kept paper," Menon said, almost fondly, the way a craftsman speaks of a rival's tools. He was sixty, soft in the body, exact in the hands, and he turned the pages of a column-book the way a doctor turns a patient. "No screen ever betrayed him. No KYC, no Aadhaar, no UPI trail to read off a phone. A boy with a phone is a man with his throat already open. Brij Mohan knew that in nineteen eighty-five and never unlearned it."

"And we can read it?"

"He wrote in trade. Invoices. So many bales of poplin to Dubai, so many to Bangkok, the value padded or shaved by an agreed hand. The money walks inside the cloth. You don't read the rupees. You read the difference between what the cloth is worth and what the paper says it's worth, and the difference is the message." He laid two fingers on a line. "Here. Forty thousand metres of shirting to a buyer in Deira at a price no shirting on earth has ever fetched. That is not cloth. That is a man in Punjab telling a man in the Gulf that the consignment is clean and the次 lot moves Thursday."

Sita let him talk. It steadied him, and a steady Menon was a faster Menon, and the clock she carried behind her eyes did not stop for either of them. Lanka stood forty-two days out and closing. Baba had given the enemy a wound that bled money, and the bleeding had a schedule; somewhere in these columns was the artery, the place where the seized pipeline emptied into the network's hidden treasury, and from the artery she could read where the network would have to put its hand when it grew desperate enough to grab.

That was the whole point of the night. Trace the financing, find the convergence, understand the trap from the inside so that when it shut she would know who had been standing where.

They worked. The candle burned a finger's width. From the lane below came the iron clang of a shutter, a scooter, a dog settling its grievance with another dog. Twice Menon found the artery and twice it was a tributary, a feeder line, and he followed it back and shook his head and turned more paper. He was not a hunter. He was an accountant, and an accountant does not stop at the answer he wanted; he stops when the columns close, both sides equal, nothing left over.

It was the leftover that caught him.

She knew it before he spoke, by the change in his hands. They stopped turning. He went back a page, then back two more, then laid the ledger flat and drew the torch close enough that the paper went white-gold under it.

"What," she said. Not a question. An instruction.

"A standing entry." He did not look up. "Small. The smallest line in the book. Every quarter, regular as a tax, a sum goes out from a holding account Brij Mohan kept under a code he used for nothing else. It is not cloth. It buys no cloth and washes no money. It is a fee."

"A fee for what."

"Storage." He said the word the way a man names a smell he cannot place. "Safekeeping. There is a notation. A godown reference, a custody charge, paid out and never drawn back, never reconciled against any consignment, because nothing in this account is ever meant to move. You pay to keep a thing where it is. You pay so that the man who holds it never asks why he is holding it." He turned the torch a degree. "And it has been paid like this, without a single missed quarter, since two thousand and eight."

The number sat in the room.

Sita said nothing for a moment. Eighteen years. Eighteen years of a dead man's hand reaching out four times a year to keep something untouched, through changes of currency, through the night the old notes were killed and reborn, through Brij Mohan's own slow dying, the instruction surviving the man who gave it because that was how these accounts were built: a standing order with no name on it, serviced by reflex, the original purpose long since buried with whoever had set it running.

"Who opened it?" she asked.

"No name. That is the discipline of it. A name is a door." He ran the torch back up the column to the top of the page, where Brij Mohan had written a header in a tiny, upright Devanagari hand. "Only this. He kept the code separate. He treated the account the way you treat a thing you are afraid of. He did not let it touch the other money. In forty years of his books I have not seen him afraid of money before."

She came around the charpai and crouched beside him so the page was hers as well. The header was a single line. A reference for the godown, a date, and beside the date a string of syllables that were not a name and not a sum and not any trade-word she had learned in two months of learning the rails. Five sounds. A man might have read them as a code-word, a key, the kind of nonsense-string a careful hand chooses precisely because it means nothing to anyone but the one who set it.

She read them once, in her head, the way you read anything.

And the floor of her went out from under her without moving an inch.

It was not a thought. Thought would have been a thing she could have held and turned and named. This was lower than thought, in the body, in the back of the neck and the soft place under the breastbone: a few notes of something, a melody that was not music, the shape of a voice she had no memory of ever hearing, gone before she could catch its edge. Her skin came up in gooseflesh along both arms in the warm room. Her mouth had gone dry the way it went before violence, but there was no violence here, only paper and a dead man's careful fear.

Her hand had gone to her throat.

She did not decide it. It was an old movement, older than the work, older than anything she could date, the thumb finding the small silver weight that had hung at her collarbone her whole life and lay against the skin there worn smooth as a river stone, its few shallow marks long since rubbed past reading. She did not know she was doing it. She had done it ten thousand times in airports and safehouses and the seconds before a kill, a tic, a nothing, a thing Baba had once watched her do across a table and named, gently, her habit; you reach for it when you are afraid, he had said, and she had not believed him because she was never afraid.

She was holding it now. Holding it the way you hold a thing that has answered you.

"Sita."

Menon was looking at her. Not at the page. At her.

She let go of the pendant. It dropped against her shirt with a weight she had never once in her life found heavy.

"You were humming," he said.

"No."

"Three notes. Four." He had gone very still himself, the accountant's stillness, the man who hated a column that would not close. "Just now. Over the page."

"I don't hum."

He held her eye a moment longer, and then, because he was a wise man and an old one and had survived sixty years in a trade that killed the curious, he looked back down at the ledger and decided he had heard a scooter or the dog or his own tired blood, and that whatever had crossed his agent's face was hers and not his to carry. That was the kindness of professionals. You did not pick up another man's secret unless it was put into your hands.

"It means nothing to you," he said. It was almost a question.

"It means nothing to me," she said, and the strange thing, the thing she would turn over later in the dark and find no bottom to, was that it was true and a lie in the same breath, and she could not have told you which half was which.

She made herself work the problem the way she had been built to work it, from the outside, where the ground was firm.

A standing fee. Safekeeping. Since 2008. A thing kept where it lay, paid for by a man who was afraid of it, hidden inside the bookkeeping of the same rails that moved the network's money, so that anyone auditing the rails would skip it as a rounding error and anyone hunting the thing it guarded would never think to look in a cloth-merchant's column-book. It was not the network's money. The network did not pay to keep things still; the network paid to make things move and disappear. This was the opposite instinct. This was someone who had wanted a single object to survive, untouched, beyond their own death, and had bought eighteen years of survival for it in the only currency that left no name: a hawaladar's discretion, renewing itself by habit.

She knew, then, more than she let her face know.

There had been a burial. She had carried that for weeks now, since the monk's lesson and the steered hunt and the slow understanding that a former Chief had once found this same network at its root and put earth over it for reasons of state, and that the burying had left the one who found it exposed, and that the exposed one had not survived. An analyst. A finder. A person who had seen the whole shape of the thing two decades early and refused to look away, and had been silenced for the refusal. She had thought of that person, when she thought of them at all, as a closed file. A name on nothing. The first cost of the long crime.

But a finder who knew they were going to be silenced did not simply die. A good finder hid the proof first. Built it a grave it could be dug out of. Paid a frightened honest man to keep it where the state's long arm and the network's longer one would both walk past it for eighteen years.

The proof existed. Somewhere physical, somewhere a hand could reach and a hand could carry, there was a thing the dead finder had set down in 2008 and arranged to keep breathing after them: the ledger, the recording, the original sin written in a form no Pegasus could reach into a phone and no analyst could quietly edit out of a registry, because it was an object and objects had to be found and stolen, not deleted. And the account that guarded it had just surfaced in a dead man's column-book by the smallest line in the book.

She understood what it was worth. It was the one thing in this entire war that did not need Lanka. Baba was going to spend a deniable night and a quantity of his own soul to roll up a network the state could not admit existed, because there was no document strong enough to make the state act in the daylight. But there had always been a document. It had been sitting in a godown for eighteen years, paid for by a ghost, waiting for someone with the eye to find the line.

She should tell Baba. The thought arrived clean and complete, the way her trained thoughts did. This changed Lanka. This was leverage, or a weapon, or a key, and Baba would know which.

And underneath the clean thought, unbidden, came the other movement, the one from below: her thumb travelling again to the small silver weight at her throat, the worn marks under the pad of her thumb that she had touched her whole life and never once read, because there had never been anything written there to read, only the habit of the touch, only the river-stone smoothness of a thing she had carried so long it had become a part of her own body, like the scar on her knee, like the sound of a voice she could not remember and had just, for the space of three notes, almost heard.

She took her hand away. She was aware of having done it twice now, and that Menon, however kindly he had looked away, was a man who counted.

"Where," she said. Her voice came out level, which she was grateful for. "The godown. There's a reference. Where is it kept."

Menon turned the torch back to the line, and read off the notation, and named a place, and it was real, and locatable, a true address in the world with a road to it and a door, and as he said it Sita wrote it into the only ledger she trusted, behind her own eyes, where it would keep.

"You'll go yourself," Menon said. It was not a question either. He had worked with her long enough to know the shape of her when a thing had become hers.

"I'll think about it."

"You should give it to Baba."

"I said I'll think about it."

He heard the edge and let it lie, and went back to closing the columns he could close, because that was his trade and the night was short and the candle was a thumb's width from the saucer. But she sat back on her heels in the smell of attar and cardamom and a dead man's fear, and for the first time since the monk's mountain she did not know, fully and clearly, what she was going to do. She told herself it was tradecraft. A thing this old, this hidden, this dangerous, you did not hand up the chain on the strength of one line in one book; you verified it first, you walked the ground yourself, you knew the weight of a thing before you put it in another man's hands, even his. That was discipline. That was the work.

It was also not the whole reason, and she knew it the way she knew when a man was lying across a table, by a coldness that came before the proof, and she did not look at the rest of it.

She reached up, without deciding to, and closed her hand around the pendant one more time, and held it, and felt nothing in it but silver and warmth and the eighteen years she had not known she had been carrying it. Then she stood, and pinched out the candle, and told Menon they were finished here.

Below in the lane the dog had stopped. Somewhere a long way east of them, a thing that had been waiting in the dark since two thousand and eight went on waiting, and the only person in the world who held the key to it had just locked the door of a dead man's flat and walked past it down the stairs, with the key against her skin, humming, very softly, three notes she could not name.
