The lamp in the back room of the Lajpat Nagar flat threw a hard yellow circle on the table, and Sita had arranged the work inside it the way she arranged everything: the photographed pages of the munshi's ledger laid in their order, the loupe to her right, a pencil she had no intention of using set parallel to the edge. Outside, the city had gone to its three o'clock quiet, the hour when even the dogs gave up. Menon slept on the daybed against the far wall with his shoes on, a habit she had stopped trying to break in him. She had told him she wanted an hour with the pages. He had not asked why. That was the one mercy of working with a man who had spent forty years being told only what he needed.

The 2008 account sat in front of her, third page from the top, where she had photographed it that morning under the hawaladar's frightened eye. In the munshi's hand it looked like nothing: a header of grouped figures, a long dead column with no movement against it, no debits, no settlements, a sleeping account that someone had paid to keep sleeping for eighteen years. A bookkeeper would have read it as a dormant deposit and turned the page. She had read it that way too, the first three times.

Then she had not.

She drew the pendant out from under her collar and laid it in the light.

She had worn it so long it had stopped being an object. It was simply the cool place at her sternum, the thing her thumb found when she was thinking, the thing that survived every reinvention Fateh had ever ordered: new names, new cities, new hair, new passports with new faces, and always this oval of worn silver staying against the skin like a second collarbone. Mira had asked about it once, in the safe house, in that careful way she had of asking about everything, and Sita had said only that it was her mother's, and had felt the lie of not knowing more close her throat. She did not know more. A keepsake. A thing a dead woman leaves a living one. Sentiment, she had thought, and she had been trained out of sentiment so early that she had carried the pendant the way other people carry an old scar, without inspecting it.

She inspected it now.

Under the loupe the silver leapt up close and strange. The front carried the design she had known all her life and never once looked at: a tree, she had always called it, a little raised tree with the branches running over to one side as if a wind held them. The back she had taken for beading, a border of tiny raised dots her thumb had rubbed half flat over the years. And around the rim, both faces, ran what she had assumed since childhood was filigree, a decorative band of scrollwork, the kind a jeweller adds to fill a space.

It was not scrollwork. It was script.

She had to breathe and look away and look back before the loupe would hold it still. The marks were minute, cut by someone with a fine point and a steady hand and time, and worn now to ghosts of themselves, but they were letters, crowded letters running rim to rim, and once her eye had caught the trick of them it could not let go. She read the way she had always read a surveillance box or a balance sheet, the whole thing arriving before the parts, the shape before the sense, and the shape of it stopped her breath because she knew it. Not from sight. From the throat.

It was the tune.

She had hummed it her whole life. She could not have said when she started; it predated memory, predated Fateh, predated the restaurant and the hotel key and the eighteen years of work. It came to her in dead time, in cars, in the seconds before a job when other operators prayed. A few notes, falling, then lifting, then falling lower; a cradle of a tune, with no words she had ever owned. She had assumed, when she assumed anything, that it was something off a radio in a childhood she could not see. She had been wrong about that the way she had been wrong about the fire. The words ran around the rim of the pendant in a hand eighteen years dead, and as her eye crawled along them her wordless tune reached up and took them, syllable settling onto note, and for the first time in her life the song had a mouth.

*Soja*, the rim began. *Sleep now.*

She did not let herself feel it. She had a discipline for that, a wall she could put up between the work and the rest, and she put it up now, brick by brick, because she could see already where this was going and she would be no use to anyone weeping over jewellery at three in the morning. She set the pendant flat, rim up, and made herself a bookkeeper again.

The header of the 2008 account was a column of paired figures. She had read them, that morning, as an account reference and its sub-codes, and they were good, they were exactly what a reference and its sub-codes should look like, which was the whole craft of it. But a reference does not need to be paired. These were paired, every figure married to a second, twelve couples down the column, and now that she had the rim of the pendant under the lamp she understood what the couples were for. The first of each pair counted lines. The second counted along the line.

The lullaby was the book. The figures were the way in.

She worked it the way she had been taught to work everything, slowly, twice, trusting nothing the first time. First couple: line three, word four. She walked the rim with the loupe, counting the falling notes against the cut letters, found the third line, counted to the fourth word, and read it. Second couple: line one, word two. She found it. The words came up out of the dark in no order, a heap of them, the way the munshi's dead code-words had come up before they resolved, and she did not try to force them into meaning yet. She only gathered. She had learned that from the ledgers and she had learned it, she understood now, from somewhere older than the ledgers, from whatever had been put in her before Fateh got his hands on it.

That was the part she could not wall off, however she stacked the bricks. The eye doing this was not the eye Fateh had trained. He had sharpened it, yes, he had spent years grinding it to the edge he wanted, and he had praised it the way a man praises a good tool, casually, as if the credit were his. *You read a room better than anyone I ever ran*, he had told her once, in the Karol Bagh days, almost tender. *You see the pattern under the pattern.* She had taken it as his gift to her, his making of her, one more thing she owed the man who had pulled her from the fire that had never happened. And it had been a lie inside a lie, because the eye was not his to give. It had been cut into her by the same hand that had cut these letters into silver, the woman who had built a machine out of a tune and a coin and then built a reader for the machine and called the reader her daughter. Two makers. She had thought she had one. She had spent eleven years grateful to the wrong one, and the right one was eighteen years in the ground with her work still ticking on the skin of the child she had wound up and let go.

Sita put the bricks back. She gathered words.

When she had all twelve she stopped and laid the loupe down and pressed the heels of her hands into her eyes until the dark behind them swam, and then she took them away and looked at the heap and let it resolve, and this was the part only she could do, because the resolving was not arithmetic. The figures gave her the words. The pendant's rim held the words. But the order of reading, which letter to lift from each word and which to drop, that lived in the worn dots on the back, the beading she had rubbed half away with twenty years of thinking, and she had to take the offset from the count of them, and there were two ways to count a worn thing and they gave two different answers and only one of the answers was a place.

She turned the pendant over and counted the dots three times and got eleven, twelve, eleven. She trusted eleven, because the falling cadence of the tune ended on a fall, on the low note, on the closed thing and not the open one, and an eleven-count read closed. That was not bookkeeping. That was the throat again, the song knowing its own end. She applied the offset of eleven, lifted the letter the offset gave her from each gathered word, and set them down in the order the figures dictated.

The first three letters made a syllable she knew. The next two finished a name. The name was a place in this city, an old place, a walled and forgotten acre near Kashmere Gate where the British buried their dead and nobody had buried anyone in a hundred years, where the graves stood in rows under the dust with their names worn the way her pendant's dots were worn, past reading, kept only by a registry no one consulted. And after the name came a number, and the number was not money. It was a row and a plot.

She sat very still.

She knew the rule. She did not write it down. She had broken a hundred rules in the last forty days, she had walked away from her maker and let a stranger fill her with doubt and learned that the foundation of her was a fabrication, but she did not write down the answer to the only question that had ever been hers alone, because the moment it existed outside her skull it belonged to whoever found the paper. She closed her eyes and put the place into herself the way she put a face or a route, the row and the plot riding in under the name, and she said the lullaby through once, all of it now, words and tune married, pressing it down into herself until it would not shift. *Sleep now*, the rim had said, *sleep now, the garden keeps you, the river does not tell.* The garden. She understood the garden now. Her mother had hidden a thing among the dead and then sung the dead a song and tied the song to a baby and prayed the baby would grow an eye that could find the grave. It was insane. It was the most patient act of faith she had ever encountered, more patient than Fateh's forty years, because Fateh at least had lived to see his trap close, and this woman had built her bet knowing she would be dead long before it paid, knowing she would never learn whether the child read the song or threw the coin in a drawer or let it slip away in some street she would never live to see. She had spent her last hours teaching a three-week-old to do the one thing that might one day kill the people who were coming up the stairs for her.

It had worked. Eighteen years late, in a back room in Lajpat Nagar, it had worked, and the woman would never know.

Sita found that her hand had closed around the pendant and that she had brought it up against her mouth, and she made herself put it down on the table flat, an object, a piece of evidence in an operation, and she breathed until her pulse came back under the line where she liked to keep it.

What sat at that grave was the proof. She did not need to have seen it to know its shape. A ledger, paper, the real one, the one the munshi's account only guarded. And the woman had been thorough, she had been trade, no question of it now, no amateur cut these letters or thought to make a child the index, so there would be a voice too, the woman's own most likely, on whatever a careful person in 2008 trusted to hold a voice: a cassette, a small black tape in a tin, sealed against the damp, waiting in the dark behind a loose stone for someone to come who could not be reached by any wire, any account, any phone, any of the machinery that owned the present and could be turned. Her mother had understood, eighteen years ahead of the people now hunting her daughter, that the only safe place left was a place a screen could not see into. A grave. A song. A coin. A girl.

The clock on the wall said ten past three. She did the other arithmetic, the one she could not put off. Forty-three days since the seizure. Fateh's window was three weeks of starvation before the network had to move to reclaim its treasury, and the three weeks were spent; the convergence he had built was days out now, maybe less, the whole sleeper structure being drawn toward a single deniable night because he had made it the only night they had. And here in her skull sat the thing her mother had died to keep alive: the proof of the same network at its birth, the cradle and the deathbed of the same lie, the beginning the state had buried and the end the state could not sanction, eighteen years apart and now both arriving in the same week, in her, the one person built by both ends to carry it. She had thought, an hour ago, that she was the bait. She was something worse than bait. She was the seam where the whole thing had been stitched, the place where her mother's craft of 2008 reached forward and touched her own hands of 2026 across the dead acre and the worn coin, and neither maker had asked her, and both had been right that she would do it.

She heard the daybed creak. Menon was awake, in the way old men wake, all at once and pretending not to. He lay still a moment longer out of courtesy, then sat up and looked at her across the room, at the spread pages and the loupe and the pendant lying naked in the lamplight where he had never seen it before.

"You found it," he said. Not a question. He had watched her read for forty days; he knew the difference between her face working and her face arrived.

She gathered the pages into their order and squared them and laid the photograph of the 2008 account on top, dormant, dead, a sleeping deposit, exactly what it had been built to look like. She lifted the pendant by its cord and dropped it back inside her collar, and it found the cool place at her sternum and lay there like nothing, like a coin a careless girl might have lost in a gutter at six years old and had not.

"There's a place," she said. "It's old. It's been there since before any of this." She heard her own voice come out level and was grateful to whatever in her kept it level. "We'll need to go quietly. No phones near it. Nothing logged."

Menon nodded slowly, watching her, and she could see him deciding not to ask the question she could see him wanting to ask, which was whether she meant to take what she found to Mira's people, or to the state through Sehgal's clean offered hand, or to Fateh, who needed it and would speak six words to her the moment she stood in front of him to make sure she gave it up. She did not have the answer for him because she did not have it for herself, and she was not going to manufacture one at ten past three to make an old man feel steadier.

"Sleep another hour," she said. "We move at first light."

He lay back down with his shoes on. After a while his breathing went long again. Sita sat in the hard yellow circle, the grave waiting in her skull and the song lying in her now where the wall could not reach it, and she did not hum the tune, because now she knew the words, and the words were her mother saying sleep now to a child who never would again.
