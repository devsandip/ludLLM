The metro would have been forty minutes faster, and she did not take it. The cameras at Kashmere Gate station read faces the way a moneylender reads notes, by reflex, looking for the one that did not belong, and hers belonged to a list now. So she went the long way, on foot and by autorickshaw, the way the old men still moved money through this city, hand to hand, leaving no wire to follow.

She had left the phone in a drawer in the room off Daryaganj, switched off and then, because off was not off, wrapped in a freezer bag and laid inside the cistern of the toilet. A dead instrument told them nothing except where it slept. She wanted it to sleep there all morning, breathing its small electronic breath against the porcelain, swearing to anyone who asked that she had not moved.

By half past five the light over Old Delhi was the colour of weak tea. The lanes behind Jama Masjid were already awake in the way they were always awake, handcarts and the first hiss of frying, a boy sluicing a shopfront with grey water, and she went into the noise of it because noise was cover. A face in a crowd of ten thousand faces was a needle the software could find if it cared to. A face that crossed its own path twice, that doubled back through a covered bazaar where the cameras were blind with dust and washing, that left by a different mouth than it had entered, was a needle that had learned to bend.

She ran the route she had set herself the night before, pacing it on a tourist map bought for ten rupees so that no search history would carry the shape of her morning. Chandni Chowk to the spice market, the throat-catching cloud of red chilli at Khari Baoli where every man coughed and nobody looked at anybody, a held breath, a turn. Then north and east through lanes that narrowed until two cycle-rickshaws could not pass, into the wholesale paper district where bundles came off trucks and the air smelled of pulp and diesel. She stopped twice. Once to buy a glass of tea she did not drink, standing with her back to a wall so she could watch the lane fill and empty. Once to tie a lace that did not need tying, low, so that she could look back along the ground at the legs of the people behind her, because faces lied and gaits did not, and a man holding distance gave himself away in the rhythm of his shoes.

Nobody held distance. By the time she came out near the GPO and the great red bulk of the gate itself, she had stripped any tail down to the city's own indifference, and she stood for a moment among the buses and the parked autos of the interstate terminus letting that indifference settle on her like the dust.

The clock she could not strip. It ran behind her eyes whether she watched it or not. Days now, not weeks, before the night Fateh had built came down. She did not know the hour and she did not need to. She knew the shape of his patience, had been raised inside it, and she could feel it tightening the way you feel weather change in an old wound. Whatever was in the ground here had waited eighteen years. It would have to be lifted in the little time that was left, and carried, and that meant she had to stand on it first with her own feet and be certain.

The cemetery was where the cipher had put it, and the cipher had been her own throat.

She had not understood, until she sat with the pendant and let the tune run, how much of her had been made to point here. The lullaby was not a memory she could date. It had no first time. It was simply there, the way her own pulse was there, the small descending phrase she had hummed under her breath in waiting rooms and on rooftops and once, she remembered now with a coldness that had nothing to do with the morning, on a hotel stair on her eighteenth birthday with a key warm in her fist. She had hummed it then to steady her hand. She had thought it was hers. It was an instruction. Someone had set it in her before she had words, knowing that a tune sits below the reach of any interrogation, that you cannot make a person forget what they never learned on purpose, and that one day a grown woman would carry it intact to the only place it described.

That was the part she kept turning over, and could not put down. Not the cleverness of it. The intent. A thing made for her, specifically, before she could refuse. Whoever had done it had not known her. Could not have. They had known only that there would be a her, and had trusted her across eighteen years of a life they could not see, into rooms they could not imagine her standing in, to here.

She did not let the thought finish. She had work.

Lothian lay behind its wall off the road that ran down past the church, a long neglected rectangle of the oldest Christian dead in the city, walled in 1808 and more or less abandoned to the trees since. The gate stood open because there was nothing left to steal. She went in past a board the municipality had bolted up and let rust, into a green hush that the traffic could not quite reach, and the change was immediate and physical, the temperature dropping under the canopy, the light going to leaf-shadow on cracked tombs.

It was the right kind of ground for it. She saw that with a professional's eye even as the rest of her went quiet. Consecrated, half-forgotten, the sort of place a city tells itself it has no use for. Nobody redeveloped a graveyard this old. No surveyor came. No camera watched the dead. The colonial stones leaned at their angles, lieutenants of regiments long dissolved, infants of three months and four days, wives carried off by the fever of an August two centuries gone, and over all of it a damp that ate the names. You could leave a thing here and the only clock that ran on it was the slow clock of root and rain. Whoever had chosen it had chosen with the same eye she was using to admire the choice, and the recognition was its own small vertigo, looking into a mind that had reached forward and arranged the ground beneath her before she existed.

She did not search at random. The cipher was geometry as much as it was a name. The tune gave her a count, the count gave her rows, and the words, the soft nonsense words she had sung ten thousand times without once hearing them, gave her a stone to start from. She walked the central path until she found it, an obelisk to a magistrate dead in 1857, the date itself a kind of grim joke from a woman who had clearly trusted nothing to be where the maps said it was. From the magistrate she paced east, counting under her breath, and the counting was the lullaby, and she heard her own voice doing it and made it stop, and counted in numbers instead, the way she counted paces to a target, flat and without music.

Twelve. The rows thinned here, the path losing itself in lantana and the long grass that grew up through the kerbs. Then a turn the verse had told her to take, ninety degrees into the wild part where the trees closed and the stones gave out altogether, and she went off the path into wet grass that soaked her to the shin, holding the geometry in her head, refusing to look up and lose her place.

She stopped where the count ended and looked at the ground.

There was nothing here that announced itself. That was the first thing, and the right thing, the absence that told her she had read it correctly. The colonial graves were behind her now, their broken angels and their fevers. Ahead the wall and the trees. And here, in the gap the old dead had not used, a low plain kerb of cement set flush into the earth, no cross on it, no scripture, no leaning stone. Just the rectangle of it under the grass, and at the head a small flat tablet with no name she could find, the surface gone to green, and pressed into the cement while it was still wet, the way a workman dates a pavement he has poured, four figures.

2008.

She crouched and put her fingers to it before she had decided to. The cement was cold and slick. She cleared the moss with her thumb and the figures came up clearer, and under them, faint, the print of a smaller mark, an initial or a number, eaten too far to read. She knelt in the wet grass and looked at it for a long time, and the operative in her assembled the facts the way she would assemble any scene, because that was the only language she had for what she was looking at.

A grave. Plain, unmarked, unconsecrated in its corner, the only thing in this ground newer than a hundred years, set here in the one year the cipher named, in the one cemetery in the city where no one would ever come to dig. Someone had arranged for the earth to be opened here in 2008 and closed again, off the books, in a place the living had stopped visiting, and had left no name, because a name was a thread and a thread could be pulled. And into that arrangement, into the only vault that a hunted person could be certain no enemy would ever open, because who opens an old grave in an old graveyard, they had put the thing she had come for.

A woman. The thought arrived sideways and she did not know where it came from, and she let it stand because her instincts had earned the right to be heard. Not a number, not a box only. The shape of the kerb was a person's length. Someone had died, or been brought here dead, in 2008, and had been made the keeper of it. She did not let herself ask the rest of the question. The rest of the question was not operational. The rest of the question had nothing to do with the work, and she had learned, long ago and from the best teacher there was, to set down what had nothing to do with the work.

She set it down. It would not stay down.

Her hand was flat on the cement now, the whole palm, and she noticed that it was not steady, and she watched it not being steady with the detached interest she usually kept for other people's hands in the moment before they understood what was happening to them. She had killed without her hand moving like this. She had walked out of burning buildings, real and false, with a pulse a doctor would have called enviable. And here, over four numbers in old cement and a grave with no name, in a place she had no memory of and no reason to mourn, her body was doing something her training had no protocol for.

The lullaby came up in her throat.

She felt it rise the way nausea rises, from below, without permission, and she pressed her lips together against it the way she had pressed them together her whole life, in waiting rooms, on rooftops, on the stair. It would not be pressed down. It came anyway, not the counting version, not the flat numbers she used as a leash, but the tune itself, soft and descending, the small sad fall of it, and she heard her own voice let one line of it go out loud into the green air over the kerb, three or four notes, no more, before she caught it.

The cemetery took the sound and gave nothing back. A crow shifted in the canopy. The traffic murmured beyond the wall, a city going to work, ten million people who did not know this corner existed. And she knelt over the unmarked grave with her hand on the cold cement and the unsanctioned tune dying in her throat, and understood, in a way that had not yet found its words and might never, that this had been made for her. Not for the state. Not for an officer with a warrant. For a particular woman who would one day stand exactly here with exactly this song in her, and who would know, when the count ran out and the music came up unbidden over the ground, that she had been expected.

She stayed down a moment longer than the work required. Then the discipline took her, the way it always took her in the end, and she lifted her hand from the stone and looked at the grass to see what she had bent and bruised in her kneeling, and pressed it back upright with her fingers so that the place would not read as touched. She marked the magistrate's obelisk in her memory, and the count, and the turn, and the trees that screened the corner from the wall. She judged the soil, dark and worked-soft with two centuries of leaf-fall, a single night's labour for one set of hands and a folding spade. She judged the angles of approach, the broken board at the gate, the blind hour before dawn.

She would come back for it in the dark, and soon, because the other clock was running and would not wait for her to understand what her own throat had told her. She rose, and the wet grass gave up her knees, and she did not look at the four figures again, because she found that she could not and keep her face the way she needed it for the lanes.

At the broken board she stopped and let the street resolve itself before she stepped into it, the buses, the chai stall, the indifferent river of the morning. Behind her the woman had kept her eighteen years and would keep her a few hours more. Ahead, the city that had hunted her, and the man who had made her, and the night he had built. The convergence was no longer a line on a ledger or a pattern in a hawaladar's accounts. It was here, under her feet, where two times she had not chosen had come down at last on one piece of ground, and waited for her to bring the spade.

She walked out into the heat, humming nothing, her hands very still.
