The spade went in at a slant, the way Kabir had once shown her to enter anything that resisted, edge first, weight behind it, no swing that a watcher could read as labour from across a wall. She had bought the spade and the folding entrenching tool that afternoon from a hardware stall off Esplanade Road, paying cash, taking the old man's change without counting it so that he would remember a woman in a hurry and not a woman who watched. The folding tool was Chinese, stamped for gardening, the kind that sat under a lakh of car seats in this city. The spade she would leave in the grave.

Lothian was not lit. The Archaeological Survey had run a cable to the gate years ago and then forgotten it, and the lamp on its pole threw a cone of sodium orange that died ten metres short of where she knelt. Beyond that the dark was complete in the way only walled ground in a lit city can be dark, the glow of Kashmere Gate held off by brick and the high crowns of the gulmohars. She had timed the metro. The last train south out of the interchange ran at eleven, and after it the road noise thinned to single autos and the occasional bike, and the caretaker, a Survey peon who slept in the chowkidar's room by the arch, had put his light out at ten past. She had watched the bulb behind his curtain for forty minutes before she moved. A man who is going to get up again does not turn fully onto his side.

The earth here was not the packed laterite of the older quarter. Someone had turned this plot, and turned it recently by the standard of eighteen years; the top spit came away in clods that broke wet against the blade, and under it the fill was looser than the ground to either side, a column of disturbed soil sunk into undisturbed clay. She had read the difference with the tool before she trusted her eye to it. A grave dug once and filled once settles into a single subsiding mass. This had the feel of a shaft refilled with care, the spoil returned in the wrong order, dark over pale, so that whatever lay below sat in soil that did not match its own depth. Janaki, if it was Janaki down there, had not done this herself. Someone had done it for her, fast, in a single night, and had got the layers wrong because a person working against the clock fills a hole the way water fills it, from the top.

She did not let herself hold the name. She had been holding it since the pendant gave up its grammar to her on the floor of the Daryaganj room, the lullaby unfolding into bearings and dates the way a paper boat unfolds back into a flat sheet creased forever, that can never lie flat again. A woman. Buried in 2008. Buried for her. The arithmetic of it was three weeks old in the child's life when the woman went into the ground, and that was a fact she could carry as arithmetic and not as anything else, so she carried it as arithmetic and put the blade in again.

The work was slower than a film would have it. She had perhaps four hours of true dark, and a grave is not a posthole; she went down in a rectangle barely wider than her shoulders, throwing the spoil onto a sheet of blue tarpaulin so that nothing fell on the neighbouring plots to be read at dawn, stopping every fifteen or twenty minutes to listen with her whole body the way the conditioning had taught her to listen, which was a thing she now did and hated doing, because the listening was his and not hers, his patient voice somewhere under the floor of it, saying nothing yet. Sweat ran into the collar of the dark kurta and she let it. At a metre the smell of the ground changed, the green wet smell going under to something mineral and old, and she breathed through her mouth and went on.

She thought, against her will, about the timing of the body, because the timing was operational and the operational she could think about. She thought: he came back. He took me from a house with my mother dead in it, Mira's papers say strangulation and not fire, and three weeks later this woman went into the ground here with the proof she had made. So either the woman is my mother and the dates are a lie, or the woman is my mother and Mira's date is a lie, or the woman is not my mother at all and the grave is only a vault that a clever, frightened analyst dressed as a grave because no service on earth searches a grave. She turned the third possibility over because it was the one that let her keep digging. A grave is the only safe-deposit box that takes no key, files no register, sends no message to a phone the enemy owns. Aadhaar did not reach into the ground. A grave had no KYC. Whoever had built this had understood, eighteen years before it was true of everyone, that the only thing you could truly hide in the age that was coming was a thing made of paper and acetate and tape, a thing with no address that a server knew.

At a metre and a half the blade struck and rang.

She stopped. She crouched in the shaft with the sweat going cold on her and cleared the strike-mark with her gloved hands, scraping the soil sideways and lifting it out by the double handful, and the surface that came up under the torch she held in her teeth was not stone and not the lid of a coffin. It was iron. A trunk lid, the curved-top kind that had come north on the railways a hundred years ago and sat in every government quarter and every dowry, painted once and rusted since into a brown crust that flaked under her fingers. She knelt back from it. Whoever had buried this had buried a box and not a body, or had buried a body inside a box, and either way the answer was thirty centimetres down, and she found she did not want to clear the last of it.

She cleared it.

The trunk was perhaps three feet by two, and the lid was held not by its own broken hasp but by a strap of webbing run twice around the whole and buckled, the webbing perished to threads where it crossed the top but sound underneath where the soil had kept the air off it. She cut it rather than fight the buckle, the folding knife going through the rotten weave in two passes, and she lifted the lid by its edge with both hands, her heart going at a rate she noted and disregarded, the way she would note a target's pulse and disregard her own.

There was no body. Her breath went out of her in a sound she was glad no one heard.

There was, instead, the inside of a careful mind. The trunk had been lined. A layer of thick polythene, the grey builder's sheeting they sold by the metre off rolls, taped down the sides with a tar-based tape gone to amber, and inside that a second skin of waxed cloth, oilcloth, the kind that used to dress a kitchen table, folded over the contents and tucked beneath them with the deliberate care of a thing meant to outlast the hand that folded it. She lifted the oilcloth back. Under it, packed in newspaper that had yellowed but not rotted, the air sealed twice away from it, lay the proof.

She knew it for proof before she had a single piece of it in her hand, because it was made the way you make a thing you mean to outlast you. There were tins. Flat circular tins, six of them, the size of a chapati, the lids taped, and on each, in a hand she had never seen and recognised in her body before her mind would allow it, the small upright Devanagari and English of an analyst's marginalia, a date, a Roman numeral, a single word. She lifted one and shook it gently and felt the slither and weight of a spool of film against the metal. Microfilm. Sixteen millimetre, she guessed from the tin, the medium a registry had used in the years before scanning, the medium that needed a reader bolted to a desk and a lamp and a lens and could not be opened by anything you carried in a pocket, or anything you reached across a network. She set the tin on the oilcloth and lifted the next and it answered the same way, and she understood that the woman who had built this had not trusted a single copy, had photographed her case onto film a frame at a time on a registry machine she must have signed for, and had then taken the film and not the signature and put it in the ground.

Under the tins, a ledger. A real ledger, cloth-bound, the boards swollen and the red edges of the pages furred with damp held off but not wholly defeated, and she opened it under the torch with two fingers and saw columns, hawala notations she had spent the last weeks learning to read in a dead munshi's books, the same grammar, the same dead code-words standing in for the same living men, but here annotated, here cross-written in a second column in that upright hand, a code-word linked to a name, a name to a posting, a posting to a date of placement. She read three lines and shut it. Three lines were enough to tell her that what she held was not a copy of the munshi's account but its key, the thing the whole forty-year cipher resolved against, and that a person who had this and a reader for the film could stand in front of a court or a Cabinet Secretary and turn a rumour into a list of names, with addresses and the serial numbers of the postings they had bought their way into. Missile commands. The cyber directorate. She did not need to read further to know the shape of it. She had been told the shape of it already, by Baba's own munshi's ledgers and by Sehgal's clean steered hunt and by everything Mira had given her that was true. The shape was already in her. The proof only made it a thing she could put on a table.

And under the ledger, wrapped alone in its own square of oilcloth as if it were the most fragile of the three and not the most robust, a cassette.

She lifted it out and turned it in the torchlight. A TDK, the ferric kind, sixty minutes, the cellophane long gone but the shell intact, the small window showing the tape wound mostly onto the left spool, so that whatever was on it had been recorded and then rewound by a hand that wanted it ready to play. On the inlay card, in pencil now, not ink, as if the analyst had run out of one certainty and reached for another that could be rubbed away, two words and a date. The date was a month before the grave. The two words she read and read again, and her hand was not steady, which she observed from a great distance, as a fault in a tool she would need.

The two words were a name and a relation. The name was a man's. The relation was not.

She did not have a player. That was the thought she made herself have, the operational thought, because the operational thought was a floor she could stand on. There was nothing in the city she could put a cassette into without driving across it; the machines that read this tape had gone the way of the machines that read the film, into a few obsessive flats and the back rooms of one or two studios in Chandni Chowk that still transferred a dead uncle's bhajans for a fee. The film needed a reader. The tape needed a deck. The ledger alone she could photograph, and she would not, because a photograph went into a phone and a phone was a window the enemy owned, and the whole genius of the woman in the ground who was not in the ground had been to build a case that could not be reached through a window. To use it she had to carry it. That was the trap and the gift of it together. The proof could not be leaked, could not be mailed, could not be sent up to a cloud and pulled down in Dubai. It could only be carried, by hand, by one person, to one room, and set down on one table in front of someone who could not then pretend the table was empty. The woman had built a weapon that could be fired only at point-blank range, and she had built the person who would carry it to the muzzle, and that person was kneeling in her grave at half past two in the morning with the carrying about to begin.

She packed it as she had found it. The tins back in their newspaper, the ledger over them, the cassette in the ledger's hollow where the swollen boards left room, the oilcloth folded over and tucked beneath, a child put down to sleep. She had brought a bag for it, a cheap nylon holdall of the kind a labourer carries his tiffin and his change of clothes in, nothing a camera would hold its eye on, and the trunk's contents filled it and weighed it, and she set it up on the lip of the shaft on the tarpaulin and climbed out after it.

The refilling took less time than the digging, the way it always does. She tipped the spoil back and trod it and tipped more, and she did not try to make the surface a lie, because a lie took daylight to dress; she made it only a low untidy mound that the first gardener would read as a settled grave heaved by the monsoon, and she folded the tarpaulin in on its own soil so that nothing spilled. The spade she laid flat in the open trench at the foot of the plot, where a length of broken kerb already lay, so that a spade among the Survey's own rubble was a spade and not a clue. The entrenching tool went into the holdall against the proof. Her gloves she would burn.

She stood a moment over the closed ground with the bag's strap cutting her shoulder, and she let herself, for the count of three breaths and no more, not be operational.

The woman had known. That was the thing that would not stay in its box with the film and the ledger. The woman had known, in 2008, that the age coming would be an age of windows, that everything would have an address, that a daughter could be found through a SIM and a face read off a lamppost and money leave a wake of light wherever it ran, and the woman had answered the whole of that future with a trunk full of tin and tape and a song. She had hidden the only thing they could not reach inside the only place they would not look, and entrusted it to the only courier they could not turn, because the courier did not know she was carrying anything until she was eighteen years grown and standing on the grave. It was the most patient act Sita had ever held in her two hands, and it had been done for her, and it was, she understood now with the bag's weight settling into her like a second skeleton, the exact mirror of the other patient thing that had been done to her, in the same years, by the man who had taken her out of that house. Two makers. One had built her into a key and put the key in the ground to wait for her. The other had built her into a weapon and kept the trigger in his own mouth. She had spent her life as the second one's. She was, as of half past two in the morning, the first one's instrument as well, and the two of them ran in opposite directions through the same woman, and she did not yet know which way she would finally point.

The gate. She went to the gate.

She came out onto the road on the south side and walked, not fast, a tired woman with a labourer's bag going home off a late shift, and the sodium light took her and let her go. The first auto that came she did not take, because the driver looked at the bag; the second she took, because he did not. She gave him Kashmere Gate, and would change there for somewhere that was not where she slept. The proof rode on her knees with her hand flat on it, and through the nylon and the oilcloth and the newspaper she could feel the cold edge of the tins, the analog cold of a thing that no current ran through, that no machine on the planet had ever known existed, that had waited under the city for eighteen years to be lifted into a living hand, and was now lifted, and was now hers to carry, and could be carried only one more time, to one table, and would have to be carried soon. Because in the days she had left before the convergence the man who had built her trigger would want her close, and would say, when he wanted her closest, the words she had hidden from everyone that she could not survive hearing in his voice.

The auto turned onto the road. Behind her, in the dark of the walled ground, the grave settled over what was no longer in it.
