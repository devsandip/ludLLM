The reader had come from a shuttered newspaper in Daryaganj, and it smelled of it: hot dust on a cooling bulb, the particular sourness of a machine that had spent thirty years in a room where men smoked. Mira had carried it up the four flights herself, refusing help, and now it sat on the table between them with its hood lifted like the bonnet of an old car, its ground-glass screen the colour of weak tea.

Sita threaded the first reel. She did it the way she did everything with her hands, without hurry, feeding the leader between the glass flats, seating the sprockets, drawing the slack through until the film lay flat and the take-up spool bit. Her mother had wound this. The thought arrived without permission and she set it down again, the way you set down a weapon you are not ready to fire.

"Lamp," Mira said.

Sita turned the switch. The bulb hummed, warmed, threw its square of light up through the film and onto the screen, and the first frame swam into focus.

It was a page. Typed, single-spaced, with the header sliced off by whoever had photographed it, which was itself a discipline: no letterhead, no registry number, nothing that would tell a later reader which building it had been stolen from. Just the body. A list of seven wire transfers routed through a money-changer in Karol Bagh between January and March of 2008, each one small, each one beneath the threshold that would have drawn a filing, each one terminating in the personal account of a man whose name meant nothing to Sita and a designation that meant everything.

Joint Director, Technical. Strategic Forces.

"Missiles," Mira said softly. She had pulled a kitchen chair close and sat with her forearms on her knees, not touching the machine. "She's into the missile command on the first page. She didn't build up to it. She started there."

"She started where the proof was cleanest." Sita advanced the crank. The page slid down and out and the next rose in its place. "You don't lead with the part you can argue. You lead with the part that can't be argued."

The second frame was a reconciliation. Her mother, eighteen years dead and four feet from Sita's hands as printed light, had taken the seven transfers and laid them against a hawala settlement sheet, and against a trade invoice for machine tools that had moved through Dubai and never existed, and against the dates a particular officer had been posted, transferred, promoted. She had drawn the lines herself in a fine hard pencil that the camera had caught as silver. Money in. Posting out. The man rising through the technical directorate on a current nobody had thought to measure because measuring it would have meant admitting the current was there.

Sita read it the way you read a thing made by someone whose hands you have inherited. Not the conclusion. The method. The patience of it. Her mother had not guessed and then gone looking for the proof of her guess, which was the sin of every frightened analyst and every frightened killer; she had let the numbers say it first and only then permitted herself to believe them. There was no triumph anywhere on the page. There was no fear. There was a woman doing arithmetic in a room where the arithmetic could get her killed, and doing it cleanly.

"How many," Sita said.

"Keep going."

She kept going. Frame by frame the network assembled itself on the tea-coloured glass, not as a diagram, never as a diagram, because a diagram could be dismissed as a theory and her mother had refused to give them a theory to dismiss. She gave them instead a ledger of separate, deniable, individually trivial facts, and let the weight of them accumulate until the only thing that could carry the weight was the conclusion she never once wrote down. A signals officer in the south. A man inside the cyber directorate when the cyber directorate was three rooms and a promise. A procurement clerk who approved the right contracts and bought a flat he could not afford in a city he had never been posted to. Each one funded the same way, through the same washing rails, in the same small increments, by the same invisible hand reaching out of Dubai and Bangkok and the long grey corridor of the hawala that also, Sita knew, carried heroin north out of the Crescent and methamphetamine west out of the Triangle. The same pipes. The poison and the placements ran in the same pipes. Baba had told her that as if it were his own discovery, his own dark insight into the unity of the world's filth, and here it was, photographed onto sixteen-millimetre film by a dead woman the year Sita turned eighteen and went up in a hotel lift to do her first sanctioned murder.

She was very still for a moment. Mira noticed, because Mira noticed everything, and said nothing, which was a kind of mercy and a kind of tactic and Sita had stopped trying to tell those two apart.

"Twenty years," Sita said. "He told me twenty years of building. He had the shape of it exactly. He must have read this."

"Or he reconstructed it himself, later, from the money." Mira's voice was careful. "Two people can find the same elephant in the dark. It doesn't mean they're holding hands."

"It means there was an elephant."

The crank turned. The reel was thinning on the feed side, fattening on the take-up, and the lamp had warmed the little room until Sita could smell the film itself, the faint vinegar of acetate that had begun, even sealed in its tin in the dark under Lothian, the slow chemistry of its own decay. Eighteen years. It would not have given them another eighteen. Her mother had buried a clock and the clock had nearly run out, and the only reason it had not was that the clock had been wound around the neck of a child and the child had survived.

The placements ended. There came a different kind of page.

This one had not been typed by her mother. The font was wrong, heavier, an old daisy-wheel machine that struck the paper hard, and the layout was the layout of an order and not an analysis. It had been photographed straight, square to the camera, with a care that told Sita her mother had wanted it read exactly, had framed it the way you frame the one piece of evidence you cannot afford to have called a forgery.

It was a minute sheet. An internal note moving an inquiry from one status to another. The inquiry was identified only by a file number and a phrase, and the phrase was her mother's working title for what she had found, dry and bureaucratic and therefore unbearable: ANOMALOUS PLACEMENT, STRATEGIC ESTABLISHMENT. And the note said, in the flat language that institutions used when they were doing the worst thing they would ever do, that the inquiry was to be closed. Not paused. Not transferred. Closed, the holdings sealed, access restricted, and the matter not to be reopened save on the authority of the office that had closed it. The justification was a single clause. Sita read it twice.

Surfacing at this juncture assessed to compromise live equities and precipitate consequences disproportionate to the demonstrated threat.

"There," Mira breathed. She was leaning in now, the carefulness gone out of her, the analyst in her surfacing whole. "That's it. That's the institution killing it. That's the thing they'll deny exists. She didn't just catch the network, she caught the building burying the network. Sita, this is the whole" She stopped, because Sita had not moved, and Sita not moving was a sound louder than anything Mira could have said.

Because the minute sheet was initialled.

It carried, at the foot, the seal-block of the office that had ordered the closing, and beneath the seal a set of initials in ink, the ink gone brown the way ink goes, photographed sharp enough that the pen's pressure showed, the little hook where the hand had lifted. And beside the initials, because her mother had wanted no ambiguity, no later argument about whose hand it was, she had photographed alongside it a second document, an establishment list, a roster of appointments for that quarter, with one line ringed in the same fine silver pencil. The seal-block matched the line. The line carried the name.

Luthra. Sunil Luthra. Chief.

Sita heard the bulb hum. She heard, four floors down, a scooter cough and fail to start and cough again. She heard her own pulse, which she had been trained out of hearing, and which she heard now.

"You know the name," Mira said. It was not a question. She had read it off Sita's face, the one place Sita's training had never quite finished its work, and there was no point in lying to it.

"I know the name."

She knew the man at one remove and at the deepest remove there was. She had sat on a wooden floor in the mountains across from a man who slept beside a dead watch and a faded photograph and a silence he had bought at the price of everything, and she had asked that man the only question she had carried up the mountain, which was whether you could break from your maker and live, and he had answered her by being alive. Kabir. Kabir had killed his maker. Colonel Luthra, former Chief, who had been, the way Kabir said it, at peace with dying at his protege's hands, a man choosing the manner of his ending. Sita had taken from the monastery a method and a warning and the cold company of a man who had done the thing and survived it hollow. She had not taken his maker's name as anything but a wound in another man's story.

And here was the name again, in brown ink, at the foot of the order that had sealed her mother's discovery and, in sealing it, had left her mother standing alone in the open where the network could reach her. The man Kabir had killed. The man who had buried her mother before the network finished the job. The two people who had guided her out of Baba's hands, the dead analyst whose lullaby she had hummed her whole life without knowing it was a map, and the living killer who had taught her how to spend the one moment of slack a leash allowed, were joined at this single brown initial. Luthra had made Kabir. Luthra had buried Janaki. Sita had been steered, the whole way, along a track laid by one man's decision in 2008, and the man was already dead, killed by the only friend she had, for reasons that had nothing to do with her and now had everything.

"He sealed it for reasons of state." Sita said it slowly, testing whether saying it changed anything. It did not. "He looked at this, all of this, and he decided the country couldn't survive the medicine. So he buried the diagnosis and let the patient keep the disease."

"He let her die." Mira's mouth was a thin line. "Sealing it left her exposed. She had the only copy outside the registry and the registry was closed against her. Whoever the network used didn't have to fight the building to get to her. The building had already stepped back."

"He didn't kill her."

"He didn't have to."

Sita looked at the screen for a long time. The order sat there in its square of light, patient, brown, four feet from her hands and eighteen years from the hand that had photographed it. She thought of how her mother must have stood over this very minute sheet, in whatever room she had stolen it in, and understood in that moment that the institution would not save her, that the highest office in the building had read her work and called it a disproportion, and that she was therefore already a dead woman with a clock and a child and a silver pendant and one cold idea. Sita had assumed, when she let herself assume anything, that her mother had been a frightened woman who had stumbled onto something too big. She saw now that her mother had not been frightened in any way that mattered. She had been precise. She had built this, every clean pencil line of it, knowing it would be the last thing she built, and she had built it not to save herself, which was already lost, but to be read. By whoever could read it. By a daughter she would never meet, taught a song she would not know was a key, sent up out of a grave eighteen years later to thread a reel of dying film in a dead newspaper's machine and learn her mother's competence by reading it.

The competence was the indictment. Not the conclusion, the competence. A frightened amateur could be dismissed; a theory could be sealed. But this was not amateur and it was not theory. This was a professional doing professional work to a professional standard, and the building's answer to professional work, in the one case where professional work touched the thing the building could not afford to have touched, had been to close the file and walk away from the woman who did it. The institution had not failed to see. It had seen perfectly, and chosen. That was the wound the proof carried up out of the ground. Not that the state was blind. That it could see, and weigh, and decide that a daughter's mother was the lighter pan of the scale.

"This is enough to burn them," Mira said, and there was something almost gentle under the hunger in it, as if she knew she was reaching into a grief and trying to make it useful, which was her job and her nature and her affliction. "Do you understand what you've got? The placements name the network. The seal names the man who buried it. We don't need the cassette. We don't need anything else. This alone, in the right hands"

"In whose hands." Sita said it without heat. She had reached up and stilled the crank, though the crank was already still. "Luthra's dead. Kabir killed him six years ago in a mountain we both came down from. The man who signed this is gone. So who do I take it to. The building that sealed it? It's the same building. The faces have changed and the seal-block is the same seal-block." She turned and looked at Mira directly, the way she rarely did, because looking directly was a thing you spent and she was spending it now. "You want this to be the end of a road. A name, a seal, a villain who can be shown to the world. But it isn't the end of a road. It's the start of one. Because the disease he buried is still in the body. Twenty years older. About to wake up. And the only man moving against it tonight is the man who stole me from this woman's house."

Mira held her eyes and did not look away, which Sita respected, and said nothing for long enough that the bulb's hum became the loudest thing in the room.

"You're going back to him," Mira said at last. Flat. Not an accusation, quite. The recognition of a thing already decided in a place neither of them controlled.

Sita did not answer that. She reached past the screen and lifted the take-up spool, freeing it, and began to wind the reel back by hand, slowly, returning her mother's work into its dark, frame by frame, the missile officer and the cyber clerk and the procurement man and the brown initial all sliding back up off the tea-coloured glass and into the tin where they had waited eighteen years for someone with the right eye. The lamp threw the last empty frame, white and humming, and then she switched it off and the room went to the colour of the street outside, sodium and shadow.

In the new dark her hand found the cassette where it lay on the table, the one she had carried up out of the grave and had not yet been able to play. The plastic was cold. The metadata on its label, in her mother's small hard hand, said only a date and a word, and the word was not a name, and she had told herself in the cemetery that the cassette could only be wielded by being carried and presented in person, which was true, and was also the lie she had used to keep from listening.

She put it in her coat. Not the reels. The reels stayed in the tin, and the tin would go where Mira's people could keep it safe and Sita could not, because the proof was no use to anyone if it died with her in whatever the next week was going to be. But the cassette went into the inside pocket, against her chest, where for eighteen years a different object had hung that had also turned out to be a map she could not read until the right night.

"Not in the right hands," she said, answering the question Mira had stopped asking. She stood. "In the only ones."
