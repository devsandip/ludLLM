The room was on the second floor above a tyre shop in Mori Gate, and it had been cheap because the window gave onto a wall. Sita preferred it for that. A wall did not watch back. She had paid in cash for two nights, given the boy at the desk a name that was not hers and a face he would not remember, and carried the trunk up herself in two trips, wrapped in a jute sack so that it might have been anything, a mechanic's tools, a length of pipe.

Now the contents lay on the floor on a spread bedsheet, because the floor was the only clean surface and because she wanted them where she could see all of them at once. Six tins of microfilm, small and grey, each scored on the lid with a number she had not yet earned the means to read. The ledger, its cover swollen with damp, its pages a column of dead words against a column of living ones. And the cassette, in its cracked plastic shell, the label gone soft and brown with eighteen years of ground water, a single line of pencil on it that the years had reduced to a ghost of graphite.

She had bought the deck that morning from a repair lane off Lajpat Rai, a two-in-one the size of a brick, the kind that took four batteries and played a radio nobody listened to any more. The man had wanted to test it for her. She had told him she trusted him and paid before he could insist. He had thought her strange and had been glad to see her go. People were always glad to see her go; it was one of the few honest reactions she ever drew from them.

The deck sat now on the bedsheet with the cassette beside it, and she had been looking at the two of them for the better part of an hour without touching either.

This was not like her. She knew the symptom and she named it, the way Baba had taught her to name things so they could not own her. Avoidance. She had carried the trunk out of the grave at Lothian and she had read the ledger twice and she had let her mind walk all the way up to the cassette and then turn aside, every time, the way a horse refuses a jump it has decided is a wall. There was a thing on the tape. She had known there was a thing on the tape since the cemetery, when she lifted it out of the wet earth and felt the small slack weight of it and her own hand had not wanted to close around it.

She made herself take the ledger again instead, because the ledger was paper and paper could be read coldly.

It was a working document. That much had been plain from the first. A cipher key, a Rosetta in a dead woman's hand: on the left a column of code-words, the munshi's nonsense, the harmless freight names she had spent weeks decoding out of the hawaladar's books; on the right the things they stood for. Names. Postings. A man set against a missile regiment in the desert. A man set against a cell that wrote the country's defence software in a glass building in the south. Twenty years of patient placement laid out like a seating plan, the foreign hand that had done the placing visible in the spaces between, the way a sniper is visible in the line his shot makes and nowhere else.

She had read all that. She had understood, in the cemetery, that this was the diagnosis her mother's murder had been meant to bury, the thing Luthra had sealed for reasons of state and left a woman to die over. She had carried that understanding for two days now and it had not moved her the way she expected understanding to move her. It had sat in her like a stone swallowed whole, present, weighty, undigested.

What she had not done, until now, was look at the front of the ledger.

Analysts began their books the way clerks did, with a header, a hand they were trained to keep neutral because the book might be read by a tribunal one day, or an enemy. The header on this one had been written in the same neutral hand as the rest. A reference number. A classification she was no longer cleared to see and had never been cleared to see. A date, which she read and held against the date carved into the grave and found to be the same year. And a name, in the box where the originating officer signed her work so that the building would know whose judgement to trust or to break.

Janaki.

She sat with it. It was a common enough name. There would be ten thousand Janakis in this city alone, women selling vegetables, women teaching school, women who had never seen a hawala ledger in their lives. The name meant nothing. She told herself the name meant nothing and she went on telling herself this while her thumb moved, without instruction, to the hollow of her throat, to the small flat shape that had hung there against her skin since before she had words, the keepsake she had worn through eleven years of work and never once removed, not in the shower, not under the knife, not on the nights she had killed and the nights she had been nearly killed. The thing Mira had taught her was a cipher. The thing she had decoded with a tune she had never been taught and had always known.

The tune was in her now. It had been in her, faintly, for two days, the way a smell stays in a room. She caught herself humming it and stopped.

She put the ledger down. She picked up the cassette.

It was a small mechanical act, the kind she had performed ten thousand times with magazines and bolts and the bodies of strangers, and her hands did it without trembling because her hands had been trained out of trembling. She thumbed the eject. The deck's door swung up with a tired click. She fitted the cassette into the spindles, checked that the tape had not gone brittle and broken in the years underground, wound the slack out of it a quarter turn with her little finger the way you did with old tape so it would not foul on the heads. She closed the door. She set the volume low, because the wall was thin and because some animal part of her did not want the room to hear this any louder than it had to.

She pressed play.

For a moment there was only the hiss, the dense underwater hiss of old ferric tape, and the slow mechanical breathing of the deck, and she thought, with something that was almost relief, that the ground had got into it after all, that there would be nothing, that she had carried up two flights of stairs an hour of damaged silence.

Then a woman spoke.

The voice was low and close to the microphone, the way you speak when you do not want to be overheard, when the recording is being made in a flat at night with the lights off and an engine idling in the street below. It was an unremarkable voice. A Delhi voice, educated, tired. It said a date, the same date, flatly, for the record, the way an officer logs a tape so the tape can stand up later. It gave a reference number that matched the one on the ledger. It said, This is for my daughter, and then it stopped, and Sita heard the woman swallow, a small wet sound the tape had kept for eighteen years, and begin again.

The voice did not weep. That was the thing Sita would carry afterwards, when she carried very little else cleanly. The voice did not weep and did not plead and did not waste a second of the tape on what the woman felt, because the woman had measured how much tape she had and how much night, and she had decided that her feelings were not load-bearing and her daughter's life was. She spoke the way Sita herself spoke on the rare debriefs when everything had gone wrong and there was no profit in saying so. She gave the location of the proof in the clear, in case the cipher failed and the child never learned to read it. She gave the names of two people who might be trusted and the longer list of those who could not, and Luthra's name was on the longer list, spoken without heat, a fact entered into evidence. She said that the men who would come had been her own people once and that this was the part the child must understand, that the house had not held; that there was no outside to run to because the rot was on the inside, in the walls, in the people who were paid to be the walls.

She said, They will come within the month. I have perhaps that long. I am sorry I cannot give you more than a song and a box in the ground.

And then, because there was tape left and because there was nothing else of use to say, the woman began to hum.

Sita knew it on the second bar. She had known it, in truth, from the first note, before her mind would let her, the way you know a face in a crowd a full second before you let yourself believe it is the face you have been afraid to see. It was her tune. It was the tune she had hummed over corpses and in the backs of cars and on a cot in a safe house with Baba's hand on her hair, the tune she had thought was hers the way her own pulse was hers, made in her, belonging to no one. It was not hers. It had been put into her, the way the cipher had been put into the pendant and the pendant put around her neck, by this woman, in this room, on this night, with these men idling in the street below, in the last hour she had been allowed to be anybody's mother.

The deck breathed. The tape hissed. The humming went on, unhurried, a lullaby sung at the proper speed for a child who must be soothed and not frightened, sung by a woman counting the minutes she had left to be alive against the bars of a song, and Sita understood, all at once and far too late and exactly on the schedule the woman had set for her eighteen years before, what she was hearing.

She was hearing the analyst.

She had spent two days reconstructing the analyst the way she reconstructed everyone, from the documents the analyst left behind, with a professional's respect for a professional, building the woman up out of her ledger and her tradecraft and the cold clean nerve it had taken to copy what could not be copied and hide what could not be hidden and trust the work to an infant because there was no adult left who could be trusted. She had admired her. That was the obscene part, the part she would have to live inside now. She had stood at the foot of a stranger's grave and admired the dead woman's craft as one operator admires another, and the woman in the grave was her mother, and the work she had admired was the work of a mother spending the last of her life to reach across eighteen years and pull her daughter's head out from under the water.

Janaki. The name on the ledger and the body in the ground and the voice on the tape and the hand that had tied the cord around a newborn's throat so gently, the same throat that wore it still. One woman. Not three threads she had been holding apart without noticing she held them apart. One woman, who had been murdered to keep this song from being sung, and who had sung it anyway, into a tape, into a child, into the dark, against the engine in the street.

Mummy.

The word arrived in her unbidden and in a child's register, a word she had no memory of ever using because the woman who owned it had been taken from her before she could form it, and it went through her cleanly, the way the only honest things ever did, without resistance, because she had no training against it. There had been no drill for this. Baba had built her against fear and against pain and against the dying words of strangers, against everything a man could imagine an enemy doing to her body or her resolve. He had not built her against her mother's voice. He could not have. To build her against it he would have had to let her know it existed, and the whole architecture of her life, she saw now, had been the work of keeping her from this room and this tape and this single unremarkable Delhi voice humming a child to sleep while the men waited downstairs.

He had been in this. Not on the tape; the tape was older than her knowing of him. But after. He had come to the house, or sent the men, or stood among them; the order of it did not matter and she would probably never have the order of it. He had taken her out of the wreck of this woman's life and called it rescue and built a fire that had never burned, and he had raised her on the milk of that lie, and every soft thing he had ever done for her, every steady word, every time his voice had reached into her and made her calm, had been done by a man who had heard this tape's existence as a threat and the child wearing its key as an asset to be turned.

She did not weep either. She noticed that about herself with a flat, distant contempt. The woman on the tape had not wept and the woman listening to it could not, and the failure was the same failure, except that the mother had chosen hers and the daughter had had hers installed.

The humming faded. The woman did not say goodbye. She let the song run down the way a real lullaby runs down, into quiet, into the assumption of sleep, as though she were only stepping out of a child's room and not out of the world, as though there would be a morning. The tape ran on into hiss. Sita let it run. She let it run all the way to the end and listened to the empty tape the way you sit with a body after the breath has stopped, because to reach over and press the key would be to do the thing the men in the street had come to do, and she found she could not be the one to stop the sound.

The deck reached the end of its side and the play key released itself with a loud plastic snap that went through her like a round going off, and the room was only a room again, a rented room above a tyre shop with a wall outside the window, and a young woman sitting on a bedsheet among the relics of a murder that turned out to be the first fact of her own life.

She put her hand to her throat and held the small flat shape of it, warm now from her skin, the cipher and the keepsake and the cord her mother's fingers had knotted, and she did not take it off. She had thought, dimly, in the cemetery, that the thing in the trunk would be a weapon she could carry to someone who knew how to use it. She had been right and she had been a fool, the way the well-trained are fools, mistaking the size of a thing because they have measured only its outside. This was not intelligence. The ledger was intelligence; the tins were intelligence; she would carry those to Kabir as she had decided, and Kabir would know what a man did with such things in a country whose walls did not hold.

The tape was not intelligence. The tape was her mother, and she had just buried her, eighteen years late, in a room nobody knew, with no one to help her carry it.

She wound the cassette back to its head with her little finger, slowly, the quarter-turn and then the rest, so the tape would not foul, so it would play again, for her, when she was strong enough to be that weak. She fitted it into the inner pocket against her chest, beside the pendant, where the two of them lay over the same few inches of skin her mother had reached for in the dark. Then she folded the ledger into the oilcloth and stacked the six grey tins and began, with steady and entirely untrustworthy hands, to pack for the road north, toward the monastery and the one man alive who had killed the man who made him, because he, at least, would not flinch from what she was about to ask, and because there was no one left between her and Baba but Kabir and the song she now knew was a leash her mother had tied first.
