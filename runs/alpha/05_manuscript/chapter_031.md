The room had no air conditioning, only a pedestal fan that pushed the heat from one corner to another and called it relief. Sita sat on the floor with her back against the bedframe and let it find her, lose her, find her again, and after a while she stopped registering it at all. Outside the shuttered window Delhi cooked. It was the worst week of June, the city holding its breath for a monsoon that had not come, and the day's heat had soaked into the concrete and stayed there long after dark, so that the walls were warm to the touch and the air in the room tasted of warm cement and her own sweat.

On the bedsheet in front of her she had laid out her mother's work.

She had laid it out the way she laid out anything she meant to understand. Six microfilm tins in a row, lids up, the spools inside dull as old coins. The cipher-key ledger open to the page where the dead code words married the living names. The cassette rewound and set square on its own in the centre, apart from the documents, because it did not belong with them. The documents were evidence. The cassette was a voice.

She had played it three times. She would not play it again. She knew it the way she knew the lullaby, which was to say she had known it before she heard it, the words arriving a half beat ahead of the tape as though they had been waiting in her all her life for someone to press the right key. A woman saying her name. Not the name Baba gave her. The other one, the first one, the one nobody had used since she was three weeks old.

She had stopped weeping some time ago. The weeping had been a physical event, like being sick, something the body did and then was done with, and what came after was worse because it was clear. She made herself work. Work was the only room left in her that she could still stand inside.

So she did what she had been built to do, which was to take a thing apart until it could not surprise her, and she began where an analyst would begin, where her mother would have begun, with the question: who else had known, and when, and how had it been closed.

Because a secret like this did not stay sealed by itself. Janaki had hidden the proof and died. The proof had survived in the ground under Lothian for eighteen years. For eighteen years no one had dug it up, and that was not luck. Luck did not last eighteen years against a service that hunted for sport. Someone had kept the file closed. Someone had walked the perimeter all that time, finding the threads and cutting them before they could be pulled.

She thought about the threads. A journalist who had caught a scent. A retired officer who had kept a copy of a thing he should have burned. A man Janaki had trusted, who knew there was a record and knew it had not surfaced, and who one ordinary morning decided the country deserved to hear it. Over eighteen years there would have been several. Loose ends did not announce themselves. You found them and tied them off, quietly, with deniability, and you used the cleanest instrument you had.

She was reaching for the ledger when the memory came up through the floor and took her by the throat.

It did not arrive as a thought. It arrived as a smell. White tablecloth and the green bitterness of crushed mint, the punishing cold of a hotel restaurant in summer where they kept the air low so the rich could wear their good clothes without sweating. She was eighteen. It was her birthday. Baba had put her in a dress, which he had never done before and never did again, and watched her across the table with that particular stillness of his that she had spent her whole life learning to read as love.

She had not been afraid. That was the thing she remembered most clearly afterward, in the years when she let herself remember it at all. She had not been afraid because he was there, and where he was, the world was arranged correctly. He let her finish the meal. He let her have the dessert, a thing of spun sugar and saffron she had not wanted but ate because he had ordered it for her, and when the plate was cleared he had laid the room key on the cloth between them and slid it across with two fingers.

Nine zero four. He had not said a word. He had not needed to. She had known the shape of the work the way she knew her own hands, and she had been ready for it for two years, hungry for it, because it was the door into being what he was, into being useful to him in the one way that mattered. She had picked up the key. She had gone up alone, watching her own calm face in the brushed steel of the lift, and let herself into nine zero four.

The man inside had been expecting room service.

Older. Sixties, perhaps. A soft, grey, careful man in a hotel dressing gown, reading glasses pushed up on his forehead, a folder of papers open on the bed beside him. He had looked at her and understood at once, the way some of them did, the ones who had lived near the trade long enough to recognise its face. He had not shouted. He had not gone for the telephone or the door. He had taken the glasses off his forehead, folded them, set them down on the papers, and looked at her with an expression she had had no name for then and could not be rid of now.

He had not been afraid either. That was what had lodged in her, a splinter under the skin for eleven years. She had killed men who wept and men who bargained and men who soiled themselves, and she had filed them all under the heading of the work and felt nothing. This one had looked at her like a man recognising a debt come due. He had said something she had not understood, and she had done the work and gone down in the lift and let Baba fold her into the car and drive her home, his made thing, his daughter, blooded at last.

She sat now on the floor of the hot room with her mother's tins before her and made herself hear it again. All of it. Not the version she had filed, which was short and clean and meant nothing, but the thing itself.

He had looked at her, and then his eyes had moved, just slightly, down from her face to her throat, and they had stopped there. On the pendant. The cheap dull silver thing she had worn against her skin since before memory, that Baba said had come off her dead mother and that he had let her keep out of mercy, the only object in the world older than her life with him. The man on the bed had looked at the pendant, and something had gone out of his face, some last small hope, and he had said, very quietly, almost as though it were a kindness:

"So she kept her word."

And then, when she did not move, when she only stood there because the words meant nothing and were therefore not an obstacle: "You don't know, do you. They sent her own."

She had thought, eleven years ago, that he was raving. That the nearness of death had loosened something and he was speaking to a person who was not in the room. She had been trained to discount what the dying said. The dying said anything. Harish Bedi had told her with his last breath that he was innocent, and she had let it slide off her like water, because Baba had chosen him, and Baba did not choose the innocent. Every target had a story in which he was wronged. You learned not to listen. Listening was a door, and through the door came doubt, and doubt got operators killed. She had not listened. She had done the work cleanly, the way she did everything cleanly, and she had been proud of it, and Baba had told her she was ready, and she had carried that pride for eleven years like a medal pinned to the inside of her chest.

She understood now what the man on the bed had been.

She understood it with the cold completeness of an analyst summing a column, the figures finally coming out. He had known her mother. He had known there was a record, that it had been hidden, that it had not been found, because if it had been found there would have been no further reason to go on killing the people who knew. He had been one of the threads. Perhaps he had come into possession of a corner of the thing, or only of the knowledge that it existed and roughly where to look, and he had decided, in his soft grey careful sixties, to do what Janaki had died trying to do, to carry it up the line to someone who would have to act.

And he had glanced up from his folder and seen, walking through the door to kill him, a young woman wearing Janaki's keepsake at her throat. He had not been raving. He had been the only person in eleven years to see the whole of it in a single look. He had understood, in the last half minute of his life, that the men who used the service had taken the murdered woman's infant daughter and grown her into the instrument that would close the very file her mother had opened with her death. They had sent her own. He had said it to be merciful, to let her know, and she had not had the ears to hear it. She had killed him for the cover up of her mother, and not known, and gone home and thanked the man who arranged it.

She got up off the floor, because if she stayed down she was going to be sick again, and she crossed to the window and put her forehead against the warm slats of the shutter.

She had thought, when she first understood that Janaki was her mother, that the wound was the murder. That the thing done to her was the killing of the woman on the cassette, eighteen years ago, before she could form a memory, a clean grief, terrible but at least belonging to the past, done to her when she was too small to be a party to anything. A wound she could lay flowers on.

This was not that. This was worse, because she had been a party to it. She had been the hands. The blooding that made her his, the moment she had treasured above every other moment of her life, the door into being his daughter, that was the same act as the murder of her mother's last witness. The leash and the wound were not two things she carried side by side, one in each hand, to be set down and picked up separately. They were one object. The proudest thing she had ever done and the most violated thing ever done to her were the same event, the same room, the same key crossing the same white cloth under the same two fingers. And she had loved him for it. She had gone down in the lift loving him for it.

Had he known.

That was the question that sat under all the others, the one she had been circling without letting herself land on it. When Baba slid the key across the cloth, when he chose nine zero four for her eighteenth birthday and dressed her like a daughter and let her have the saffron sugar, had he known what the man upstairs was. Had he known that the loose end he was sending his made thing to tie off was a thread that ran straight back to the woman whose pendant the made thing wore at her throat.

She wanted, with a violence that frightened her, for the answer to be no.

She wanted him handed a name and a room number off the books by the same machine that handed everyone names and room numbers, the cover up running so deep and so old that even Baba did not see the whole of it, that he had blooded her on her mother's witness by an accident of bureaucratic cruelty and never seen the shape of it himself. She wanted that because if it were true then the love had been real even where the rest was poison, and a person could live on a real love, even one. She had seen Kabir live on less, on the cold fact that the man he killed had wanted to die. You could build a survivable thing on a single true beam.

But she had spent her whole life being lied to by this man with exactly this kind of mercy. The staged rescue. The fire that never burned. The mother killed by an enemy who had in fact been Baba's own hand on the cord, and the long gentle story told to the child to make the unbearable into something she could sleep beside. He arranged things. He arranged meaning. He did not leave symmetry to accident. He was an artist of the made moment, and the made moment of her blooding had been built with the care he gave only to the things that mattered most to him. A man like that did not choose nine zero four by chance on the one birthday in her life he dressed her as his daughter.

He had known.

The certainty did not land as a blow. It came as a settling, the last weight finding its rest, the file closing under its own cover. He had taken her mother. He had grown her up against the day he would need an instrument no one could trace and no one could turn, and somewhere along the road, with the economy of a man who wasted nothing, he had spent her first edge to bury her mother's last witness, and called it her birthday, and watched her thank him for the gift.

She stood with her forehead on the shutter and looked through the gap in the slats at the slice of street below. The parked autos with their meters hooded against the dew that would not fall. A dog asleep in the dust by the kerb. A single tea stall still lit at this hour, the boy banking his coals, the kettle going, the small ordinary unbearable machinery of a city that did not know. The kettle reached its note and the boy lifted it off and the street was quiet again, and none of it had moved at all in the eleven years she had needed to understand one sentence spoken to her by a dying stranger.

Eleven years she had carried that man's last words filed under the things the dying say. She would carry them now as the truest thing anyone had ever told her, said by a soft grey stranger in a dressing gown who had looked at the pendant and read the whole architecture of her in the time it took to fold a pair of reading glasses, and who had spent his last breath not begging but telling, because he must have thought, must have let himself hope, that knowing was a thing she could be given and would one day be able to use.

You don't know, do you. They sent her own.

She knew now.

She straightened up from the window. Behind her on the bedsheet the cassette sat in the centre of the documents where it did not belong, the voice that was evidence of nothing a court would take and everything she would ever again be. She did not turn to look at it yet. She stood a moment longer in the warm dark and let the fan reach her one last time, and this time she let herself feel it, the moving air across her wet face, because it was the only thing in the room that touched her without wanting anything, and then she crossed the floor, knelt, and began to gather up the tins.

Her hands were steady. That was the part of him she could not give back. She fitted the lids to the spools, one by one, and set them in the bag in the order her mother had numbered them, and somewhere under the steadiness, where the work could not reach it, she began for the first time to think not about who had closed the file, but about what it would cost the man who had taught her to close it, when she carried it to him open in her two hands.
