The annex had no windows, and the air ran cold enough that the analysts kept their jackets on. Sita had learned long ago to read a room by its temperature. Cold meant the people who built it expected to be here a long time, and did not expect to sweat. She stood at the back, by the door, where she always stood, and watched the screens do their patient work.

There were nine of them on the wall, tiled in two rows, and a tenth on a stand that someone had wheeled in and not bothered to align. The big feed in the centre was a drone picture, infrared, the cold world rendered in greys and the warm bodies in bone-white. A timestamp ran in the top corner in twenty-four-hour time, ticking through 0214, 0215, and beneath it a string of letters and digits that meant nothing to anyone who had not been briefed and everything to those who had. The corridor it watched was a transhipment yard south of the Beas, the place where the seized rail came to ground before it went on into the plains. She knew the yard. She had inherited it eleven weeks ago, washed money through it, signed for it in a hand that was not her own.

Mira stood beside her with one earpiece in and the other dangling, her head tilted toward the traffic on the net, and on her face was something Sita had not seen there before. Not triumph. Mira was too careful for triumph. It was the looser thing that comes after a held breath, the small slackening at the jaw of a person who has decided the danger is behind her.

"They went in at zero two hundred," Mira said, low, for Sita alone. "No contact on the perimeter. They walked it."

"They walked it," Sita repeated.

"Two vehicles in the yard. Both accounted for." A pause while she listened. "They have the consignment. They have the books."

On the centre screen the white shapes moved with the economy of trained men, splitting at the loading dock, flowing along the walls, folding a third shape down onto the concrete without ceremony. A man, kneeling, then prone, then still. The drone held him the way a cat holds a thing it has finished with.

At the long table in the middle of the room, Vikrant Kaul stood with his weight on his knuckles and his reading glasses pushed up into his grey hair, and watched the same picture she watched, and said nothing. He had a way of receiving good news that gave nothing back to the room. A younger service might have wanted noise from its Chief at a moment like this. Kaul gave them stillness, which was its own kind of confirmation. When at last he spoke it was to the duty officer at his elbow, and quietly.

"Confirm the principal."

"Working, sir."

"I want a face, not a callsign."

"Yes, sir."

The face came up four minutes later on the tenth screen, the one nobody had aligned, so that the man's head sat at a slight tilt as though he were listening to something below the floor. They had turned him over and pulled the hood and put a torch on him for the camera, and the cold infrared gave way to ordinary colour, and there he was. Older than the picture she carried in her chest. The same long bones in the face. A cut above the eye that had already stopped bleeding because his blood pressure had dropped to the floor with his life.

Fateh. Baba. The man who had walked her up to the eighth floor of her own self and shown her the room she would live in for the rest of her days.

Somebody in the room let out a breath through their teeth, a thin satisfied sound. Sita kept her hands at her sides and her face the surface of still water, and inside her nothing rose at all, which frightened her more than grief would have. She had braced for grief. She had not braced for the absence where it should have been. They had taught her, the two of them, her mother in the cradle and her maker at the table, that she could be made into an instrument that felt the recoil and not the wound. Here was the proof of their work. Her teacher was on a screen with a torch in his face and she was reading the screen for tradecraft.

"That's him," Mira said. She said it to Sita, but it travelled, and a junior at the near terminal half turned, hungry to be the one who had been standing next to the woman who knew.

"That's him," Sita said.

Kaul straightened off his knuckles and pulled his glasses down onto his nose and looked, properly, the way a man looks when he is signing for a thing. "Status."

"Down, sir. He didn't surrender."

"No," Kaul said. "He wouldn't."

There was a respect in it that she had not expected, and she filed it, because everything was evidence now and she did not yet know what it would prove. Kaul had hunted this man for the better part of a year and had wanted him alive, and had got him dead, and was disappointed in the clean professional way of a man who would have liked the interrogation. He did not know what he had lost. None of them knew. That was the thing she stood at the back of the cold room and understood alone, and could not say.

Because the picture was perfect. She made herself look at it the way the room looked at it, to be sure she was not inventing the flaw out of her own need for one. A rogue unit, off the books, no owner, funded by the poison it pushed through Punjab. Run by a former officer who had gone past the wire and built his own war. For a year he had been a rumour with a codename, and tonight the rumour had a face and the face was cooling on a stand-mounted screen, and in the yard south of the Beas the service had its hands on the consignment and the books, the whole filthy ledger of the thing, the proof that a cancer had grown in the body and had now been cut out entire. The pipeline seized. The unit cornered and ended. The evidence in the bag. Every box on the board with a tick beside it. Kaul could go to South Block in the morning and lay it on the table and it would hold. It would all hold.

It was too clean, and she knew exactly why, and the why was the worst thing she had ever carried into a room.

"Arvind." Kaul turned, including the table. "Your people built the financial picture that put us in that yard. I won't forget it."

Arvind Sehgal had been standing a little apart, near the climate unit, with the patience of a man thirty years into a career that had taught him patience was the only thing that compounded. He inclined his head, accepting the credit without reaching for it, which was the correct amount to reach.

"The credit is the desk's, Chief. I only carried it up the stairs." A small, exact smile. "I'm glad it came to something. One is never sure, with money. It points, but it doesn't fire."

"It pointed true tonight."

"It did." Sehgal's gaze moved, unhurried, across the wall of screens, across the prone shapes in the yard and the still face on the stand, and came at last to rest, for no longer than it rested on anything else, on Sita by the door. There was nothing in it. That was what she marked. A man encountering an unfamiliar asset in a secure room would let some question cross his face, even a trained man, even for a quarter-second. Sehgal let nothing cross his. He looked at her the way one looks at a chair, and moved on, and said to Kaul, "We should let the desk write it up while it's warm. The first account is always the true one. By morning everyone will have improved it."

"By morning," Kaul agreed, "the Cabinet Secretary will want to know it was always inevitable."

A low laugh went round the table, the laugh of tired men who have won and are allowed, for an hour, to be human about it. Mira's shoulder, beside her, came down another half-inch. On the net in her dangling earpiece a voice was reading a manifest, slow, item by item, the way you read when somebody senior is going to hold you to every word: so many kilos, so many bundles of currency, serial blocks, the count of weapons, the count of bodies. Two dead in the yard. The principal. One taken alive at the gate and already in a vehicle heading south to a place that did not officially exist. The arithmetic of a finished thing.

Sita let the arithmetic run past her and watched the edges of the screen.

It was a habit a dead man on a stand had not given her. A living one in a monastery had. Watch where the hunt refuses to look, Kabir had said, and you will find the hand that is steering it. She had used the lesson to find the steering on the hunt that had been chasing her, and she used it now, on the hunt that had won, because the lesson did not care whose night it was.

The big feed had widened while she wasn't looking, the drone climbing to take in the approaches, and there at the margins of the picture, at the grey limit where the infrared began to lose faith in itself, she found them. Two vehicles. Three. Moving away from the yard on the service road that ran east toward the canal, moving without lights and without haste, in the disciplined spacing of people who had decided some minutes ago that the night was lost and the correct response to a lost night was not to run but to leave. Nobody was following them. Every white shape that the service controlled was inside the yard, folding the take, counting the bundles, holding the torch on her maker's face. The eye of the operation was full of its prize and had no attention left for its margins, and through the margins, unpursued, the vehicles went out of the frame one after another and were gone into the dark country toward the canal.

She knew what those vehicles were. She had spent three weeks learning what they were. They were the reason the pipeline had been seized and not simply burned. They were the assets the seizure was meant to summon into the open, the deep-buried people who could not let the rail stay in another man's hands and so had had to come, tonight, here, to this yard, to take it back. The whole architecture of her maker's last war had been built to make them surface and converge, so that a single deniable hour could close over all of them at once and roll them up before the thing they had spent twenty years building could ever be switched on. They had surfaced. They had converged. They had driven into the trap exactly as the trap required.

And the service, hunting the man who had built the trap, had walked into the yard at zero two hundred and sprung it on the wrong jaw. They had taken the rail and the rogue and the books and the credit, and while they took them, the people the trap was for had counted their losses, decided the rail was a price they could pay, and reversed out through an opening that no longer had anyone watching it, because the only operation in the world that had been watching it was lying prone on the concrete with a torch in his face.

The catastrophe was not in the room. The catastrophe was in the margins of the screen, driving east without lights, and she was the only one alive in this cold place who could see it, and she could not say a single word.

She ran it forward, fast, the way she had been built to run a thing forward. Tell them. Stand off the wall and say to Kaul, the network is real, the men you let drive away are the network, you have just destroyed the only operation that could have caught them and now it is loose and live and closer to its hour than it has ever been. And then Kaul says, what network, and on what authority, and who are you, and the only authority she had was a dead analyst's microfilm in a buried trunk and her own face on a watchlist as the codename they had just told themselves they had ended tonight. The instant she made the network real to this room she made herself real to it, and the room would not hear the second sentence before it acted on the first. And there was the other reason, the colder one, standing near the climate unit with his exact small smile, watching the arithmetic with the patience of thirty compounding years. She did not know, she could not prove, that Sehgal was the hand. The fit was too clean and she distrusted clean fits the way she distrusted cold rooms. But she knew this: whoever the hand was, it was somewhere in the chain that had just turned the state's blade so precisely onto Fateh and so completely away from the men by the canal, and to speak the truth aloud in this room was to speak it into that hand's ear. She had learned, at eighteen, in a hotel room she had not understood, what happened to people who knew too much about this with no one to protect them. Her mother had taught her the rest of it from inside a grave.

So she said nothing. She had been made for saying nothing. She let her face be water.

"You're somewhere else," Mira murmured. The earpiece was out now, both of them, coiled in her fist. Her eyes were warm, and tired, and pleased, and they searched Sita's with the particular care of a person who has handled another person across a great distance and brought her home and wants, now, only to be told the journey was worth it. "I thought you'd want to see it. After everything. I thought you'd want to be the one in the room when it ended."

It hasn't ended, Sita did not say. You have shown me a true thing and believed it was the whole thing, and you turned me with the part you could see, and the part you cannot see is driving east toward the canal with its lights off. You are the best of them and you cannot see it either, and if I make you see it I unmake you, and I have run out of people I am willing to unmake.

"It's a clean job," she said instead. "Whoever planned it."

"The desk planned it. Arvind's people."

"I know." She looked across the room at the still long bones in the man's face on the unaligned screen, the cut above his eye that would never finish healing now, and she felt the absence again where the wound should have been, the structure standing with no one home in it, and she understood that Kabir had been telling her the plain truth and not a riddle. "It's the cleanest thing I've seen."

On the centre feed the drone began its slow descent back toward the yard to count the take a second time, the way the service always counted twice, and the margins of the picture went dark and empty and ordinary, the service road a grey line running off to nothing, no warm shapes on it at all, as if there had never been any. The duty officer was reading the manifest into the record for the third time. Kaul had sat down at last and taken off his glasses and was rubbing the bridge of his nose, a man at the bottom of a long climb, allowing himself one minute on the summit before the descent.

It was a false summit. She had stood on enough of them to know the feel of the ground. You came up out of the cloud onto the level place and the body told you it was over and the map told you it was not, and the only mistake that killed you was believing the body. The service had believed the body. They had the man, the money, the proof, the night, and every dimension of it read as the end, and it was the end of nothing except the one thing that could have saved them.

She put her back against the cold wall and watched them celebrate the catastrophe, and somewhere east of the Beas, under a sky with no moon, the men who would finish what her mother died trying to stop drove home to their cover lives and their command posts and their places at India's table, unwatched, unhurried, and warned in time. Warned, she thought, by someone who had been in a room exactly like this one. Perhaps this one. And she let her eyes travel the wall of screens, slow, edge to edge, the way you read a yard before you walk into it, and she did not let them stop on Sehgal, because a chair does not look back.
