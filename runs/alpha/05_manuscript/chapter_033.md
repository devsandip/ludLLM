The room smelled of cold solder and the river, and the fan turned without moving the air. Sita sat on the floor with her back to the wall, the way she had been taught to sit in a room she did not own, so that the door was always in the corner of her eye and the window threw its light past her and not on her. On the cracked tiles in front of her lay a single sheet of paper, folded twice, sweated soft at the creases. It had come up through three hands to reach her, each hand poorer than the last, and the last had pushed it under the shutter of the motor-parts shop below at four in the morning and gone away on a scooter without its headlight on.

She had read it eleven times. She read it again now, not for what it said.

It was the after-action summary of the Lanka sweep, sanitised, the kind of thing that would be walked around the building in a folder no one signed for and shredded by the weekend. Whoever had typed it had been pleased. You could feel the pleasure under the dead official verbs. A rogue armed network, designated ALPHA, financed through narcotics, neutralised in a single coordinated action on neutral soil. Principal deceased. Logistics seized. Treasury secured. The Crescent rail, the fake notes, the hawala bridges into Bangkok and Dubai, all of it rolled into the state's hand in one night. A clean win. The cleanest in twenty years, the sheet seemed to say, almost shyly, the way a careful man brags.

Baba was the principal. Principal deceased. They had given him three words and no name.

She let the grief move through her once, the way she let a cramp pass in a long lie-up, without fighting it and without following it. It came up through the sternum, sat behind the eyes, and went. She had learned the trick from him. There was an obscenity in that she did not have the energy to examine tonight.

Kabir had taught her the other thing, the thing she did now. You do not read a steered hunt for what it pursues. You read it for what it will not look at. A man building a lie chooses his facts the way a sapper chooses where to leave the ground untouched, and the untouched ground is the map. So she stopped reading the verbs and read the gaps between them.

Treasury secured. Three weeks she had spent in the munshi's ledgers learning what the treasury was. It was not a vault. It was a living thing, a braid of accounts that breathed through Dubai and Bangkok and forty smaller mouths, money that was always somewhere else by the time you named where it was. You could not secure it in a night any more than you could secure a river by closing one sluice. To take it you would have had to take the men who ran it, the hawaladars, the trade-laundering brokers, the patient signatures. The sheet did not name one of them. It said treasury secured and looked at the pipeline, the trucks, the pressed bricks, the things you could photograph and lay out on a tarpaulin for the Chief to walk past with his hands behind his back.

The money had walked. The paper had not even turned its head to watch it go.

She drew a line in her mind from that silence to the next. Principal deceased. Logistics seized. Every noun in the summary was a thing of Baba's. His rail, his notes, his couriers, his house. Not one noun belonged to the other side. Forty-eight days she had been alive to the shape of the other side, the sleeper net, the twenty-year placements in the missile commands and the cyber cells, the names she now carried in a canvas bag against the small of her back because she could not bear to set them down. The summary did not contain a single one of them. It did not contain the category of them. To read it you would think the only enemy India had ever had was the man who had spent his life and his soul trying to kill that enemy off the books.

That was not an omission. An omission is a thing forgotten. This was a thing built. Someone had drawn the frame of the night before the night happened, drawn it exactly the size of Fateh and no larger, so that when the guns went off they would fill the frame and nothing outside it would show.

She heard the scooter come back before she heard the knock, four soft taps and a pause and one, and she was up and to the side of the door with the paper already in her teeth before her mind had finished the count.

It was Mira.

"You look like a corpse," Mira said, coming in past her, and stopped, and looked at the sheet of paper on the floor as if it were a corpse too. She had a thermos and two glasses in a string bag and she set them down on the windowsill with the small domestic care of a woman who has decided to be calm in front of someone she is worried about. "You've seen it."

"I've seen it."

"Then you've seen it's over." Mira poured. The chai had gone the colour of wet brick in the thermos and she poured it anyway, two glasses, and held one out. Her hand was steady. Her eyes were not. There was something working at the back of them that Sita had learned to recognise in Mira over these weeks, a hope that kept trying to land. "Sita. We did it. Whatever he was to you, whatever he made you, the thing he ran is finished. The unit's gone. The money's gone. The man is gone. You're out from under him. That's what we came for."

Sita took the glass because not taking it would have been a sentence in a conversation she did not want to have yet. The heat of it was good against her fingers. She looked at Mira over the rim and saw a clean woman, a woman who had told her a true thing once in a safe house and believed she had told her the whole of it, and who was standing now in the wreckage of an operation believing it was a garden.

She thought: you brought me a knife and told me it was the only knife in the room.

"How many bodies," Sita said.

Mira blinked. "What?"

"At Lanka. The convergence. He pulled them in to one ground on one night, the whole pile of them, the men who run the net. How many bodies came off that ground."

"The summary says, ah." Mira's eyes went sideways, to the paper, and Sita watched her go to it and not find it. "Principal and the cell around him. The shooters. They're still counting the outer cordon."

"They're not counting the outer cordon. There was no outer cordon." Sita set the glass down on the tile, very gently, because she did not trust her hand the way Mira trusted hers. "He laid a trap, Mira. The whole pipeline was bait. The one thing the other side cannot do without is the rail, because the rail is how the money moves, and the money is how you wake twenty years of sleepers without screaming it across a cable that Kaul can read. So he took the rail. He took it loud. He made it the only asset they could not afford to leave with him. And they would have had to come for it. All of them. To the same ground. On a night he chose. That is what Lanka was for. Lanka was the net closing on the net."

"And it closed."

"It closed on him."

The two glasses steamed between them. Somewhere below a shutter went up, the first chai-seller, the city clearing its throat for the day.

"They went in and they killed the man who built the trap," Sita said, "and they called it the trap working. They took the bait off the ground and let the fish swim. Read it." She did not pick the paper up. She did not need to. "Treasury secured. The treasury walked. Every man named in that sheet is one of ours. Not one name on it is theirs. You don't write a report like that by accident. You write it like that because someone told you where the edges of the night were before the night came."

Mira was quiet for a moment. Then, carefully, the way you step on river ice, "Kaul ran Lanka. Kaul is the cleanest officer in that building. Thirty years and not a smudge. You're telling me Vikrant Kaul sold the net to the net?"

"No." And here it was, the thing that had kept her on the floor of this room for two days while the city told itself it had won. "No. That's the part. Kaul did everything right."

She heard her own voice go flat and exact, the voice she used on a range, and let it.

"He owned the operation. He kept it deniable but he kept an owner on it, the way he always does, a man who can answer. He sanctioned it through the proper dead channels. He gave it a real target. That's the thing, Mira, the target was real. Alpha is real. Baba was running an off-books murder squad financed on heroin, and every word of that is true, and Kaul looked at it and did what a clean officer does. He saw a cancer and he cut it out. He moved fast. He moved precisely. He moved the way the best of them move. And he was aimed."

"Aimed by who."

"That's the face I can't see."

Sita got up and went to the window because she could not say the next part sitting down. Below, the lane was filling, scooters and a buffalo cart and a man carrying a tray of glasses on his head, and on the corner a camera on a bracket turned its slow grey eye across all of it, and she stood back from the light without thinking, the way she breathed.

"Someone fed him the right target for the wrong reason," she said to the glass. "Somebody who knew exactly what Baba was building, and knew that the one operation on this earth that could roll up the sleeper net was Baba's, and could not stop Baba himself because to stop him you would have to admit the net exists, and the net cannot be admitted. So he didn't stop Baba. He pointed the cleanest blade in the service at Baba's throat and let the service's own correctness do the work. He let Kaul be right. He used Kaul being right. You can't catch a man like that, because he never does a wrong thing. He just stands next to the lever and waits for an honest hand to pull it."

"That's, " Mira started, and stopped. Sita heard her sit down on the cot, the thin frame complaining. "That's a ghost. You're describing the absence of a man."

"I'm describing where he refuses to let the light fall." She turned around. "I had a name for him three weeks ago. Sehgal. Additional Secretary, thirty years, owns the financing, owns the tasking. He's the man who told me the hunt for Alpha was Kaul's, sanctioned, real. He stood in a clean office and offered me a chair in the state and a name on my orders. And he fit so well I put him down. Because the fit was too clean. A net that's run for twenty years from inside doesn't leave you a suspect who matches on every line. It leaves you a man you'd be a fool to accuse and a fool to clear." She picked the glass back up off the tile, drank from it though it had gone lukewarm, set it down. "So I don't have a face. I have a hand. I can see the hand on the lever in the dark, and I cannot turn it over to see whose it is, and the one man who could have closed it for me is in a body bag with three official words for a headstone."

The fan turned. The chai-seller called his first price up the lane.

"They didn't just kill him," Sita said, and now the thing she had been holding off all night came up and she let it, because Mira had to hear it and because she could not carry it alone for another hour. "They killed him in a way that buries her too. Don't you see the second floor of it? The win has to be loud. It has to be in the morning meetings, has to be the cleanest action in twenty years, has to fill every cable. Because when it's that loud, anyone who walks into the building afterward and says, wait, there's a net still live, there's a foreign principal, there's twenty years of proof in a grave near Kashmere Gate, that person is a lunatic spoiling a victory. The louder they cheered tonight, the more impossible they made my mother's evidence. A win is a wall. They built a win to wall her in."

She had not meant to say my mother. She heard it leave her and watched it land on Mira and saw Mira not understand it, file it, decide not to ask, all in a half second, and was grateful for the discipline that let her not ask.

"Then surface it," Mira said. "You have it. You have the tins, the ledger, the recording. Take it to Kaul. Tonight. If he's clean, like you say, then he's exactly the man who'll move on it."

"He's the cleanest man in the building and he's the most dangerous door I could walk through, and they are the same fact." Sita said it gently because Mira had not spent forty-eight days learning to think like the hand. "Whatever I carry to Kaul goes across one desk before it reaches him. The desk that owns the financing and the tasking. The desk that has been right about everything. I would be handing twenty years of proof to the one man best placed to make it disappear, and I'd be doing it inside a victory that's already decided I'm a madwoman. He doesn't even have to kill the evidence. He just has to let the building's relief do it for him. Same trick. He stands by the lever. I pull it myself."

"So you take it nowhere. You sit in a room over a parts shop with the only proof in the country that the war isn't over, and you sit on it."

"No." Sita crouched and unfolded the soft paper one more time and looked at it, the pleased dead verbs, treasury secured, principal deceased, and she folded it small and held it in her fist. "There's one man left who knows how to read a steered hunt, because he taught me, and who has done the only thing left to be done with a service that's been turned from inside. He did it once already. He killed the man who buried this the first time, eighteen years ago, and walked into the mountain and never came down. He's the only address I have that the hand on the lever cannot reach, because there's no lever there. There's just a man and a cold watch and the cost."

"Kabir." Mira's voice had no hope left in it now, which was at least honest. "You want to take it to a man who lives in a monastery because he can't be in the world anymore."

"I want to take it to the one man who already paid for understanding this," Sita said. "Because I think the bill is mine now too."

Outside the eye on the bracket finished its slow turn and started back the other way, patient, indifferent, recording the lane and the cart and the man with the glasses and the closed shutter of the motor-parts shop where nothing, its grey logic said, was happening. Sita watched it come round toward the window and stepped back into the dark of the room before it arrived, out of its sweep, the way she would for the rest of her life now, and put the folded paper inside her shirt against the canvas bag, against her mother's names, against the warm small weight of the pendant she had worn since before she could remember and had only just learned to read.

She thought of Baba's three words. She thought of the building cheering. She thought: somewhere in that building a man is standing very still, very pleased, near a lever he never has to touch, and he does not yet know that the instrument he could not account for is still in the world, and reading the dark for him.

"Drink your chai," she told Mira. "We won't get another hot thing for a while."
