The kettle was a cheap one, coil-element, and it ticked as it cooled on the windowsill where Mira had set it. She had made the tea too strong again, the way the recruits in the safe flats made it, boiling the leaves until the milk went the colour of wet sand. She drank it without complaint because complaining was a kind of intimacy and Sita had not decided, yet, what she owed her.

The room was above a shuttered cloth merchant's on a side street north of Karnal, two hundred rupees a night to a man who had not asked for an Aadhaar card because the lodge did not exist on any register and the man preferred it that way. One bulb. A ceiling fan that turned the heat over rather than moving it out. On the charpoy lay the cloth bag, and in the cloth bag the six tins, and the ledger, and the cassette she had not played a second time. She had carried them four hundred kilometres folded against her body like a second spine.

Mira was at the window, two fingers parting the curtain, reading the street the way she had been taught to read it. She did it well. That was the thing Sita kept returning to, the way you return to a tooth with your tongue. Mira did all of it well. The contact protocols, the documents, the patient unhurried voice that had taken Sita's foundations apart brick by brick in a borrowed flat eight weeks ago and left her standing in the rubble grateful. Mira had been very good, and that goodness had a maker, and the maker was not in the room.

"There's a Bolero that's been on the corner since the namaz call," Mira said. "Driver hasn't moved. Could be waiting for a fare."

"Could be."

"You don't think so."

"I think a man who waits forty minutes for a fare he isn't going to get is either lazy or paid. We'll watch him." Sita did not move from the floor where she sat with her back against the cool of the wall. "Sit down, Mira. Leave the curtain. If he's ours we won't fix it by staring, and if he isn't, you're showing him a shape in the window."

Mira let the curtain fall and came and sat on the edge of the charpoy, near the bag, and Sita watched her not look at it. She had a way of not looking at things that wanted looking at, which was discipline, and the discipline had been put into her by people who knew their business.

"You're working something," Mira said. "You've been working it since Lanka. You go away behind your face and I lose you."

"I'm tired."

"You don't get tired. That was the first thing they told me about you."

There it was, the small open door. Sita let it stand a moment. The fan turned. Somewhere below a generator coughed and caught.

"Who told you about me," Sita said. Not a question. A door held open in return.

Mira shrugged, an easy movement, nothing hidden in it, which was the trouble. "You know how it works. A handler. A folder. You read in, you don't read who wrote it."

"Tell me about the folder."

"Sita."

"Tell me about the folder. The one you brought me. My mother. The fire that wasn't a fire. The man who took me out of the house and made up a rescue. The strangulation report with the medical seal on it." She heard her own voice go flat and careful, the operational register, and she did not soften it. "I want to know how it came to your hand."

Mira set the tea down on the floor. She understood, in the way of people who do this work, that the temperature in the room had changed and she did not yet know why, and she went still to find out. "It came through Kaul's office," she said. "Not from him direct. Through the chain. A cut-out in Chanakyapuri handed me a sealed packet and a brief and I worked from the brief. That's all I had. That's all anyone has."

"And the brief said."

"The brief said you were real, you were Alpha, you'd been taken as an infant and built into a weapon, and that the truth would break you off your maker if anyone could get close enough to hand it to you." Mira's jaw moved. "It said you were worth the risk. It was right."

"It was right," Sita agreed.

That was what made it unbearable. The brief had not lied. She had spent the journey north testing every join of it against the trunk she had dug out of the ground at Lothian, against the lullaby in her own mouth, against the dead account in the munshi's ledger, and every plank of it held. Janaki had been strangled. The fire was a fabrication. Baba had taken her from a murdered woman's house and grown her in the dark like a forced bulb and called it rescue. All of it true. She had built a new self on it, the way she had once built a self on his version, and the new self stood.

Only the floor under it was the same floor.

"What was in the folder," Sita said, "about why."

"Why he took you?"

"Why she was killed. My mother. Twenty-six years a R&AW analyst, murdered in her own flat with a cot in the room. Did the folder say why."

Mira was quiet. She reached for the answer and Sita watched her find that there was none. "It said enemy action. The same line everyone got. Foreign hand, a service settling a score." She frowned. "It didn't dwell on it. It wasn't the point. The point was you."

"The point was me." Sita let the words sit. "Mira, you put a sealed medical report in front of me. Strangulation, ligature marks, the lot. Documents that should have been buried so deep the building forgot they existed, and they came up clean and indexed into your hand in a packet from Kaul's chain. Did that not strike you."

"I thought we'd got lucky. I thought someone inside had grown a conscience and slipped it out."

"Someone inside grew a conscience," Sita said softly, "and the conscience reached precisely as far as the page that turned me against Baba, and not one page further." She drew her knees up. "Tell me what wasn't in your folder. There was no sleeper network in it. There was no twenty years of money walking into missile commands and cyber desks and a foreign principal counting the days. There was no Sehgal. There was no line, anywhere, that said the man who stole you also spent forty years bleeding himself white to starve the thing that killed your mother. None of that was in your folder."

"Because it isn't true," Mira said, but she said it the way you say a number you are no longer sure you remember. "Because it's what he wanted you to believe at the end. He turned you back, Sita. The last weeks, you went back to him. I watched it happen and I couldn't stop it, and it nearly cost you your life at Lanka."

"Lanka." Sita almost smiled, and it was not a good thing on her face. "They killed him at Lanka and called it a victory and the network walked out the side door while everyone was clapping. I stood at the margin and watched the convergence break apart in the dark and the only people who got rolled up were his people. The thing he built the whole trap to catch is still in the ground, Mira. Still live. Counting days. You and I helped clear the one man who was hunting it. That's the victory."

The fan turned. Below, the generator settled into its rhythm.

"You don't know that," Mira said.

"I know it the way you know your Bolero. Forty minutes is too long for a fare." Sita reached over without rising and drew the cloth bag toward her by its strap and set it between them, not to open it, only so it was a third thing in the room. "I dug my mother out of the ground myself. Six tins of film, a key ledger, names against placements, every gram of the proof she paid for with her life. She left it for me. She made me the lock and the key both, eighteen years before I could read it. And every officer who should have used it chose burial instead. Luthra buried it as Chief because the truth would blow live lines or break something he loved more than her. The building buried it. And then, eight years on, somebody opened the grave just wide enough to let one fact out, the fact that turned me, and sealed it again behind that fact." She looked at Mira. "That isn't a conscience. That's a hand. A patient one. It knew exactly which truth to feed me and which to keep, because a fed truth is the strongest leash there is. I should know. I wore one my whole life and called it love."

She watched it reach Mira. She had wanted, somewhere low and ugly in herself, to watch it reach her, because she had stood in the rubble alone and it was an obscenity that the one who handed her the hammer should go on standing on solid ground. But the wanting drained out as it landed, because what came up in Mira's face was not the face of a woman caught. It was the face of a woman falling through the same floor Sita had fallen through, and reaching, on the way down, for the same things to hold, and finding them gone.

"No," Mira said. Quietly. Then, with the discipline going out of her voice, "No. I read it myself. I read every line. I checked the seal. The medical seal was real, I pulled the register reference and it matched, I am not a child, Sita, I do not get handed a story and swallow it, I verified."

"You verified what they wanted verified." Sita's voice was gentle now, and she hated the gentleness because she recognised it. It was his. "Of course the seal was real. Of course the page checked out. That is how it is done properly. You don't lie to the asset, you choose the truths. You give her the document that survives every test and you withhold the document that would point the gun the other way. Then she does the verification for you and trusts the conclusion because she reached it herself." She paused. "Whoever ran you knew you were too good to be lied to. So they didn't lie. They curated. You're not careless, Mira. You're the opposite. They needed someone careful, because a careful officer's certainty is worth ten times a careless one's, and they spent yours."

Mira had her hands flat on her knees. Her knuckles stood up. "I turned you to save you. I believed that. I still." She stopped. "I held your head when the conditioning nearly took you back and I talked you through it. That was real."

"It was real. I know it was real." That was the part Sita had not been ready for, on the road, when she first saw the whole shape of it. If Mira had been a mole, a plant, an officer of the other side wearing rescue like a coat, then there was somewhere for the grief to go. There was a throat for it. You could find a mole and you could finish her and the books would balance. But Mira had meant every word. Mira had spent her own certainty on Sita the way you spend savings on someone you love, and the savings had been counterfeit and Mira had not known, and so there was no one to finish, no throat, nothing to balance. The injury had been done by a true thing through a true person. There was no clean target anywhere in it. That was the design. That was the signature of the hand. It left nobody to kill.

"Which is worse," Sita said, half to herself.

"What?"

"That you meant it. If you'd been theirs I could have understood you. I understand people who are pointed at me. I've been one all my life. But you weren't pointed at me, you were pointed past me, at him, and you never felt the hand on your shoulder doing the pointing. They didn't recruit a liar. They recruited a believer and let her believe." She looked at the tins in their cloth. "He did the same to me. Forty years he ran me on the truths he chose. Fire instead of strangulation. A war I was a soldier in instead of a child he stole. He never once handed me a lie I could catch. He just kept the rest of the page." She made a small sound that was not a laugh. "You and I were made the same way, Mira. We were just made by different offices."

For a while neither of them said anything. The Bolero, when Mira finally crossed to the window again and looked, had gone. She let the curtain fall and stood with her back to it.

"Sehgal," she said.

"I think so. I can't prove it from inside this room. But the hunt looked everywhere except at the state's own money, and it pointed Kaul at Baba like a man steered by the elbow, and the elbow has to belong to someone Kaul trusts above suspicion. Thirty years' service. Over the financing. Over the tasking. The one chair in the building no one audits because auditing it would be an insult." Sita turned the strap of the bag in her fingers. "If I'm right, he turned before either of us was born. He arranged my mother's death and dressed it as the enemy and then spent the rest of his career making sure no one ever read what she left. And eight weeks ago his chain reached down through a cut-out in Chanakyapuri and handed you the one page that would aim me at the only man hunting him."

"Then we take it to Kaul," Mira said. "We take the tins to Kaul and we show him he's been walked."

"Kaul's office is where your folder came from. Kaul's office is where every cut and seal was made. I'm not putting my mother's life into the same channel that buried it twice and used it once." Sita got to her feet, slowly, feeling the road in her knees. "There's one address that chain doesn't reach. A man in the hills who killed his own maker to get inside a thing no one else could touch, and who has spent six years paying for it. He's outside the building. He's outside Sehgal. If proof like this has a place to go, it goes through him, or it doesn't go anywhere."

"Kabir," Mira said. She had read his name somewhere in her own folder, then, in whatever pages they had let her keep. "They say he won't see anyone."

"He'll see this." Sita lifted the bag and felt the weight of it, the dead analog weight of film and tape that no implant could reach into and no cloud could swallow, that had to be carried in a hand and put down in front of a face. "We move at three. No phones from here on, batteries out, in the bag. Cash only, no UPI, nothing with your name on the road. We change vehicles at Ambala and again before the hills. We stay off the trunk road where the cameras read faces. Two days, maybe three, if the passes are open." She was already counting it, the clean cold arithmetic of a run, and it steadied her the way it always had. The work did not care what had been done to her. The work was the one true floor.

Mira nodded. She was putting herself back together; Sita could see it, the discipline returning to her hands, and under it something that would not go back together, ever, the way Sita's own had not. They were the same now in that too.

"Sita." Mira had crossed to take the kettle and stow it, an ordinary motion, packing a room. "When this is finished. If it finishes. Tell me you'll let me read the whole page. All of it. Whatever's on that tape. I don't want to be the careful one ever again."

Sita looked at her, at the open want in her face, the want to be trusted with the entire truth and never again be spent on a partial one. It would cost nothing to say yes. It would cost almost nothing to mean it.

"Yes," she said.

And even as she said it she heard herself decide, quietly, in the place below speech where the maker's voice had always lived, that there were things on the cassette Mira did not need yet, that some of it would be better carried for her a while longer, that a careful kindness was sometimes a withheld page. She heard the thought arrive whole and reasonable, in a voice she knew, and she let it stand, and she picked up the bag, and she did not tell Mira that she had just done to her, in the span of a single yes, the exact thing she had crossed four hundred kilometres to grieve.

"Sleep two hours," she said. "We move at three."
