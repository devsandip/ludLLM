The room smelled of kerosene and old paper, and he had made the tea himself.

That was the first thing she marked, the way she marked everything now, with the part of her that no longer trusted the rest. Two glasses on the steel tray, the milk skinned over because he had boiled it too early, expecting her sooner. He had set out sugar in a twist of newspaper. He had laid the spoon on a folded square of cloth the way a man lays out instruments he intends to use with care. Fateh, who had never in her memory poured anything for anyone, standing at a single-ring burner in a caretaker's room behind a cold-storage godown off the Rohtak road, making tea for the daughter he had stolen.

She had come in clean. Three changes of auto on the way out from the ring road, the last two hundred metres on foot through the loading yard where the CCTV had been dead since before the season, a thing she knew because Menon had told her and because she had checked it herself with a torch held low against the housings. No phone. The phone was in a biscuit tin under a culvert four kilometres back, powered down, because a powered phone was a confession you signed in advance and she had stopped signing things. She had come the way you come to a meeting you might not walk out of, and she had found an old man and a tray.

"Sit," he said. "You're standing like the floor owes you money."

She sat. The chair was iron-framed, cold through her clothes. He lowered himself across from her with the slowness she had been refusing to see for a year now, one hand to the table edge, the breath held and let go. The generator outside found its rhythm and held it. Somewhere in the godown the compressors ran, keeping someone's mangoes or someone's heroin at temperature, and the hum came up through the concrete into her feet.

"You changed your route," he said. It was not a question. "You used to come straight up the stairs. Tonight you walked the yard twice."

"You taught me to."

"I taught you a great many things." He pushed a glass toward her. "Drink it before it's a skin and water."

She did not drink it. She watched his hands instead, the way you watch the hands and not the face, because the face had been lying to her for thirty years with a skill she could now name, and the hands were only old. The knuckles had swollen. There was a tremor in the left one he was holding still by pressing it flat to the tabletop, and the pressing was itself a kind of tell, and she hated that she could read him now the way she read a target across a crowded floor.

"You know," he said.

"I know some of it."

"You know enough to have come with a way to kill me and a reason." He lifted his own glass, drank, set it down. "I'd have been disappointed otherwise. I didn't make you to be lied to twice."

There it was, the thing he did, the thing she had loved her whole life and now watched operate like a hand inside a glove. He took the worst that could be said of him and said it first, in his own voice, gently, so that by the time you reached it yourself it was already his, already shaped, already forgiven in the saying. Mira had not been able to do it. Sehgal's curated folder had not been able to do it. Only he could turn a confession into an embrace, and she sat very still and let him, because she had come to hear it and because some part of her, the eighteen-year-old part that would never grow up or die, still wanted to be held.

"Tell me about my mother," she said.

He was quiet for a while. The compressors cycled off and the silence came down hard, and in it she could hear the kerosene burner ticking as the metal cooled.

"Her name was Janaki," he said. "But you have that. You found that yourself, or they gave it to you. I won't insult you by handing it over like it's mine to give." He turned the glass a quarter turn on the steel. "She was the best analyst the building had in twenty years and the building killed her for it. Not the enemy. Us. A man we trusted signed her into a grave dressed up as somebody else's bullet, and the Chief at the time looked at the file and decided the country couldn't survive the truth, and so he buried her diagnosis with her. That's the part nobody will ever put in a citation. She wasn't betrayed by the enemy. She was correct, and being correct was the unsurvivable thing."

"And you."

"And me." He met her eyes then, the first time, and held them. "I took you out of that house. You'll have been told I murdered her and lifted you out of the smoke. You'll have been told I was the smoke. Both are tidy and neither is true, and I'm too old to let you carry a tidy lie for the rest of your life, so listen. I did not kill your mother. I came too late to save her and just in time to take what she'd left, which was you. I knew what you were the moment I lifted you. I knew what was around your neck. I knew that whatever she'd hidden, she'd hidden it in you, that she'd made her own child into the index, because she was cornered and brilliant and out of better options, and a cornered brilliant woman does monstrous tender things."

Sita's hand had gone, without instruction, to the pendant. She made it come back to the table.

"So you took the key," she said.

"I took the key." He said it flatly, the way you read out a charge against yourself. "And then I did the thing you came here unable to understand. I never turned it."

The burner ticked.

"Eighteen years," he said. "I had the lock and I had the key and I never put one to the other. You think I couldn't have? I had the cemetery in three weeks of work. I had her methods. I knew the shape of where she'd put it. I could have walked you to that grave when you were nine and used your own eyes to read your own mother's cipher and lifted the proof and burned the world down with it, and there were nights, girl, there were a great many nights, when burning the world down would have cost me less than not." He stopped. The left hand had come off the table; he put it back. "I didn't turn the key because to turn it was to use you. On the one thing in this world that was yours and not mine. Everything else I made of you I made for my own purposes and I will not pretend otherwise. That one thing I left in the ground. It was the only clean act of my life and I performed it by doing nothing, every day, for eighteen years."

She understood that he wanted this to be the centre of it. She understood it the way you understand the weight a man puts behind a particular sentence, the small lean of the body, the lowering of the voice to make a thing intimate. It might even be true. She had learned that the cruellest part of him was that the true things and the salted things came out in exactly the same tone, and that he knew it, and that he used the truth as cover for the silence the way she used a crowd as cover for a kill.

"Penance," she said.

"Don't dignify it." He almost smiled. "Penance is for men who expect to be heard. I'm not building toward a forgiveness. I want that clear before we go further, because I can feel you waiting for me to ask, and I'm not going to. I took a woman's child and her death and her life's work and I bent all three to a war she'd have understood and never sanctioned. There's no arithmetic that nets that out to zero. I raised you because you were the key and because I could not put you down. Both. At the same time. If you've come here hoping one of those cancels the other, go back down the stairs, you've wasted a clean route."

She drank the tea then. It was cold and the skin caught on her lip and she drank it because her throat had closed and she needed something to do with her hands that was not the pendant and not the weight at the small of her back.

"The man who signed her into the grave," she said. "Tell me about him."

And here, she watched, here he became careful. Not evasive. Careful, the way a surgeon is careful at the edge of something he does not intend to cut today. He had been loose and terrible a moment ago and now he gathered himself in.

"There's a man," he said slowly, "high in the building, who has been the enemy's for thirty years. Longer than you've been alive. He was turned when I was still young enough to think the lines meant something. He is the reason your mother is dead, and he is the reason the network she found is still breathing, and he is the reason this country is about to be hurt in a way it cannot see coming. And here is the joke, girl. Here is the thing I want you to keep, because it's the truest thing I know and the one nobody will believe. He and I want the same grave kept shut."

She waited.

"Your mother's murder," he said. "The lie of how she died. He needs it buried so that nobody pulls the thread back to him. And I need it buried so that nobody pulls the thread back to you, and to what I made of you, and to the war I'm fighting off the books with money I mint out of other people's poison. He is the rot at the centre of the service and I am the thing the service would disown in a heartbeat if it knew I existed, and we are bound at the hip by a single dead woman we both need to stay dead. We have never met. We will never meet. And we have spent thirty years protecting the same corpse from opposite ends. If you ever want to know what my life amounts to, it amounts to that. I made myself the mirror image of the man I most despise. We are the two halves of the same lie about your mother."

"Who is he," she said.

He looked at her for a long moment and did not say the name.

"No," he said.

"Baba."

"No." The old word, the name she had called him since before she had words, and he heard her use it and something moved in his face and was put away. "Not because I don't trust you. Because the moment you have the name, you become the shortest line between him and the grave, and he has thirty years of practice killing the shortest line. You are useful to me alive right up until you are a name away from him, and then you are a body in a culvert and the proof is back in the ground and I have spent you for nothing. You'll get there. You have her eyes, you'll read it. But you'll read it after, when reading it can't kill you. Not from my mouth, in this room, tonight."

It was the salting. She felt it go in, clean, almost kind. He was telling her the truth in order to keep the last true thing from her, and dressing the withholding as care, and the worst of it was that it was care, that both were true at once, the way everything with him was both at once, the way she herself was now both his weapon and the woman deciding whether to spend the one moment of slack she had left.

She thought of Kabir on the cold floor of the monastery. You get one moment. You weaponize the leash, you don't break it. The only thing that justifies it is that real harm was stopped.

"You built a word into me," she said.

His eyes did not move.

"You built a phrase and a protocol," she said, "so that if I ever turned, you could reach in and pull the lever and I'd do the thing in your voice the way I did it at eighteen and never know my hand had been on the knife. I felt it work. In the ambush. You weren't even there and I heard you and my body did what your voice told it before my mind had a vote. So don't sit there and pour me tea and tell me about the one clean act of your life. You left a hand inside me. You left it there on purpose. It's still there now."

For the first time the old man looked away from her, down at the steel tray, at the skinned milk and the twist of newspaper, and she watched him decide whether to lie and watched him choose not to.

"Yes," he said.

"Use it."

The generator missed and caught. The single bulb wavered and steadied.

"Say the word," she said. Her voice had gone very flat and very level, the operational voice, the one that came when the rest of her had stepped back from the glass. "You're frightened of me now. You should be. I came up those stairs with a reason and a way and you know it, you said it yourself. So reach in and pull the lever. Make me pour the rest of this tea and walk back down the stairs and forget I was ever angry. You can. We both know you can. So do it, and let's neither of us pretend any more about clean acts."

He sat with his ruined hands flat on the table and he did not say the word.

She had not known, until the silence ran on past the point where a man would have used it, that she had been holding her breath. He could have. She had handed it to him. She had stood in the open and dared him and the word was right there behind his teeth, thirty years of conditioning waiting on one phrase, and he sat in a caretaker's room with cold tea between them and let the moment pass unspent, the way he had let eighteen years of moments pass without turning the key around her neck.

"There," he said at last, very quietly. "You wanted to know what the penance was. That's the penance. Not the years. Not the not-turning. This. Sitting here with the word in my mouth and your throat open and putting it down. I built the leash because I needed a weapon I could trust past the point where trust runs out. And the only thing I have to set against everything I did to you is that, in the one room where it would have saved my life to use it, I didn't." He pushed himself up from the table, the breath held, let go. "Drink your tea or don't. I have a night's work coming that ends one of two ways, and I'd as soon you weren't near it. Go down the way you came. The yard cameras are still dead. They'll stay dead till four."

She stood. The weight at the small of her back was just a weight now, a tool she had not used, would not use, not tonight, not in this room. She had come to find out whether she could kill him and she had her answer, and the answer was that she could, easily, and that he had just spent his last hour proving he would not stop her, and that this made it impossible, and that he had known it would.

At the door she turned. He was already at the burner, lifting the kettle to see if there was a second glass left in it, an old man making tea for nobody now.

"The grave," she said. "You said I'd read it after. After what?"

He did not turn around.

"After Lanka," he said. "If it goes the way I've built it, the man with no name has nowhere left to stand, and you can walk to that cemetery in daylight and lift your mother out of the ground and nobody alive can touch you for it." He set the kettle down. "And if it goes the other way, then you'll be the only person left who knows where she's buried and why, and you'll understand at last why I never let you carry that alone until tonight."

She went down the stairs and across the dead yard into the cold, and behind her, in the lit room above the godown, the old man stood over his burner and waited for a war he believed he was about to win.
