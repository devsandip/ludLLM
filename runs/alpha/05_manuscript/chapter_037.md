The room had been built to forget the weather. Forty feet under a colony of married officers' flats, in a quarter of Delhi that appeared on no duty roster, it held nineteen degrees the year round, and the men who worked in it learned to read the season off one another's collars. Tonight the collars came down dark at the points, and the air carried wet tarmac brought in on the soles of shoes. Above ground it was raining, the first rain of the year, early and undecided. Below, the only weather was the light off the screens.

Sita sat where she had been put, on the long side of the table with her back to the door and the bank of monitors filling her eyes. They had given her tea in a paper cup and she had not touched it. At the head sat Kaul. At his right hand sat Sehgal, who had not taken off his jacket, and whose stillness she had come to study the way she had once studied locks.

She had built the picture they were now watching. Eleven weeks of ledgers, of names sweated out of a munshi's coded columns, of UPI rails that meant nothing and hawala chits that meant everything, all of it reduced to the board on the centre screen. A map of the subcontinent and its money, with the seized pipeline marked in a dull amber that pulsed once a second, like something kept alive on a machine.

"Net check," said the duty officer. He was young, his voice flattened by the headset. "ANVIL."

"ANVIL, ready." Punjab. The choke point, where the Crescent rail crossed into the plain and the heroin and the false notes had moved for years under three different flags.

"HOUND."

"HOUND, ready." Moreh, on the Manipur line, where the Triangle came up through the hills.

"KESTREL."

"KESTREL holding." The watch on Dubai, run off a liaison desk and a feed Sita was not cleared to see the provenance of, only the product.

Kaul let them call the roll to the end and then said nothing for a while. He had a way of using silence the way other men used a pen, signing things by withholding himself. On the board the amber pulse went on, patient as a pulse should be.

The principle of the night was simple, and it was not theirs. Sita knew whose it was. You did not chase a sleeper network across twenty years of careful nothing; you could not see it, that was the whole of its art, it had been built so that it could not be seen. So you took from it the one thing it could not afford to leave in your hands, and you waited for it to come back. The pipeline was the network's treasury. Starve it at the pre-activation moment and it had two choices, both fatal: go dark and die of thirst, or reach for the rail and show its hand. The man who had taught her to tie a knot had built this trap with the same economy. You did not break the leash. You made the other man pull it.

She kept that thought behind her teeth.

At 23:14 the first node moved.

"KESTREL has motion," the desk said. "Settlement instruction, Dubai to Karol Bagh, routed through the Sharjah front. Value." A pause while someone read. "Equivalent of four point one crore."

"Source account?" Kaul did not raise his head.

"Tagged. It's on the analyst's list." The duty officer's eyes came to Sita, briefly, and went away again. The analyst's list. Her list. A name she had drawn out of a dead column three weeks ago, a trading company in Bangkok that imported nothing and exported less, surfacing now to push money toward the rail. She felt the old clean pleasure of a thing seen first and then proven, and underneath it, immediate as nausea, the knowledge of what the pleasure was for.

"There it is," Sehgal said quietly. The first time he had spoken. He said it the way a man remarks that the rain has started, to no one, confirming the world to itself. "They have to feed it. They cannot help themselves."

Kaul made a small sound that might have been agreement.

On the wall the Dubai node bloomed from grey to a hard white, and a line drew itself across the water toward Delhi, and the room, which had been holding a breath since the net check, let a portion of it go. A liaison officer behind her murmured something and got a low laugh in answer. They were good men. They had hunted a shadow for the better part of a year, and the shadow had just put a hand into the light, and they were allowed, for a moment, to feel that the long blind work had been work after all.

Sita watched the margins.

It was the only thing Kabir had given her that she trusted without reservation. You could not read a hunt by what it struck. The strikes were theatre; the strikes were what the network expected to lose, the disposable muscle and the couriers and the hawaladars who could be replaced inside a week. You read a hunt by where it refused to look. You found the steer in the silence, in the node that lit and then was allowed to fade, in the target designated and then, with no fuss at all, undesignated.

"Action KESTREL?" the desk asked.

"Hold," said Sehgal.

Kaul did not contradict him.

"Hold KESTREL," the desk repeated, and the Dubai node, white a moment ago, was permitted to cool a half-tone, marked now with a small annotation she could not read at this distance but could guess the shape of. Held for exploitation. Let it run. Reasonable words. She had used them herself. A live money channel was worth more watched than rolled; that was doctrine, that was sound, and a man could say it across a table all night and never once be wrong on the face of it.

That was the part she could not get her thumb under. Each call, taken alone, was the call she would have made.

She filed it and went on watching.

At 23:31 Moreh moved.

"HOUND has a crossing," the desk said, and the flatness went out of his voice for a sentence. "Foot, single male, came over below the fence line at the old timber track. Facial match." A beat. "We have him. Cross-referenced to the photo set the analyst flagged. This is a handler, not a mule. Dark since 2019."

The room came forward as one body. Even Kaul lifted his head.

Here it was. Not a courier, not a hawala clerk, but one of the quiet men, one of the two-decades-buried, surfacing because the treasury was gone and the network had no one left disposable enough to send. The trap had reached the layer it was built for. The sleeper had to expose itself to save itself, and it was exposing itself, exactly here, exactly now, on the night and at the place the design had promised. Sita felt the thing she had been refusing to feel all evening, which was that it worked. The dead man's geometry worked. He had been right about all of it, the rail and the hunger and the night, and wherever he was now, past needing rooms, he had been right.

"Designate," said Kaul.

"HOUND, designate the crosser, take on my mark," the desk said.

"Belay that." Sehgal's voice had not risen. He had a thin folder open in front of him now, and he turned a page in it without hurry. "We don't have his onward contact. He'll make a meeting inside six hours. If we lift him at the wire we get one man and a cold phone. If we let him run to the meeting we get the cell." He looked up, and his eyes went round the table and rested, for a courteous half-second, on Sita, as if inviting the professional in the room to confirm the professional point. "Patience. We've waited nineteen years. We can wait until breakfast."

It was good tradecraft. That was the whole of the trouble. It was good tradecraft, and Kaul was nodding, and the desk was already saying HOUND, hold, observe and report, do not action, and the man who had come over the timber track at Moreh, who had stayed dark since 2019 and surfaced tonight because he had been left no choice, was walking now into the dark of the Manipur hills with two watchers a respectful distance behind him and no hand anywhere near his collar.

One node held in Dubai. One handler let to run at Moreh.

Sita kept her face the way she kept her hands, loose and still and showing nothing, and inside her she began to count.

She knew now how a steered hunt felt from the inside, the way you know a room is tilted before the water in your glass tells you. The strikes were going to fall. She did not doubt that. Before the night was out the board would fill with white, the couriers would be lifted, the hawaladars sat under lights, the ANVIL teams would walk into a warehouse in Punjab and find the pipeline's muscle waiting to be arrested, and in the morning there would be a number. A clean operational number. Men detained, kilos seized, notes recovered, a number a Chief could carry up the stairs to people who needed numbers. The theatre would be magnificent.

And the buried men, the ones the whole of the trap had been built to surface, were each being handed, by an even and reasonable voice, exactly enough rope to walk back into the ground.

She made herself hold the other reading alongside it, because honesty was the only instrument she had left and she would not bend it to comfort. It was possible the held node was a held node and nothing more. It was possible the freed handler was a sound bet on a fatter prize at dawn. It was possible she was the bent thing in the room, that a month of learning her own life was a lie had taught her hand to find a steer in any clean surface, the way a man with a sore tooth finds it with his tongue and calls the tooth the world. She could not, from this chair, tell the difference between a traitor's caution and a careful man's. That was the part that frightened her, more than the held nodes. Whoever had built the steer, if a steer was what it was, had built it so that it could only be read as judgement. From inside the room there was no figure you could point to and name. There was only a string of decisions, each of which a good officer would defend, and a pattern that lived in the spaces between them, where nothing could be filmed.

"ANVIL is in position," the desk said. "Warehouse perimeter, Tarn Taran. Movement inside. Six, seven bodies. Vehicles loading."

"That's the muscle," Sehgal said, and now, for the first time, there was something almost like warmth in it. "That's the part that fights back. Take it cleanly." He closed the thin folder. "Action ANVIL."

"ANVIL, you are clear to action, clear to action, on the desk's mark."

The hammer came down where hammers were allowed to come down. On the centre screen the Tarn Taran node went white and stayed white, and a drone feed opened in the corner, grey-green, a flat roof and a yard and small running figures, and the room watched its victory in silence. It was a real victory. Those were real men with real weapons being taken off a real rail that had poisoned real towns for thirty years. And it touched the buried layer not at all.

Sita watched the corner of the board where the Moreh handler's track crept on through the hills, unhunted, and understood, without being able to prove a word of it, that she was watching a man be saved.

She had spent her life understanding that the loud thing was a cover for the quiet thing. It was the first grammar she had ever been taught, before she could name it, before her eighteenth birthday gave the lesson a shape she could hold. You make the noise here so the work happens there. She had been the work, more than once. She had gone up alone while the world below looked at something arranged for it to look at, and she had done the quiet thing in a quiet room, and she had been proud of it, because the man who arranged the noise was never wrong.

So she knew the trade from the inside. And the trade was being run tonight, in this room, with a loud hand and a quiet one, and the quiet one was leaving the network its breath. Whether it knew what it spared, or believed with its whole thirty-year heart that it was doing the patient and correct thing, she could not see, and might never see, and the not-seeing was so total that she had to wonder whether it had been arranged to be total. A steer you could name was a steer that had failed. This one had not failed.

"KESTREL," the desk said. "The Dubai channel's gone quiet. They've completed the settlement and shut down."

"Good," said Sehgal. "We have the route. We'll roll it at our leisure."

We have the route. We let the men who own it walk, and we keep the route warm, and a warm route had a way of being wanted again, by someone, later. She heard the sentence land and could not have said whether it was treason or thrift. Both wore the same coat.

She did not look at him directly. She had learned long ago that the surest way to be seen seeing was to look. She kept her eyes on the screens and built him in her side vision instead: the jacket still on in a room kept cold enough to need it, the folder squared to the table edge, the patience that was the most reasonable thing in the room. Thirty years of service. The building believed in him the way she had once believed in a voice on a telephone. She thought: I would have believed in him too. A month ago I would have sat in this chair and felt safe, and I would have been right to, because there is not one thing on this board I could carry up the stairs and call a crime.

At 00:02 the strike package came up.

"Principal location confirmed," the duty officer said, and the room changed key again, dropped a register, because this was the part everyone had been waiting for under the part they admitted to. "Fix on the head of the seizing element. Alpha command. High confidence."

Sita's pulse did not change. She had trained it not to, years ago, with the same voice that was now a fix on a screen, a head of element, high confidence. On the board a new marker came up, north and east, a single point with a kill-box drawn around it, neat as a stamp.

"Assault element is staged," the desk said. "Awaiting clearance."

Kaul looked at the marker for a long moment. Whatever he carried, he carried it without showing the table. Then he turned, fractionally, to the man at his right hand, the way a Chief turns to the officer whose judgement has owned the operational picture all night, the man whose folder had held and run and shaped every designation since the net check.

"Recommendation," Kaul said.

Sehgal did not hesitate, and he did not hurry. He looked at the kill-box around the head of Alpha, around the man who had built the trap they were all standing inside, the man whose design had surfaced the entire network and who was, at this exact minute, the only instrument in the country pointed at the thing that mattered. He let a small space open before he answered, the way a man does when he wants his answer to seem drawn from him rather than carried in ready-made.

"Clear it," Sehgal said. "He's the one we can't leave running."

It was the only designation of the night that had come without a pause for exploitation, without a folder turned, without a patient word about the fatter prize at dawn. Every other target on the board had been worth more alive, watched, run to its meeting, kept warm. This one, and only this one, was worth nothing but dead, and quickly, before he could finish what he had begun. Sita sat with her hands loose in her lap and her cold tea going colder at her elbow and did not let her face move, and she could not prove a syllable of what she now believed, and she believed it to the floor of herself: of all the men the room had touched tonight, the single one nobody had argued to spare was the one trying to do what the room said it was for.

"Assault element," the desk said, "you are clear."

She picked up the paper cup so that her hand would have somewhere to be, and drank the cold tea down to the leaves, and across the table the most trusted man in the service watched his recommendation become an order, and was satisfied.
