The pouch was lined with copper mesh and smelled of other people's hands. She slid the phone in herself, the way she always did, and watched the boy from the security cell zip it and write a chit, and she thought, as she thought every time, that a clean service did not need to take your phone if it trusted the room. The taking was the tell. Somewhere behind the plaster of this bungalow a box was killing every signal in a forty-metre bubble, and the men who built that bubble called it hygiene and meant fear.

They walked her down a corridor with photographs on the wall, Chiefs in monochrome, men who had owned things and answered for them and were now only frames. At the end of it a door stood open on lamplight.

Sehgal rose when she came in. That, too, she catalogued. Thirty years of seniority and he stood for a contract assassin the building would disown by breakfast if a single page surfaced, and the standing was good manners and the good manners were a wall, smooth, load-bearing, without a handhold anywhere on it. He was in shirtsleeves at this hour, collar open, a cardigan the colour of wet ash. A glass of water on the blotter, no whisky. A man who had outlived the need to perform appetite.

"Sit," he said. "You look like you haven't."

"I haven't."

"None of us have." He lowered himself back into the chair with the small care of a body that had begun to negotiate with itself. "It was a long night. It will be a longer week. But you should know the building is pleased. Quietly. In the way the building is ever pleased with work it can never name."

She sat. She let her hands rest where he could see them. She had decided, walking the corridor of dead Chiefs, what this was. It was not a debrief. A debrief had a stenographer and a register and a number at the top of the page. This had a glass of water and a closed door and a man who had sent the stenographer home. This was a man finding out what she had taken out of the ground.

So she would let him fish, and she would watch the line.

"Fateh," he said, and stopped, and corrected the name into a function the way a careful officer files a man under what he was rather than who. "Alpha is closed. The principal is closed. The rail is ours, what's left of it, and the men who came for it on the night walked into the light one by one and were photographed doing it. You gave us that. The convergence. You held the centre while it happened." He turned the glass a quarter-turn on the blotter. "I want you to understand that I know the cost of holding a centre while the man who built you comes apart in front of you. I have asked people to do that. I have not always been able to look at them afterward."

It was beautifully done. The admission that was not an admission, the grief offered like a gift so that she would feel the obligation to give grief back. She gave him a measured nothing, the face she had worn into a hundred rooms, and underneath it she was reading the way her mother had taught her to read without ever being in a room to teach her: not the words, which were always clean, but the spaces the words were built to keep her out of.

"You wanted to see me," she said. "At this hour. With the stenographer gone."

"I wanted to see you because you are the only person who was inside it." He said it simply. "Kaul has the after-action. Kaul has the bodies and the photographs and the seizures, and Kaul will brief the people who need a victory, and they will have one. But you and I both know that an operation of this kind leaves a residue. Things picked up in the dark. Things a man like Fateh would have positioned for exactly the moment he stopped being able to use them." He let that sit. "He spent eighteen years building you to read what other people leave buried. I'm asking you what he left buried."

There it was, then. Laid almost in the open. He was asking her, in the dialect of reasons of state, whether she had found her mother's box.

She knew the box. It sat in her mind with a weight that had nothing to do with kilograms: six tins of sixteen-millimetre film in a sealed trunk, a ledger that turned names into placements, a cassette she had not finished hearing because she could only bear to hear it once, in front of one man, in a monastery she had not yet reached. She knew the exact heft of it against her ribs in the bag she had carried away from Lothian. And she sat in front of the man who had spent a career steering hunts away from that ground, and she gave him the flat, useful voice of an asset reporting up.

"He left a money trail," she said. "Most of it I've handed to Kaul's people already. Hawala nodes through Bangkok, two trade fronts in Dubai he was using to wash the rail. Nothing that survives him. He cleaned as he went."

"And the rest."

"There is no rest. He didn't keep paper. You know that. You hunted him for two years on the strength of him not keeping paper."

Something moved behind his face. Not displeasure. Calculation, resettling. He believed her or he chose to behave as though he did, and either way the line went slack and she felt, for the first time, that she might walk out of this room.

"You're tired," he said gently. "I won't keep you long." He looked at the photographs on his own wall, the dead Chiefs, and she understood that the look was for her benefit, a man composing himself into the role of elder, and she let him compose. "Do you know, this whole architecture, the thing Fateh thought he alone could see, the thing he burned himself down to reach. It was seen before. Long before him. Eighteen years ago a woman on the analysis floor saw the first thread of it and pulled, and pulled, and would not stop pulling."

The room did not change. The lamp did not flicker. She kept her hands where they were.

"There was an analyst," she said, because to say nothing now would itself be a tell, and she had learned to feed a man a little of the thing he was reaching for so he would not notice the size of what she withheld. "I came across the case. It was sealed."

"It was sealed because it had to be." He said it without heat. "The state cannot always afford what is true. You of all people have earned the right to know that. She had a piece of the truth and the truth, surfaced then, would have blown three live things and cost more than it bought. So it was buried. And she was left where the burying left her. That is the part no one tells you. We did not protect her. We could not. The thing she found reached out and took her, dressed up as the enemy, the way these things always dress up, and we wrote it down as the enemy because by then it was the kindest lie available." He drew a breath. "Janaki. Her name was Janaki."

He had said the name before she had given it. She had only said the analyst. She filed the small fact of it, neutral, the way you note the make of a parked car you will need to describe later, and she kept her eyes soft and her breath even, because the man was not finished, and her mother had taught her without ever holding her that the buried thing comes up at the end of a sentence, not the start, when the speaker thinks the dangerous part is already behind him.

"You knew her," Sita said. Not a question. An offered hand.

"Everyone on that floor knew her." He smiled, and it was the first true thing his face had done, a small unguarded warmth that arrived before he could vet it. "The best mind the building had in twenty years, and the worst instinct for self-preservation I have ever watched in a human being. She had a habit. When she was certain of a thing and could not yet prove it, she would go very quiet and very polite, and you learned to be afraid of her politeness, because it meant she had already won and was only waiting for the rest of us to catch up." He turned the glass again. "We told her to stop. I told her to stop. I sat across a table from her, much like this, and I told her she was about to walk off the edge of something with no bottom, and she looked at me with that terrible courtesy and she did not stop."

His voice had changed register. She heard it the way a tuned instrument hears another go a quarter-tone flat in a hall: not loud, not obvious, a thing only the trained ear catches and the untrained crowd never will. The careful officer recounting a sealed file had stepped back, and a man recalling a face had stepped forward into his place, and the man recalling a face was using the present habits of the dead.

"She had just had the child," he said. "Three weeks. Less. She came back to the floor inside the month, which no one asked her to do, and she worked nights, and I remember thinking the work was a place she went to not be afraid in the flat." A pause, and the pause was the wrong length, half a beat too long, the length of a man stepping around a stone only he can see in the dark. "She wasn't afraid at the end. That much I can give her. It was quick. She didn't hear the street."

She didn't hear the street.

Sita sat with her hands open on her knees and did not move, and inside the stillness something went through her like cold water finding a crack in stone, fast and total and silent. She didn't hear the street. The file said the street. The file said a device, foreign hand, the enemy reaching out of the dark; the file said Janaki had died in the noise of it, with the rest of the noise, one casualty among the staged casualties of a staged attack. A clean officer who had only ever read the file would have said she died in the blast. Would have had to. The blast was the whole architecture of the lie, the thing the lie existed to make true.

But a man who knew she had not heard the street knew she was already gone before the street happened. Knew the order of it. Knew the noise came after, for the cameras, for the file, for the page that called it the enemy. There was only one chair in the world from which you could know that the noise came second. It was the chair of the man who had arranged for the noise to come second. You did not read that order in any after-action on earth. You set it.

And he had set it gently. That was the part her mother's eye would not let her look away from, the part procedure could never have handed her, the part no money trail and no steered hunt and no curated folder had ever quite reached, because all of those were structure, pattern, inference, the architecture of a case that a careful lawyer could still call coincidence in a closed room. This was not structure. This was a man who had strangled a woman in her flat at the end of a month and dressed the flat in the enemy's clothes and walked out, and who eighteen years later, tired, in shirtsleeves, in the slack hour after a victory he believed he had won, had let slip that he had carried the manner of it as a mercy. She didn't hear the street. I've kept her from hearing the street for eighteen years. He had loved the courtesy of her. He had killed her for her courtesy. And the killing had cost him the one thing he had thought himself long past being able to spend, and he had paid it, and he had been paying the change of it down ever since in small private coins like this, and he did not even know he had just spent one across a desk to the only person alive who could count it.

He caught himself. She watched him catch himself, watched the careful officer come back up over the man like water closing over a thrown stone, smooth again, level again, the surface giving nothing back. It took him perhaps a second. To anyone else in the building it would have been nothing at all, a senior man pausing over an old grief, the most forgivable thing in the world.

"Forgive me," he said. "It's late, and the dead get nearer when you're tired. You should go and sleep." He stood, the contract over, the line reeled in empty, his composure a wall again without a single handhold on it, and he believed, she could see that he believed, that he had given her nothing but an old man's sentiment and learned that she had nothing to give him in return. He believed the night had closed clean. He did not know the woman in front of him. He did not know whose child had been three weeks old. He had spent eighteen years not finding that child, and the child had walked into his lamplight on her own feet and surrendered her phone at the door, and he had looked at her across a desk and seen an instrument, a useful residue of Fateh's long madness, a thing to be debriefed and disowned by breakfast, and he had not seen his own work standing finished in front of him.

She rose when he rose. She let him take her hand. It was dry and warm and steady, the hand of a man who slept well, and she returned the pressure of it exactly, neither too little nor too much, the way she had been built to return everything, and she thought, holding the hand that had done it, that her mother had been right about the politeness. You learned to be afraid of it because it meant the certainty had already arrived and was only waiting for the rest of the room to catch up.

"Thank you for the work," he said.

"Thank you for the trust," she said.

At the door the boy from the security cell had her phone ready in its chit, zipped out of the copper mesh, the screen waking with its small accusing column of the world she had missed, the messages, the missed calls, the wake of an identity that could not be put down anywhere in this country without leaving its print. She took it back into her hand and it found the towers and began, dumbly, to tell everyone where she was.

She walked out under the porch into the wet Delhi dark, fifty-two nights deep into the thing she had once thought was a hunt and now knew was an inheritance, and the certainty sat in her with the flat cold weight of the box against her ribs. Not a case. Not a pattern a lawyer could unmake in a closed room. A man's hand in hers, dry and warm and steady, and one sentence that only the murderer could say, said softly, to the one ear in the world her mother had grown for exactly this.

She did not look back at the lit window. She had what she had come without knowing she came for. Now there was only the carrying of it, north, to the single address left on the earth beyond the reach of the hand she had just shaken.
