The flat was on the second floor of a government block behind Sardar Patel Marg, the kind of address that had once housed a joint secretary and now waited between tenants, its walls the colour of weak tea. Kaul had chosen it himself. No one in the building knew the two of them were in the city together; the duty log said she was in Amritsar and he was at a family function in Dehradun, and both lies would hold for one night. She had left her phone in the glovebox of a Maruti two streets away, battery out, the way you left a dog you did not trust in the yard. He had done the same. A man could be followed by the device in his pocket more faithfully than by any watcher, and they both knew whose implant they were afraid of.

There was a ceiling fan turning slow above a table, and a window unit that laboured and dripped into a steel bowl. June pressed against the glass. Kaul had taken off his jacket and folded it over the chair back with a care that told her more about his state than his face did. On the table he had laid out what she had asked him to bring, and nothing that was not on paper. No screen. No recorder. Three buff folders, old enough that the string ties had gone soft, and a fresh pad of ruled foolscap on which, by the end, they would write the thing down once and then burn it in the kitchen sink.

"Tell me again," he said. "Slowly. From the money."

So she told him from the money, because the money was the part she could give him whole.

She walked him back along the rail she had spent eleven weeks tracing, the hawala legs through Dubai and the trade paper out of Bangkok, the fertiliser invoices that carried no fertiliser, the steel that was never rolled. She showed him where the hunt for Alpha had been allowed to run hot and where it had been kept cool, and she made him see the shape of it, not as a list but as a hand. A hunt left to itself sprawled. It chased everything, it wasted itself, it looked under every stone because it did not know which stone. This one had been disciplined. It had been kept from certain stones with a consistency no accident produces. For two years it had pursued Fateh outward, into the Triangle, into Punjab, across borders, and for two years it had declined to turn and look at the accounts nearest its own feet.

"You showed me this before Lanka," Kaul said. "I read it as caution. Compartmentation. There are trails we do not pull because pulling them collapses live work."

"Yes, sir. That is what it was built to look like."

He did not answer at once. The fan turned. Somewhere below a generator coughed and caught, and the window unit steadied.

"A hand inside the hunt," he said, as though setting down something heavy on a shelf above his own reach. "You are telling me the hunt was steered."

"I am telling you what the money tells me. The steer was not clever. Clever leaves marks. This was patient. Whoever did it never reached too far in any one month. He moved the hunt the way you move a heavy thing across a floor, a little, then you wait, then a little more, so that anyone watching sees only a thing settling." She let the words sit. "You do not steer a hunt for two years out of caution, sir. You steer it for two years to protect something you cannot afford to have found."

"And what is found, when the hunt finally turns and looks at its own feet?"

"A network," she said. "Older than the hunt. Older than Fateh's war on it. Planted, not recruited. Twenty years in the ground, in the places that matter. Not couriers. Men with desks and clearances." She watched him receive it. "Fateh did not invent his enemy to justify himself. The enemy is real, and it is inside, and someone senior has spent a long time keeping our eyes off it."

Kaul rose and went to the window. He stood with his back to her, a square, careful man in shirtsleeves, and looked down at a street where a chowkidar was wheeling his bicycle along the kerb. She let him have the minute. She had needed her own.

"You have a name," he said to the glass. It was not a question. He had known her too long to mistake the way she had laid the table.

"I have a slip," she said. "The name I will not say first, because if I say it first you will spend the next hour defending him to me, and we do not have the hour."

He turned at that. There was the ghost of something on his face, not amusement, the cousin of it, the look of a man who has been read accurately and resents only that it was done so cleanly.

"Then give me the slip."

She told him about the debrief. Not all of it. She gave him the architecture and kept the floor she had been standing on. There had been a moment, she said, when the conversation had crossed an old case, a 2008 file, an analyst killed in what the record carried as a terrorist incident, a blast in a market, a woman in the wrong place. The kind of death the system files and forgets. And the man across the table from her had said a thing about that death that the record gave him no reason to know. He had spoken of the analyst as someone who had already moved her material before she died. Already passed it on. He had said it the way a man says a thing he has carried so long it has stopped feeling like a secret, as a settled fact among colleagues, and then he had moved on, and the moment had closed over itself like water.

"The file says random," she said. "The file says a woman buying vegetables when a device went off two stalls down. There is no material in that story. There is nothing to pass on. A clean officer reading that file his whole career would never know there had been papers, because the whole purpose of how she died was that there were no papers." She kept her voice level, the way you keep a hand level carrying something that must not spill. "He knew there were papers. He knew because he was there at the other end of them. You do not arrange a death as a market blast unless you are afraid of what the dead woman wrote."

Kaul came back to the table and sat. He did not reach for the folders. He put his two hands flat on the wood as if to feel whether it would hold.

"Arvind," he said.

She said nothing. She had promised herself she would make him say it, and he had, and there was no satisfaction in it, only the small cold click of a thing seating itself.

"He sat my financing for nine years," Kaul said. "Before that, operations, the Gulf, Southeast Asia, the trade desks. Thirty years of service. I have signed his appraisals. I put him over the very accounts you have been crawling through." His jaw worked once. "Do you understand what you are asking me to believe. You are asking me to believe that the man I made the keeper of my money is the reason the money lies."

"I am asking you to believe nothing yet," she said. "I am asking you to count with me."

So they counted. This was the part she had come for, the part the slip and the steer only opened the door to, and they did it the way her mother's trade had always done it, though Kaul did not know whose trade it was and she would not tell him. They took the buff folders and they took the long career and they laid them against each other, and they asked the only question that mattered, which was not is he guilty but how old is the wound.

You did not date a penetration from when you caught it. You dated it from the first operation that should have worked and did not. They went back. The Gulf rollup in the early 2000s that had gone to ground a week before the net dropped, blamed at the time on a leaky liaison service. A Bangkok laundering case in '04 that had been closed for reasons of resourcing, the file thin in exactly the places a file goes thin when someone wants it thin. The analyst in 2008, who had found the network at its root and been buried with it. The way, again and again across two decades, the service's reach had failed at the last inch when it came near a certain kind of money and a certain kind of man, and the failure had each time been dressed in a plausible institutional clothing, a budget, a liaison, a reorganisation, a reason of state.

"You can explain any one of these," Kaul said. "Each of them, alone, I have explained. I was in the room for some of them."

"That is the point, sir. Each one alone is weather. Together they are climate. No service is this unlucky in one direction for twenty years. Luck does not have a direction." She turned a page she had marked. "And it is always the same direction. It always fails toward the same shadow. Whoever has been steering us has been steering us since before there was an Alpha to steer us onto. Fateh is recent. The hand is not."

Kaul was quiet for a long time. The window unit dripped into its bowl, one note, then another, the only clock in the room.

"How far back," he said at last, and his voice had changed. It had gone flat and careful, the voice of a man doing the sum and not liking the total.

She had her answer ready and she gave it slowly, because it was the centre of the night. The earliest failure she could find that fit the pattern, that failed toward the same shadow with the same plausible clothing, sat in the middle of the 1990s. After the Soviet collapse, when the old certainties had come apart and half the trade had been up for sale, when men who had built their lives believing in one architecture of the world had watched it dissolve and had been offered, by someone, a different one. Not money, necessarily. Men of his rank were rarely bought with money alone. They were bought with a thesis. With the flattering idea that nations were sentiment and only power endured, and that a clear-eyed man chose the side that would still be standing.

"Thirty years," Kaul said.

"About thirty," she said. "He would have been young then. Mid-career. Trusted enough to be useful, junior enough to be invisible. You do not place an asset like that and use him in a year. You let him grow. You let him become exactly the man his service most relies on, the safe pair of hands, the one they put over the money. By the time he matters, no one can imagine him. That is the whole craft of it. The deeper the man, the more unthinkable he becomes, until being unthinkable is his best armour."

She watched the number land on Kaul and settle, and she saw it do to him what it had done to her in the cemetery, though she could not tell him she knew the weight from the inside. Thirty years was not a betrayal. Thirty years was a life. It meant every promotion he had signed, every account he had trusted, every door he had held open in good faith had been held open onto this. It meant the building itself, the thing they both served, had been carrying the contamination in its own blood since before half its present officers had been recruited. You could not quarantine that. There was no clean tissue to fall back to. The rot was load-bearing.

"And behind him," Kaul said. He did not finish it.

"Behind him, yes." This was the part she could not draw, only feel, and she felt it again now in the close heat of the room, the way you feel a man standing behind you in the dark before you have heard him. A discipline like the steer's did not come from ambition. Ambition was greedy; it reached; it left marks. What she had been tracing all these weeks was not a man building his own empire. It was a man being run. The consistency over decades, the willingness to wait, the refusal ever to over-reach, the way the network had been planted and grown and never spent until the one blow it was planted to deliver, all of it spoke of a single will somewhere out of frame, patient on a scale no single ambitious officer could sustain across a working life. Someone had set the analyst's death in motion through Arvind Sehgal and had never once shown his own hand. Someone owned the pipeline Fateh had seized, and wanted it back, and wanted the blow delivered, and was content to be felt only as the shape of an absence, the place the hunt would not look, the gravity that bent every trail toward a centre that was never there when you arrived.

"We will not see him," she said. "I want you to understand that, sir, before we waste a month looking. We will never put a face to the one who runs Arvind. He has spent thirty years being a man no one can see. We will only ever see him the way you see wind, by what it moves."

Kaul looked at her then with something she had not seen from him before, and which she filed away without examining, because to examine it would cost her more than she had tonight. It was the look of a commander realising the operative across the table can see further into the dark than he can, and not being sure whether to be grateful or afraid.

"Then we prove it," he said, and reached for the pad. "We build the case. The failures, the dates, the steer, the slip. We take it up the chain, carefully, to the people the network has not reached, and we cut him out."

She let him write three lines before she stopped him, because this was the turn and she had known it was coming since she sat down, and she had hoped, the way you hope for weather you know will not come, that she would not have to be the one to say it.

"Sir. Read it back."

He read it back. The failures that each had an innocent explanation he himself had once accepted. The steer that lived in patterns of negative space, in where the money did not go, evidence that a defence counsel would call the absence of evidence and be right. The slip, which had been spoken to one operative in a room with no recorder, and which she could swear to and he could deny, his word a thirty-year career against hers, an off-books killer with no file and no name. The thirty years, which were not a fact but an inference, a climate read from weather. She made him see it the way the panel upstairs would see it, the way a court would, the way Sehgal himself would smile at it across a table, sorrowful and reasonable, asking with real or perfect pain whether they truly meant to set a deniable assassin's intuition against the work of his life.

"There is no clean case," Kaul said. It was not a surrender. It was an inventory.

"There is a true case," she said. "And there is a case that survives daylight. They are not the same case, and we have the first one." She closed the folder under her hand. "He is the keeper of the money. The proof of what he did lives in the money, and he keeps it. He is the witness against himself, and he owns the witness box. Every door we would open to prove him, he is standing behind. To indict him cleanly we would have to open the one operation that has no owner, and admit the one war the state cannot admit it fought, and disinter what a former Chief buried for reasons of state. The instant we reach for proof that holds, the whole floor goes out from under us, and he knows it. That is why he can sit in his office tomorrow and let us count. He has counted too. He arrived at the same number. He is not afraid of the truth. He is only afraid of evidence, and he has spent thirty years making sure the truth and the evidence would never be the same thing."

The generator below them faltered and the window unit died, and the room went to the bare turning of the fan and the deeper dark beyond the window. Kaul did not move to find a torch. In the half-light he looked older than his rank, a man who had given his life to a building and learned at the end of it that the building had a tenant he had never met, who had been there longer than he had, and would be there after.

She thought, and did not say, that she had just done to him precisely what had been done to her. She had brought him a truth that was real and partial, and she had let him build on it the part she chose, and she had kept the floor she stood on out of his sight, the grave, the tins, the woman in the ground who was the reason she could read any of this at all. She had handed him a calibrated thing and watched it turn him, and told herself the calibration was mercy. She knew the taste of that lie because she had swallowed it from another hand and called it freedom. The knowledge sat in her like cold water and changed nothing she intended to do.

"What do you want, then," Kaul said into the dark. "If not the case."

She had her answer for that too, but she did not give it to him yet. Out beyond the dead window the city ran on, its cameras patient on their poles, its towers logging every clean and KYC-bound identity that crossed beneath them, a whole apparatus built to see, and at the centre of it a man it had been built to trust, and behind that man, unbreathing, unhurried, never once stepping into the light, the one who had been winning since before she was born.

"First," she said, "we make sure he believes we are still counting."
