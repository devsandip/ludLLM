The fallback was a brick agent's office out on the Yamuna flats, past the last of the lit roads, where the city stopped pretending to be a city and became kilns and standing water and the chalk smell of fired clay. She had it by heart. Eleven years of his teaching had put a dozen such places in her, each one chosen for the same reason: nobody would choose it. He had taught her the principle with this exact address, walking her out here one December with the fog on the river, making her memorize the turn off the ring road, the dead canal, the Hanuman calendar nailed to the inside of the door so a man could find the latch in the dark. A lesson in where to die quietly. She had not understood, then, that he was furnishing his own.

The boy at the door was one of Menon's, nineteen, a country pistol held low against his thigh in a way that said he had never fired it and hoped he never would. He knew her. He stepped back without a word and let the cold in behind her.

She smelled it before she saw him. A gut wound has its own announcement, copper and the sourness underneath, the body's plumbing opened to the air. They had laid him on a charpoy against the far wall, under the calendar, with a hurricane lamp turned down low because even out here a generator's hum carried and a light through the shutter slats could be read from the embankment by anyone with the patience to sit and watch. No drip. No surgeon. There would be none. He had built Alpha to have no paper, and a man with no paper cannot be carried into a trauma bay where the camera reads his face the moment the doors hiss open and the duty clerk asks for an Aadhaar that does not exist. He had made deniability into a fortress, and the fortress had no door that opened inward. He was dying of his own architecture, exactly as designed, and he would have appreciated that if it had happened to somebody else.

"You came the wrong way in," he said. His eyes were shut. "Off the canal. I heard the gate."

"There's no right way in. You taught me that."

"I taught you there's always a right way." A thread of the old dryness. "You were lazy."

She crossed the floor and crouched by the charpoy. Up close the lamp showed her the rest of it. They had packed the wound with a field dressing and a folded gamcha and the packing had gone dark and stayed dark, which meant it was not the surface bleeding that would kill him but the slow private one underneath, the one no hand could reach. His skin had the grey of tallow. He had hours. He had less than hours.

She had rehearsed, on the drive out, the things a person was supposed to feel arriving at this. She had felt none of them yet. That would come later, she understood, the way the recoil of a heavy round arrives a fraction after the muzzle, when the mind has already moved on. For now there was only the work of the eyes, the inventory she could not switch off because he had wired it into her: the boy at the door, the second exit behind the gunny sacks, the phone lying face-down on the upturned crate, the way his right hand kept finding the edge of the cot and gripping and letting go.

"They took the rail back," he said.

"No. They didn't."

"Tell me the truth, not the kind one."

"The sweep ran. Three nodes lit. The convergence happened the way you said it would, on the night you said it would." She kept her voice flat, a report, because a report was the only register in which she could say it without breaking. "They came for the pipeline. You were right about every gram of it. The bait drew them in."

A long breath went out of him, and something in his face eased, and she hated that she had given him that, because she had to take it back in the next sentence.

"And then they were let go," she said. "The men in the missile chain. The cyber placements. The deep ones. Someone inside the cordon opened a lane and walked them out through it while the cameras pointed at you. The network is alive, Baba. You sprang the trap and the thing you were trapping stepped over it and kept walking. You won the night and lost the war in the same hour, and the building is calling it a victory because the architect is dead and the architect was you."

He was quiet for a while. Out on the flats a dog started and stopped. The river made the sound rivers make, which is no sound, only a lowering of the air.

"Sehgal," he said. Not a question.

"Sehgal."

"You're sure."

"He told me himself. He didn't know he was telling me." She found she had taken his hand without deciding to. It was cold and the grip in it was already going. "He slipped on a detail. A time. The kind of thing only the man who arranged the night would carry. About my mother. Only I caught it, because she's the one thing he can't speak about cleanly, and because you built me to hear the place where a careful man stops being careful. You made the instrument that found him. You. Nobody else could have."

His mouth moved. It might have been the start of a smile, or only the cold working at him.

"Then it wasn't wasted," he said.

"It can't be used," she said, and her voice cracked on it for the first time, a hairline, and she pressed it shut. "There's nothing to take to a court. The proof points at the murder and the murderer holds every chain of custody that touches it. He owns the file on himself. Kaul knows and Kaul can't move. I see the whole shape of it and the shape is a wall. I can name him and the naming dies in the room. You see? That's the country you bled forty years for. It can't even afford to know what you knew."

"I never thought it would thank me," he said.

She looked at him then, fully, the way she had not let herself since she walked in, and she saw the two men he had always been, lying together in the one failing body. The one who had been right. The one who was unforgivable. There was no seam between them. She had spent the days since the cemetery trying to find the seam, the place where the patriot ended and the thief began, so that she could grieve one and condemn the other and keep them apart in herself the way a clean officer keeps two accounts. There was no such place. He had taken Janaki's daughter from the wreck of Janaki's murder because she was the one person who would one day be able to read what her mother had hidden, and because he had loved or failed the mother, and he had raised that thing in her into a weapon and never once turned it toward the part of it that was hers, eighteen years of a hand he would not let close, and that withholding was the nearest thing to tenderness he had, and it did not unmake the theft. He had poisoned a province to starve a network and called every gram a coin minted for the nation, Ravana made literal, the king who burns his own people to keep a worse fire off them. He had murdered her mother's witness through her own hand on her eighteenth birthday and let her carry the pride of it for eleven years. All of it true at once. All of it him.

"You knew about room nine-oh-four," she said. "When you sent me up the stairs. You knew what he'd say if I let him talk."

"Yes."

"You made me the cover-up of my own mother and you let me thank you for it."

"Yes."

He did not reach for softening. He never had. It was the one mercy he was capable of and he gave it now, plainly: he did not ask her to understand. She had braced for the speech, the architecture laid out one last time, the justification she had heard him give to others a hundred times and that always, always closed. It did not come. He had spent the last of himself, it seemed, on the one word that did not defend him.

"I helped them," she said. The thing she had carried out here under the report-voice, the thing she had not said even to Kaul. "I turned. I believed a true story with the centre cut out of it and I gave the hunt everything it needed and the hunt was steered by him. Every step I took toward the truth I was walking his errand. He killed you with my hands as surely as he killed her with somebody else's. I am the reason the one man who saw the whole of it is lying on a string cot in a brick agent's office bleeding into a folded towel where no doctor will ever come. I did that. So don't tell me it wasn't wasted. I'm the waste."

His hand turned in hers, the cold fingers closing with the last of what they had.

"You found him," he said. "That's not the errand. That's the war. The thing I did to you is the thing that finds the rot. I knew that when I did it." A pause to gather air that did not want to come. "I have known for eighteen years that the only justification was if you turned out to be the one who could read the lie nobody else could see. And you are. So I was either the worst man who ever loved a child, or the only one who could have stopped this. I have never been able to choose between those, and now I don't have to."

"That's not absolution," she said.

"No. I'd have said no to that anyway. You can't carry it if I hand it to you cheap." His eyes opened, found her, held. "Kabir told you the same thing, I'd guess. He's right. You don't get to be forgiven. You get to be the one who knows. It's worse. It's the only thing that's worth anything."

The lamp guttered and steadied. Somewhere over the flats, high and thin, she heard the insect note of a rotor, a drone holding a box pattern in the half-light, and her whole trained body went still around the sound, gauging the height, the arc, whether it was theirs or the building's or the principal's, whether it could read heat through a tin roof at that altitude in this cold. The reflex was so complete that for a moment it took her out of the room entirely, and she was only an operator again, listening. Then the note drifted east and faded and she came back and found his eyes still on her, and she understood he had heard it too, had watched her read it, and that he was glad of it, glad in the last minutes of his life to see the machine he had made working flawlessly, even now, even on him.

That was the unforgivable thing and the vindicated thing in a single look. She did not know how a person was meant to hold both. She held both.

"The bag," he said.

She had set it down by the door without thinking, in the line of the exit. Her mother. The tins, the ledger, the cassette, the small remains the cemetery had given back. He could not see it from where he lay. He only knew she would have brought it, because he had built someone who did not leave the one irreplaceable thing behind.

"It's here," she said.

"You didn't give it to him. In the room. When I made you give it to me." His breath snagged. He had triggered the conditioning, that night, the phrase and the protocol, had let himself believe the leash still answered, and she had let the leash close fully and spent the half-second of slack it bought her to take the bag instead of surrender it. He had reckoned every move she could make but the one she did, which was that he would rather lose to her than win. "You spent it on the bag."

"I spent it on her."

"Good," he said. "That was the only thing worth spending it on." And then, lower, almost to himself: "I never used it to keep you. You understand that. I had the words my whole life and I could have made you stay any day of eighteen years and I never said them, not once, not even at the end when saying them would have brought a surgeon and saved my life, because the moment I turned what was hers against her, for myself, I'd have proven I was only ever the thief. So I let it sit. I'm letting it sit now. That's all I have to give you. The one thing I never did."

She understood, then, what he was telling her, and the report-voice failed completely and did not come back. He could have spoken the phrase a minute ago and made her carry him out across the flats to a hospital that would have logged his face and ended Alpha and saved him. He was choosing the cot and the folded towel over that. He was dying to keep the one promise he had never spoken, the promise not to spend her on himself. It was the most loving thing anyone had ever done for her and it was being done by the man who had stolen her from her mother's body, and there was no court in the world, his country's least of all, that could weigh those two truths against each other and return a verdict, and she was the only person alive who would ever hold both in one hand and feel exactly how much each of them weighed.

"Baba," she said.

"Hum it," he said. "Not for me. So I hear it land where it belongs. So I know it got home."

She had hummed it her whole life without knowing it was a map, the lullaby threaded through the pendant against her skin since she was three weeks old, her mother's voice carrying a dead-drop's coordinates in a tune for a baby. She hummed it now, low, in the brick room with the lamp turned down and the river making its no-sound outside and the dog quiet and the drone gone east. She watched his face while she did it. She watched him hear, in the thing that had outlived its maker, the proof that he had held his own hand against itself for eighteen years and finally, at the cost of everything, let the two ends meet. The mother and the daughter. The grave and the name. His design, proven. His methods, beyond saving. The same breath carrying both out of him.

He went between one phrase of the lullaby and the next. She did not stop on the seam. She finished the tune, because her mother had written it to be finished, and because the only thing she could give him at the end was the sound of the machine running true.

When it was done the room was very quiet and the light had begun to come up grey through the shutter slats, the flat dishwater dawn of the river country, and the boy at the door shifted his weight and looked at her and waited to be told what men do now. She stood. Her knees were stiff from the cold floor. She lifted the bag and set it back in the line of the exit, the placement automatic, drilled past thought, his hand cold on the cot behind her and his discipline still moving in her hands. Whatever he had made of her would go on standing after him, because that was the only kind of monument he had ever built.

Out on the embankment the city was waking, lakhs of phones blinking alive on their chargers, the towers logging each one, the cameras opening their eyes on the arterial roads, a whole country of identity leaving its wake in the light. Somewhere in it Sehgal was rising, shaving, choosing a tie, a man of impeccable thirty-year service whom the building believed utterly, who had killed her mother and steered her hand and would walk untouched into a morning meeting where they would commend him for the victory of the night the architect died.

She knew his name. She could not say it. She was the one who knew, which was worse, which was the only thing worth anything.

She picked up the bag and went out the wrong way, off the canal, into the grey.
