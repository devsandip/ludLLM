The hotel had changed its name twice in the years since, but the lobby still smelled of cut tuberose and chilled marble, and the doorman still wore white gloves he did not need. Sita gave the revolving door a half-turn slower than the man behind her wanted, and stepped into the cold.

She had dressed for it. Not the girl's clothes, the stiff new salwar Baba had bought her that first evening so she would look like somebody's visiting daughter, but a charcoal suit cut close, flat shoes, a coat she could move in. A woman who belonged. The camera over the concierge desk found her face and held it, and she let it. There was no point any longer in being unseen. She was inside the building now, in every sense the word carried; her face sat in the service's own galleries with a clean file beside it and a colonel's signature underneath. Whatever the lobby's system logged, it logged a person the state had already decided to keep.

The restaurant was to the left, past the lift bank, behind a screen of brass and frosted glass. They had refurbished it. The carpet was new, a deep green where it had been red, and the chandeliers had been swapped for something recessed and discreet, the kind of lighting that flattered older men and the women they brought. But the bones were the same. The long banquette down the right wall. The maître d's lectern with its leather book. The corner table, second from the window, where a tall man in a grey bandhgala had once ordered for them both without looking at the card, because he had eaten here before and wanted her to know it.

"Madam has a reservation?"

"Khanna," she said. "Eight o'clock. A table for one."

He ran a finger down the page. She had made the booking that afternoon, in a name that meant nothing, paid a hold against a card that would settle and vanish. Old habit. There had been no need. She could have telephoned the duty desk and had the whole floor cleared, or walked in cold and watched them find her a table out of fear of the suit. The handler's privileges. She had not wanted them tonight. She had wanted to be a woman called Khanna who had booked ahead.

"This way."

He did not give her the corner. He gave her a two-top against the window, which suited her better, and she let him pull the chair and shake out the napkin across her lap as though she were the sort of person such ceremonies were invented for. When he had gone she sat very still and let the room arrange itself around the memory.

Eleven years.

Baba had sat where the window now framed the headlights crawling up Janpath, and she had sat with her back to the room, which she had not liked even then, eighteen years old and already counting exits without knowing she was doing it. He had ordered galauti kebab and a dal that took six hours, he said, and she had eaten because he watched her eat, and somewhere between the kebab and the bringing of the dal he had set a key on the white cloth between them and slid it across with one finger, the way the maître d' had run his finger down the page just now, and said nothing at all.

A brass key. Heavy, old-fashioned for a hotel even then, with a teardrop fob and a number stamped into the brass. Nine-oh-four. She had picked it up because he wished her to, and she had understood without being told what it was and what it asked, because he had spent the eighteen years before that evening teaching her to understand exactly that and nothing else.

She had gone up. She had come down. They had eaten the dal.

The waiter set still water before her and asked if she would like to hear the specials. She would not. She ordered the galauti and the dal, and a lime soda because she was working, and he went away pleased, and she put her hand flat against the front of her coat where the inner pocket sat over her sternum.

The key was there. She could feel the shape of the fob through the wool.

She had kept it. That was the thing she had turned over on the drive in, the thing that would not lie flat. She had killed nine people she could put a face to and a great many she could only put a method to, and she had kept almost nothing, because keeping things was how the careless were caught and how the sentimental were ruined, and Baba had drilled both lessons into her until they were reflex. And yet she had kept the key. It had lived eleven years in a tobacco tin under the false floor of a kit bag, wrapped in a strip of chamois, and she had told herself, when she let herself think of it at all, that she kept it because it was tradecraft to keep nothing the enemy could find and everything you might one day need to prove a thing to yourself.

She knew better now. She had kept it because a part of her, the part that did not get a vote, had always meant to come back and stand in that room and find out what was true.

The kebab came. She ate it because the body needed feeding and because a woman alone who did not eat drew the eye, and the meat dissolved on her tongue the way it had eleven years ago, and she thought about the man she had killed upstairs.

She had not been told his name. That was the cleanliness of it, Baba's whole art: she had gone up with a name on the key and not on the man, and she had done the thing and come down and never asked, and for eleven years he had been a closed file in her own head, a debt discharged. A money man, she had assumed, the way they were always money men, somebody who had skimmed or talked or simply outlived his use. She had built a small false biography for him over the years without meaning to, the way the mind tidies a room while you sleep, and the biography had let her keep eating dal across the table from the man who made her.

The truth she had now was worse and simpler. He had not been a money man. He had been a witness. He had known what had been done to a R&AW analyst in 2008, that she had been murdered to keep a thing buried and that the proof had not died with her, and he had been on the edge of saying so to someone who would write it down. And in the last second of his life, with her hands already at it, he had looked at her throat, at the silver lying against her collarbone where it had lain since she was three weeks old, and his face had changed. She had read the change at the time as fear and filed it as fear. It had not been fear. He had recognised the pendant. He had known whose daughter she was, and he had understood, in the time it takes a man to understand the last thing he will ever understand, that the dead woman's child had been sent to close the dead woman's mouth a second time.

She had been protecting her mother's killers from her first sanctioned breath, and she had done it well, and she had been proud.

Sita set down the fork.

The dal came. She let it sit.

Across the room a party of four was being seated at the corner table, two men her own age and two women, all of them loud with the particular ease of people who had never once in their lives counted an exit. One of the men was telling a story with his hands. The maître d' lit the small candle at their centre. None of them looked at her. The camera in the cornice looked at her, and at them, and at the waiter, and recorded a quiet restaurant on an ordinary cold night in a city that ran now on faces and numbers, where a person left a wake of glass and silicon wherever she went and the old trick of being no one had become a discipline of being someone harmless. She had spent two months learning how thoroughly she could be seen. There was a freedom in it she had not expected, and it was not a clean freedom. It was the freedom of a thing the state had decided not to hunt.

She paid before she had finished, in cash, which made the waiter blink, and added enough on top that he stopped blinking. She did not want the meal to end with a card terminal and a signature. She wanted to be able to stand up and walk out of the room as though she had simply chosen to, which she had.

The lift took her up alone. Nine-oh-four. She had not needed the brass key to learn the room was empty tonight; she had needed only to make a single call from a desk that could ask such questions and be answered, and the floor manager had confirmed it without curiosity, because curiosity about the room someone in a charcoal suit was asking after was not a thing that prolonged a career. The handler's privileges again. She had used exactly one of them, the smallest, and it sat oddly with her, the ease of it, the way a door that had cost a man his life eleven years ago now opened because she telephoned downstairs and said a number.

She let herself in with the housekeeping card the manager had left for her at the desk in an envelope with no name on it. The card worked the lock with a small green light and a click, and she stood a moment in the doorway feeling the absurdity of it, that this was how you entered a room now, and that somewhere in the building a server was writing down that card number eight-seven-something had opened nine-oh-four at twenty-one fourteen, and that none of it mattered, because nothing she did here tonight was a crime anyone would ever be permitted to name.

The room had been refurbished like the restaurant. New bed, new art, a console television where there had been a tube. But the window was the same window, and the bathroom was through the same door on the left, and she stood where she had stood eleven years ago and let the geometry of it come back into her hands.

He had been by the window. He had turned when the door opened, expecting room service or a girl or both, and she had been eighteen and slight and unremarkable, and he had relaxed, and that had been the last mistake of a long and probably careful life. It had taken under a minute. Baba had taught her a method that left a clean cause of death, and she had used it, and she had checked the pulse the way she had been taught, with two fingers and patience, and she had washed her hands in the bathroom on the left and gone down to the dal.

She crossed to the window now and looked down at the same headlights. Her reflection hung in the cold glass over the city, a woman in a dark coat with one hand at her chest.

She took the key out.

It was heavier than she remembered, or her memory had made it lighter to carry. The brass had gone dull where her thumb had worried it over the years. The number was still legible, deep-stamped, nine-oh-four, a room that no longer used keys like this and perhaps no other room in the city still did. An object out of its time. Like her. Built for a war the building no longer admitted to fighting, by a man the building had killed, against an enemy the building had pinned a medal on and posted abroad.

She had thought, driving in, that she might leave it here. Set it on the console where the next guest would find it and hand it down to the desk, and a key without a lock would travel back into the hotel's lost-property drawer and end its days among phone chargers and reading glasses, and the loop would be closed the way you close an account, by zeroing it out and walking away.

She turned the key over in her fingers and knew she would not do that.

To leave it was to let it be a thing that had happened to her. A key handed across a table by a man who owned her, a debt she had not known she was paying, a murder she had committed against her own blood while believing herself loved and trusted and clean. If she left it in the room it would stay his. It would stay the night he made.

She closed her hand around it instead, hard, until the teardrop fob bit into her palm, and she understood that this was the only act of ownership available to her in the whole vast ledger of what had been done. She could not arrest the man who had killed her mother; the committee had given him a residence and a sash and a flight out, and the proof her mother had died to preserve sat sealed in a box that the state would never open because opening it would unmake the state. She could not avenge Baba, who had been right and a liar in the same forty years and was dead either way. She could not say her mother's name aloud to a single living soul who would be permitted to write it down. She was the custodian of a silence, and silence had no door you could lock or unlock; it had only the person who agreed to keep it.

But the key was hers. She had carried it eleven years without knowing why, and now she knew, and she could carry it the rest of the way as her own thing and not his. Not a kill he had handed her. A night she had survived and come back to and stood inside on her own feet, by her own choosing, with no voice in her ear but the one she had decided to keep.

The conditioning was still in her. She felt it the way you feel an old break in cold weather, a phrase and a protocol set into her below the floor of her own will, and she did not pretend it was gone. It would never be gone. But it had not brought her here. Nobody had spoken the words that would have brought her here. She had brought herself, on a quiet evening she had stolen out of the middle of an ordinary week, and that small fact, that nobody had sent her, was the whole of what she had won, and it would have to be enough, because it was all there was.

She put the key back in the inner pocket, over her sternum, where her mother's silver already lay against her skin. The two of them sat together there now, the cipher her mother had tied around an infant's neck so the daughter might one day read the truth, and the brass that the daughter had been handed so she would help bury it. A betrayal and its undoing, carried in the same warm pocket against the same heartbeat. She did not try to make them mean one thing. They were both true. She had learned to hold that.

She switched off the lamp she had not turned on and stood a moment longer in the dark by the window, and then she let herself out of nine-oh-four and pulled the door until the lock took it.

The corridor smelled of carpet and recycled air. The housekeeping card she left in the envelope on the floor manager's desk on her way through the lobby, face down, and the man thanked her without looking up. The doorman in the gloves opened the cold for her. Down on Janpath the headlights had not changed in eleven years or in a hundred, and she walked north into them with her hand flat over her coat, feeling the small hard shapes of what she carried, and did not look back at the lit windows of the floors above to count which one was hers.
