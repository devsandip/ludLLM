The bus from Kalka shed its passengers a few at a time, and by the last climb before the monastery road there were only four of them left, and then there was only Sita.

She got down where the metalled surface gave out, at a turn the driver took without slowing because he had taken it ten thousand times, and she stood in the diesel smell until the tail lights were gone around the shoulder of the hill. Then there was the other smell, the one she had forgotten a body could hold onto for years: woodsmoke and cold stone and the particular mineral breath of snow that has not yet fallen. She had been here in autumn. It was spring now, or the high country's grudging version of it, and the rhododendrons were out along the lower path in a red that looked, in the last of the light, like something that had been done to the slope rather than grown on it.

She carried the bag herself. She had carried it the whole way, which was its own kind of discipline, because the correct tradecraft would have been to break the load, to consign the tins to three separate parcels under three separate names and let the couriers never meet. She knew the method. She had taught the method. But the method assumed you trusted a system somewhere to reassemble the parts, and she trusted no system now, so she carried the whole of it on one shoulder up a mountain like a woman bringing her own grave goods to be sorted.

Six tins. A ledger. A cassette she had listened to once and would not listen to again. Forty years of a country's rot, indexed in her dead mother's exact analyst's hand, and she had walked it up here in a canvas holdall bought for two hundred rupees at the Kalka stand because the holdall said nothing about her and the price had been paid in notes.

The gate was open. It was always open, she understood now; the security here was not in the gate but in the distance, in the four-hour climb, in the simple arithmetic that anyone who wished you harm had to want it badly enough to be seen wanting it for a very long time before they arrived. Kabir had chosen the place the way he chose everything, by subtraction. You did not hide behind a wall. You hid behind the wall's absence, and the cost of the journey, and the fact that no professional could come at you without first becoming conspicuous.

He was in the courtyard splitting kindling, and he did not stop when she came through, and he did not look up, and that too was a message, that her arrival changed nothing he was required to do before dark. She set the bag down against the wall under the eave, out of the wet, and she stood and let him finish the billet he was on. The axe went up and came down and the wood gave with a clean tearing sound, and he set the next piece on the block, and only then did he straighten and look at her, and what was in his face was not welcome and not surprise. It was a kind of recognition that she had learned to read in the months since she had last seen him. He was looking at the thing she had become and checking it against the thing he had warned her she would become, and finding the two had met.

"You did it," he said.

"It's done."

He nodded slowly, as if she had told him the price of grain. He picked up the split wood, an armful, and carried it to the stack by the kitchen door, and she followed because there was nothing else to do, and because she had walked four hours to be in a room with the only other person on earth who would not require her to pretend.

Inside, the light was a single bulb run off a panel and a battery, and a butter lamp besides, and the small mean warmth of an iron stove. He put water on. He did not ask her if she wanted tea. He made it the way it was made up here, with salt and a knob of fat, and he set the cup in front of her on the low table and took the other side himself, his back to the wall, the door in his eye, the way they both sat, the way they would always sit, in any room, for the rest of their lives.

"Tell me what's in the bag," he said.

So she told him. She told it the way she would have written a contact report, flat, sourced, no adjective that did not carry weight. The grave at Lothian and the trunk in it. The microfilm her mother had shot in 2008, frame by frame, of documents that no longer existed anywhere else because the men who could destroy them had destroyed them. The ledger that married the names to the placements: this man to the missile command, this man to the cyber directorate, this man, dead now, to the desk that wrote the rules for who was allowed to know what. The cassette. She did not describe the cassette beyond its existence, and he did not ask her to, and she understood that he had heard the shape of a thing like it before and knew you only made a man listen to such a recording if you hated him.

When she finished he was quiet for a while. The stove ticked. Somewhere up the slope a bell rope was moving in the wind, not ringing, just knocking, a wooden clapper against a wooden rim, patient and senseless.

"And you want me to keep it," he said.

"I want it somewhere it can't be reached and can't be used."

He looked at her then with something that was almost amusement, the dry thing that passed for it in him. "Those are different wants."

"I know."

"It can't be used," he said. "You know that already, or you'd have used it. The evidence is real and the chain is broken. Every page of it passes through hands the man you'd indict still controls. You put it in front of a court, it disappears between the registry and the bench, and you disappear a week after it. You put it in front of a journalist, the journalist disappears, and the story becomes a story about a deranged woman and a forgery. There is no door in that building that opens onto daylight. You found the rot. The rot is load-bearing." He said it without heat. He had clearly known it for six years. "So you don't want it used. You want it true. You want it to exist somewhere so that the lie they're going to live inside isn't total. So that one day, if the wall ever comes down, someone can climb up here and find that it was known."

She did not answer. He had said the thing she had not let herself put into words on the whole long way up, and hearing it said in his ruined level voice did not bring the relief she had half hoped it might. It brought only the dull confirmation of a diagnosis. She had carried the proof up a mountain not to win with it but to keep it from being lost, which was a smaller and quieter ambition than the one she had set out with at the cemetery wall, and the distance between the two was the exact measure of what the last sixty days had taught her.

"He's been promoted," she said.

Kabir waited.

"Not promoted. Honoured. There's a posting. An institute. A chair in his name in everything but the name, somewhere with a view and a car and a flag on it, where he can write his memoirs and never finish them and die in his bed with two ministers at the funeral." She turned the salt cup a quarter turn on the table and did not drink from it. "The committee decided. The same way they decided about my mother. They looked at what exposure would cost and they decided the country couldn't afford the truth, so they bought the lie at retail and called it stability. They did the exact thing in this room"; she stopped, because there was no this room in it, the room was a committee room a thousand miles south and four hundred metres lower, but she let the slip stand because he understood it; "the exact thing that killed her. Eighteen years apart. The same arithmetic. They've learned nothing. They were never going to learn anything. The machine isn't built to learn. It's built to continue."

"No," he agreed. "It isn't built to learn."

"And I made it possible." This was the thing. This was the thing she had come four hours up a mountain to say to the one man who could hear it without flinching and without comfort, because she could not say it to anyone who loved her, there being no such person, and she could not say it to the dead. "He had it. He had the whole of it in his hand. The pipeline, the trap, the one night that would have rolled them all up before they could activate, every sleeper, the whole twenty years of it, finished in the dark and never spoken of. He was the only instrument the country had that could do it, because the country couldn't admit the country needed it done. And I"; she heard her own voice go very even, the way it went when the rest of her was not even at all; "I read the hunt wrong, and then I read it right, and either way I was the thread they pulled. They turned me onto him with a folder of true things. They used the eye she gave me to find the one man who was on her side. I was the weapon that took down the only person fighting her war." She made herself finish it. "He saw the whole board. He saw it clearer than anyone. And the clearest eye in it was the one that got him killed, and the eye was mine, and he built it."

Kabir let the silence sit, because he was not a man who would insult a thing like that by answering it quickly. The clapper knocked up the hill. The fat lamp guttered and steadied.

"You think I came here six years ago feeling different," he said at last. It was not a question. "I killed the man who made me. He let me. He sat there and let me, because he understood why it had to be done, which was the worst part, because if he'd fought me I could have hated him cleanly. Instead he was at peace with it, and he left me the peace, and the peace doesn't keep. I've had six years to test it. You don't get absolution up here. The air's too thin. What you get is a place to put it down for a few hours a day."

"Is that the offer."

"There's no offer. There's never an offer. You asked me once whether you survive the break from your maker, and I told you the truth, that you survive it only the way I survived it, which is as something that goes on working after the part that mattered has died. You did the thing. The thing was real. A real harm was stopped, or some of it; the network's broken, the activation's blind, the blow doesn't land on the day it was built for. That's not nothing. It's the only justification there is and it's a thin one and it's true. And it doesn't fill the room back up. Nobody lives there now. I told you that. You didn't believe me, because nobody believes that part until they're standing in it."

She looked at her hands around the cup. They were her mother's hands, she had been told once, by the one man positioned to know, and she had not believed him then and could not unbelieve him now. They had done a great deal. They were steady. They were always steady; that was the design.

"I believe it now," she said.

"I know you do," he said. "That's why you walked up instead of sending it."

He took the bag. He did not make ceremony of it. He carried it to the back of the room and lifted a board she would not have known was loose, and there was a space under the floor lined against the damp, and he set the holdall into it the way you set a thing down that you have agreed to carry for a long time, and he laid the board back, and the floor was a floor again. The whole transfer took less than a minute. Forty years of a country's secret history went under a monastery floor between one breath and the next, and the bell rope knocked, and nothing in the world acknowledged it.

"I'll keep it true," he said, sitting back down. "I won't keep it safe. There's no safe. If they come for it they come for me first, and that buys you the warning, which is all anyone up here ever has. If I die of being old, there's a man at Kalka who knows to climb up and find it. After that I don't know. After that it's the wall coming down, or it's nothing." He drank. "That's the most anyone could do with it. You knew that. You did it anyway. That's not a small thing, to do the most when the most is almost nothing."

She stayed the night because the road down in the dark was a way to die for no reason, and dying for no reason was the one indulgence the work had cured her of. She lay on the floor in the next room under a blanket that smelled of other men's sleep, and she did not sleep, and somewhere past the middle of the night she put her hand to her throat out of a habit eighteen years deep, and found the pendant, and held it.

It was just metal now. That was the thing she kept arriving at and could not get past. For her whole life it had been the one constant against her skin, the small weight she touched without knowing she touched it, and she had never once suspected it was anything but an ornament a dead woman had left a baby. Then it had been the key, the literal key, the cipher her mother had hidden in a lullaby and a string of beads and a daughter's trained ear, the most dangerous object in the country worn in plain sight by the one person who could read it and didn't know she could. And now it had been read. The lullaby had been sung. The grave had been opened and the trunk lifted and the proof carried up a mountain and put under a floor. The pendant had done the only thing it was made to do, and it had done it, and what was left in her hand was a piece of worked silver gone warm from her body, encoding nothing, pointing nowhere, a keepsake again at last, which was all her mother had ever been able to call it out loud.

She thought she would feel her mother in it. She had thought, in the worst and most childish part of herself, that when it was all done and the proof was safe she would hold the thing and feel some hand reach back through it. There was no hand. There was the silver and the cold and her own pulse moving it very slightly, and the knowledge, which was not consolation and was not absent, that the woman who had tied it on her had done so in a room with an engine idling in the dark outside, betting a daughter she would never see grow up against a system she already knew would not save her, and winning the bet, and gaining nothing by it but this: that the truth existed under a floor in the mountains, and one living woman knew it, and could do nothing with it but go on.

In the morning she went down before the others were properly awake, because goodbyes were a luxury for people who expected to meet again, and she did not. The path was wet and the red flowers were heavy with it and the valley below was still in cloud, the way the high valleys are at that hour, a white floor you could believe you might walk out onto. She came down out of it into the world by stages. By the road head the cloud had thinned to mist and by the first village it was only morning, ordinary morning, a tea stall opening its shutter, a boy driving two goats, a man at the stall counting out coins into another man's palm, slow, deliberate, watching the other's face as he did it.

She watched him too. She could not help it; that was the last thing, the thing she would carry past the pendant and past the proof and past all of it, the thing that had no off. She watched the man count the coins and she read in the half-second hitch of his hand and the way his eyes went to the third coin and not the others that he was short, and knew it, and was going to pretend he wasn't, and was deciding in that instant whether the other man was the kind to check. A nothing. A few rupees over goats. And she saw it whole, the lie forming and the calculation under it and the small fear under the calculation, saw it the way her mother had seen a whole foreign war hidden in a column of clean accounts, because it was the same eye, the only inheritance that had come down to her entire, and it would never close again as long as she lived.

She drank her tea standing. She paid in coins, exact, and let the man see her count them, and walked on toward the bus while the mist burned off the road in front of her and the day, which had learned nothing and would forget her by noon, opened up ahead and asked her nothing at all.
